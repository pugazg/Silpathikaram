# 6. The Working of Past Deeds

## The Power of Fate

*Cilappatikāram* advances with the assistance of *ūḻviṉai*, the ripening force of past action. Most people in our country possess great faith in it. Hinduism, Jainism, and Buddhism all emphasise it. In the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*, Tiruvaḷḷuvar devotes ten couplets to a chapter titled “Fate,” and may be said to instil belief in the power of past deeds.

However much people plan and however carefully they attempt an action, events proceed according to the strength of fate; nothing is stronger than fate. One couplet alone is enough:

> “What power is greater than fate?  
> Whatever else one plans,  
> fate arrives first.” (380)

Yet Vaḷḷuvar also says that people who work continuously and energetically without weariness may push fate aside and defeat it:

> “Those who labour without wavering  
> and never sink in effort  
> will see even fate turn its back.” (620)

The particle “even” in “even fate” marks exceptional greatness. Saying that they conquer even unconquerable fate implies that fate is ordinarily unconquerable.

## What Is Fate?

What is *ūḻ*? The usual explanation is this: if one did good in a former birth, good happens in the present birth; if one did evil in a former birth, evil happens now. Likewise, if one does good in this birth, good will happen in the next; if one does evil now, evil will happen in the next.

## How Fate Was “Discovered”

How can anyone know in this birth whether one did good or evil in a former birth? Similarly, how can anyone know now whether good or evil will happen in the next birth?

The ancients reflected for many days and years: a person who does good in this life suffers, while one who does evil prospers—why? They found no properly fitting answer. With no alternative, they reached an inference: one who receives good now may have done good in a former birth; one who suffers now may have done evil then. Likewise, a bad person who now receives good will suffer in a later birth, while a good person who now suffers will receive good later. It was a conjecture, an estimate, an assumption. They then crowned such circumstances with the name “fate” and placed the burden upon people generation after generation.

## Tiruvaḷḷuvar’s Difficulty

Even Tiruvaḷḷuvar struggles at one point. Since the prosperity of a malicious, envious person and the ruin of an upright person free from envy contradict the expected moral order, he says the situation must be pondered:

> “The prosperity of the envious-hearted  
> and the ruin of the upright  
> are matters to be examined.” (169)

It was on this basis that the ancients invented something nonexistent and ill-fitting, called it fate, and loaded it upon the heads of succeeding generations.

## A More Fitting Explanation

Within a single lifetime, one who does good earlier may receive good later; one who does evil earlier may receive evil later. The name *ūḻviṉai* may properly be applied to good and bad actions done earlier and yielding results later in the same life.

Auvaiyār says in *Koṉṟaivēntaṉ*, “What is done in the forenoon ripens in the afternoon” (74). Tiruvaḷḷuvar similarly says that if one harms another in the morning, harm will come of itself in the afternoon without anyone deliberately causing it:

> “If one causes pain to another in the morning,  
> pain will come of itself to oneself in the afternoon.”

He does not merely connect the earlier and later parts of one lifetime, but the morning and afternoon of a single day. This may be called a genuine and very great kind of fate.

Thus the earlier and later portions of a life, or even the morning and afternoon of one day, provide one reasonable solution.

There is another. Whether good or bad happens to good people, or bad or good to evil people, events may occur naturally, accidentally, and unexpectedly. Death in a traffic accident, or loss of property or life through cyclone, flood, earthquake, volcano, war, or murder, are harms that may occur naturally or accidentally. Inheriting the property of a childless person or suddenly becoming rich through a lottery are benefits that may likewise arrive by chance. To bring in a separate force called fate and connect it with these events is like tying a knot between a bald head and a knee—an artificial and unfitting connection.

With this foundation, let us turn to *Cilappatikāram*.

The epic emphasises past deeds in many places and even presents a story of a former birth. Yet, viewed by scientific analysis, the events appear to be consequences arising later from what was done earlier in the same day or life, or else natural and accidental occurrences. Let us enter the text and examine them one by one.

## The Prologue

The prologue itself refers to fate. Because the time had come for Kōvalaṉ’s deed to ripen, the Pāṇḍya ordered his death without inquiry:

> “Because it was the time when the deed would bear fruit,  
> the neem-garlanded king failed to discern anything.” (27–28)

Three truths are said to become known through *Cilappatikāram*: righteousness becomes Death to rulers who fail in governance; the great praise the renowned Pattini; and past action ripens, comes, and forces its fruit:

> “Righteousness becomes Death to those who fail in rule;  
> the great praise the Pattini of celebrated speech;  
> and past action ripens, comes, and compels its fruit …” (55–57)

## The *Kāṉal Vari*

Kōvalaṉ suspects that Mādhavi’s yāḻ song contains a deceptive double meaning. His fate joins the suspicion, and he releases his embrace of the woman whose face is like the full moon:

> “He placed his thought upon the yāḻ music;  
> because his fate came and ripened,  
> his embracing hands loosened  
> from the woman whose face was the full moon.” (52:4–5)

## The Canto in Which the Dream Is Told

Tēvanti, Kaṇṇaki’s Brahmin friend, tells her that in a former birth she failed to observe a vow connected with her husband:

> “You are not abandoned by destiny;  
> in a former birth you broke a vow for your husband—  
> may that fault now perish.” (55–56)

The old force of past deeds is also said to bring matters together so that, before sunrise, Kōvalaṉ decides at dawn to leave Pukār with Kaṇṇaki:

> “Old action brought the end together;  
> he resolved upon the journey in the night  
> before the dense rays spread their feet.”

These are the closing words of the final *veṇpā*.

## The Canto of Seeing the Country

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki set out toward Madurai because fate drove their minds:

> “Fate pressed them onward; their minds urged them forth.” (4)

While they rest in a grove, a Jain wanderer appears. Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki, together with Kavunti, fall at the ascetic’s feet and pray that their ancient deeds be destroyed:

> “When the wanderers who proclaim dharma appeared,  
> they fell at their feet with discerning Kavunti, saying,  
> ‘May our ancient old deeds be shattered.’” (163–165)

One wanderer tells Kavunti that when fate has ripened and arrived, no one can dismiss it merely by saying, “Go away.” Like a planted seed that sprouts, matures, and yields fruit, action must bear its result:

> “See, Kavunti of surpassing excellence:  
> harsh action will not depart when told ‘depart’;  
> like a seed once sown, it comes to meet us,  
> and when the time of attachment arrives  
> it cannot be cast aside.” (170–173)

The *Tirukkuṟaḷ* similarly says that things not allotted by good destiny will not remain however painfully guarded, while things allotted will not leave even if poured away:

> “What is not one’s portion will not become one’s own though guarded;  
> what is one’s portion will not depart though carried out and poured away.” (376)

A *Nālaṭiyār* verse offers another comparison. When rain does not fall, no one can command it to fall; when it pours abundantly, no one can command it to stop. Likewise, one cannot prevent fate from bringing harm or turn away the good it grants:

> “What must be suffered cannot be removed from those who must suffer;  
> what must be received is likewise fixed.  
> When the rain fails, none can make it fall;  
> when it is abundant, none can restrain it.” (104)

## The Canto of Seeing the City

Kavunti tells Kōvalaṉ that the ignorant fail to accept and follow the instruction of the great. When evil action ripens and arrives, they suffer greatly because of their ignorance:

> “People empty of learning do not accept the natural order;  
> when evil, painful action ripens,  
> they suffer greatly with folly as the cause.” (30–32)

She further asks whether it was not harsh fate that made Naḷa, ruined by gambling, leave his wife in the deep forest at midnight:

> “Was it not harsh action  
> that made him abandon the jewelled woman  
> in the forest wilderness  
> during the middle watch of night?” (54–56)

While Kavunti, Kōvalaṉ, and Kaṇṇaki stay outside Madurai, the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ arrives. Praising Kōvalaṉ, he says: “As far as I know, you have performed good righteous deeds in this birth. The reason you have come here with Kaṇṇaki to suffer in isolation may be an action from a former birth.”

> “I know the good deeds you have done in this life.  
> Is it the fruit of a former life  
> that you have come here to suffer alone  
> with this noble shoot of a great jewel?” (91–93)

## The Canto of the Killing Ground

Before Kōvalaṉ leaves for the marketplace to sell the anklet, he speaks emotionally to Kaṇṇaki:

“Kaṇṇaki, is the cause of our suffering some enchantment practised by another, or our own powerful old fate? My mind is confused; I understand nothing.”

> “Is it sorcery? Is it harsh fate?  
> My mind is confused; I know nothing.” (61–62)

The goldsmith tells the Pāṇḍya that the thief who stole the anklet has been caught. Because it is the time for the old evil deeds of both Kōvalaṉ and the Pāṇḍya to bear fruit, the king orders the “thief” killed. Kōvalaṉ, already caught in the net of matured evil action, is slain:

> “Because it was the time when the deed would bear fruit,  
> the neem-garlanded king failed to discern anything.” (148–149)

> “He approached Kōvalaṉ,  
> who stood caught in the mature net of evil action.” (156–157)

Because Kōvalaṉ’s old evil action ripens, the Pāṇḍya’s straight sceptre bends into tyranny and Kōvalaṉ falls cut down:

> “The guardian’s straight sceptre bent, and Kōvalaṉ fell,  
> as his ancient fate ripened.” (216–217)

The canto ends with this *veṇpā*:

> “Both kinds of action will reach you;  
> therefore approach only good righteousness.  
> Through Kaṇṇaki’s husband, the sceptre that never bent on earth  
> was bent by an act returning as the fruit of the past.”

Because the Pāṇḍya possessed old evil action, his sceptre bent through Kōvalaṉ. The poet therefore advises the people of the world: both good and evil actions come to us; always perform good and righteous deeds.

## The Canto of Explanation

Madurāpati, guardian goddess of Madurai, tells Kaṇṇaki that once old action begins to ripen and act, present austerity cannot help those without rectitude:

> “When the action from your former birth comes and ripens,  
> austerity performed now cannot help the unrighteous.” (171–172)

## The Mountain *Kuravai*

After Madurai and the Pāṇḍya are destroyed, Kaṇṇaki reaches Neṭuvēḷ Hill. When the mountain people ask who she is, she replies that harsh old fate ripened, destroying Madurai and its king, and that she is the woman of cruel destiny who lost her husband there:

> “When harsh action came and ripened,  
> bringing ruin to fragrant Madurai and its king,  
> I lost my husband there and came away—  
> I am a woman of cruel fate.” (5–6)

## The Canto of the Water Consecration

The Brahmin Māṭalaṉ tells Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, who has returned with the stone borne on the heads of Kanaka and Vijaya, the story of Kōvalaṉ. At the shore of Pukār, he says, old evil action ripened; Kōvalaṉ found a contrary meaning in Mādhavi’s *vari* song, separated from her, and returned to Kaṇṇaki:

> “During the lovers’ quarrel, as fate rose and ripened  
> through the modes sung by the maiden Mādhavi,  
> he separated without reunion,  
> and with the creeper of his clan  
> entered ancient, towered Madurai …” (58–61)

## The Canto of the Memorial Stone

When Māṭalaṉ urges Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ to perform a righteous sacrifice, he says that the soul travels according to the path of deeds already performed; this is the lofty doctrine spoken by those whose vision is true:

> “The soul travels along the path of the deed it has done—  
> this is the doctrine of those whose vision does not lie.” (167–168)

This may be compared with the *Puṟanāṉūṟu*:

> “As a raft follows the course of water,  
> precious life follows its appointed course—  
> so we have understood from the vision of the discerning.” (192:9–11)

## The Canto of the Granted Boon

Mālati nurses the child of a co-wife. When an old evil deed ripens, Death takes the infant’s life:

> “Mālati nursed the child of a co-wife;  
> an old deed ripened, and Death took the child’s life;  
> she grieved for the infant.” (74–76)

Thus *ūḻviṉai* is mentioned in more than about twenty places in *Cilappatikāram*.

In the Canto of Explanation, Madurāpati is said to tell Kaṇṇaki the story of Kōvalaṉ’s former birth. In Kaliṅga, Vasu ruled Ciṅkapuram and Kumaraṉ ruled Kapilapuram. They were enemies and tried to defeat each other. No one moved within a six-*kātam* zone between their territories.

A jewel merchant named Caṅkamaṉ secretly travelled with his wife Nīli from Kapilapuram to Ciṅkapuram and traded in gems. Parataṉ, a servant of the king of Ciṅkapuram, falsely denounced Caṅkamaṉ as a spy for the enemy and caused the king to kill him.

Nīli, wife of the dead Caṅkamaṉ, suffered greatly. On the fourteenth day she climbed a mountain and cursed: “May the man responsible for my husband’s death be falsely accused and killed in his next birth. May his wife attain the state I have attained.” She then died.

That Parataṉ was reborn as Kōvalaṉ. In accordance with Nīli’s curse, he was falsely accused and killed. His wife Kaṇṇaki ended her life on a mountain summit on the fourteenth day. This is familiar to all.

The tale appears to be a constructed story intended to make us believe in past action and its power.

## Performances with the Sounding Anklet

Only because past action ripened and compelled its fruit did the renowned Pattini come to be praised by the great. Although many temples of Kaṇṇaki in Tamil Nadu have been converted into temples of Draupadī or other goddesses, priests in goddess temples still follow an old custom: they hold anklets in both hands, make them sound, and sing narrative songs in performance. The growth of this art in Tamil Nadu is connected with Kaṇṇaki’s anklet. Is this too a fruit of fate ripening and compelling its result?
