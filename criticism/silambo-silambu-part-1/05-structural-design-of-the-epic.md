# 5. The Structural Design of the Epic

Definitions have been offered of what elements make a work an epic, what makes a minor epic, and what makes a great epic. Different people may define these matters differently; no one can force a single definition upon everyone. It is enough if most of the indispensable major structures are present. On this basis, let us examine some elements of epic construction in *Cilappatikāram*.

## Benediction

“Let us praise the moon; let us praise the sun; let us praise the great rain; let us praise flower-rich Pukār” may be treated like an invocation and salutation to the divine. Because this section begins auspiciously, it is titled the Canto of Auspicious Benediction.

## Announcement of What Is to Come

The declarations that righteousness becomes Death to rulers who fail in justice, that the great praise the celebrated Pattini, and that past deeds ripen and compel their fruit announce what is to come. The prologue’s naming of the thirty cantos that follow serves the same purpose.

## The Four Aims

All four—righteousness, wealth, pleasure, and liberation—appear in the work. The teachings of Kavunti, Māṭalaṉ, the Jain wanderers, and others represent righteousness. Kōvalaṉ’s attempt to seek wealth, the Pāṇḍya’s governance, and Cēra military victories belong to wealth and polity. The joy of Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ, the festivities of Mādhavi and Kōvalaṉ, and the townspeople’s happy recreation belong to pleasure. Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ’s ascent to the higher world, the Cēra’s sacrifice, and worship at Kaṇṇaki’s shrine belong to liberation.

## Peerless Hero and Heroine

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki, born in great merchant families, are peerless epic protagonists.

## Descriptions of Mountain, Sea, Country, and City

The taking of the stone from Himālaya and the Mountain *Kuravai* provide mountain settings. The Canto of the Sea-Bath, in which the people of Pukār bathe in the sea, concerns the ocean. Descriptions of the Cōḻa and Pāṇḍya lands, Madurai, and the Cēra realm supply country and city descriptions.

## Seasons and Times

The arrival of summer in the Canto of Summer represents the major season. The Canto Celebrating the Evening represents a minor division of time.

## The Two Luminaries

The moon drives away the mischievous evening and rules among the stars. In the Canto of Seeing the City, the sun rises as birds call and lotuses open, awakening Madurai. In the Canto in which the City Celebrates Indra’s Festival, the sun rises from the eastern mountain and removes Mother Earth’s dark covering with its hands of rays. At Vēṅkaṭam in the evening, the moon appears in the east and the sun in the west.

## Marriage, Coronation, Groves, and Water-Play

The marriage is that of Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ. After the death of Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, conqueror of the northern army, his younger brother Veṟṟivēl Ceḻiyaṉ is crowned.

The *Kāṉal Vari* shows the people of Pukār enjoying the grove. During Indra’s festival, they play in the water in the Canto of the Sea-Bath.

## Drinking, Children, Quarrel, and Union

The citizens of Pukār rejoice in drink. Maṇimēkalai is born to Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi. Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ appears as a child to comfort Mālati, and a naming festival is held for Maṇimēkalai.

Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi, the Pāṇḍya and his queen, and the citizens of Pukār experience lovers’ quarrels and later union.

## Counsel, Embassy, March, and Victory

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ holds council. Mādhavi sends Vayantamālai and Kōcikaṉ as messengers to Kōvalaṉ. Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ marches north and wins victory. The internal division into thirty cantos is itself an epic structural feature.

## Emotional Modes

The eight embodied emotional flavours named in *Tolkāppiyam*—laughter, sorrow, abasement, wonder, fear, pride, anger, and delight—are all present in *Cilappatikāram*.

### Laughter

Separated from her husband, Kaṇṇaki gives a false smile to gladden her father-in-law and mother-in-law. When her husband returns from Mādhavi, she welcomes him with a smile. When Cāliṉi praises her, Kaṇṇaki grows shy and smiles.

After Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki have travelled only one *kāvatam* from Pukār, Kaṇṇaki’s feet hurt. Sighing, she gently shows her teeth in a smile and asks, “Where is ancient Madurai? How much farther is it?” Kōvalaṉ laughs and replies that Madurai is nearby. Tolkāppiyar says laughter arises from ridicule, youth, innocence, or folly. These smiles may be attributed to Kaṇṇaki’s innocence.

Elsewhere the work tells how some northern kings, afraid of Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, escaped in various disguises. That passage is highly comic and may be classified as laughter arising from ridicule.

### Sorrow

The lamentations of Kaṇṇaki, Mālati, and others represent sorrow.

### Abasement

A man and woman who mocked Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki are transformed into jackals and suffer degradation.

### Wonder

The Jain wanderers’ arrival through the sky, Kavunti’s curse turning the mockers into jackals, Kaṇṇaki’s wrenching off her left breast and burning Madurai, and knowledge of former and later births all produce wonder.

### Fear

Fear appears when an elephant seizes an aged Brahmin with its trunk; when Mātari and others see evil omens; and when Kōvalaṉ is accused by the state of theft and murder.

### Pride or Heroic Exultation

Kōvalaṉ subdues the elephant and shines as a warrior of compassion and a benefactor of the poor. Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi possess skill in playing the yāḻ; Mādhavi excels in dance and song; Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ wins in the north.

### Anger

Kaṇṇaki’s wrath destroys the Pāṇḍya, his queen, and Madurai. Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s wrath makes Kanaka and Vijaya bear the stone. Veṟṟivēl Ceḻiyaṉ imposes the death penalty upon the goldsmiths.

### Delight

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki enjoy domestic virtue. Through the splendour of his wealth, Kōvalaṉ joins Mādhavi and experiences pleasure. The people rejoice in sea-bathing and recreation in groves.

In addition, the epic contains the three crowned kings, doctrines of several religions, temples of many gods, numerous public halls, many natural scenes, commerce, many performances of dance and song, the Hunter’s Song, the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*, and the Mountain *Kuravai*. By all these features, *Cilappatikāram* unquestionably deserves the great name of a structurally complete great epic.
