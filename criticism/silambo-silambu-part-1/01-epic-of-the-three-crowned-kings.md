# 1. The Epic of the Three Crowned Kings

In ancient times, when monarchy prevailed, kings fought one another. Even in the twentieth century, when republican government is practised, there is no shortage of war. Nations fight one another at the instigation of their leaders. Although the natural impulse toward aggression exists in everyone, political leaders often seem not to accept the responsibility of sublimating it, redirecting it, and overcoming it.

Tamilakam offers a particular example. In antiquity, the three crowned dynasties—the Cōḻas, Pāṇḍyas, and Cēras—along with lesser rulers and chieftains who were either allied with them or independent of them, fought one another almost without pause. If we read the books written throughout the world under the heading “history,” we find that they are filled largely with accounts of wars.

In ancient Tamilakam, poets such as Auvaiyār appear to have travelled as emissaries between kings and laboured to preserve peace. We also find poems in which poets praised the rulers who supported them. But if there was one exalted poet of unity who regarded the Cēra, Pāṇḍya, and Cōḻa—the three crowned kings—with equal vision, we may say that it was Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ, the poet of *Cilappatikāram*.

Aṭikaḷ divided his work into three great books: the Pukār Book, belonging to the Cōḻas; the Madurai Book, belonging to the Pāṇḍyas; and the Vañci Book, belonging to the Cēras. For this reason his *Cilappatikāram* possesses the distinction of being “the Epic of the Three Crowned Kings.” There are many further proofs.

## The Canto of Summer

In the Canto of Summer, the poet describes the arrival of the early summer season. He sings that early summer, the joyful companion of Māraṉ, the Love-god who rules Madurai, Uṟantai, Vañci, and Pukār in Tamilakam—whose boundaries are Tiruvēṅkaṭam in the north and Kumari in the south—has arrived:

> “In the cool, well-watered land whose Tamil boundary lies  
> between the hill of the Tall One and the sea of the bangle-wearing maiden,  
> in towered Madurai, in exalted Uṟantai,  
> in bustling Vañci and Pukār,  
> early summer has come—the delightful companion  
> of King Māraṉ, famed in speech,  
> who sits enthroned and rules them.” (1–7)

When the poet merely wished to say that early summer had arrived, there was no necessity to mention the capitals of all three crowned kings. Yet he names Madurai of the Pāṇḍyas, Uṟantai and Pukār of the Cōḻas, and Vañci of the Cēras. He has used this place as an opportunity to express his feeling of unity.

“The Tall One” is Tirumāl. “The bangle-wearing maiden” is Kumari. The word *pauvam*, meaning sea, tells us that the southern boundary, like the eastern and western boundaries, was ocean. At this point the commentator Aṭiyārkkunallār explains at length how the cruel sea swallowed the Kumari ridge.

## Metrical Craft

One feature of Iḷaṅkō’s metrical design deserves attention here. He often places alliteration corresponding to the first metrical foot in the third or fourth foot; when he does not use alliteration, he supplies second-syllable rhyme. The quoted passage makes this clear. *Neṭiyōṉ–toṭiyōḷ* form second-syllable rhyme; *Tamil–taṇ* form alliteration; *māṭa–pīṭār* form second-syllable rhyme; *kali–oli* form second-syllable rhyme; *aracu–uraicāl* form second-syllable rhyme; and *maṉṉaṉ–makiḻ* form alliteration. The same feature can be observed elsewhere.

## The Canto of the Memorial Stone

Through the three crowned kings, Kaṇṇaki is said to have imparted three lessons. Through the Cōḻa she declared that women’s chastity can flourish only when rulers govern without allowing the straight sceptre to deviate. Through the Pāṇḍya she showed that just kings cannot remain alive when their sceptre bends. Through the Cēra Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, conqueror of the northern mountain, she demonstrated that kings do not abandon their anger until they have fulfilled the vow they uttered. The poem says:

> “The ancient good saying of cool Tamil declares:  
> ‘Unless mighty rulers administer justice,  
> chastity cannot flourish among women of great renown.’  
> Because she was the Pattini whom the earth worships and praises,  
> she gave that lesson to the king  
> whose crown bears the ātti garland;  
> she gave the southern guardian king the lesson  
> that life cannot continue when the straight sceptre bends;  
> and, so that the kings of the northern quarter might know  
> that sovereigns do not lay aside fierce anger  
> until their declared vow is fulfilled,  
> she gave that lesson to the conqueror of the western quarter.” (207–217)

“The king whose crown bears the ātti garland” means the Cōḻa. “The guardian of the southern land” means the Pāṇḍya king who guards the southern part of Tamilakam. “The conqueror who lives in the western quarter” is Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, ruler of western Tamilakam. Here too Aṭikaḷ brings all three great dynasties together.

## The Canto of Benediction

### The Benedictions

In the Canto of Benediction, women take turns singing about the three crowned kings. Under the heading “Benediction,” we read:

> “Long live, long live  
> the ancient line of the lord of Madurai,  
> encircled by the ever-flowing waters of the Vaigai!” (13)

> “Long live, long live  
> the ancient line of the lord of Vañci,  
> encircled by the cool Porunai  
> of ever-flowing waters!” (14)

> “Let us sing, let us sing  
> the lord of the Kāviri land,  
> O flower-haired women of Pukār!” (15)

Thus the lord of Madurai, the lord of Vañci, and the lord of the Kāviri land are all blessed. The water-richness of their countries is also celebrated. The Cōḻa is lord of the Kāviri whose waters keep coming—*varupuṉal*, “ever-flowing water.”

### The *Ammāṉai* Songs

*Ammāṉai* is a game in which three women toss counters or a ball while singing and dancing. The first woman raises a question; the second answers it; the third brings the thought to its conclusion. After the benedictions there are four *ammāṉai* songs about the Cōḻa. We may take the first as an example:

> “Who is the mighty one who ruled the world  
> ringed by swelling waters  
> and guarded the lofty fortress  
> of the king of the gods—tell me, *ammāṉai*!  
> The one who guarded that lofty fortress  
> is the Cōḻa who hurled down the three suspended forts  
> in the high sky—see, *ammāṉai*!  
> Sing the Cōḻa’s city of Pukār, *ammāṉai*!” (16)

The three women end each line of their song with “*ammāṉai, ammāṉai*” as they dance. There are three more such songs. We may next turn to the Pāṇḍya.

### The Ball Songs

*Kantukam* means ball; *vari* means song. These are songs sung while playing ball. The section contains three four-line songs. The third and fourth lines of one song recur as the third and fourth lines of all three:

> “We strike the ball as we move, crying,  
> ‘Long live, long live the southern king!’  
> We strike the ball, crying,  
> ‘Long live the chest adorned with the gods’ garland!’” (21)

These lines close all three songs.

### The Swing Songs

The Cōḻa receives the *ammāṉai* songs; the Pāṇḍya receives the ball songs. Third, three swing songs are sung for the Cēra. *Ūcal* means swing. The closing lines of the three songs are:

> “Shall we sing the emblem of the curved bow and swing?” (23)
>
> “Shall we sing how he felled the kadamba and swing?” (24)
>
> “Shall we sing the emblem of the mighty bow and swing?” (25)

They refer to the Cēra’s bow emblem and his victory in felling the kadamba tree.

At the end of the Canto of Benediction, one pestle song is sung for each of the three dynasties. A *vaḷḷai* song is sung about a leader while pounding some substance in a mortar with a pestle. The songs are:

### The Pestle Songs

> “The women of Pukār,  
> beneath the shade of flowering kāñci trees,  
> pound splendid pearls  
> with sweet sugarcane as their fine pestle.  
> Their true song is the song  
> of the broad, mountain-like shoulders of the Cempiyan,  
> whose firm chariot bears the discus-banner  
> and whose garland is thick with fresh flowers.  
> It is the song that makes maidens cry aloud!” (26)

> “The women of towered Madurai  
> pound song-worthy pearls  
> with a coral pestle.  
> Their true song is the song  
> of the fish-banner of the Pañcavaṉ,  
> whose shoulders shine with the garland  
> received from the king of the gods.  
> It is the song that crushes the hearts  
> of those who wear neem garlands!” (27)

> “The women of Vañci  
> pour excellent jewelled pearls  
> into a sandalwood mortar  
> and pound them with a pestle of elephant tusk.  
> Their true song is the fame  
> of the Cēra who crossed and destroyed his enemies  
> and felled the kadamba.  
> It is the song that spreads over and covers the earth;  
> it is the song that captures hearts with the palmyra wreath!” (28)

These are play-songs. The women are imagined as pounding with pestles made of sugarcane, coral, and elephant tusk; the mortar itself is made of sandalwood. Is this not literature fit to be savoured? Māṇikkavācakar’s *Tiruvācakam* likewise contains a section called *Tiruppoṟcuṇṇam*. Each of its twenty songs ends with the refrain, “Let us pound the sacred golden powder.”

When we examine this Canto of Benediction, we are tempted to wonder whether Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ used the whole canto chiefly to praise the excellence of the three crowned kings.

## The Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*

At the very opening of the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*, the poem declares that the fish emblem of the Pāṇḍyas, the tiger emblem of the Cōḻas, and the bow emblem of the Cēras were engraved upon Mount Himālaya:

> “Upon the brow of Himālaya where the carp was drawn,  
> beside it were drawn the tiger and the bow …” (1–2)

## The Canto of Explanation

The Canto of Explanation is spoken to Kaṇṇaki by Madurāpati, the guardian goddess of Madurai. Here too the excellence of all three dynasties is expressed.

### The Cēras

The poem celebrates these Cēra achievements: Utiyaṉ Cēralātaṉ’s provision of the Great Meal to warriors during the Bhārata war; Palyāṉai Celkeḻu Kuṭṭuvaṉ’s aid to the Brahmin Pālaik Kautamaṉār; Neṭuñcēralātaṉ’s victory in felling the kadamba; and the engraving of the bow upon Himālaya. The verses include:

> “The broad hand of the firm-speared king  
> who provided the Great Meal;  
> the great and sacred seat he gained …” (55–56)

> “Hearing the liberality of the Cēra of the clustering spear …” (62)

> “The strong and tall-speared Cēral  
> who gave a dwelling in heaven  
> to the Brahmin of resonant Tamil …” (63–64)

> “Long live the guardian who felled the kadamba of the sea!  
> Long live the king who carved the bow on the mountain cleft!  
> Long live the Poṟaiyaṉ of the cool, flower-filled Porunai!  
> Long live the king Māntarañ Cēral!” (81–84)

We may next consider the Cōḻas.

### The Cōḻas

The poem records the greatness of two Cōḻas: Cibi, who mounted the balance and cut flesh from his own body for the sake of a dove; and Manu-nīti Cōḻa, who killed his own son and rendered justice to a cow whose calf had been killed. The verse says:

> “The one who entered the balance for a dove,  
> the one who rendered justice to a cow—  
> the king of Pukār, whose fields bloom beside flower-rich waters.” (58–59)

We may next consider the Pāṇḍyas.

### The Pāṇḍyas

During the reign of Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, it is said that no sound was heard except the sound of the Vedas—not even the sound of the bell of judicial appeal. He was a conqueror who understood the just order of the straight sceptre, not a cruel ruler whom his citizens accused:

> “Apart from the sound of Vedic tongues,  
> he never heard the tongue of the appeal-bell.  
> None but kings who refused to bow at his feet  
> could accuse his sceptre before his people.” (31–34)

### The Concluding Statement

The concluding section of the Canto of Explanation speaks at length of Pāṇḍya excellence. The Pāṇḍya line possessed righteousness, valour, and power. Ancient Madurai grew in refinement. It possessed splendid festivals, a flourishing population, abundance of food, the wealth bestowed by the great Vaigai, the water-richness of unfailing skies and new rain, *vari* dances, *kuravai* dances, and many other forms of prosperity. It produced Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, who, when his sceptre failed, abandoned the royal throne with his queen, fell dead, and thereby straightened the bent sceptre. The passage says:

> “Among the three crowned kings,  
> those of the broad-armed, weapon-bright Pāṇḍya line  
> possessed righteousness, valour, and power.  
> Their ancient, mighty city grew in excellence;  
> festivals multiplied in splendour;  
> the immortals came there.  
> In their land of unceasing delight  
> the people prospered and food increased;  
> their mighty Vaigai poured forth wealth;  
> the unfailing sky shed new rain.  
> The two dramatic forms, *ārabhaṭi* and *sāttuvati*,  
> the timely *vari* and *kuravai*,  
> and all these together with stores of other wealth  
> appeared in a distinctive and harmonious order.  
> Having crossed the army of the northern Āryas  
> and brought all southern Tamilakam beneath his sight,  
> Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ  
> died upon the royal throne  
> with his queen of faultless chastity …” (1–18)

Thus, by looking upon all three crowned dynasties with equal vision and celebrating each, Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ has made his *Cilappatikāram* worthy of the title “Epic of the Three Crowned Kings.” If such a vision of unity found a place in the present world, the world of suffering might become a world of happiness.

## An Epic of Citizens

Because the epic honours all three crowned kings, it may be called an Epic of the Three Crowned Kings. Yet its hero and heroine, Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki, are ordinary citizens. It may therefore also be singled out as an Epic of Citizens.
