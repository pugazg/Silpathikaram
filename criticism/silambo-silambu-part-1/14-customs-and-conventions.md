# 14. Customs and Conventions

We can identify in *Cilappatikāram* a number of customs and conventions observed among the people of its age.

A courtesan’s first formal dance debut began only after the ceremonial *talaikkōl* had been taken in procession through the city and worshipped. The *Canto of the Debut Performance* says:

> The *talaikkōl*, saluted and ritually worshipped …  
> *(3:120)*
>
> … was carried in procession through the city,
> brought inside, and placed before them.  
> *(3:128)*

When entering a mansion or entering a wedding house for the first time, women placed the right foot first. Mādhavi mounted the stage in this way:

> Placing her right foot first, she ascended the stage.  
> *(3:131)*

There was belief in dreams and omens; an entire canto is entitled *The Canto that Reveals the Nature of the Dream*.

> The dark eye of Kaṇṇaki and the reddened eye of Mādhavi
> throbbed in due order, the one on the left and the other on the right.  
> *(5:27)*

This reveals belief in bodily omens. Mādhavi’s eyes are called red because she was then enjoying union.

Warriors sometimes offered their own lives so that their king might gain victory:

> “May the victorious king obtain triumph!”—
> they made the noble altar receive the auspicious offering,
> while the thunderous cry rose from those whose lives became the sacrifice.  
> *(5:85–87)*

On the fifth day after a child’s birth, the period of birth impurity was ended and a naming ceremony was held. This happened after Mādhavi gave birth to Maṇimēkalai:

> The tender-shoot-bodied maiden Mādhavi
> bore a milk-mouthed child; and after
> the five days of purification had passed,
> the venerable courtesans said,
> “Let us establish an auspicious name for Mādhavi’s daughter.”  
> *(15:22–26)*

Before an elephant came through the streets, a drum was beaten to warn the people:

> Attendants bearing the royal broad-handed emblem
> caused the drum to rise and sound.  
> *(3:124–125)*

> With the mahout dismounted and drums beaten everywhere,
> the furious charging elephant came to seize him.  
> *(15:46–47)*

Certain people received the title *Eṭṭi*. Cāyalaṉ, for example, was an *Eṭṭi*-title holder.

When people are weighed down by private grief and someone asks, “Why do you look so troubled?”, they sometimes conceal the truth by saying, “Nothing—just a slight headache.” Queen Kōpperuntēvi became jealous because the Pāṇḍya king Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ was absorbed in the dancers’ music and performance. Yet she hid her resentment and claimed that a headache was troubling her. Do people not still do this? Is “headache” not one of the familiar reasons entered on an office leave application? Even the queen followed the practice:

> Thinking that the forms of the Madurai women’s dances,
> the divisions of their songs, and the effects of their modes
> had captured the guardian king’s heart,
> the chief queen concealed the resentment within her,
> placed the blame upon a troubling headache,
> and withdrew without joining him.  
> *(16:131–136)*

Sacrifice to deities existed then. There were many forms of worship, including reverence for chaste wives. Jainism and Buddhism were widely followed.

Belief in fate and former birth has been discussed at length elsewhere. There was also belief in spirits, ghosts, and demons, including the belief that a spirit would strike and devour wrongdoers: “the spirit-square where the spirit strikes and eats” (5:134).

Courtesanship existed. During festivals men and women wandered about happily and sought pleasure.

Although there were many religions, many people acted without sectarian discrimination. When Kōvalaṉ left Pukār with Kaṇṇaki, he worshipped at temples of Viṣṇu, the Buddha, and the Arhat:

> He passed after circling the shrine of the jewel-hued Lord
> who lay in rare sleep upon the beautiful serpent.  
> *(10:9–10)*

> The green-leaved Bodhi with its five high-spreading branches …  
> *(10:11)*
>
> He passed the seven Indra monasteries …  
> *(10:14)*
>
> At the shrine of the Arhat established in five forms …  
> *(10:18)*
>
> he worshipped the radiant stone seat and circled it.  
> *(10:25)*

The first is Vaiṣṇava, the next Buddhist, and the third Jain. Kōvalaṉ evidently made no religious distinction.

There is another instance. Before Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ left to campaign in the north, he worshipped Śiva at Vañji and wore upon his head the flower garland given there. When he later reached the temple of Viṣṇu, he wore the sacred remnant—the garland offered there—upon his shoulder. This is described in the *Canto of Taking the Auspicious Sword and Umbrella*.

## Marriage Customs

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki married according to a particular custom. Kaṇṇaki was twelve years old—“a maiden of two times six years”—and Kōvalaṉ sixteen—“a youth of two times eight.” The commentators on *Tolkāppiyam* also mention these ages.

In his commentary on the aphorism beginning “birth, lineage, manliness, and age” in the *Meyppāṭṭiyal*, Pērāciriyar explains that womanhood and manhood mature respectively at twelve and sixteen.

Iḷampūraṇar, commenting on *Kaḷaviyal* 2, says that equality of age means correspondence of maturity: after childhood, the male is sixteen and the female twelve.

Modern law does not permit marriage below eighteen for a woman or twenty-one for a man. Child marriage occurred in the old period. I remember reading somewhere that Mahatma Gandhi, speaking of his own child marriage, said that he thought a little girl had merely been brought to play with him. Child marriage was once compulsory among Brahmins and gradually infected other communities. Today, by contrast, some “young old women” who have passed thirty-five find it difficult to obtain a bridegroom. Alas, cruel society!

Because the parents of the *Cilappatikāram* couple were exceedingly wealthy, they seated ornamented women upon elephants and sent them around the city to announce the wedding:

> Seating jewelled women upon the backs of elephants,
> they proclaimed the marriage to the great city.  
> *(1:43–44)*

This appears to have been a custom among the upper classes. Could it be done today?

A day when the Moon joined the star Rohiṇī was considered auspicious, and the marriage took place then. Rohiṇī is one of the twenty-seven stars beginning with Aśvinī. Myth makes these stars daughters of Dakṣa and wives of the Moon. The Moon loved Rohiṇī above the others, provoking Dakṣa’s anger. Viewed through astronomy, the basis of the story becomes intelligible.

A venerable Brahmin guided Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki according to Vedic rule, and they circled the fire. Women carried sandal, incense, lime powder, lamps, and sprouting ceremonial pots. The women blessed Kaṇṇaki, who resembled Arundhatī, and “raised her onto the auspicious bed”:

> Praying, “May evil depart,” and scattering small flowers,
> they raised upon the auspicious, lovely bed
> her who was like Arundhatī in this wide world.  
> *(1:62–64)*

Commentators merely gloss the phrase as “placed her on the bed.” I, Sundara Shanmuganar, venture a timid suggestion: why should it not mean that on the very night of the wedding they placed her on the bed and arranged the first night? Otherwise, why not say that they seated her on a chair? *Amaḷi* means a bed. Since a bride may hesitate in shyness on the first night, the poet says they “raised” her onto it.

Some families conduct the first night on the wedding day; others do not. Some say that it is best to unite the couple at once rather than guard them for several days under a regimen of abstinence. Others prefer that they first become acquainted and lose their shyness. Some couples begin direct action without the elders’ knowledge before an auspicious day can be selected. In some families an auspicious day is chosen for the first night and a feast is held as though it were a separate festival.

The marriage of Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki raises a controversy. *Cilampu* says that a priest conducted rites with a sacred fire. Some call this an Āryan, not a Tamil, method and demand its abolition. Others say it may be followed precisely because it appears in *Cilappatikāram*.

Every family may have its own custom. The presence of one rite in *Cilappatikāram* does not prove that all Tamils of the period followed an Āryan method. In 1960 I presided over the marriage of P. Sāmiyappaṉ at Elukāṉvalacu in the former Periyar district. When I asked about their old practice, they said that from generation to generation they had never employed a Brahmin; a respected Tamil elder conducted the marriage. In 1969 I presided over the marriage of Kumara Ñāṉapūraṇam at Kuruntampaṭṭu in Ramanathapuram district. There too they said that a Tamil elder had always conducted their weddings.

Thus the rite described by *Cilappatikāram* may have belonged to one section of society. It cannot bind everyone.

## Other Customs

Shops raised announcement flags to show what was sold there:

> In the splendid street, dealers who tested bright gold
> raised shining flags and removed the buyer’s uncertainty.  
> *(14:203–204)*

Foreign merchants such as Yavanas were numerous in Tamilakam.

Those who circumambulated an object did so three times. Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ circled Kaṇṇaki’s sanctuary three times:

> Following the rightward course, he came around three times,
> worshipped, and stood before the sovereign of the world.  
> *(30:155–156)*

Men seem to have ridden mules. The *Canto of the Sea Festival* says that Kōvalaṉ rode one to the shore:

> The sky-generous-handed woman mounted the *attiri*.  
> *(6:119)*

*Attiri* means a mule, though some call it a kind of horse. Some village horses look very much like donkeys; perhaps it was such an animal.

Kōvalaṉ tells Māṭalaṉ of his dream and fears that, because dreams seen in the latter part of the night are said to come true, some harm will quickly befall him:

> In the midnight darkness I saw a dream as vivid as waking;
> I fear that it will swiftly come upon us here.  
> *(15:105–106)*

Some commentators interpret “midnight watch” as the last watch. The phrase itself does not literally mean the last watch; they seem to infer it from “will swiftly come.” A verse in the *Dream Treatise* provides internal evidence. A dream in the first watch bears fruit within a year; one in the second within eight months; one in the third within three months; one in the fourth or final watch within ten days:

> What is fashioned in the first watch bears fruit in one year;
> what is seen in the second, they say, arrives in eight months;
> what falls in the middle, the third watch, in three months;
> but the dream of the final watch yields its fruit within ten days.  
> *(3)*

The fourth watch extends from about three to six in the morning. Because the poem says “swiftly,” commentators probably concluded that Kōvalaṉ meant this last watch.

In moments of public joy, kings released prisoners and remitted taxes. The victorious Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ did so:

> “Open the prison fortress everywhere;
> in every good town marked by taxation, grant remission.”
> He dispatched Aḻumpil Vēḷ and the revenue accountants
> throughout the ancient land ringed by sounding seas.  
> *(28:203–206)*

The *Canto of Explanation* says that the Pāṇḍya king acted similarly:

> “Open every prison fortress;
> remit the dues of all people burdened by taxation.”  
> *(23:126–127)*

Another custom concerned serving food to one’s husband: the wife washed his feet and sprinkled water on the floor before placing the leaf. Kaṇṇaki did so when feeding Kōvalaṉ. Merchant families apparently performed certain rites before eating:

> With her fragrant-flower hand she received
> the water from her beloved’s feet in a fired-clay bowl.  
> *(16:38–39)*

> She sprinkled water and smoothed the ground with her hand …  
> *(16:41)*

> For those who came after kings, she completed once
> the proper rite according to the rare scripture and their tradition.  
> *(16:44–45)*

## Spirit Possession

Women were believed to become possessed by a deity and utter oracles. In the *Hunter’s Song*, the deity possesses Cāliṉi, priestess of the hunter community:

> Loud-mouthed Cāliṉi, bound by an ancient debt,
> became filled with the god; the hairs of her body stood erect …  
> *(12:7–8)*
>
> In the assembly at the centre of the settlement she shifted her feet and danced.  
> *(12:11)*

The *Canto of Granting the Boon* says that the deity Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ possessed Tēvanti and spoke:

> Tēvantikai rose, possessed by the deity,
> before the lord of the kuṟiñci land rich in fresh shoots.  
> *(30:45–46)*

> “I am Pācaṇṭaṉ; I have entered the Brahmin woman.”  
> *(30:69–70)*

Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ thus spoke through Tēvanti to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ. The poem also says that Kaṇṇaki manifested through Tēvanti and addressed Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ:

> She appeared in splendour upon Tēvantikai.  
> *(30:172)*

Kaṇṇaki is said to have spoken many things through Tēvanti’s mouth.

Possession, ecstatic dancing, and oracle-giving continue today. People ask questions of the possessed woman, who says whatever comes to her mind. If she cannot answer, she may cry, “Hey! Are you testing me? Are you testing me? I am going back up the mountain!” and abruptly end the possession. Scientists are said to regard some emotionally induced possession as a form of hysteria. A conclusion is possible only after further investigation.

## The Custom of Greeting a King

One must also mention the custom of beginning an address to an elder or king with a blessing, and sometimes ending with another blessing. *Cilappatikāram* follows this convention in several places.

Before telling the Pāṇḍya king that Kaṇṇaki waited outside, the gatekeeper began: “Long live our king of Koṟkai, long live!” (20:30).

Before speaking to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ about Maṇimēkalai, Tēvanti blessed him:

> May the prince’s victory rise without diminution,
> and may the land pour forth great abundance.  
> *(30:6–7)*

When the hill people began telling Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ about Kaṇṇaki, they said:

> We are your servants through seven births; may your victory live!  
> *(25:56)*

They concluded:

> May you live for many hundreds of thousands of years!  
> *(25:63)*

The Tamil *nūṟāyiram*, “one hundred thousand,” is the native equivalent of the Sanskrit-derived *lakṣam*.

Before recounting earlier events to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, Māṭalaṉ praised him:

> “King of kings, live long!”  
> *(30:118)*

He ended:

> Guarding the world age after age,
> may you live long, great one!  
> *(30:145–146)*

When Cāttaṉār, author of *Maṇimēkalai*, finished telling Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ what had happened at Madurai, he said:

> I have related it here without omission;
> may your victorious triumph flourish through age after age,
> generation after generation.  
> *(25:91–92)*

Before Villavaṉ Kōtai addressed Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, he too blessed the king:

> “May your victory live for many years!”—
> thus Villavaṉ Kōtai spoke to the king.  
> *(25:150–151)*

The king was blessed not only in direct address but also when his command was publicly proclaimed by drum:

> Long live our king, the great one among rulers;
> may he guard the world through every age!  
> *(25:181–182)*

Only after this blessing did the herald announce the order. Blessing kings was a settled convention.

This reminds me of a song we sang before class when I was a schoolboy:

> Long live, long live King George the Fifth;
> long may he live!

Such are the transformations of time.

## A Courtesan’s Debut and Choice of Patron

*Cilampu* reveals another rare convention. A young woman born into the courtesan community and trained in dance and music appears to have gained the right to choose a patron or enter professional artistic life only after formally displaying her art before the king. After Mādhavi’s debut, she announced that whoever purchased the emerald garland given by the king might obtain her, and sent it to the market through a companion. Kōvalaṉ, wandering there, bought the garland and reached Mādhavi. People say, “Honour has boarded a ship and sailed away”; here, before going that far, the honour of Mādhavi and Kōvalaṉ climbed onto the marketplace itself. It was a method lacking both culture and civility.

*Maṇimēkalai* also indicates this performance convention. When Mādhavi and Maṇimēkalai did not attend the Indra festival at Pukār, public gossip arose:

> Because Mādhavi did not come with Maṇimēkalai,
> unappeased distress and gossip rose from mouth to mouth.  
> *(2:4–5)*

Mādhavi’s mother Cittirāpati summoned Vayantamālai and asked her to tell Mādhavi that the whole city was talking and to bring her back:

> Calling Vayantamālai to come, she said,
> “Go tell her of the gossip raised in the prosperous great city.”  
> *(2:8–9)*

The twin epics therefore show that such a convention existed. It is said to survive here and there today in a diminished form: in the past the debut had to occur before the king; today it may take place before some prominent leader. With time this too will diminish and disappear. Time is by nature changeable.

## The *Muṭaṅkal*—Letter

Today the letter overcomes distance and time through the postal service; then came the telephone and later television, making communication immediate and uninterrupted. This is science’s divine play. Among Tamil literary works, *Cilappatikāram* is the first to introduce the writing of a *muṭaṅkal*, a letter. Mādhavi sent two letters to Kōvalaṉ, one through Vayantamālai and one through Kōcikaṉ. The custom had already begun in that age.

## Worship of the Chaste Wife

It appears possible to say that *Cilappatikāram* first introduced Pattini worship into Tamil literature. Hero-stone worship should not be confused with it. Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ built a sanctuary for Kaṇṇaki and inaugurated the worship of the chaste wife. Kanaka, Vijaya, and the other released northern kings, along with other rulers freed from prison, the Koṅkus of Kuṭaku, the king of Māḷuva, and Kayavāku of sea-girt Laṅkā, prayed that the goddess Kaṇṇaki should appear and grant grace in their countries too:

> Before the sovereign of the world stood
> the Āryan kings released from harsh captivity,
> the other kings freed from the great prison-fort,
> the Koṅkus of Kuṭaku, the king of Māḷuva,
> and Kayavāku, king of sea-girt Laṅkā.
> They worshipped and prayed:
> “As at the auspicious festival performed
> by the king whose boundary is the Himalaya,
> come also to our countries and bestow your presence.”
> A voice arose: “I have granted the boon.”  
> *(30:156–164)*

This makes it clear that northern kings too built temples for the Pattini. Kaṇṇaki worship, once spread across Nāvalam Island—India—began to recede after the Mahābhārata story became established in the south. Researchers say that some Draupadī Amman temples in Tamil Nadu were formerly Kaṇṇaki temples: Draupadī occupied Kaṇṇaki’s place.

The female deities worshipped in Tamil village-goddess temples were originally human women. Examine the stories of the many goddesses called Māriyammaṉ, believed to cause rain, and this becomes apparent. One story says that Tiruvaḷḷuvar’s wife became Māriyammaṉ. Others say that Nāgavalli, wife of the sage Bhṛgu, or Rēṇukā, wife of Jamadagni, became Māriyammaṉ. Paraśurāma, Rēṇukā’s son, is said to have cut off her head for a grave offence. Therefore in some places only a neck or severed head is worshipped. This is one story behind “Neck Māriyammaṉ.” There is no need to retell the story of Draupadī, imperishable chaste wife shared by five husbands.

From all this we may accept without doubt that Kaṇṇaki was worshipped in a temple as the Pattini goddess. The convention began in the period represented by *Cilappatikāram*.

Near the border of Madurai district stands the Maṅkaḷatēvi temple, now within Kerala. Newspaper readers know that the Tamil Nadu government has asked Kerala to transfer that territory. The Maṅkaḷatēvi temple is identified as the Kaṇṇaki temple. *Cilampu* calls it “the sanctuary of the auspicious maiden” (30:88).

## Tying the Tāli

Whether the bridegroom tied a *tāli* around the bride’s neck in the age of *Cilappatikāram* remains a question.

The first canto, *The Auspicious Blessing Song*, contains the phrase “the auspicious ornament rose through the broad city” (1:46). Traditional commentators explain this as “the auspicious cord went in procession through the town.” The auspicious cord is the *tāli*. On that basis, one may say that the custom existed.

## Capital Punishment for Theft

A harsh custom of the period must also be mentioned: thieves could receive the death penalty. Today capital punishment is imposed only for murder, and even some murderers escape with life imprisonment. In that age it applied not only to killers but to thieves. When Kaṇṇaki argued that the execution of her husband was unjust, Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ replied that killing a thief was not tyranny but just rule:

> “To put a thief to death is not a crooked sceptre;
> behold, it is the victory of the bright-speared just sceptre.”  
> *(20:64–65)*

A proverb says, “The thief’s wife and the king’s wife are widows.” Kings often died in war, leaving their wives widowed; thieves were executed, leaving theirs widowed too. This explains the proverb.

It is said that in some Arab countries theft is punished by cutting off a limb, and that in some Arab lands and in the Soviet Union, property remains where it is left because no one touches it. Travellers report so. Mutilation also existed in our country. *Nālaṭiyār* says that if a man desiring another’s wife is caught, his legs may be cut off, while disgrace and misery follow:

> If seen, it disgraces the clan; if caught, the legs are cut;
> while committing the unmanly act, there is fear;
> and it yields long torment in hell.
> O debased adulterer, tell me:
> what measure of pleasure did you find in it?  
> *(14)*

The words “if caught, the legs are cut” deserve attention.

I remember reading some years ago that two government officers in Russia—the Soviet Union—were executed for taking bribes. Tiruvaḷḷuvar also accepted severe punishment. Killing cruel criminals is like removing weeds that destroy a crop:

> A king’s punishment of those cruel in killing
> is like a farmer’s removal of weeds from the green crop.  
> *(Tirukkuṟaḷ 550)*

This is also an age in which some demand the abolition of capital punishment. Every matter receives its answer in the proper time.

## Evening Worship and Dress

As day ended and evening approached, women mixed jasmine with paddy, scattered it through the house, lit lamps, and worshipped. They then put on clothing suited to the night:

> Throughout the spacious houses, slender-waisted women
> scattered opening jasmine with lustrous paddy,
> lit jewelled lamps when daylight died,
> and assumed a form of dress suited to the night.  
> *(9:1–4)*

Here *nakar* means house and *nikar* means radiance. Nakkīrar records the same convention in *Neṭunalvāṭai*:

> Scattering paddy and flowers, they joined their hands in worship
> and celebrated evening in the prosperous market-town.  
> *(43–44)*

Even today some women other than the poor wear nightdress. Some also wear during the day a long gown resembling the clothing of Catholic nuns, thereby diminishing the visible grace of womanhood.

It was believed that a woman abandoned by her husband could regain him by worshipping after scattering a mixture of Bermuda grass, *ciṟupūḷai* flowers, and paddy. Tēvanti performed this for Kaṇṇaki and blessed her:

> Thinking in her young heart that some misfortune
> had befallen the good Kaṇṇaki, she approached,
> scattered *aṟuku* grass, *ciṟupūḷai*, and paddy,
> and said, “May you regain your husband.”  
> *(9:41–44)*

This custom does not seem to be followed everywhere today.

When Kauṇti learned that Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki had died, she fasted to death; Mātari entered fire. When relatives or intimate companions die, some people still choose death in such ways. The practice therefore existed then as it does now.

Thus numerous customs and conventions prevailed in that age.
