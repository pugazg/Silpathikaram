# 28. The Excellence of Vañji and the Cēras

The Cēras who ruled from Vañji possessed many distinctions. The Cēra king Māntarañcēral Irumpōṟai is said to have granted the Tamil Vedic Brahmin Kōtamaṉ life in the upper world through a sacrifice.

A Brahmin named Parācaraṉ, who came from the Cōḻa country, received abundant wealth from a Cēra king and blessed him thus:

> “Long live the mighty one whose victory has ripened to fullness!
> Long live the guardian who felled the sea’s *kaṭampu* tree!
> Long live the king who engraved his curved bow upon the mountain cleft!
> Long live the Poraiyaṉ of the cool, flower-filled Porunai!
> Long live King Māntarañ Cēral!” (23:80–84)

When Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ learned that Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ had died because his sceptre departed from justice, he said that kingship was a difficult profession. If rain fails, or if subjects die unjustly, the world says that the king’s sceptre has failed. The thought produces great fear. To be born into a royal line obliged to protect the people justly while fearing tyranny is therefore a source of suffering rather than an honour worthy of worship:

> “If the wealth of rain is withheld, there is vast fear;
> if life is lost through error, there is greater fear.
> Guarding the people and fearing the tyrant’s sceptre,
> birth in the noble line that protects humankind
> brings suffering; it is not a thing to be worshipped.” (25:100–104)

Comparable passages include the *Puṟanāṉūṟu*:

> “Though the rain may fail and the reservoirs diminish,
> this wide world blames its guardians.” (35)

The *Periyapurāṇam* says of Manu:

> “He did not think, ‘He is the one son of my own line.’
> Knowing it his duty to follow the path of justice,
> King Manu drove the wheel of his chariot
> over the tender body of his own son.
> Is rare kingship difficult—or is it easy?”
> (*Tiruvārūr*, 44)

The *Tirukkuṟaḷ* likewise says:

> “In the land of the king who wields the sceptre according to nature,
> rain and harvest gather together.” (545)

and:

> “If a king acts in violation of justice,
> the sky will violate its order and refuse to rain.” (559)

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ was a great warrior who completed what he resolved. Unable to endure the northern kings’ contemptuous words about the southern rulers, he swore a fierce oath: if he went north and failed to return with a stone from the Himalaya for making an image of Kaṇṇaki, he would be a sword incapable of making enemies tremble in battle and a tyrant whose sceptre made his own people tremble:

> “Unless I bring a stone upon the crowned heads
> of the kings of the northern quarter
> and fashion the deity,
> if my sword returns empty,
> may I become one who, in the fierce splendour of war
> with close-set warrior’s anklets,
> cannot make hostile kings tremble,
> but whose sceptre makes the people
> of the prosperous land tremble instead.” (26:13–18)

He vowed to bring the stone borne upon the crowns of northern kings. The expression “to fashion the god” is delightful. Instead of merely saying, “If I return empty-handed,” he says, “If my sword returns empty,” which is even more vivid.

In the “Canto of the Water Consecration,” Māṭalaṉ praised Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ: “At Nēri Gate, in a single day, you destroyed and defeated the nine collateral Cōḻa kings who came to fight your kinsman Kiḷḷi.” Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s mother Naṟcōṇai was the daughter of the Cōḻa Maṇakkiḷḷi; Maṇakkiḷḷi’s son Kiḷḷi was therefore Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s maternal cousin. This victory is also recorded elsewhere in the epic:

> “He defeated in fixed battle at Nēri Gate
> the nine kings wearing abundant *ātti* garlands.” (28:116–117)

The fifth decad’s prologue in the *Patiṟṟuppattu* also records it:

> “He encamped outside the gate
> and, in insatiable war, caused nine claimants
> of the Cōḻa line to fall,
> cutting them down in established battle.” (Prologue 5:18–20)

The Cēra also defeated the minor ruler Paḻaiyaṉ of Mōkūr by cutting down his long neem, the community’s guardian tree:

> “With the strength of his uplifted sword,
> he cut at the root the long-branched neem
> guarded by Paḻaiyaṉ.” (27:124–125)

The *Patiṟṟuppattu* similarly says:

> “He caused the entire trunk, down to its full root,
> of the dark-branched neem guarded by Paḻaiyaṉ
> to be cut apart.” (Prologue 5:13–14)

His other achievements included victory at Iṭumpil and driving the sea backward.

Several further distinctions belong to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ:

- He fulfilled his mother’s wish by taking her to bathe in the Gaṅgā.
- He praised the Pāṇḍya king’s honourable death.
- Though Kanaka and Vijaya were defeated captives, he treated them with dignity, housed them in a separate palace, and provided every necessary comfort.
- When he released Kanaka and Vijaya, he freed other prisoners as well.
- He remitted taxes.
- He possessed the honour that could not endure northern kings insulting southern rulers.
- He carved an image of Kaṇṇaki, built her a temple, and established her worship.

Because no event comparable to those in Pukār and Madurai occurs in Vañji, the city’s own excellence does not receive equal space in the epic.

## Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s Birth

There is disagreement about Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s parentage. The following passage in the prose interlude of the “Canto of Blessing” shows that he was the son born to Neṭuñcēralātaṉ through Naṟcōṇai, daughter of the Cōḻa Maṇakkiḷḷi:

> “Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, who fought the people of Koṅku at Ceṅkaḷam
> and reached the bank of the great Gaṅgā,
> was the son whom the radiant daughter of the sun-like Cōḻa
> bore to Cēralātaṉ, who ruled the world
> by placing one language from Kumari to the northern Himalaya.”

Here “the Cōḻa” is Maṇakkiḷḷi and “the Cōḻa’s daughter” is Naṟcōṇai. The statement was written by Iḷaṅkō, their second son.

But the fifth-decad prologue of the classical *Patiṟṟuppattu* reads:

> “Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, the son whom the Cōḻa Maṇakkiḷḷi bore
> to Neṭuñcēralātaṉ, lord of the Kuṭavar,
> desiring a stone for the divine Pattini...
> drove the sea backward.”

The relationship expressed here is wrong. It makes one man the son born from another man’s womb. Instead of “the son whom the Cōḻa Maṇakkiḷḷi bore,” it should have read: “Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, the son born from the womb of the daughter whom Cōḻa Maṇakkiḷḷi begot.” Scribes copying the palm-leaf manuscript may accidentally have omitted the two words “daughter’s womb” between “begot” and “son.”

## Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s Religion

Several internal passages in *Cilappatikāram* suggest that Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ was a Śaiva:

> “He bowed his head, which bowed to none,
> circumambulating and adorning it with the victorious Vañji garland
> before the red feet of the exalted one
> whose vast dark head bears the moon’s ray
> and whose form contains the world.” (26:54–57)

> “O celestial child who appeared in Vañji,
> radiant through the grace of the lord of red matted hair!” (26:98–99)

> “The king who appeared through the grace
> of the bull-riding lord
> and made the great earth shine.” (30:141–142)

> “As some stood praising with the sacred remnant
> of him who reclines in divine sleep in the Āṭaka hall,
> because he had placed upon his jewelled crown
> the radiant red feet of the god of red matted hair
> who conceals the clear water,
> he received that remnant
> and bore it upon his ornamented jewelled shoulder.” (26:62–67)

“The exalted one whose head bears the moon,” “the celestial lord of red matted hair,” “the bull-riding lord,” and “the god of red matted hair” all denote Śiva. These passages show Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ as a Śaiva. Yet he was no enemy of other religions; Śaivism merely held first place for him.

## Love of Language

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ possessed great attachment to Tamil, his mother tongue. Iḷaṅkō makes him transfer the martial valour and power of Tamils onto the language itself:

> “Kanaka and Vijaya, whose tongues knew no restraint,
> did not understand the power of difficult, noble Tamil.” (26:159–161)

Iḷaṅkō also sings, “Let us behold the power of southern Tamil” (26:185), and “the power of southern Tamil belonging to the king with close-set warrior’s anklets” (27:5). The younger brother possessed the same love of the mother tongue as his elder brother. Alas, Tamil!
