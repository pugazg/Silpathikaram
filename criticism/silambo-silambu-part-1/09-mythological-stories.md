# 9. Mythological Stories

Many ancient literary works contain mythological stories that modern scientific minds cannot believe. *Cilappatikāram* too has a share in this tradition. Let us consider several such stories.

## The Demons and Mucukunta — Canto of the Sea-Bath

Demons troubled King Mucukunta, and a spirit is said to have protected him:

> “When hosts of fierce demons gathered  
> and attacked the king who guarded the great city  
> with the strength of the cruel-striped tiger,  
> they were defeated by the ankleted ruler.  
> Their hearts grew dark; they pressed their deceitful assault.  
> The great spirit that removed that deception,  
> sent by the god to the lord of the straight spear,  
> remained in the place where it received sacrifice …” (7–13)

A large story lies concealed in this brief passage. Garuḍa had carried away the nectar of immortality. Indra entrusted Pukār to King Mucukunta, who promised to guard it, and left a spirit to assist him. The demons fought Mucukunta and were defeated. They then discharged an arrow that created darkness everywhere. By its power the spirit removed the darkness and helped Mucukunta. When Indra returned, he commanded the spirit to remain as Mucukunta’s bodyguard. It therefore stayed in Pukār.

Aṭiyārkkunallār, the old commentator on *Cilappatikāram*, tells this story and quotes a supporting poem. In summary, the poem says that Indra, setting out to recover the nectar taken by Garuḍa, wondered who would guard the city. Mucukunta rose and promised, “Until you return, it is my duty to sustain it.” Indra gladly placed the praised spirit at his disposal. The fierce demons gathered, fought, lost, and then decided that only deceit could defeat him. They loosed an iron-headed arrow that buried even the sun’s two eyes in dense darkness. The great spirit removed the deception.

The final line of that unidentified poem—“the great spirit that removed the deceit”—is identical with line 11 of *Cilappatikāram*. This deserves notice.

## Urvasi, Jayanta, and Agastya

Another story follows. Urvasi danced at Indra’s command. During the performance she and Indra’s son Jayanta exchanged looks of desire; dance and music consequently lost their order. Sage Agastya cursed Urvasi to be born on earth:

> “Nārada’s skilfully discerned song upon the vīṇā  
> and the formal song of the dancing woman  
> filled the ears of the thousand-eyed one.  
> Urvasi failed in the drama; auspiciousness was lost.  
> ‘Let the vīṇā and this woman remain upon earth,’  
> said the curse she received.” (18–23)

The full story is this. Agastya came to Indra’s court. Indra commanded Urvasi to dance with song. As she danced, Urvasi and Jayanta gazed at one another with desire. Dance and song departed from proper rule; Nārada therefore plucked a hostile string on his vīṇā. Unable to tolerate the disorder, Agastya grew angry. He cursed Nārada’s vīṇā to lie upon earth as wood, Urvasi to be born in the human world, and Jayanta to appear there as bamboo. When they pleaded with him, he prescribed release from the curse.

Aṭiyārkkunallār relates the story and gives a supporting poem. It says that when the sage who loved the great Potiyil mountain entered the court, the immortals worshipped him. Indra ordered the ornamented Urvasi to dance with song. The stage-master arranged costume, performance, music, and dance according to the manuals. But when Urvasi saw Jayanta’s face, desire weakened her dance, and all the instruments and song lost order. The sage perceived the mutual desire in both hearts and cursed Indra’s son to become bamboo. Jayanta bowed and begged for release; the sage declared that at the end of Urvasi’s curse he would become the ceremonial staff taken from the mountain and mounted upon the stage. He also cursed Nārada’s vīṇā to become measureless wood, then granted that it would return to music after sounding upon earth.

That poem is in *nēricai āciriyappā*. Like the cantos of *Cilappatikāram*, it ends with the word *eṉ*—another point worth noting.

## Other Mythological Allusions

The epic contains many further mythological stories or references:

- Śiva burns the three cities at the request of the gods.
- Kaṇṇaṉ defeats Kaṃsa and Bāṇāsura.
- Murukaṉ defeats Sūraṉ.
- Koṟṟavai defeats the demons.
- Kāma performs the eunuch-dance.
- Mālati loses the child of her co-wife; a spirit carries it away; Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ comes as the child and later marries Tēvanti.
- A Pāṇḍya hurls his spear and makes the sea recede.
- A Pāṇḍya throws his bracelet upon Indra’s crown.
- A Pāṇḍya seizes and chains a cloud.
- Naḷa leaves his wife and enters the forest.
- Rāma, separated from his wife, goes into the forest.
- Indra cuts the wings of mountains.
- Kaṇṇaṉ breaks the kuruntu tree, uses a calf as a throwing-stick to bring down fruit, marries Nappiṉṉai, churns the ocean, eats earth, eats butter, measures the worlds with his feet, and goes as messenger for the Pāṇḍavas.
- Murukaṉ splits Mount Krauñca and cleaves the sea to kill Sūraṉ.

Thus *Cilappatikāram* contains numerous mythological narratives.
