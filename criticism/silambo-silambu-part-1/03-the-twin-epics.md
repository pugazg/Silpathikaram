# 3. The Twin Epics

It is customary to call two things “twins” when they possess some kind of similarity or connection. Twin children, the Twin Poets, a two-bullock cart, and twin cities such as Hyderabad and Secunderabad are examples.

Accordingly, in Tamil literature the title “the Twin Epics” refers to *Cilappatikāram* and *Maṇimēkalai*. *Cilappatikāram* concerns Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki. *Maṇimēkalai* concerns Maṇimēkalai, the daughter born to Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi, the woman who captured his heart. Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ composed the former; Cāttaṉār, the grain merchant of Madurai, composed the latter. Let us consider the connections between them.

Kōvalaṉ, Kaṇṇaki, Cittirāpati, Vayantamālai, Mādhavi, and Maṇimēkalai appear as characters in both works.

One is the story of the daughter; the other is the story of her parents. Mādhavi even refers to Kaṇṇaki as a parent or mother of Maṇimēkalai, Mādhavi’s own daughter. This represents an elevated culture. In the Canto in which the Town’s Slander Is Reported in *Maṇimēkalai*, Mādhavi tells Vayantamālai, who has been sent by Cittirāpati:

> “Maṇimēkalai is the daughter  
> of the great Pattini  
> who kindled roaring fire  
> in the guardian king’s great city.” (2:54–55)

This resembles the noble kinship language of the *Rāmāyaṇa*, in which Kaikeyī is called Rāma’s mother and Rāma Kaikeyī’s son, while Kausalyā is called Bharata’s mother and Bharata Kausalyā’s son. The two works may therefore be called a family epic.

Cāttaṉār met Iḷaṅkō, who had accompanied Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ to view the wealth of the mountain. He reported the killing of Kōvalaṉ, the deaths of the Pāṇḍya and his queen, and the burning of the city. He added that he knew how Madurāpati, the city goddess, had later appeared before Kaṇṇaki and told her the story of the curse from her former birth and that she would see Kōvalaṉ on the fourteenth day. The same news was told to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ.

Iḷaṅkō immediately said, “Let us compose an epic under the name *Cilappatikāram*.” Cāttaṉār asked Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ himself to compose such a work, common to all three crowned kings, and Aṭikaḷ composed *Cilappatikāram*. Cāttaṉār in turn composed the epic *Maṇimēkalai*.

The end of the prologue to *Cilappatikāram* says:

> “Aṭikaḷ of celebrated speech graciously composed  
> the poem with songs set amid prose,  
> and Cāttaṉ, the grain merchant of Madurai, heard it.” (87–89)

The end of the prologue to *Maṇimēkalai* says:

> “As Prince Iḷaṅkō graciously listened,  
> Cāttaṉ, the prosperous grain merchant,  
> set forth the renunciation of Maṇimēkalai,  
> in the ample power of Tamil,  
> and made it known in thirty songs.” (95–98)

Both passages deserve notice.

Still more significant are the closing lines of the “Statement on the Work” at the end of *Cilappatikāram*:

> “The matter of its discourse,  
> which is fulfilled in *Maṇimēkalai*,  
> is complete;  
> *Cilappatikāram* is complete.” (17–18)

This means that *Cilappatikāram*, whose subject reaches completion when joined in narration with the continuous poem *Maṇimēkalai*, has come to its end.

We may infer from this that Iḷaṅkō and Cāttaṉār discussed their plans and composed the Twin Epics while informing one another of their work.

Many further features correspond in the two epics. Both contain thirty cantos. In each, every canto ends with the terminal word *eṉ*.

Other shared details include: different birds seated like troops in an encampment; the beating of a drum before an elephant; the spirit-square; the story of Mucukunta; the story of Toṭittōṭ Cempiyan who destroyed the suspended forts; Jain and Buddhist affiliations; accounts of many religions; the loss of a husband figured as the loss of the sun; morning scenes; evening scenes; poetic descriptions of sun and moon; seashores and groves; the excellence of countries, cities, and rulers; grief at Maṇimēkalai’s renunciation as though a fine jewel had fallen into the sea; and the declaration that chaste women do not worship another deity. The list would grow if expanded.

Despite all these connections, it may seem surprising that Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ appears to have been affiliated with Jainism while Cāttaṉār was affiliated with Buddhism. Yet today fathers and sons may belong to different political parties; elder and younger brothers may belong to different parties; close friends and relatives may differ politically while still joining one another in many activities. Iḷaṅkō and Cāttaṉār should be understood in the same way.

It is noteworthy that Iḷaṅkō praises Cāttaṉār as “Cāttaṉ, master of cool Tamil” and “Cāttaṉ, poet of the good book.”
