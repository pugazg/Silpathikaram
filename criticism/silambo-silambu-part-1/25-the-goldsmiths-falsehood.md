# 25. The Goldsmith’s Falsehood

The goldsmith who caused Kōvalaṉ’s death held the rank of royal goldsmith. He wore the garment and carried the staff appropriate to that office. He became the messenger of Yama to Kōvalaṉ and came accompanied by a hundred goldsmiths.

When Kōvalaṉ asked whether he could appraise the anklet, the liar bowed and replied modestly: “Though I am not skilled in valuing ornaments for the feet, I make crowns for kings.”

On seeing the anklet’s rich workmanship and value, he decided to use it as a substitute for the queen’s anklet he himself had stolen. He asked Kōvalaṉ to remain near his house and went to see the king.

## The Skill of Thieves

The Pāṇḍya king was on his way to the queen, intent on ending her estrangement through love, when the goldsmith told him that the thief who had stolen her anklet was waiting near his house. The king ordered: “Kill him and bring the anklet.”

The goldsmith returned with the guards and pointed out Kōvalaṉ. Seeing Kōvalaṉ’s appearance, some said that he could not possibly be a thief. The goldsmith opposed them. Thieves, he said, were accomplished in using eight weapons—spell, deity, drug, omen, stratagem, place, time, and instrument—and could deceive people through each in different ways.

He added an example. One night, while a prince slept, a thief stole the necklace from his chest. The prince immediately drew his sword. The thief snatched away the sword’s sheath. Whenever the prince thrust at him, the thief manoeuvred so that the blade entered the empty sheath; in the end he vanished. “Such men cannot be trusted,” the goldsmith said.

One of those present then recalled that on a previous night he had encountered a thief. When he raised his sword to kill him, the thief seized it and ran away. “They cannot be trusted,” he agreed. As they spoke, an ignorant and drunken guard struck Kōvalaṉ down with his sword.

After Kaṇṇaki pleaded her case and Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ died, his younger brother Veṟṟivēl Ceḻiyaṉ ascended the throne and is said to have sacrificed a thousand goldsmiths.

Through the wicked deed of one goldsmith, Kōvalaṉ, Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, Kōpperuntēvi, Kaṇṇaki, Kaṇṇaki’s mother, Kōvalaṉ’s mother, Kauṇti, and Mātari all died. Did sacrificing a thousand goldsmiths bring any of them back?

Every profession has concealed practices. People commonly suspect that concealment may be somewhat greater in goldsmithing. Yet killing a thousand goldsmiths in a manner aimed at uprooting the entire community was also tyranny. One person’s act can bring disgrace upon a whole community.

When Mahatma Gandhi was killed by the Brahmin Nathuram Vinayak Godse, people for a time called other Brahmins “Godse.” That comparison is relevant here—but such collective condemnation was no more just.

This part of the story reveals that there was a science or manual of theft, that training in stealing existed, and that some people became accomplished practitioners. Even today it is said that young people are trained in theft in certain places.

Theft might gradually diminish if thieves received the death penalty. The Pāṇḍya king declared, “Killing a thief is no tyranny; it is the victory of the white spear,” and under the law of his time ordered the death of Kōvalaṉ, whom he believed to be a thief. Yet even while such a law existed, there were manuals and schools of theft and criminals like the goldsmith. The thought makes one’s head spin. In street theatre this goldsmith is called Vañcip Pattan.

Iḷaṅkō describes one thief as:

> “A thief who escaped through his learning.” (16:199)

That is, by means of his education and training in the science of theft, he moved away from the place and vanished. Even without studying a manual of theft, certain “scholars” who append all twenty-six letters from A to Z after their names engage in theft, bribery, and embezzlement. What then need be said of those who have received formal training in stealing?

## The Single Anklet

Although I condemn theft, forgive me for introducing an argument that might assist it. Iḷaṅkō ought to have made the goldsmith present one further piece of evidence to the Pāṇḍya king, but he omitted it.

When people sell a matched pair of ornaments, they generally sell both at the same time. If one sells only one of a pair of earrings, the earring being sold loses value, and the single earring left at home also loses value. If a new earring is later purchased to match it, the two will rarely look exactly alike; the replacement will be inferior. A person selling only one earring may therefore be suspected of having stolen it somewhere.

The same applies to an anklet. The goldsmith might have strengthened his accusation by saying: “The very fact that this man has brought only one anklet is sufficient proof that he stole it somewhere.” If this crafty argument that occurred to me had also occurred to Iḷaṅkō, the episode would have been even more effective. Let us proceed.

Every caste and profession has been mocked through degrading phrases, proverbs, and stories. I belong to the Kaikkōḷar community, and sayings exist that disparage us too. Just as some members of our community steal weaving thread, some goldsmiths steal gold. Members of other trades also try whatever dishonest hand they can manage.

Let this chapter therefore end with an appeal that workers of every profession conduct themselves with generosity and honesty.
