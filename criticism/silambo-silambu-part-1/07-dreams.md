# 7. Dreams

*Cilappatikāram* contains a canto titled “The Canto in Which the Dream Is Told.” Many people have expressed many views about dreams. A writer named Poṉṉavaṉ even composed a work titled *Kaṉā Nūl* (“Book of Dreams”), consisting of thirty *kaṭṭaḷaik kalittuṟai* verses linked in an *antāti*. Some common views about dreams are these:

- While a person sleeps, the body remains on the bed but the life or soul leaves, performs various acts, then returns and rejoins the body.
- A person’s thoughts or desires blossom as dreams during sleep.
- Events and sensations previously experienced through the five senses reappear in dreams with many transformations.
- A good dream is followed by good; a bad dream by bad.
- A dream seen at dawn comes true exactly—*Cīvakacintāmaṇi* 219.
- Dreams have no connection with events that later occur in waking life.
- A dream is a sign that announces during sleep what will happen later in the day.
- Thinkers such as Plato held that dreams reveal human likes and dislikes.
- Thinkers such as Aristotle held that dreams arise because certain internal organs remain active and stimulate the sleeper.

Although many people have offered different theories, each person may use the dreams he or she has experienced—that is, personal experience—to infer which view is correct.

With this foundation, let us turn to *Cilappatikāram* and examine its three major dream episodes.

## 1. The Canto in Which the Dream Is Told

While living in Pukār, Kaṇṇaki tells her Brahmin friend Tēvanti of a dream:

“My heart is troubled by the dream I saw. My husband took my hand and led me somewhere. At last we entered a great city. There, people placed upon us a statement that should never have been made; they laid upon me a false and cruel accusation like a scorpion. I heard many say that some evil had befallen my husband Kōvalaṉ. Unable to bear it, I went before the ruler of that city and argued my case. As a result, harm came to the ruler and to the city. The harm was so terrible that I have no wish to tell you more. Later my husband and I attained an exalted state. If I tell you that, it will provoke laughter.”

The poem says:

> “My heart is troubled by a dream.  
> He took my hand, and we entered a great city.  
> In the city we reached, the townspeople placed upon us  
> a word that should never be uttered,  
> setting upon me a scorpion-like charge.  
> I heard that some evil had befallen Kōvalaṉ.  
> I went before the guardian and stated the case.  
> Then harm came to the guardian and the city.  
> It seems a fiery evil, O close-bangled one;  
> if you hear of the good state later attained  
> by me and the man joined to me,  
> it will become a cause for laughter.” (45–54)

To “set a scorpion” upon someone means to impose cruelty like placing a scorpion on the body. Some people may throw a false scorpion made of wax or another material upon a person to frighten them. Here “scorpion” means a cruel accusation that brings pain like a scorpion’s sting.

In ordinary life, one who sees a bad dream may be ashamed or afraid to reveal it. People are often unwilling to report a dream in which harm has befallen another.

The dream foreshadows the false theft charge placed upon Kōvalaṉ as though a scorpion were set upon him. The “fiery dream” indicates the coming destruction of the Pāṇḍya and his queen. Kaṇṇaki’s mind is unwilling to speak of it.

The meaning of “If you hear of the good state, it will cause laughter” is that Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki will finally be welcomed by the gods and ascend to the higher world. Because such a blessing is difficult to obtain, Kaṇṇaki fears that if she claims it for herself, people may mock her: “Ah! Do these two possess such immense ambition?”

After Kōvalaṉ is killed and Kaṇṇaki falls into great grief, she remembers this dream and thinks events have unfolded according to it:

> “‘I shall see the evil king and ask him this,’ she said.  
> She rose; she remembered the distressing evil dream.  
> She stood; she thought; tears fell from her long carp-like eyes.  
> She stood; she thought.” (*Ūrcūḻ Vari*, 71–74)

Did Kaṇṇaki truly see such a dream? Or did Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ invent it for epic effect, as foreshadowing? The second is probably true. Can anyone dream all these details beforehand without omission, as though they had been dictated in advance?

## 2. The Canto of Refuge

After Kōvalaṉ has walked around Madurai and returned to the settlement outside the city, he tells the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ of a dream:

“I dreamed as vividly as waking that, through a base little man, in the guarded king’s city, Kaṇṇaki suffered trembling grief; that my garment was stripped from me and I rode upon a pig; that Kaṇṇaki and I attained the state of those who have severed attachment; that Mādhavi placed Maṇimēkalai before the Righteous One, while the god of Love threw down his flower-arrows upon empty ground and stood helpless in grief.”

> “Kōvalaṉ said: ‘Through a base little man,  
> in the guarded king’s fortified city,  
> the woman of fragrant fivefold hair suffered trembling grief;  
> my garment was taken, and I rode a tusked beast;  
> with the jewelled woman of lovely curling hair  
> I attained the state of those who have severed attachment;  
> the god of Love threw his great flower-arrows  
> upon empty ground and stood helpless in grief;  
> before the Righteous One beneath the splendid Bodhi,  
> Mādhavi gave Maṇimēkalai.  
> At midnight I saw this dream as though awake.  
> It will swiftly happen here.’” (95–106)

The “base little man” is the goldsmith; the fortified city is Madurai; “fragrant fivefold hair” refers to Kaṇṇaki; the tusked beast is a pig. Attaining with the jewelled woman the state of those who have severed attachment means reaching with Kaṇṇaki the higher world attained by ascetics without attachment. The Love-god becomes helpless because Maṇimēkalai can no longer be used for erotic pursuit. The Righteous One beneath the Bodhi is the Buddha.

The passage recalls an old worldly practice: a person guilty of a grave crime was shaved, marked on the face with black and red spots, insufficiently clothed, mounted upon a donkey, and paraded through the town. Here a pig replaces the donkey. The image signifies that Kōvalaṉ will be subjected to cruel humiliation; Kaṇṇaki will thereby suffer; and Mādhavi will make Maṇimēkalai renounce the world.

At the end Kōvalaṉ says, “It will swiftly happen here”—that is, in Madurai. This rests upon the belief that events follow dreams. Kōvalaṉ may have had a dream with some relation to these events; or this too may be Iḷaṅkō’s invention for the flavour of epic foreshadowing.

## 3. The Canto of the Lawsuit

On the night before Kōvalaṉ is killed, the Pāṇḍya queen Kōpperuntēvi tells her companion of an evil dream:

“Friend, the king’s straight sceptre and white parasol fell to the ground. The bell of appeal was struck. The eight directions trembled. Darkness swallowed the sun. A rainbow appeared at night. Stars fell in broad daylight. I saw these things in a dream. I shall report the dream to the king.”

> “There … I shall see the parasol and sceptre fall;  
> I shall hear the sweet-voiced bell at the gate tremble—see, friend!  
> All eight directions will shake;  
> darkness will swallow the sun—see, friend!  
> At night the streaming bow-banner will appear;  
> in burning daylight fierce-rayed stars will fall—  
> I shall see these things, friend!” (1–7)

“Friend” renders *ellā*. The gate-bell is the bell of judicial appeal hung at the palace entrance for petitioners. *Ira* means night. “The bow-banner streams” means that the rainbow, Indra’s bow, appears like a many-coloured banner. Repeating “I shall see—see, friend!” three times gives literary and epic relish.

The queen then tells the Pāṇḍya the same dream:

> “The straight sceptre and white parasol  
> overturn and fall upon the dense earth;  
> at our king’s victorious gate  
> the bell trembles, and my heart trembles;  
> at night the bow appears, by day stars fall,  
> and the four directions shake.  
> Some suffering is coming—  
> let us tell the king.” (9–12)

The same information is stated twice in song: once for the companion to hear, and again for the king and others.

The sceptre and parasol do not merely fall as though lying down; they overturn headfirst. The appeal-bell had always hung at the palace gate but had never been struck because there was no defect in the king’s government. In the dream its sound is heard. The text does not merely say the bell moved and sounded: it “trembled,” making the heart tremble. The wording is full of meaning.

It is easy to explain “a rainbow at night” today. A rainbow normally appears in daylight. In the morning, if the sun shines in the east while rain falls opposite it in the west, sunlight passes through the rain and is dispersed, producing a curved many-coloured form still farther west. In the afternoon, when the sun is in the west and rain falls opposite it in the east, the same dispersion produces a rainbow in the east.

Thus a rainbow normally appears in daylight. Even if it rains at night, there is no sunlight to produce one. A dream of a rainbow at night was therefore treated as a sign of approaching sorrow.

Other literary works too present an evil dream seen before a king’s death as a sign of that death. By the method of literary comparison, let us consider some examples, beginning with the *Kampa Rāmāyaṇam*.

### Trijaṭā’s Dream in the *Kampa Rāmāyaṇam*

Trijaṭā tells Sītā the evil dreams she has seen:

“Mother, listen. Rāvaṇa’s head was slick with oil. Wearing red clothing, he rode southward toward the land of Yama in a chariot yoked to donkeys and ghosts. Rāvaṇa’s sons, relatives, and others also went south and did not return. The sacrificial fire he maintained went out, and termites swarmed over the place. An untrimmed jewelled lamp suddenly blazed with immense light. Thunder split Rāvaṇa’s palace.

“Listen further. Not only male elephants but female elephants too entered must. Drums sounded by themselves though no one struck them. The sky thundered explosively though there were no clouds. Stars fell. The sun seemed to shine in a night that had no daylight. The celestial garlands worn by men gave off the smell of flesh. Festive arches broke. Elephants’ tusks snapped. Water in full ceremonial pots foamed like liquor. Stars rose after splitting the moon. The sky rained blood. The mace, discus, sword, and bow began fighting one another.

“The marriage pendants of women broke by themselves and fell upon their breasts though no one touched them. The hair of Maṇṭōtari, daughter of Maya and wife of Rāvaṇa, came loose. Two lions descended from a mountain with a host of tigers, entered the forest where elephants lived, encircled them, and crushed them to death. A peacock in the forest left the forest and went away.” The two lions signify Rāma and Lakṣmaṇa; the mountain tigers are the monkey armies; the elephants are the rākṣasas; the elephant-forest is Laṅkā; and the peacock is Sītā.

“Listen further. Lakṣmī carried a sacred lamp shining with the light of a thousand lamps from Rāvaṇa’s house to Vibhīṣaṇa’s house.” The implication is that Rāvaṇa will perish and Vibhīṣaṇa will come to power.

This appears in the Canto of the Vision in the Sundara Kāṇḍa of the *Kampa Rāmāyaṇam* (40–53).

### The *Cīvakacintāmaṇi*

A similar sign appears in the *Cīvakacintāmaṇi*. While pregnant with Cīvakaṉ, his mother tells her husband, King Caccantaṉ, of an evil dream. Caccantaṉ understands in advance that he may die. He therefore trains his wife beforehand to sit in a mechanical peacock and fly away from the palace. As the dream foretold, Caccantaṉ is killed by his minister Kaṭṭiyaṅkāraṉ.

### Shakespeare’s *Julius Caesar*

A similar episode occurs in Shakespeare’s play *Julius Caesar*. One night Caesar cannot sleep and tosses in bed because circumstances are unfavourable. His wife Calpurnia sees an evil dream and cries out in terror. When Caesar consults religious interpreters, they advise him that it would be better not to go out. Calpurnia insists strongly, falls at his feet, and begs him not to leave the palace the next day. She sends word through Antony that the king is unwell and cannot attend the senate. Despite all this, the very courageous Caesar goes to the senate and is killed. This is a foreshadowing dream.

### Abraham Lincoln

Abraham Lincoln, President of the United States, is said to have learned through a dream that he was going to be assassinated and to have recorded it in his diary. This too is foreshadowing.

### Kiḷḷi Vaḷavaṉ’s Enemies in the *Puṟanāṉūṟu*

A *Puṟanāṉūṟu* poem attributed to Kōvūr Kiḻār and addressed to the Cōḻa Kiḷḷi Vaḷavaṉ who died at Kuḷamuṟṟam expresses such a dream vividly. The Cōḻa’s enemies see an evil dream and think of the suffering about to reach them. Fearing they may die, they kiss and fondle their children beforehand to satisfy their longing. They hide the evil dream from their wives, thinking the women will be frightened.

In the dream, fiery meteors fall in all eight directions; dry trees catch fire; the sun appears in many places; evil birds utter harsh cries; teeth fall to the ground without being struck; oil is poured upon their hair; they ride on pigs; their clothing is removed; and their weapons overturn and fall together with the racks on which they had been placed. These details appear in *Puṟanāṉūṟu* 41, beginning “Even Death waits for its time.”

From all these examples, it is clear that people held—and still hold—the belief that evil follows an evil dream. We also see what kinds of dreams were thought to announce death in advance.

All three dreams in *Cilappatikāram* indicate a death that will occur later. The comparative examples given here resemble those in *Cilappatikāram*. The full poems may be consulted in their respective works; quoting them all here would greatly increase the number of pages.
