# 16. Similes and Metaphors

Many similes and metaphors occur in *Cilappatikāram*. If one says “lotus-face” meaning “a face like a lotus,” it is a simile. If one says “the face-lotus,” meaning “the lotus which is the face,” it is a metaphor. Let us examine some examples.

## Similes

### The Canto of Establishing Domestic Virtue

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki seated together on the bed appeared like the Sun and Moon united:

> Like the sight of the two lights seated together,
> the lights that illumine the whole world encircled by the ancient sea.  
> *(2:30–31)*

The plural sense in “the lights together” indicates two rays or luminaries. However great the claims to equality made by women today, when we consider Kōvalaṉ’s early amorous play and Kaṇṇaki’s modest restraint, we may take Kōvalaṉ as the Sun and Kaṇṇaki as the Moon. Since the two heavenly lights are never actually joined, this is an impossible-object simile, called *abhūta-upamā* in Sanskrit.

The closing *veṇpā* of the canto contains two similes. The couple appeared like Kāma and his wife Rati. They entwined their bodies and floated inseparably in pleasure like a male and female serpent twisting around each other:

> Like two pure serpents joining and immersing in one another,
> they clasped hands like Kāma and his wife …

The serpent simile possesses strong suggestive value.

### The Canto Celebrating the Evening

When a great emperor is absent, cruel minor rulers oppress the people and exact taxes. In the same way, when the Sun disappeared, evening arrived:

> At the painful hour when tribute-bearing subjects suffer,
> placing hands upon their heads,
> when the royal army with its victory is absent
> and intrusive kings take possession of one region
> together with rebellious communities …  
> *(4:8–12)*
>
> evening came and settled upon the prosperous ancient city.  
> *(4:20)*

The Sun is the emperor; evening resembles a petty ruler. When the Sun leaves, evening comes.

The final *veṇpā* gives another comparison. A king’s umbrella gives cool shade to his subjects and burning heat to his enemies; similarly, the night Moon gives delight to Mādhavi and pain to Kaṇṇaki:

> Shade to those who are joined, scorching to those who are not,
> like the guardian king’s white umbrella,
> the night in which the sky-going Moon spread its light
> and opened flowers revealed itself differently
> to united Mādhavi and to Kaṇṇaki.

It is conventional to describe good rule by saying that people live happily in the shade of the king’s umbrella. This does not mean that the entire land literally lies under one umbrella. Even in common speech people say, “We live only in your shade; you must protect us.” Compare the word “shaded” in *Puṟanāṉūṟu*:

> Even to those singular rulers whose white umbrella
> shaded the sea-girt world without sharing it …  
> *(189:1–2)*

If the umbrella gives coolness to the king’s own people, it is appropriate to say that it gives the opposite—heat—to enemies. In the same way, the Moon gives pleasure to Mādhavi, who is united with Kōvalaṉ, and pain to Kaṇṇaki, who is separated from him. The Moon’s torment of separated lovers appears in innumerable literary works.

### The Canto that Reveals the Nature of the Dream

When Mālati fed milk to the child of her husband’s other wife, the child choked and died. Terrified, she prayed to the gods to restore it. A demoness named Iṭākiṉi swallowed the body, and Mālati lamented “like a peacock struck by thunder”:

> She yearned and wept like a peacock struck by thunder.  
> *(23)*

This simile seems difficult. It is generally said that when rain approaches, peacocks spread their tails and dance. But the line suggests that a peacock is frightened and distressed by thunder. Is that correct?

A hymn from Ñāṉacampantar’s Tiruvaiyāṟu *Tēvāram* comes to mind. Peacocks dance; clouds thunder; monkeys, seeing the dance and hearing the booming sound, fear that rain is coming, climb to the tops of trees, and look at the clouds:

> Women who came in procession danced;
> the *muḻavu* thundered; thinking it rain,
> a few monkeys, confused, climbed trees
> and gazed at the clouds—such is Tiruvaiyāṟu.  
> *(3:1)*

The women in procession are the dancing peacocks; the temple drum is thunder. The monkeys mistake the women’s gait for that of peacocks and the drum for thunder. The literary charm of Ñāṉacampantar’s verse is delightful.

What this hymn suggests is that peacocks dance joyfully when thunder announces rain. There appears to be a contradiction between Iḷaṅkō and Ñāṉacampantar. How is it to be resolved? Perhaps a peacock becomes afraid when thunder is exceptionally violent and continuous. A specialist in birds and animals may be able to answer.

### “Passageway of the World”

In the *Canto of Seeing the Country*, Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki pass through the outer gateway of Pukār. Because merchants and travellers from across the world came and went by that route, Iḷaṅkō calls it the “passageway of the world.” Bombay is called the “Gateway of India”; Pukār’s gateway is honoured as the “Gateway of the World.” The road leading outward begins at the towered gate and resembles a great river beginning in a mountain. The gateway is the mountain; the street is the river:

> Leaving together the passageway of the world,
> which resembled a great river taking its source in a mountain …  
> *(10:26–27)*

Rivers usually rise in mountains. “Taking the mountain as its head” means having the mountain as the place of origin. Compare *Paṭṭiṉappālai*: “Kāviri, with the mountain as her head and the sea as her end” (6). Long streets are similarly likened to rivers in Sangam literature:

> The broad, long street lay like a river.  
> (*Neṭunalvāṭai* 30)

> The broad, long street lay like a river.  
> (*Maturaikkāñci* 359)

Does not “passageway of the world” confer a new distinction upon Pukār?

The mingled cries of many birds sounded like the front of an army commanded by victorious kings:

> The calls of waterfowl, land birds, and every kind of winged creature
> spread as a great confusion of many groups of voices,
> like the battlefront of kings victorious in war.  
> *(117–119)*

The uproar of victory may be understood today from the noise made by candidates and their supporters after an election victory.

### Impermanence of the Body

The Cāraṇa ascetics offer Kauṇti moral instruction. No one can prevent evil karma from yielding evil. As a planted seed inevitably becomes a crop and gives its fruit, good karma must likewise produce good results. As a lamp goes out in an open space swept by strong wind, life leaves the body at the appointed time:

> Powerful karma does not depart when told to depart;
> like the sown seed, it comes forth and reaches its fruit.
> When the hour arrives, none can prevent it.
> Like a great flame in a vast place beaten by fierce wind,
> lives do not remain forever joined to bodies.  
> *(10:171–174)*

Whether desired or not, what must happen will happen. Compare *Nālaṭiyār*:

> Even those who suffer cannot remove what must befall;
> and what must be obtained will likewise come.  
> (*Past Karma*, 4)

### The Form of the Lord of Vēṅkaṭam

The Brahmin from Māṅkāṭu tells Kōvalaṉ that on the summit of Mount Vēṅkaṭam, between the Sun visible on one side and the Moon on the other, Viṣṇu stands like a dark cloud wearing lightning as a garment of new gold and the rainbow as an ornament. In one lotus hand he holds the discus, in the other the white conch; a necklace shines on his chest, and a flower-coloured garment wraps his body:

> On the summit of the lofty mountain Vēṅkaṭam,
> where swollen waterfalls descend,
> the spreading-rayed Sun and the Moon shine,
> rising on either side of the middle space.
> There, like a cloud of lovely hue
> clothed in lightning and adorned with the radiant bow,
> the red-eyed Tall One stands,
> bearing in his graceful lotus hands
> the hostile-destroying discus and milk-white conch,
> wearing a splendid necklace upon his chest
> and shining in a gold-flowered garment.  
> *(11:41–51)*

A similar image appears in *Paripāṭal*:

> Because the season had arrived, like the signs
> of the two different orbs adorning the double sky,
> his hands bore the discus and the curved conch.  
> *(13:7–9)*

The “two different orbs” are Sun and Moon.

The other twin epic, *Maṇimēkalai*, also depicts Sun and Moon together. The woman called Pukār seems to wear a white earring on one side and a gold earring on the other: the Moon shines in the east and the Sun in the west:

> The flower-creeper Pukār, surpassing the world of poets …  
> *(5:109)*
>
> The mature Moon at the eastern side
> and the descending ray at the western side
> were like a silver-white earring and an earring of gold.  
> *(5:119–121)*

An English poem too describes the Moon and Sun visible at once in the evening—Lord Tennyson’s “The Lotos-Eaters.” The warriors of the Greek king Ulysses, returning to Ithaca after conquering Troy in Asia Minor, halt on an island:

> They sat them down upon the yellow sand,
> between the sun and moon upon the shore.  
> *(5:1–2)*

These three comparisons from other works have been introduced through the method of literary comparison to enhance our appreciation of Iḷaṅkō’s poem.

### Transformation of the Landscape

When the Sun’s fierce heat joined summer, the Mullai and Kuṟiñci landscapes lost their nature and became arid Pālai. A fitting simile is offered: the land changed like a country made to suffer when its king and his ministers abandon just governance:

> As a vast land loses royal order
> when the victorious king goes astray
> together with the officers of the crown,
> so, when the lord of summer joined the hot-rayed king
> and both perverted their proper goodness,
> Mullai and Kuṟiñci departed from their order,
> lost their lovely nature, inflicted trembling distress,
> and assumed the form called Pālai.  
> *(11:60–66)*

“Officers of the crown” means ministers and other royal functionaries. Summer is personified as an aged lord; it corresponds to the ministers, while the hot-rayed Sun is the king.

### The Canto of Resting in the Outlying Settlement

Kōcikaṉ sees a withered *mādhavi* creeper and asks whether it too suffers, like the maiden Mādhavi afflicted by Kōvalaṉ’s departure:

> “When Kōvalaṉ left, the great-flower, long-eyed Mādhavi
> endured cruel sorrow; like her,
> stripped of your flowers by this mighty summer,
> you too seem to grieve, O *mādhavi* creeper.”  
> *(13:48–51)*

The woman Mādhavi is compared with the creeper of the same name. It is a fitting simile and a play on words.

Kōcikaṉ tells Kōvalaṉ that because of his departure, his father, mother, and relatives lie disordered like a serpent deprived of its precious gem and a body deprived of life:

> The lord of the two treasures and the lady of the great house
> became like a serpent that had lost its rare gem;
> the close circle of kin, like a body that had lost dear life,
> fell into an ocean of sorrow.  
> *(13:57–60)*

Kōvalaṉ is compared both to the serpent’s jewel and to life. The renowned ancient city itself became distraught like Ayodhyā when Rāma departed:

> Like Ayodhyā deprived of mighty Rāma,
> the renowned ancient city fell into great bewilderment.  
> *(13:65–66)*

Here Kōvalaṉ is compared with Rāma.

### The Canto of the Vision

When Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ camped upon a sandbank beside the great river, mountain people brought many kinds of tribute and waited to present them. Their waiting is compared to the defeated kings who, bearing tribute in submission, waited for an opportunity to see the Cēra:

> Like enemy kings who, finding no opportunity anywhere
> to see the royal lord, stood bearing tribute.  
> *(25:35–36)*

The phrase suggests that they could not obtain an audience even after going to Vañji to pay tribute; learning that the king had gone to view the mountain wealth, they came there too, yet still could not see him quickly or easily. This testifies to the Cēra’s greatness. Even today, do people not wait a long time in queues to see certain eminent persons who think themselves very great?

### The Canto of Taking the Auspicious Sword and Umbrella

When Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ left Vañji to fight his enemies, he resembled Indra leaving his own city to war against the demons:

> Like the lord of heaven leaving his own city
> to advance against the Dānavas,
> he left Vañji.  
> *(26:78–79)*

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ sprang upon his enemies like a lion rising for prey and leaping into a great herd of elephants:

> Like a lion risen in the hunt for prey,
> delighting at the sight of a vast herd of great elephants
> and springing upon it …  
> *(26:188–190)*

### The Moon as the King’s Face

The anklets on Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s feet are said to rub against the crowns of kings he has conquered and enslaved. Yet when he bestows grace upon his own subjects, his face is cool and blossoming. The Moon appeared over the ancient city like that gracious face:

> The flower-rayed Moon appeared for the world to worship,
> like the auspicious face of ankleted Kuṭṭuvaṉ—
> whose feet rub the outer surfaces of conquered crowns—
> when he protects and favours his subjects;
> it showed itself to the famed ancient city and moved on.  
> *(28:37–40)*

It is common to compare a woman’s face to the Moon. Here, in an innovative reversal, the Moon is compared to the Cēra’s face, on account of coolness.

A *Puṟanāṉūṟu* poem by Auvaiyār may be compared. A great elephant fights enemies fiercely, yet behaves gently with village children who wash its white tusks at the waterside. In the same way Atiyamāṉ is terrible to enemies but sweet to poets such as Auvaiyār:

> Like the great elephant that enters the bathing place
> while village children wash its white tusks,
> you are sweet to me, great lord;
> but like its unapproachable musth,
> you are harsh to those who oppose you.  
> *(94)*

How graceful the thought!

### The Canto of Benediction—Prose-and-Song Interlude

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ already intended to go north and bring a stone from the Himalaya for the image. When he was told that northern kings had insulted the southern rulers, his anger flared. This is compared to striking with a short stick a jewelled wheel already rolling by itself, making it run even faster: “like driving a rolling jewelled wheel with a goad” (Canto 29, prose-and-song interlude).

At the end of the work, under *Concluding Statement on the Text*, there is a skilful simile. Just as a long and massive mountain may be made visible in a small mirror, an immense history is compressed into the book called *Cilappatikāram*:

> Like one who reveals a long, great mountain
> within the reflected image of a mirror,
> the meaning of the narrative that extends into *Maṇimēkalai*
> has been displayed: *Cilappatikāram* is complete.  
> *(15–18)*

## Metaphors

### The Returning King

In the evening sky the king who is the Moon appears, drives away the enemy who is dusk, spreads his milk-white rays, and rules the subjects who are stars:

> In the evening sky the white crescent appeared,
> drove away the miserable petty rebellion of dusk,
> spread its milk-white rays without departing from its nature,
> and, in the silver radiance, ruled the stars.  
> *(4:23–26)*

Because evening is called a hostile petty rebellion, the white crescent must be understood as a victorious king. As kings rule people from the earth, the crescent rules the stars from the sky. “The rebellion of evening” is a localized metaphor.

### The Pond as a Woman

The pond-woman possesses the swan as her gait, the fragrance of the water-lily as perfume, the lotus as her red mouth, fine dark sand as hair, bees as bards singing modes, and blue lilies as eyes. Around her resounds the drum formed by bird calls:

> The clear-water pond, with the swan’s soft gait,
> wore the honey-filled fragrance breathed by water-lilies;
> the lotus was her red mouth, cool dark sand her hair.
> Bees with minstrels’ mouths sang the *Nērtiṟam* mode;
> lovely blue lilies opened as her flower-eyes,
> while bird-mouth drums resounded around her …  
> *(4:72–77)*

### Ploughing

The Brahmin Māṭalaṉ ploughs with the plough that is his Vedic tongue and sows the seed of lofty moral counsel in the field that is Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s ear:

> The Brahmin ploughed with his Vedic tongue
> and sowed the lofty meaning in the field of the king’s ear.  
> *(28:187–188)*

### The Maiden Earth

The earth is a woman whose garment is the sea, whose breasts are mountains, whose necklace is the great river, and whose hair is cloud. The Sun rises, removes the dark covering over her, and spreads its rays:

> The maiden earth, whose garment was wave-filled water,
> whose breasts were mountains, whose necklace the great river,
> whose hair was cloud, and whose expanse held broad eyes,
> cast off the covering-sheet of enveloping darkness.
> From the summit of the great eastern mountain
> the Sun appeared and spread blossoming rays
> of radiant light that made the world shine.  
> *(5:1–6)*

### The Unfailing Creeper of the Pāṇḍya Line

The Vaikai is personified as a woman. Flowers swept down from many trees form her garland and girdle; the banks are her hips, sand mounds her breasts, *murukku* petals her red mouth, jasmine her teeth, fish her eyes, and dark fine sand her hair:

> *Kuṭacam*, bamboo, luxuriant *pakaṉṟai*,
> *piṭavam*, *mayilai*, and other mingled trees
> gave flowers that joined everywhere
> as garland and girdle upon her curving banks.
> The broad raised spaces enclosed by them were her hips;
> flower-rich islands heaped with sand rose on either side
> as radiant young breasts facing one another.
> Fallen *murukku* petals were her red mouth;
> cascading jasmine, her ornamented smile;
> long black fish, moving and leaping, her eyes;
> shining dark sand never free of fragrant flowers, her hair.
> Praised upon poets’ tongues, she was the flower-creeper
> of lofty, world-sustaining, unfailing course—
> the Vaikai, the unfailing creeper of the royal line.  
> *(13:157–170)*

The named plants are species of tree or creeper; *vālukam* means a mound of sand; the bright petal is that of the *murukku*. The flower-creeper is the woman-river Vaikai, praised by poets because her water flows without failing. Compare Kampaṉ’s description of the Godāvari as embracing a “cool, righteous course.” The Vaikai belongs to the Pāṇḍya line.

### Disease and Medicine

While warriors remained in the battlefield camp, they suffered by imagining the seductive sidelong glances of their wives. Where are those glances situated? Beneath the bow of the eyebrow, upon the Moon that is the face within the cloud that is hair, stand the eyes—red-lined because they have defeated Kāma’s flower arrows. Now the warriors return victorious from camp. Their wives send their sidelong glances as messengers, awakening love. The eyes that, when remembered during separation, caused the disease of longing now become medicine for that disease and delight their husbands:

> The maidens with flower-like, collyrium-dark eyes,
> their uncontrollable, rounded, striped breasts burning with heat,
> their soft hair spread like cloud scented by *akil*—
> beneath the curved dark bow upon the moon within that cloud,
> their rich sidelong glance, red-streaked after driving away
> the flower arrows of the fish-bannered god,
> became both messenger and medicine.  
> *(28:15–21)*

Cloud is hair, Moon is face, bow is eyebrow, and the sidelong eye is messenger.

The idea that eyes which caused disease in separation become medicine in union recalls a *Tirukkuṟaḷ*:

> In this collyrium-dark eye of hers are two glances:
> one is the glance that causes disease;
> the other is the medicine for that disease.  
> *(1091)*

Some people develop a headache at four in the evening; it disappears after tea. What is the medicine? Tea. What caused the headache? The very same tea—when absent, disease; when obtained, medicine. Wine is perhaps the same; and woman too!

Compare a passage from *Kuṇa Nāṟpatu*:

> It cannot be cured by medicine, nor removed by gems;
> even severe ascetic effort can hardly drive it away.
> Like the clear liquor of honey-buzzing wine,
> which itself becomes the medicine for the illness it caused,
> the fullness-destroying sorrow brought by you
> can be cured by nothing but your grace.

The “clear wine” is liquor; “you” is the heroine. The hero addresses her. Her glance is like pinching the child and then rocking the cradle: its attraction awakens his erotic illness, while its sign of love becomes ambrosial medicine and consoles him.

Did *Kuṇa Nāṟpatu* receive this idea as a gift from the quoted *Tirukkuṟaḷ*? When Iḷaṅkō makes the eye both disease and medicine, is it his own imagination, or another gift from the *Kuṟaḷ*? *Kāṉal Vari* contains a related fancy: only a woman’s breasts can cure the disease caused by her eyes:

> The incurable, abiding sickness caused
> by the straight carp-eyes in the radiant full-moon face
> seems curable only by the soft breasts
> mottled with spreading beauty.  
> *(7:8:3–4)*

“The breasts cure” means that an embrace cures.

In this way Iḷaṅkō polishes and enriches his epic through similes and metaphors.

Simile and metaphor are arts of the people, used even in the speech of those without formal education, and are indispensable ornaments of literature. Some works gain great value from them; poets who use many apt similes may receive the title “Poet of Similes.” But writers who calculate that they must insert a fixed number and force them into the work cannot attain greatness. Similes must flow naturally through the current of thought and give delight. Iḷaṅkō adorns the epic by using them naturally, where needed and in the measure required.
