# 15. Depiction of Natural Scenes

It is natural for literature to depict and describe scenes of nature. There are even small works devoted entirely to such description. Need it then be asked whether natural description finds a place in the great epic *Cilappatikāram*?

In the *Auspicious Blessing Song*, the Moon sustains the world, the Sun circles Mount Meru, and the great rain pours down water.

Mountains, sea, rivers, clouds, birds, animals, varieties of trees, groves, countries, and cities are described here and there throughout *Cilampu*.

In the *Canto of Seeing the Country*, Kauṇti explains the conditions of the route: the fierce heat of summer, the cruelty of the arid tract, the scorching of trees such as *marām*, *ōmai*, *uḻiñcil*, and bamboo, and the cries of deer unable to find water.

In the *Summer Canto*, the arrival of summer is announced by the messenger-breeze from Mount Potiyil. The cuckoo, belonging to Kāma’s army, calls out, and many kinds of flowers bloom in abundance.

The songs of *Kāṉal Vari* are rich in natural scenery. The *Hill-Kuravai* depicts the wealth of the mountains.

In the *Canto in Which the City Celebrated the Indra Festival*, the descriptions of Paṭṭiṉappākkam and Maruvūrppākkam in Pukār occupy a distinguished place.

The seashore description in the *Canto of the Sea Festival* touches and delights the heart.

In the *Canto of Seeing the City*, Madurai is painted as a verbal picture.

Though descriptions of Pukār and Madurai are not strictly scenes of untouched nature, Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ must have used his imaginative eye to envision what those great cities contained.

## The Description of Evening

Evening is finely portrayed in the *Canto Celebrating the Evening*.

The Sun sets. The earth-woman grieves because she cannot see her husband, the Sun. Then, wearing the sea as her garment, she cannot yet see the princely Moon either; her face pales in all four directions, her heart grows cold, and she turns in distress. There is a short interval after sunlight fades and before moonlight begins to shine. That moment is personified in this way.

The phrase “her face paled and her heart grew cold” carries two levels of meaning:

> The faces of the directions grew pale; the red-flower eyes
> poured full streams of water, and the whole body grew wet with dew.  
> *(4:5–6)*

Because the Sun has disappeared, the face formed by the directions pales. The flower-eyes shed liquid—that is, flowers release honey. The earth’s body begins to become wet with dew.

When this hour arrives, those separated from their lovers suffer; those not separated rejoice.

Cowherds play the *Mullai* mode on their flutes. Bees place their mouths upon jasmine flowers and blow as though upon instruments.

The breeze scatters flower fragrance everywhere; women light jewelled lamps.

Just as the Pāṇḍyas defeated their enemies even while young, the white crescent defeats the petty rebel called evening and rules the stars. The word *kuṟumpu* means rebellious minor chiefs. Evening is personified as such a rebel; the meaning is that evening begins to pass away.

Those like Mādhavi, joined with their lovers, revel in joy. Those like Kaṇṇaki, separated from their lovers, sink more deeply into sorrow. Kāma moves about in triumphant delight. This is the sequence of events at evening.

Iḷaṅkō devotes an entire canto to this alone. Did he perhaps do so because the epic needed to reach a total of thirty cantos?
