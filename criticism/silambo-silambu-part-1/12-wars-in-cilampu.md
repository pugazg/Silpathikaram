# 12. Wars in *Cilampu*

Although the wars mentioned in *Cilappatikāram* have been referred to here and there under several headings, they may be gathered together in this chapter.

The Cōḻa king Mucukunta fought the demons. The broad-shouldered Cempiyaṉ destroyed the suspended fortresses in the sky.

Karikāla Cōḻa marched north, defeated the northern rulers, struck the Himalaya with his royal goad, planted the tiger banner there, and returned.

The Pāṇḍya king Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, while still young, defeated seven enemies at Talaiyālaṅkāṉam. The Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ who “crossed the Āryan army” conquered as far as the Himalaya and the Ganges.

One Cēra king defeated his enemies at sea and felled their sacred *kaṭampu* tree.

The Golden-handed Pāṇḍya hurled the weapon called the *vaḷai* at Indra’s crown and shattered it.

Neṭuñcēralātaṉ campaigned northward as far as the Himalaya, won victory there, received the title “He whose boundary is the Himalaya,” and ruled the whole of Nāvalam Island—India—under one language of command.

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ performed the battlefield sacrifice at the red field of the Koṅkus; defeated the forces that opposed him when he went to bathe his mother in the Ganges; and, when nine collateral Cōḻa princes opposed his brother-in-law Kiḷḷi at Nērivāyil, defeated them and secured Kiḷḷi’s throne. At Mōkūr he cut down the neem that served as the guardian tree of Paḻaiyaṉ and wore the garland of victory. He conquered Viyalūr and fought a fierce battle at Iṭumpil, defeating his enemies.

Later, the northern kings Uttaraṉ, Vicittiraṉ, Uruttiraṉ, Pairavaṉ, Cittiraṉ, Ciṅkaṉ, Taṉuttiraṉ, and Civētaṉ joined Kanaka and Vijaya in battle against Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ. He defeated them all, placed on the crowns of Kanaka and Vijaya the black stone from which Kaṇṇaki’s image was to be carved, and compelled them to carry it south.

Besides these wars of kings, the epic also makes scattered reference to battles fought by Śiva, Viṣṇu, Murukaṉ, and Koṟṟavai.

If every battle were described in detail, the discussion would expand into a large book of its own.
