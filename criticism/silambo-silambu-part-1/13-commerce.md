# 13. Commerce

Human society cannot live without trade—within a town or beyond it, within a country or with foreign lands. No one can produce every necessary thing. In ancient times people exchanged what each had produced by barter. Barter gradually assumed several forms and finally developed into the commercial system known today. During the Sangam age and the period adjoining it, goods were carried overland on foot and in carts for exchange within the country. Ships also brought commodities from other lands by sea and carried other goods away in return.

## The Canto of Establishing Domestic Virtue

In ancient times Pukār, capital of the Cōḻas, was a great commercial centre. Varied commodities from newly encountered countries were brought there both overland and by ship and accumulated together. The *Canto of Establishing Domestic Virtue* says:

> Rare and precious goods from unfamiliar lands,
> gathered together as though those lands themselves had assembled,
> were brought and amassed by ship and by land.  
> *(5–7)*

“Unfamiliar lands” means newly reached countries. One land may produce one commodity in abundance and become famous for it. Because the products of many countries were gathered at Pukār, the city looked as though many nations themselves had assembled in one place.

## The Canto Celebrating the Evening

### Commodities from the Four Directions

In the evening, Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi and the other people enjoyed themselves on moonlit terraces. In summer they did not burn the crystalline resin brought from the west or the dark *akil* wood brought from the east. Instead, they ground sandalwood from the southern mountain on a circular stone brought from the north and applied the paste:

> Leaving aside the white resin from the western quarter
> and the dark *akil* from the eastern quarter,
> on the lustrous round stone born in the northern mountain
> they ground sandal born in the southern mountain.  
> *(35–38)*

In cold weather resin and *akil* were burned; their smoke gave both firm warmth and fragrance. In summer sandalwood was rubbed on a circular grinding stone and applied. I know that households once possessed such stones because there is one in our own house. In my youth, after bathing, one applied sacred ash to the forehead, placed a spot of sandal paste in the centre, and then a smaller spot of vermilion within that. The household sandal-grinding stone has now become rare.

The following lines of *Neṭunalvāṭai* invite comparison:

> They burned white resin with dense-grained *akil*.  
> *(56)*

> The lustrous round stone brought by northerners
> lay with sandal from the southern land.  
> *(51–52)*

It is astonishing that the two works correspond as though their authors had agreed beforehand.

The passage shows that resin came from the west, *akil* from the east, grinding stones from the north, and sandalwood from the south, all through trade.

A doubt may arise: Pukār stood on the seashore, with the sea to its east; how could *akil* come “from the east”? It could have been shipped by sea from an inland region lying eastward. It might also have come from countries beyond the Bay of Bengal. Goods from the other three directions need not all have come by ship; they could have arrived overland.

## The Canto in Which the City Celebrated the Indra Festival

### Maruvūrppākkam

Pukār had two divisions, Maruvūrppākkam and Paṭṭiṉappākkam.

Under French rule Puducherry too had two sectors, the European quarter and the Tamil quarter. The European settlement lay along the shore and the Tamil settlement to its west, separated by a canal. Traces of this arrangement remain. Even after the Europeans departed, their quarter remained clean; the Tamil quarter cannot be praised to the same degree. Following the psychological method of moving from the near to the distant, the present to the past, the easy to the difficult, and the known to the unknown, we may proceed from Puducherry to Pukār.

Just as Puducherry had a European quarter by the sea, Pukār had Maruvūrppākkam, where people from many countries stayed. Paṭṭiṉappākkam lay to its west. *Pākkam* is one of the words for a settlement, while *paṭṭiṉam* means a city; Paṭṭiṉappākkam therefore indicates the large, urban part of the settlement.

What does Maruvūrppākkam mean? *Maruvu* means to join, mingle, or become associated. The word occurs in the same sense in the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*: “Associate with the friendship of the faultless” (800). The quarter where numerous foreigners came, joined, and mingled was called Maruvūrppākkam. In parts of the old South Arcot district, the first ceremonial meal taken by a bridegroom in the bride’s house, or by a bride in the bridegroom’s house, is called *maruvu-uṇṇutal*, “eating after joining.” The two families are usually called *campantikaḷ*, a Sanskrit-derived word. It would be more beautiful to call them in Tamil *maruviṉōr* or *maruviṉavar*—those who have joined.

### What Maruvūrppākkam Contained

Maruvūrppākkam contained the splendid and profitable residence of the Yavanas, so attractive that it detained passers-by; lodgings where migrants who had brought goods by ship from many lands lived together; streets where vendors cried out and sold coloured pastes, lime powder, sandal paste, flowers, incense, and perfumes; districts selling innumerable textiles, gems, grains, prepared foods, fish, meat, salt, betel, and many other things; and quarters occupied by craftsmen of numerous trades. Iḷaṅkō sings:

> Near the harbour mouth stood the dwellings of the Yavanas,
> rich beyond measure and arresting all who saw them;
> within the shining waterfront lived together
> migrants whose wealth had come by ship.
>
> Through the city streets moved sellers of pigments,
> lime powder, cooling sandal, flowers, incense,
> and every desired fragrance.
>
> There were the quarters of subtle-fingered weavers
> who worked in silk, wool, and cotton thread;
> broad streets where wealth lay bewilderingly heaped—
> fine cloth, garments, pearl garlands, *akil*,
> flawless pearls, gems, gold,
> and rare treasures beyond measure or reckoning.
>
> There was the grain street, where classified wares
> and heaps of grain were amassed;
> and the meat-rich quarter crowded with sellers of *piṭṭu*,
> bakers of cakes, vendors of toddy,
> fisherfolk selling fish, merchants in white salt,
> betel sellers, perfume dealers, workers in many fats,
> and sellers of oil.  
> *(9–27)*

The poem continues at length. *Kāḻiyar* are sellers of *piṭṭu*; *kūviyar* are bakers; *pācavar* sell betel; *ōcunar* sell oil. People from China, Arab lands, Greece, Egypt, Rome, and elsewhere were referred to as Yavanas or Cōṉakars.

This passage makes the commercial prosperity of Pukār unmistakable.

### Merchants’ Flags

Merchants who brought many goods by ship from many countries spread them for sale on the white sand of the shore. In each place where a particular commodity was heaped, they planted a flag announcing what was sold there:

> Migrants whose wealth came by ship,
> on the white sand beside the spreading waves,
> raised flags in the market streets to proclaim their wares,
> along the ordered merchant quarter.  
> *(130–133)*

Some merchants today advertise by writing on broad rectangular cloth banners. The custom of cloth advertising seems to have existed then as well. The phrase *mālai-c-cēri* deserves attention: *mālai* means order or arrangement, and *cēri* here denotes the separate section in which each class of goods was sold.

It is painful that Kāvirippūmpaṭṭiṉam, which flourished with such distinction, was swallowed by the sea. So far we have considered the commercial wealth of the Cōḻa city of Pukār. We may now turn to that of the Pāṇḍya city of Madurai.

## The Canto of Seeing the City

Kōvalaṉ left Kaṇṇaki in the care of Kauṇti in the outlying settlement and went to look around Madurai. He saw a market street selling the nine gems; a quarter where flags advertised gold; streets selling hundreds of varieties of cloth woven from cotton, silk, and different kinds of hair or wool; and vendors who moved about at every hour with pepper bundles, balances, measures, and loads of grain. He also saw many other commercial establishments and quarters of the city:

> Experts who distinguished every kind of gem
> gathered in the secure and prosperous street
> that knew no fear of hostile attack.
>
> Dealers who tested the shining metal and dispelled doubt
> raised bright flags in the splendid street,
> selling the four renowned forms of gold—
> Cātarūpam, Kiḷicciṟai, Āṭakam, and Cāmpūnatam.
>
> In the cloth street lay hundreds of folded varieties,
> impossible to classify fully,
> woven from ordinary thread, hair or wool,
> and the finest silk thread.
>
> Bearers of balances, men with broad weighing pans,
> and keepers of measuring vessels moved everywhere;
> without regard to hour, with sacks of black pepper,
> they heaped grain in the grain street.  
> *(199–211)*

The street of gem experts was so well protected that it knew no fear of enemies. Sellers of the four kinds of gold displayed flags to advertise their trade.

The canto explains the properties of the nine gems and the expertise required to examine them. Trade will be considered further under the chapter on Madurai. The commercial prosperity of ancient Tamilakam inspires great wonder.
