# 8. Omens

An *nimittam* is an omen: a sign that indicates beforehand some good or evil that will occur later. One who knows and explains such signs is called a *nimittikaṉ*, an interpreter of omens. Royal courts sometimes maintained such a person. The word *cokiṉam* is used in the *Puṟapporuḷ Veṇpāmālai* with the same meaning:

> “The bamboo-shouldered woman feared  
> when the words overheard and the omens differed,  
> and so the prescribed act was undertaken.” (264, introductory verse)

The old commentary on introductory verse 310 also uses the word:

> “Because of a variation in the omen, the woman whose shoulders resembled tall bamboo grieved when the spear-bearing hero failed to return.”

*Tolkāppiyam*, *Patiṟṟuppattu*, and *Puṟapporuḷ Veṇpāmālai* also use the name *uṉṉam*:

> “The condition of the omen-tree that stood against the opposing king …” (*Tolkāppiyam*, Poruḷ, Puṟam 5:8)

> “Though the thin-stemmed omen-tree drooped  
> before Nārmuṭi Cēral of garland-rich strength …” (*Patiṟṟuppattu* 40:16–17)

If the *uṉṉam* tree flourished, it was a good omen; if it drooped, a bad one. Yet the king did not fear even when it drooped.

> “Our king is the enemy of the thin-stemmed omen-tree,  
> with its small leaves and flowers like gold.” (*Patiṟṟuppattu* 61:5–6)

Even when the golden-flowered, small-leaved tree revealed a bad omen, the king is said to have marched against it as though against an enemy.

> “The fame of the ankleted king, difficult to approach,  
> grew abundant when joined to the omen-tree.”

And:

> “O omen-tree, unfurl all your branches!  
> The enemies who would not bow before  
> the close-ankleted lord of the unapproachable army  
> have been crushed in conflict;  
> the kings bore every assault, but battle has ended.” (*Puṟapporuḷ Veṇpāmālai*, Omen-State 243)

The song tells the tree: “The enemies are destroyed; our king has won; therefore you may flourish even more.” Because the *uṉṉam* tree served as an object of divination, *uṉṉam* itself came to mean omen.

The cry of the black bird called *kāri* was also considered evil. A *Puṟapporuḷ Veṇpāmālai* poem says that the bird cried in the enemy’s territory, thereby indicating victory for the speaker’s king:

> “Among the gem-like cattle of the hostile men  
> wearing veṭci flowers,  
> the black bird cries in distress.” (3:3–4)

The same belief appears in the *Cīvakacintāmaṇi* and other works. The *Puṟanāṉūṟu* also says that if old birds depart and new birds arrive, it is an evil omen.

Good and bad were sometimes inferred from whether words accidentally overheard contained something auspicious. This practice is called *viricci* or *vāyppuḷ*. Going to the edge of a small settlement to listen for chance words is called *pākkattu viricci* in the *Puṟattiṇaiyiyal* of *Tolkāppiyam* (58). The terms *viricci* and *vāyppuḷ* also occur in the *Puṟapporuḷ Veṇpāmālai*.

Some believe that a cat or jackal must not cross one’s path. The old saying deserves remembrance: “What does it matter whether the jackal goes right or left, so long as it does not leap upon you and tear you?” Some also treat the approach of a solitary Brahmin or a widow as an evil omen. That is not right.

Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ, well acquainted with all these beliefs, introduces several omens into his work.

## The Canto in Which the City Celebrates Indra’s Festival

On the day of Indra’s festival in Pukār, Kaṇṇaki’s left eye twitches and Mādhavi’s right eye twitches. One omen-system holds that, for women, twitching on the left brings good and twitching on the right brings harm; for men the reverse applies.

The good approaching Kaṇṇaki is that Kōvalaṉ will soon leave Mādhavi and return to her. The harm approaching Mādhavi is that Kōvalaṉ will soon leave her:

> “Kaṇṇaki’s dark eye and Mādhavi’s red eye  
> hid the fullness within and shed secret tears;  
> in corresponding order, the left and the right twitched  
> on the festival day of the king of the gods.” (237–240)

The line must be read in ordered correspondence: Kaṇṇaki’s eye twitched on the left and Mādhavi’s on the right. *Eṇṇumuṟai* is the Tamil equivalent of “respectively.”

## The Canto of Seeing the Country

It is harmful to the land if the dark planet Saturn smokes and takes a hostile position in enemy houses; if the comet called Dhūmakētu, which ought not to appear, becomes visible; or if Venus, which should rise in the east, appears in the south. Even under such conditions, the Kāviri is said never to fail in water—though one might add, “in those days.”

> “Though the dark one smokes, though the smoke-banner appears,  
> though broad-rayed Venus travels through the southern quarter …” (102–103)

Such occurrences are evil omens.

## The Hunter’s Song

A warrior sets out wearing veṭci flowers to seize the enemy’s cattle. He also receives Koṟṟavai’s favour. The black bird cries in the enemy settlement, foretelling harm to them:

> “When a warrior of the small, fortified town  
> sets out to seize cattle,  
> it seems the white-sworded Lady of the Battlefield too  
> desires him to wear the veṭci flower.  
> If she desires him to wear the veṭci flower,  
> the black bird in the hostile town  
> seems to announce it in a harsh voice.” (12)

The untimely call of birds such as the black bird was treated as a bad omen.

## The Canto of the Killing Ground

If a bull comes forward to gore someone leaving home, that too is an evil sign. Kōvalaṉ takes one anklet from Kaṇṇaki and leaves Mātari’s house to sell it in the market. A bull attempts to gore him; somehow he escapes. He does not recognise the obstruction as a bad omen, because he does not belong to a community trained to interpret it. Instead of returning home, he continues to the market, is accused of theft, and is killed:

> “Leaving the house of the many-cowed herdsmen,  
> he went through the street with a faltering gait.  
> A humped bull confronted him; he did not know it as a fault,  
> for he was not of the community qualified to understand.” (98–101)

The phrase “he went through the street with a faltering gait” movingly suggests that he walked weakly under the weight of anxiety.

In ordinary life, some people who see an evil sign after setting out on an errand return home. Kōvalaṉ’s written destiny was different. What could be done?

Thieves too are said to examine omens before stealing. Even if a rare treasure seems likely to fall easily into their hands, they will not proceed if the departure omen is bad:

> “Unless the omen is favourable,  
> they will not enter at all,  
> even when a rare treasure comes into their hands.” (178–179)

One may wonder whether thieves also consulted omens. More than others, thieves had reason to do so. Because theft in those days could receive the death penalty, they needed to return successfully without being caught.

## The Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*

In the cowherd settlement, Mātari discusses several evil signs with her daughter Aiyai:

- Milk in the pot, though treated with culture, does not set as curd.
- Tears flow from the bull’s gentle eyes.
- Butter does not melt when heated.
- Goat-kids do not leap and play.
- The cattle tremble and cry as though lamenting.
- Their neck-bells break loose and fall.

The prose-song prelude says:

> “The milk in the pot does not set;  
> tears fall from the gentle eyes of the hump-backed bull;  
> something is coming.”

> “The fragrant butter in the hanging pot does not melt;  
> the kids do not leap and play;  
> something is coming.”

These omens prepare the audience for news of Kōvalaṉ’s death.
