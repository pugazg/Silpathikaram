# 27. The Excellence of Madurai and the Pāṇḍya

> “Lofty Madurai, rich in mansions,
> and the immense glory of the Pāṇḍya,
> distinguished in celebrated song.”
> (*Prologue*, 19–20)

The proem of *Cilappatikāram* thus celebrates both Madurai and the Pāṇḍya. Madurai’s distinctions belong to its king, and the king’s distinctions belong to Madurai. Let us examine some of both.

In the “Canto of Lodging in the Outskirts,” Kōvalaṉ tells Kauṇti that Kaṇṇaki’s feet cannot endure walking by day upon the stony road heated by the fierce sun. They may instead travel at night, he says, because under Pāṇḍya rule there is no cause for fear. The powerful bear kills no traveller and digs up no anthill; the striped tiger does not attack herds of deer; crocodiles, snakes, spirits, and thunder harm no living being. The fame of the land protected by the just sceptre of the southern king has spread everywhere:

> “The strong-seizing bear digs up no cruel anthill;
> the sword-striped tiger assails no herd of deer;
> serpent, spirit, prey-seeking crocodile, and thunder
> cause no affliction to those who pass.
> Great indeed is the fame spread everywhere:
> ‘This is the land guarded by the just sceptre
> of the southern king.’” (13:5–10)

It is a literary convention to imagine that where righteous people, ascetics, and sages dwell, wolf and lamb drink at one ford and tiger and deer sleep in one place. Many works contain such descriptions. Iḷaṅkō speaks in that tradition through Kōvalaṉ, but he has a particular purpose in placing the statement here; that will become clear later.

Kōvalaṉ mixed simply with the travelling bards he met and sang with them. He asked how far Madurai remained. They replied:

“The wind of Madurai, steeped in many fragrant pastes, flowers, and incenses, is already blowing upon us. Though poets praise the breeze from Potiyil, it does not equal Madurai’s wind because it is not mingled with such perfumes. Since the Madurai breeze has begun to touch us, the prosperous ancient city of the Pāṇḍya cannot be far. Even a solitary traveller will meet no one who obstructs or troubles him; the road is so secure that you may proceed by night.”

> “Not like the Potiyil breeze exalted
> by the red tongues of poets,
> behold—the Madurai breeze has come here.
> His ancient city, rich in sacred prosperity,
> is no great distance away;
> even if you go alone, none will bar your way.” (13:130–134)

Madurai’s wind is said to surpass even the breeze from Potiyil, and a person may travel alone in Pāṇḍya territory without fear. What a delightful description!

Yet in the latter half of the scientifically advanced and highly civilised twentieth century—1992—not only can one not travel alone; sometimes one cannot travel safely even in a group. When will the human herd reform?

As the three travellers approached Madurai, the drums of temples, the recitation of the Vedas by *antaṇar*, the war-drums of soldiers, elephants’ trumpeting, horses’ neighing, and wrestlers’ drums roared like the sea, sounding as though the city had come forth to welcome them:

> “As the tumultuous Kūṭal roared like the dark sea,
> its swelling sound came forth to meet them.” (13:149–150)

The phrase “came forth to meet them” may simply mean “sounded before them,” but it is more attractive to understand it as a ceremonial welcome. When honoured persons enter a town they may be received with drums and music; the passage resembles that. This is not literal fact but poetic attribution.

Could it also, playfully, mean that the sound was “contrary” to them—an ominous reception—because none of the three would live in Madurai and all would die? That is only an amusing secondary interpretation.

They next reached the Vaigai, whose water was hidden from sight. As though the good woman Vaigai knew the death awaiting Kaṇṇaki and did not wish to look upon the travellers in grief, she covered her entire body with a garment. The “garment” was the mass of fragrant flowers floating upon the current. She also restrained her tears:

> “The unfailing banner-woman of the ancient line,
> the Vaigai, as though she knew what would befall the lady,
> covered herself in a garment of holy fragrant flowers
> and concealed and restrained the long water filling her eyes.” (13:170–173)

Iḷaṅkō gives a double meaning to “the long water filling her eyes.” The word *kaṇ* can mean both eye and place. The Vaigai restrained the waters filling its channel and flowing from afar, keeping them within its banks so that they did not overflow, destroy anything, or frighten the travellers. The line combines punning and poetic attribution: a natural river covered in flowers and held within its banks is represented as deliberately behaving so for their sake.

The three crossed the Vaigai on a wooden raft, reached its southern bank, circled Madurai, and lodged in an outer settlement.

The blue lilies, water-lilies, and lotuses along their road seemed to know beyond doubt the suffering awaiting husband and wife. They grieved to sorrowful music, shed tears, and trembled when the wind touched them:

> “The dark long blue lilies, water-lilies, and lotuses,
> as though they knew without doubt
> the lonely grief awaiting the woman and her husband,
> heard the musical bees lament in compassion;
> they shed tears and trembled as the wind struck.” (13:184–188)

To put it bluntly, the bees beat their breasts for wages and sang funeral music on behalf of the flowers. The flowers paid the bees in honey. “Tears” also carries the sense of intoxicating liquid—honey—and “trembling when the foot touched” also means swaying when the wind (*kāl*) blew. This too is poetic attribution.

The long cloth banners flying above the walls stretched toward the travellers as though extending hands to stop them and warn, “Do not come to Madurai”:

> “The long flags upon the hard-won fortress,
> waving across their path, showed hands
> as though saying, ‘Do not come.’” (13:189–190)

Literature often represents banners moving in the wind as either barring someone or welcoming them. In Villiputtūrār’s *Bhārata*, the flags on Kṛṣṇa’s walls seem to stop Duryodhana, who has come seeking military aid:

> “Though you have come here, our cloud-hued lord of beauty
> will not become an ally in war for any but the Pāṇḍavas.
> ‘Turn back and go,’ the splendid banners
> upon every crest of the vast wall seemed to bar him with their hands.”
> (*Uttiyōka Paruvam*, 2:6)

That is a poem of refusal. By contrast, in the “Vision of Mithilā” in Kampaṉ’s *Rāmāyaṇa*, Mithilā’s banners welcome Rāma, Viṣṇu incarnate, who has come to marry Sītā:

> “Thinking, ‘The red goddess left the spotless lotus
> and, through our austerity, came to dwell here,’
> the fragrant city stretched out the hands
> of its rich jewelled banners toward the lotus-red-eyed lord,
> as though calling, ‘Come swiftly!’” (1)

*Ollai vā* means “come quickly.” The violent fluttering of the banners appears like an urgent summons.

Leaving Kaṇṇaki with Kauṇti, Kōvalaṉ toured ancient Madurai. He saw streets of perfume-sellers; courtesans’ streets; a bazaar of many goods; streets selling the nine gems; cloth shops; grain markets; quarters of different communities; three-way and four-way junctions; temples; bazaars; sacrificial halls; branching lanes; and streets shaded like canopied avenues by flags and standards.

In Madurai, drums, conches, and horns sounded each morning at the temples of Śiva, Tirumāl, Baladēva, and Murukaṉ, at the dwellings of the righteous, and at the king’s palace.

The market streets were crowded with carts, elephant-goads, fly-whisks, leather shields, armour, spears, objects of copper and bronze, weapons, garlands, elephant tusks, tools, and varieties of sandal paste.

Gold shops sold four kinds of gold: Cātarūpam, Kiḷicciṟai, Āṭakam, and Cāmpūnatam.

Textile shops stacked hundreds of each kind of garment made from cotton, silk, fine animal hair, and other fibres.

In the grain-market street, brokers moved with balances and measuring vessels. Bundles of pepper, areca nut, and other goods filled the place, and sixteen kinds of grain were sold.

The fortress walls carried self-acting mechanical weapons: devices that shot arrows; monkey mechanisms; stone-slings; machines holding boiling oil and molten copper; baskets of stones; hooks; devices that twisted necks or bit skulls; machines that hurled climbers from the walls; impaling stakes; arrow-bundling mechanisms; needle traps; kingfisher devices that pecked out eyes; boar, striking-bamboo, elephant, snake, eagle, tiger, cart, and ram mechanisms. These were designed to attack enemies automatically.

## The Lunar Dynasty

In the evening sky, the young crescent appears, defeats the petty chiefs of darkness called evening, spreads white rays, and rules the kingdom of the stars. How can it do this? Because, though young, it is the ancestor of the Pāṇḍya dynasty, whose kings defeat hostile rulers even in youth. The Pāṇḍyas were said to belong to the lunar line:

> “Though young, they destroy hostile kings—
> because it is the first of that battle-great southern line,
> the white crescent appears in the evening sky,
> drives away the petty chiefs of sorrowful darkness,
> spreads its milk-white rays without swerving from order,
> and, as a silver lamp, rules the kingdom of stars.” (4:21–26)

The idea that Pāṇḍyas defeated enemies while still young also occurs in the *Puṟanāṉūṟu*. Kārikkaṇṇaṉār of Kāvirippūmpaṭṭiṉam praised the Pāṇḍya Peruvaluti who died at Veḷḷiyampalam as a bull of the five Pāṇḍyas, able to destroy enemies as even a small thunderbolt can destroy a great race of serpents:

> “Though young, like the rare thunderbolt
> that strikes and destroys a branching serpent clan,
> you are the battle-great bull of the five Pāṇḍyas,
> intolerant of warriors who oppose you.” (58:6–8)

The image of a small thunderbolt suggests victory over enemies even in childhood.

Iṭaikkuṉṟūr Kiḻār similarly praised Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, victor of the battle at Talaiyālaṅkāṉam. The king was a boy who still wore children’s tinkling anklets and the five-weapon amulet that protected infants. When enemies arrived, the child removed the anklets, put on the warrior’s anklets, cast away the protective amulet, stopped drinking milk, ate solid rice, went to war, and won:

> “He removed the tinkling anklets and fastened bright warrior’s anklets...
> today he gave up milk and ate solid food...
> the new warriors who came against him in anger...
> he struck them down so that they fell upon the earth;
> yet he neither rejoiced nor swelled with pride.” (77)

Against the background of such traditions, Iḷaṅkō says that “though young, the southern kings drive away hostile rulers.”

The moon bears the name *uṭupati*, “lord of the stars,” in works including Kampaṉ’s *Rāmāyaṇa*. The expression must be read within ancient cosmology, not through modern science. Because the moon appears larger than the stars at night, it was called their lord. Mythology also treats the twenty-seven stars from Aśvinī to Rēvati as women and the moon as their husband. Iḷaṅkō’s phrase “king of the stars” carries this sense.

## The Tall One

The “Canto of Burning” says that the Pāṇḍya conquered lands, wielded a just sceptre, eliminated cruel practices, established lasting victory and fame, and protected the world:

> “The Tall One of celebrated distinction,
> who gained the earth and wielded the just sceptre,
> cut down cruel practices, secured victory,
> enlarged enduring fame, and guarded the fourfold world.” (22:57–60)

“The Tall One” denotes a Pāṇḍya king and is especially associated with the Pāṇḍya who stood while the sea washed his feet. The *Puṟanāṉūṟu* calls him “the Tall One of the festival of the three seas” (9:10). The *Maturaikkāñci* likewise speaks of:

> “The Tall One, garlanded upon the chest,
> whose great benefaction gave land,” (60–61)

and:

> “Like the Tall One of earth-giving fortune,
> of fame made splendid by united assemblies.” (62–63)

The element “long” in the name Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ may derive from the title Neṭiyōṉ, “the Tall One.” In the four lines from *Cilappatikāram* quoted above, Iḷaṅkō repeatedly portrays the Pāṇḍya from several angles.

## Madurāpati’s Praise

The city-goddess Madurāpati praises the Pāṇḍya to Kaṇṇaki after the city has been burned. Under his rule, she says, no sound had ever been heard except the recitation of sacred learning; the bell of inquiry had never rung. Apart from enemy kings who refused to bow at his feet, no citizen had ever accused him of tyranny:

> “Except the sound of the tongue of sacred learning,
> he had never heard the sound of the bell’s tongue.
> Apart from kings who would not bow at his feet,
> he was no ruler whom his subjects reviled.” (23:31–34)

She continues that the Pāṇḍya line bore no stain concerning women. It descended from the Golden-Handed Pāṇḍya, who cut off his own hand so that no reproach might arise concerning the wife of a Brahmin named Kīrantai. It also descended from the king who not only released the Brahmin Vārttikaṉ, wrongly imprisoned by royal officers, but freed all the other prisoners as well.

Madurāpati therefore declares that Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ, whom Kaṇṇaki accuses, was “born in a distinguished lineage joined to righteous conduct” (23:40).

The Pāṇḍyas were said to have made the northern golden mountain—Mount Mēru—their boundary. Iḷaṅkō indicates this by the phrase “he whose boundary was the golden peak” (23:12).

The deified Kaṇṇaki herself says:

> “The southern king was without evil;
> he became an honoured guest
> in the temple of the king of the gods.” (29:10)

Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ will always possess the distinction of straightening, with the life that left his body, the cruel sceptre that had bent.

## The Pāṇḍya’s Blame

I remember hearing in childhood, in street theatre or a stage play, Kaṇṇaki singing to the king during their debate: “O vengeful Pāṇḍya who repays blame with blame!”

Yet this chapter has so far described his excellence at length. Iḷaṅkō’s stylus even makes the palace gatekeeper bless him as “the Pāṇḍya untouched by blame.” Some researchers join Iḷaṅkō in great efforts to rescue the king from reproach. They cite the compassionate statement placed in the mouth of the rival king Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ:

> “The king straightened with his departing life
> the sceptre that cruel karma had bent,
> and made it once more a just sceptre.” (25:98–99)

But why did the sceptre bend, and why did it then need straightening? Only because it had bent did it require correction. To save the king from blame, the offence is assigned to karma: “cruel karma bent it.” If so, a murderer too kills only through karma—why then impose the death penalty upon him?

The queen had become estranged and withdrawn from the king. Eager to end her estrangement, he hurried toward her apartments. The goldsmith intercepted him and announced that the thief who stole the anklet had been caught. Without investigation, without consulting an assembly or delivering a considered judgment, the king ordered: “Kill him and bring the anklet.” Can the world, then or now, accept that the fault belonged to karma? Would it not mock Tamilakam for having possessed so degraded a king? Perhaps the experiential saying “love has no eyes” arose precisely because this catastrophe resulted from sexual estrangement and the rush toward reconciliation.

The king’s own death is sufficient evidence that he was guilty beyond measure. He must have reflected deeply: “Because Kōpperuntēvi withdrew from me, I hurried to resolve our quarrel; on that path I believed the goldsmith and committed this enormous wrong.” His heartbeat then stopped. Today this would be called a heart attack.

There is still a way for human society to forgive him. Had he been a tyrant, he could have comforted himself that an unfortunate error had occurred, sentenced the goldsmith to death, begged Kaṇṇaki’s forgiveness, offered her assistance, and closed the case. Instead, before news of his failure could spread to other kings and peoples, he gave up his life. Iḷaṅkō makes Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ say:

> “The king of kings grieved and said:
> ‘Before the words that he had departed from justice
> could reach the ears of rulers like ourselves,
> he caused the news that his life had left its abode
> to reach us first.’” (25:94–97)

Before others heard that the Pāṇḍya’s sceptre had failed, they first heard that he was dead. There is therefore no path left but to forgive his blameworthy act.

## Kōpperuntēvi’s Share of the Fault

When the king died, Kōpperuntēvi too gave up her life, proving that, like Kaṇṇaki, she possessed a fierce form of marital chastity. If a person’s mother dies, another may console them by saying, “I will be like your mother and help you.” But if a woman’s husband dies, one cannot say, “I will be your husband; do not grieve.” That cultural truth is expressed in the line:

> “For those who have lost a husband, there is none who can be shown in his place.” (20:80)

The queen’s death was caused not only by the loss of her husband; she shared in his blame, a point most people fail to consider. Instead of simply accepting that her husband had enjoyed the dances and songs of the women of Kūṭal, she imagined a hidden meaning, became estranged, left the public assembly, and went to the inner palace. The king therefore hurried after her to end the quarrel. On the way, the goldsmith accomplished his design. The queen must have understood her own role in this; death followed.

On hearing the goldsmith, the king could have thought: “The queen’s estrangement can be resolved later; first let us investigate the theft.” He might have consulted his ministers and rendered justice. There may, however, have been another reason why he gave priority to the queen—one that Iḷaṅkō perhaps forgot to mention. Her anger may partly have arisen because the king had still not recovered her missing anklet. He may therefore have placed first importance on finding it. Like the crow alighting at the exact moment the palmyra fruit falls, the goldsmith appeared then and said that the thief had been caught. The king may have ordered his men, in accordance with royal law, to kill the supposed thief and recover the anklet.

The death penalty is imposed for murder. The king and queen—who killed Kōvalaṉ or caused him to be killed—imposed that penalty upon themselves. By their own deaths they gave renewed lustre to the fame of the Pāṇḍyas.
