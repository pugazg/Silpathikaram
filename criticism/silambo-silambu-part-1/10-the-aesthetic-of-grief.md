# 10. The Aesthetic of Grief

In the *Meyppāṭṭiyal*, Tolkāppiyar identifies eight embodied emotions. Grief is one of them. *Avalam* and *aḻukai* both mean lamentation or sorrow. Tolkāppiyar says that the emotional flavour of grief arises from four causes: humiliation, loss, displacement, and poverty:

> “Humiliation, loss, displacement, and poverty—  
> these four constitute the unfailing grounds of grief.” (5)

Here we may consider only grief arising from loss. “Aesthetic flavour” does not refer only to pleasure; it includes the many feelings produced by many causes. *Cilappatikāram* has no shortage of the aesthetic of grief.

Great losses include the loss of a good life, indispensable possessions, and life itself. Kaṇṇaki first loses her married life; finally she loses her husband, and later her own life. In connection with this, Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ and Kōpperuntēvi die. Kōvalaṉ’s father and Kaṇṇaki’s father lose domestic life and become ascetics. Their mothers cannot bear the loss of their children and die. Kavunti Aṭikaḷ, who accompanied Kaṇṇaki on the road, dies through a fast; Mātari, who gave refuge, enters fire. Mādhavi and Maṇimēkalai abandon sensual pleasure and take up renunciation. The burning of Madurai is a vast loss.

Among all these, the loss caused by Kōvalaṉ’s death under a false accusation occupies the greatest place in the epic. Evil omens appear in the cowherd settlement. News of Kōvalaṉ’s death is conveyed to Kaṇṇaki. More than death itself, the false charge that he was a thief torments her.

The Canto of the Garland of Suffering paints Kaṇṇaki’s grief like a picture. Her laments melt the heart:

“I do not see my beloved; my heart melts like the inside of a furnace. I do not see the man I loved—is this deception? My heart reels. Must I perish in misery because the king has committed a fault? O lord of burning rays, is my husband a thief?” Thus she grieves and cries aloud.

Looking at her husband’s lifeless body, she wails: “Does your fragrant body lie covered in dust? Does your jewelled chest sink into the earth?” She embraces the body. Sobbing, she asks: “Are there women in this city? Are there worthy people? Is there a god?”

She goes to argue her case before the Pāṇḍya. Addressing the gatekeeper, she attacks the king through him: “Gatekeeper! Gatekeeper of the ruler who has failed in justice, whose wisdom is emptied and whose heart has lost discernment!”

Coming before the Pāṇḍya, she startles him: “Unexamining king, I have something to say.” She boils with anger: “King of Korkai, who has failed to pursue the good path!” When the Pāṇḍya asks, “Who are you, woman whose eyes stream with water?” she speaks proudly of her country, city, community, and name.

She recounts and praises the stories of the seven chaste women of Pukār. Then she vows: “If I too, born in that city, am truly a chaste woman, I will not spare you; I will destroy Madurai together with its king.” She twists off her left breast, throws it upon the city, and causes it to burn.

At last she says, “I entered by the eastern gate with my husband; I leave by the western gate without him.” She reaches the Cēra country, ends her earthly life, and enters heaven:

> “‘I entered the eastern gate with my husband;  
> let me, now destitute, leave by the western gate.’  
> Helpless, she wandered through night and day.” (23:182–184)

This passage is deeply moving.
