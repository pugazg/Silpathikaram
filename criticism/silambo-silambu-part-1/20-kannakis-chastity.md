# 20. Kaṇṇaki’s State of Chastity

## The Auspicious Blessing Song

Kaṇṇaki is the character who appears from the beginning of *Cilappatikāram* to its end. She was the daughter of Māṉāykaṉ of Pukār, a man whose generous hand poured wealth like rain. Kaṇṇaki had the form of a golden creeper, the fortune of Lakṣmī dwelling upon a lotus, a celebrated and auspicious beauty, chastity like Arundhatī, exalted qualities worthy of women’s worship, and love for everyone. She was twelve at marriage:

> In Pukār, the city where long-enduring fame and pleasure dwell,
> she was the tender shoot of Māṉāykaṉ’s line,
> whose generous hand equalled the great rain-cloud;
> like the banner of a giving sky, she was twelve years old.
>
> Women worshipped and praised her, saying,
> “She possesses the fortune of the lotus-dwelling goddess,
> a form worthy of fame, and the virtue
> of the faultless northern star.”
> Rich in great qualities and love,
> she bore the abiding name Kaṇṇaki.  
> *(Auspicious Blessing Song, 23–29)*

Storytellers addressing children begin, “Once there was a town. In it lived a father and mother. They had a daughter.” Iḷaṅkō follows the same method: there was a town called Pukār; a man named Māṉāykaṉ lived there; he had a daughter named Kaṇṇaki; when she completed twelve years, he arranged her marriage.

Stories often identify clan names. Iḷaṅkō does so indirectly. Kaṇṇaki’s father is Māṉāykaṉ and Kōvalaṉ’s father Mā Cāttuvāṉ. Both *nāykaṉ* and *cāttuvāṉ* can denote a merchant; with *mā*, each means a great merchant. These may therefore look more like community titles than personal names. Apart from them, no principal figure of *Cilappatikāram* seems to be called by a caste title; “the Brahmin of Māṅkāṭu” is a descriptive phrase of another kind.

Suppose, by way of example, that their personal names had been Muttaiyaṉ Māṉāykaṉ and Vaṭivēlaṉ Mā Cāttuvāṉ. A famous elder of the Chettiar community may be referred to simply as “Chettiar,” without his given name; in a particular setting the title points to that one person. The same occurs with “Mudaliar.” Perhaps Māṉāykaṉ and Mā Cāttuvāṉ function similarly.

One may object that a single clan should have only one title. Yet even among relatives there may be several: Mudaliar and Chettiar; Gounder, Nayakar, and Padaiyachi; and numerous titles among the Mukkulathor. Kaṇṇaki’s and Kōvalaṉ’s related families could likewise have used Māṉāykaṉ and Mā Cāttuvāṉ. If *nāykaṉ* and *cāttuvāṉ* were enough, why add *mā*? It may correspond to present expressions such as “the great farm,” “the great estate,” “the great house,” or “the great wealthy man.”

My uncertainty arises because *mā* occurs in both names. Is it natural to both personal names, or was it artificially added to show that the fathers were equals? In street-play versions, moreover, the names are reversed and altered: Kōvalaṉ’s father is Māṇākkaṉ, resembling Māṉāykaṉ, and Kaṇṇaki’s father Māccōṭṭāṉ, resembling Mā Cāttuvāṉ. Iḷaṅkō names the fathers but not the mothers, which is a deficiency. Boatmen’s songs are said to call Kōvalaṉ’s mother Vaṇṇamālai.

In 1960 I published a novel called *Malar Maṇam*. I named only the fathers of the hero and heroine. A Ramanathapuram scholar who translated it into English told me that I should have named the mothers too. But when even the fathers’ names in the epic are uncertain, how could Iḷaṅkō have known and supplied the mothers’ names?

These observations are not final conclusions. The difficulty largely arises only when the street-play names are compared with the epic and when both names are found to carry *mā* and nearly the same meaning. I leave the matter to scholars.

Kaṇṇaki did not rebuke Kōvalaṉ for abandoning her. She did not return to her parents’ home. When he called her, she did not quarrel but departed with him at once. For this she is praised as chaste like the northern star Arundhatī.

Iḷaṅkō’s phrase “Kaṇṇaki, whose name abides” is apt. *Maṉṉutal* means to remain:

> In this impermanent world, those who sought permanence
> established their fame and then passed away.  
> (*Puṟanāṉūṟu* 165:1–2)

Kaṇṇaki’s name remains today. It will remain as long as *Cilappatikāram*—indeed, as long as Tamil. Since a temple was built for her, its endurance is assured. Iḷaṅkō knew at first hand both her history and the building of the shrine by his elder brother; thus at the very beginning he draws the auspicious opening mark by calling her “Kaṇṇaki, whose name abides.” As Arundhatī remains forever in people’s minds, so will Kaṇṇaki.

## The Canto of Establishing Domestic Virtue

### Praise of Kaṇṇaki’s Beauty

The first canto narrates the marriage. The second describes their household life. Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ excelled in love and embraced so closely that their garlands mingled, like Sun and Moon joined together. Forgetting themselves in pleasure, Kōvalaṉ gazed upon Kaṇṇaki’s auspicious face with inexhaustible love and praised her beauty:

> The flower-eyed woman and her loving lord …  
> *(11)*
>
> seeing each other, they attained delight and excelled in love …  
> *(25)*
>
> like the sight of the two lights seated together …  
> *(31)*
>
> their garlands mingled, leaving them helpless …  
> *(35)*
>
> with unending love he looked upon her auspicious face,
> and Kōvalaṉ uttered praise beyond all measure.  
> *(36–37)*

The epic first praises the Moon, then the Sun; it first introduces Kaṇṇaki and then Kōvalaṉ. There is a natural correspondence: the Moon’s gentle light precedes the Sun’s fierce light just as Kaṇṇaki precedes Kōvalaṉ. The second canto likewise says “the flower-eyed woman and her loving lord,” and compares them to two luminaries impossibly united.

### Kōvalaṉ’s Love

Some researchers write that Kōvalaṉ had no genuine inward love for Kaṇṇaki. What then is the implication of Iḷaṅkō’s repeated expressions—“loving lord,” “excelled in love,” “looked upon her face with unending love,” “Kaṇṇaki trembled before her lover,” “he entered the forest with his beloved,” “for her lover in the heat of the fierce rays,” and “he embraced at once the carp-eyed, long-eyed beloved”? The intended meaning is plainly that Kōvalaṉ possessed deep love for Kaṇṇaki.

But how did “unending love” end? Here the poet leaves readers to imagine Mādhavi’s power of enchantment, and thus suggests impermanence. Still, the phrase is not false. Kōvalaṉ eventually separated from Mādhavi and returned to Kaṇṇaki with renewed love. People say that a quarrel between husband and wife does not last. Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki reunited; “unending love” is therefore appropriate.

### A Garland of Praise

Kōvalaṉ says in effect: “My precious Kaṇṇaki, you are Lakṣmī. Because the Moon was born from the sea together with Lakṣmī, Śiva has given the crescent from his crown to become your forehead. Kāma’s sugarcane bow has been split into two eyebrows. The narrow middle of Indra’s thunderbolt has become your waist. Murukaṉ’s spear has been divided into your two eyes.”

Poets conventionally compare a woman’s forehead to a crescent, brows to a bow, waist to the narrow middle of an object such as a drum, and eyes to spears. Iḷaṅkō renews these familiar similes by changing the angle: the gods themselves donate their attributes to her. Such transformation of convention displays poetic mastery.

Bhārathidāsaṉ used a comparable method while still a young student. When his teacher, the Puducherry scholar P. A. Periyasāmi Piḷḷai, died, Bhārathidāsaṉ lamented that the teacher had left his power to illuminate their small intelligence to the Sun, his affection to their mothers, his swift poetic power to the ever-sounding sea, his fame to the Himalaya, his patience to the earth, and his sweet speech to the cuckoo. Rather than merely comparing teacher and objects, he imagines the teacher distributing his qualities among them. This resembles Iḷaṅkō’s method.

Kōvalaṉ continues: the peacock, ashamed before Kaṇṇaki’s colour and grace, has fled to the forest; the swan, embarrassed by her gait, hides among flowers. The poor parrot, though humbled by speech blending flute, yāḻ, and ambrosia, remains in her hand to learn sweetness from her.

He asks why her companions burden her with ornaments when the auspicious marriage cord itself is enough. A few flowers would suffice; why a great garland—do the women have some bargain with it? The painted design upon her breasts is enough; why add pearls—do they have some private arrangement with the necklace? Why make her slender waist suffer beneath so many ornaments? Compare the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*:

> What need has she of alien ornaments,
> who possesses a doe-like innocent glance and modesty?  
> *(1089)*

His joking suspicion of a “bargain” between her companions and the garlands gives freshness to the praise.

> O stainless gold! O pearl of the right-spiralling conch!
> O flawless fragrance! O sugarcane! O honey!  
> *(2:73–74)*

These images evoke the five senses. Gold delights sight. A smooth conch-pearl gives pleasure to touch. Fragrance delights the nose. Sugarcane delights the tongue. Honey, associated with “honeyed speech,” delights hearing through words. Kaṇṇaki thus gives joy to all five senses. Compare:

> All five senses—seeing, hearing, tasting,
> breathing fragrance, and knowing by touch—
> are contained in the bright-bangled woman.  
> *(Tirukkuṟaḷ 1101)*

The praise continues:

> Rarely attainable maiden! Medicine for my precious life!
> great, modest daughter of a great merchant house!
> Shall I call you a gem not born in a mountain?
> Ambrosia not born in the wave-filled sea?
> Music not born from the yāḻ?
> O woman with long, dark, falling hair—what shall I call you?
> Thus he praised her in many imperishable expressions.  
> *(75–81)*

Iḷaṅkō calls this “imperishable discourse.” It is indeed a passage that can be quoted by people in every age.

Kaṇṇaki lived with her husband for some years, pleasing her mother-in-law and distinguished in hospitality. Thus the second canto shows her receiving both Kōvalaṉ’s praise and her mother-in-law’s support.

One researcher has claimed that Kōvalaṉ never had sexual relations with Kaṇṇaki. Where, other than the bedchamber, could he have praised her in this fashion? Was she dissolving tamarind for a curry while he delivered these lines? They lived together as husband and wife for several years.

## The Canto Celebrating the Evening

After those years Kōvalaṉ went to Mādhavi. Kaṇṇaki no longer wore anklets, girdle, breast-paint, earrings, collyrium, forehead mark, or oil in her hair. Her face lost the sweat of union and the smile meant for Kōvalaṉ. She wore nothing beyond the auspicious marriage cord and became grief embodied:

> From her lovely small red feet the jewelled anklets went;
> from the soft-clothed waist the girdle departed.
> She drew no vermilion design upon the courtyard of her breasts
> and delighted in no ornament beyond the auspicious marriage emblem.
> Casting off heavy earrings, she let her ears hang bare;
> small beads of sweat left her moon-bright face;
> her long red-carp eyes forgot collyrium;
> her coral-radiant brow lost its mark;
> Kōvalaṉ lost the gleam of her white smile;
> her dark, dense hair forgot its dressing of oil.
> Such was helpless-hearted Kaṇṇaki.  
> *(47–57)*

She lived almost like a widow. Only the marriage cord announced that her husband lived. “Kōvalaṉ lost her white radiant smile” implies that she smiled only before him. Sweat appears on the face during union; now the absence of union removes it. This was regarded as the conduct of a chaste woman separated from her husband. One cannot expect the same today.

## The Canto of Seeing the Country

### Kaṇṇaki’s Delicacy

After travelling only one *kātam* from Pukār toward Madurai, Kaṇṇaki asked how far Madurai remained. Kōvalaṉ smiled and said it was near—“five or six *kātams*”—though the full distance was about thirty:

> Her creeper-like waist bent; her feet ached;
> her fragrant, abundant hair stirred as she breathed in short gasps.
> Her little teeth shone through childish speech:
> “Which of these is ancient Madurai?”
> He laughed and said, “Beyond our broad country,
> O fragrant-haired one, it is near—only five or six *kātams*.”  
> *(38–43)*

Extremely delicate, she had barely covered one of thirty *kātams* before waist and feet hurt and she panted. With a helpless false smile and childlike words, she looked at each settlement: “Is this Madurai? If not, how far?” Kōvalaṉ laughed with pity and disguised “thirty” as “six times five” or as “five or six,” lest the true distance overwhelm her. The scene contains a hint to be explained later. Her delicacy becomes still clearer if we imagine her walking with a hand upon Kōvalaṉ’s shoulder.

A literary parallel occurs in Kampaṉ’s *Rāmāyaṇam*. Before Hanumān goes in search of Sītā, Rāma gives identifying memories. One is Sītā’s innocent question when they left the city for the forest: before even passing the outer gate, she asked where the forest was and how far away:

> On the day when the long-crowned king’s son,
> bearing his father’s command, abandoned full wealth
> and began the road, before they had left
> the commanded city and its gate,
> she asked, “Where is the forest?”—tell her that too.  
> (*Recognition Episode*, 61)

Kaṇṇaki, like Sītā, was delicate. Such questions are natural to womanly innocence and childhood.

### Kaṇṇaki’s Trembling

While Kauṇti, Kaṇṇaki, and Kōvalaṉ rested in a grove, a wicked man and a courtesan arrived. Seeing the beautiful pair, they asked who they were. Kauṇti said, “My children.” The mockers asked whether brother and sister could be husband and wife. Hearing the vile words, Kaṇṇaki tightly covered the inner openings of her ears and trembled before Kōvalaṉ:

> Hearing the evil speech, she buried the openings of her ears;
> Kaṇṇaki trembled before her beloved.  

People cover their ears at shameful words. The poem does not merely say that she covered the ears, but that she “buried the inner ear,” closing it as completely as an object buried in earth, lest even a fragment enter.

Why did Kōvalaṉ not tremble? Social convention treats a woman’s relation with a man other than her husband as more humiliating than a man’s relation with another woman. Kauṇti too understood the insult as directed against Kaṇṇaki, not Kōvalaṉ, and cursed the pair:

> “They seem to have mocked my flower-garlanded maiden;
> become old jackals in the thorn-filled forest!”—
> such was the ascetic curse uttered by Kauṇti.  
> *(231–233)*

The circumstance reveals the gendered social inferiority imposed upon women.

### Kaṇṇaki’s Compassion

When the two became jackals and howled, Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ asked that they be restored. Women often show greater pity in such circumstances, saying “Alas, poor thing!” at news of suffering. Even when both tremble at the howl, the poet names Kaṇṇaki first:

> The fragrant-flowered maiden and the young lord trembled.  
> *(236)*

Her trembling should be read here not as fear but compassion.

## Kaṇṇaki’s Theology

### The Canto that Reveals the Nature of the Dream

Tēvanti, Kaṇṇaki’s Brahmin friend, tells the abandoned and dream-troubled woman: “Your husband does not hate you. In a former birth you failed to perform the vow required for protecting a husband. This is its result. Bathe in the pools called Sōmakuṇṭam and Sūriyakuṇṭam near Kāviri’s confluence, then worship at Kāma’s temple. You will live with your husband without separation in this life and join him in bliss in the next. Separated women act so; let us do the same.” Kaṇṇaki replies that such bathing and worship are not their custom:

> To the ornamented woman who said, “Let us bathe,”
> the fine-bangled woman replied, “It is not worthy.”  
> *(63–64)*

Many will recall the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*:

> She worships no god but rises worshipping her husband;
> when she says, “Rain,” the rain will fall.  
> *(55)*

Modern rationalists reject the usual meaning and explain it anew: such a wife is as useful as a rain that would fall upon command—if such rain existed. That reading makes the woman a metaphorical rain. Such a woman would be welcome; no one denies it. But Vaḷḷuvar did not compose the couplet with this modern interpretation. He intended the traditional meaning.

Tiruttakkatēvar also describes wives worshipping husbands in *Cīvakacintāmaṇi*: they die if he commands death, cease ornament when he departs, never utter Kāma’s name, and live worshipping the husband; like Lakṣmī upon a honeyed flower, they relieve the distress of the man they join.

Vaḷḷuvar certainly knew that no woman, however virtuous, can literally command rain. The revolutionary point is: do not cling to a god while neglecting the household; attend to the family. “Rain when she says rain” is hyperbole. Elsewhere he says that the earth-woman laughs at the idle poor (1040) and that the god himself ties up his garment and runs ahead to help the person determined to raise his family (1023). The earth does not literally laugh; a god does not literally tuck up clothing. These are literary fancies. The husband-worship couplet is similar.

Yet can rationalists accept without question that Vaḷḷuvar tells the wife to worship the husband but nowhere tells the husband to worship the wife? I too am a kind of rationalist. Whoever we are, we should speak and write impartially.

The *Cilappatikāram* passage shows Kaṇṇaki’s theology: she honoured her husband as her deity.

Iḷaṅkō is skilled at arranging an apt dramatic coincidence. At the very moment Kaṇṇaki tells Tēvanti that she does not worship another god, Kōvalaṉ—having left Mādhavi—is entering the doorway. A maid announces him:

> After the fine-bangled woman replied to the jewelled woman,
> “Such bathing is not worthy,”
> a slender-waisted maid at the long-guarded doorway said,
> “Our Kōvalaṉ has come.”  
> *(63–66)*

During the same conversation Tēvanti had divined, “You are not rejected by your husband.” Like the proverbial coincidence of a crow alighting and a palmyra fruit falling, Kōvalaṉ returned moments after her prediction. The literary art deserves enjoyment.

## The Hunter’s Song

### Kaṇṇaki’s Modesty

On the road beyond Uṟaiyūr, Kauṇti, Kaṇṇaki, and Kōvalaṉ rested at a shrine of Aiyai. Cāliṉi, priestess of the hunter community, became possessed and praised Kaṇṇaki:

> “Is she not the noble woman of Koṅku,
> the lady of the western mountain,
> the maiden of southern Tamilakam,
> the shoot born from accomplished penance,
> the peerless great gem risen for the world,
> the splendid jewel?”—
> thus she spoke in divine possession.  
> *(47–50)*

Through Cāliṉi’s prophecy Iḷaṅkō hints that Kaṇṇaki will soon become a deity. A possessed oracle was an apt mouth for knowledge of the future.

Kaṇṇaki became deeply embarrassed. Saying that the wise old woman had spoken in bewilderment about one so insignificant, she withdrew behind the broad back of Kōvalaṉ and allowed a new little smile to bud:

> “The wise old woman has spoken in delusion,” she said;
> shrinking behind the broad back of the husband hard to regain,
> she stood as a new smile began to bud.  
> *(51–53)*

Some people never allow others to speak and talk only of their own greatness; when others praise them, they swell with pleasure. People of high character do not proclaim themselves and, when praised, modestly deny that they deserve it. Kaṇṇaki belongs to this second class. As the saying goes, “the eight-span body became one span,” she made herself small behind her husband’s back. He is “the husband hard to regain” because she had now recovered the man long separated from her. *Viruntu* means newness and *mūral* a smile. Because she had not smiled for years, this smile was “new.” The old proverb said, “When a woman smiles, it is over; when tobacco is spread, it is over.” Accordingly Iḷaṅkō does not make the smile fully flower; it merely buds. Modesty, innocence, fear, and reserve were perhaps prescribed for women with Kaṇṇaki in mind.

## The Splendid Deity

When Kauṇti entrusts Kaṇṇaki to Mātari, she introduces her with praise: the earth has never known the touch of this young woman’s coloured feet. Yet she has endured the fierce Sun, tongue parched, walking over stony ground and accepting trembling hardship for her beloved. Ignoring her own pain, the flower-creeper labours for her husband’s good. She is a divine woman who has undertaken the indispensable duty of chastity. “We have seen no splendid deity other than her”:

> The earth-maiden has never known
> the coloured little feet of this young creeper who came with me.
> In the fierce heat of the harsh rays,
> for her beloved she endured trembling distress,
> withered until her tongue was dry,
> and, not regarding her own suffering,
> this noble flower-creeper laboured for him.
> Other than this deity who has assumed
> the indispensable burden of chastity
> required of loving women,
> we have seen no deity of such splendour.  
> *(137–144)*

“The earth has not known her feet” means she had never walked outside the house; when she travelled, she used a vehicle. The phrase shows both delicacy and wealth.

The *Tirukkuṟaḷ* says:

> She who guards herself, protects the man who took her,
> preserves worthy speech, and never falters—is a wife.  
> *(56)*

Kaṇṇaki protected Kōvalaṉ but did not protect herself; she sacrificed self-interest for him. Vaḷḷuvar calls a wife the “companion of life”; Iḷaṅkō here calls her the “sweet companion.” The word “indispensable” in “the indispensable duty of chastity” corresponds to the meaning of the Sanskrit-derived “important.” Tamil purists use *iṉṟiyamaiyāmai*, indispensability; Iḷaṅkō teaches the usage here.

Elsewhere he calls her “the young woman of sprouting white teeth and mature wisdom” (15:202)—a woman who obtained great understanding at a young age.

## Kaṇṇaki’s Final Domestic Life

At Mātari’s house in Madurai, fine rice and many vegetables were given to Kaṇṇaki for cooking. She cut the vegetables on the curved cutting-blade. Her delicate fingers reddened; sweat fell from her face; smoke from the newly lit stove reddened her eyes. Mātari’s daughter Aiyai first kindled the fire with straw and remained nearby to help. Aiyai assisted, but Kaṇṇaki herself cooked according to the skill she possessed:

> As she cut and prepared many fresh vegetables
> with the curved-mouthed cutting tool,
> her soft fingers reddened, her auspicious face sweated,
> and her red eyes grew redder in the smoke-blackened kitchen.
> With Aiyai, who had kindled the straw fire,
> she cooked for her beloved with the culinary skill she knew.  
> *(29–34)*

Her fingers reddened because they were unaccustomed to cutting. Perhaps the English named okra “lady’s finger” after seeing such delicate fingers! *Koṭuvāy kuyam* is the curved cutting-board blade; *maṭaimai* is culinary art. Cooking must not be dismissed as simple: treatises existed for it. *Maṇimēkalai* refers to “the matter of the cookery treatise” (2:22).

Both her natal and marital homes were rich and would have employed servants; had she practised cooking earlier, fingers and eyes would not redden so quickly. Large logs do not catch at once; small twigs or straw are lit first. Appar’s hymn on Tiruvāṉaikkā says:

> When one dies, no one comes to help;
> with small firewood they kindle the flame and depart.

Though a funeral pyre uses large logs, the body is larger still, and small wood begins the fire. Here Aiyai appropriately uses straw, abundant in a cowherd household.

The phrase “cooked for her beloved” is meaningful. She did not cook for herself but for Kōvalaṉ, wishing to prepare his food with her own hand.

When it was ready, Kaṇṇaki seated him on an artfully woven palmyra mat supplied by Mātari’s house. She brought water in a clay vessel and cleansed his feet, sprinkled and smoothed the earth before him, spread the inner leaf of a young plantain, served the meal, and said, “My lord, eat the ambrosia”:

> After the prosperous man sat upon the crafted seat,
> beautifully woven by a skilful woman
> from the fair white fibre of palmyra grass,
> with her fragrant-flower hand she received
> the water from her beloved’s feet in a fired-clay bowl.
> As though removing the earth-maiden’s faintness,
> she sprinkled water and smoothed the ground with her hand,
> spread the inner leaf of an unfruited plantain,
> and said, “My lord, eat the ambrosia here.”  
> *(35–43)*

The woven mat shows that women already practised small household crafts. Kaṇṇaki’s washing of her husband’s feet reflects, to some extent, female subordination; it was the fashion of the time. The *cuṭumaṇ maṇṭai* is a kiln-fired clay bowl. Its mention suggests that she had been accustomed at home to gold or silver vessels, while Mātari’s household used clay.

The grammar allows two interpretations: she may have taken water from the vessel and rubbed his feet, or placed his feet above the vessel so that the poured water fell into it. In one house, after I ate at a table, the hosts placed a broad brass plate before me, poured water over my hands above it, and carried away the collected water. From that experience I favour the second possibility.

Comparable foot-washing occurs in Kampaṉ and *Periyapurāṇam*. In the *Kiṭkintā Kāṇṭam*, the ascetic woman Cuyampirapā, hearing that Hanumān’s party sought Sītā for Rāma, rejoices that her penance has borne fruit, washes their feet, and feeds them ambrosial food:

> Hearing them, she rejoiced: “My faultless penance
> has this very moment shown me liberation.”
> She washed their feet, lovingly fed them
> sweet food tasting like ambrosia,
> and spoke words that cooled their hearts.  
> *(56)*

In *Periyapurāṇam*, Appūti Nāyaṉār brings Appar home, washes his feet, and sprinkles that water upon himself and his family:

> He caused the sage to enter the house;
> with flower-adorned water he washed the feet before him,
> sprinkled it upon them all,
> and their hearts became full.  
> *(1807)*

Cuyampirapā and Appūti performed the act for honoured guests; Kaṇṇaki did it for the husband she revered.

The line comparing her sprinkling to “removing the earth-maiden’s faintness” is an example of the figure in which the poet attributes an imagined intention to an action. Water is still sprinkled beneath a meal leaf. It cleans the ground, deters ants from crossing the wet boundary, and keeps the leaf from suddenly sliding into a neighbour’s. Since water is sprinkled on a fainting person’s face, the poet imagines Kaṇṇaki reviving the Earth. Perhaps the Earth had fainted because she knew Kōvalaṉ would be killed within hours.

The “young plantain” is one that has not yet borne a bunch. The inner leaf is clean, broad, golden, and untouched by bird droppings; in common speech it is the *talai-vāḻai ilai*, the prime plantain leaf.

People judge a feast by the grandeur of its leaf. A comic story says that a man boasted of a rich household’s enormous leaf: the dishes at the far edge were beyond reach, so a ladle was provided to pull them closer. Another replied that at a zamindar’s feast the leaf was still larger: even the ladle could not reach, so he had to stand up and walk to fetch the dishes. The story measures hospitality by leaf-size. Mātari’s people therefore supplied a generous leaf for Kōvalaṉ.

Kaṇṇaki addresses him as *Aṭikaḷ*, “revered lord.” In dramas influenced by Sanskrit, a wife called her husband *prabho* or *prāṇa-nātha*; later Tamil drama used *attāṉ*. Kaṇṇaki uses the loftier Tamil word. This meal is her final act of household life.

A parallel in *Periyapurāṇam* describes Appūti sending his eldest son, named Tirunāvukkaracu, to cut a golden young plantain leaf for Appar’s meal. Though bitten by a snake, the boy brings it before collapsing. The mention of a young plantain leaf invites comparison with *Cilampu*.

## Kaṇṇaki’s Unchanging Heart

After Kōvalaṉ ate, Kaṇṇaki folded and gave him betel. He lamented his offences against his parents and wife: “I gave you no good, only suffering. Yet when I said, ‘Come, let us go to Madurai,’ you forgot my fault and came.” Kaṇṇaki replied:

“I did not grieve because I lacked personal pleasure during your absence. I grieved because, without you, I could not give to the righteous, honour Brahmins, receive ascetics, and welcome guests according to ancient excellence. I never displayed resentment before your parents. Seeing that I suffered because their son had left, they comforted me with loving words. To please them I smiled falsely. They understood the illness and pain hidden in that smile and grieved inwardly. My sorrow is that your conduct created such a condition. Since my life is one of unwavering attachment to you, I rose and came when you called.”

> I lost the privilege of giving to the righteous,
> honouring Brahmins, meeting ascetics,
> and receiving guests according to ancient excellence.
> The great lady who bore you and the renowned head of the house,
> the lord of vast wealth and enduring distinction,
> cast aside the anger that did not remain before them;
> with hearts rich in love they mingled words of grace
> and praised me. In the false smile with which I answered,
> the concealed illness and suffering seemed suddenly revealed,
> and their inner hearts grieved.
> You practised conduct unworthy of honour.
> Yet because mine is a life of an unchanging heart,
> when you called, I rose and came.  
> *(71–83)*

Kaṇṇaki does not lament the loss of her own pleasure, but the loss of opportunities to benefit others.

### A Note on Ordinary Life

In an ordinary family much else might have happened. Kōvalaṉ’s parents might have blamed Kaṇṇaki as an unlucky woman who brought misfortune upon their son and told her to return to her mother. Her own parents might have said, “We unknowingly married her to the companion of a courtesan; enough of her ruin,” and taken her home. Kaṇṇaki herself might have gone; it was the same town.

Instead all act with generosity. Kōvalaṉ’s parents treat her like a daughter. She conceals pain before them with a false smile. Yet “the beauty of the inner mind appears in the face,” and “as crystal shows what is placed beside it, the face shows what hardens the heart” (*Tirukkuṟaḷ* 706). They understand and grieve inwardly while maintaining an outward appearance of normality so that she will not notice. The phrase “their inner hearts grieved” is important.

Was her smile the very laughter Vaḷḷuvar recommends—“Laugh when adversity comes”? Iḷaṅkō’s account may differ from ordinary life. Perhaps everyone openly lamented; perhaps her parents invited her home and her in-laws advised her to go; perhaps she firmly refused. Who now knows what happened?

Still, Iḷaṅkō’s version is appropriate because he is not a historian but a literary author. A historian should record events without doubt or imagination. A literary author may use the event as a base and imagine what ought to happen and what society should follow. Here the implied teaching is that when a son abandons his wife, his parents must not scold or torment their daughter-in-law. They should say, “Do not fear. Your husband will return. We are here and will protect you to the end.” The daughter-in-law should remain composed. This is the better conduct.

## “Lamp of the Wide World”

Before leaving for the market with the anklet, Kōvalaṉ praises her:

> Leaving the chief kin of the household,
> the young attendants, the company of servants,
> and the circle of companions,
> you took as your great support modesty, innocence,
> the fame praised by the good, and cherished chastity;
> you came with me and removed my sorrow here.
> O gold! O creeper! O flower-adorned woman!
> image of modesty! lamp of the wide world!
> shoot of chastity! treasure of beauty!
> Taking one anklet from your little feet,
> I shall go, exchange it, and return;
> remain without bewilderment.  
> *(84–93)*

In Pukār she left four supports: principal relatives such as parents and parents-in-law; personal attendants; female servants of several functions; and her circle of friends. She brought four others: modesty, innocent reserve, the praise of the good, and protecting chastity.

Kōvalaṉ thinks his sorrow has ended, though it is about to increase. He loses himself calling her gold and creeper. “Lamp of the wide world” hints that she, formerly the lamp of a household, will become a divinity lighting the world. Literature often calls women lamps of the house:

> The radiant-browed woman who was the lamp of the house.  
> (*Puṟanāṉūṟu* 314)

> Women are the illumination of the house.  
> (*Nāṉmaṇikkaṭikai* 105)

*Maṟṟi varuvaṉ* means “I will sell or exchange it and return,” but also hints that he will return transformed—as a corpse. “Do not be bewildered” contains the hint that she will soon be overwhelmed by bewilderment.

## Kaṇṇaki’s Heroic Wrath

Four cantos—*The Garland of Sorrow*, *The Song of Circling the City*, *The Canto of the Lawsuit*, and *The Garland of the Vow*—display Kaṇṇaki’s heroic emotion and action. She hears of Kōvalaṉ’s death; walks through the streets proclaiming her vow; defeats the Pāṇḍya king in argument so completely that king and queen die; and twists off her left breast and burns the city.

People say a story may have neither head nor foot. Not every detail of *Cilappatikāram* can be accepted literally. A city does not burn because a breast is torn off and thrown. Perhaps sympathizers set fire to the quarter of the goldsmith whose action caused Kōvalaṉ’s death. Nothing can be stated with certainty. The injustice was grave, but burning an entire city is disproportionate. The episode may be an imaginative construction making Kaṇṇaki a goddess of heroic wrath.

## A Deficiency in Kaṇṇaki

After considering her greatness, one cannot ignore a possible deficiency. She lacked the power to keep Kōvalaṉ from going to Mādhavi, or at least from remaining there so long.

Some people dislike breakfast at home and go to a hotel to graze upon idli, vadai, puri, potato, pongal, sambar, chutney, kadappa, and adai curry. Kōvalaṉ’s conduct was similar. Perhaps Kaṇṇaki looked like a woman dressed in the austere form of Māriyammaṉ! Kōvalaṉ, seeking glamour, surrendered his heart to Mādhavi’s dancing, singing, swaying, shaking, and glitter. Alas, Kaṇṇaki’s life was ruined.

Some add another criticism. When Kōvalaṉ returned, she should have acted offended and made herself difficult. Instead she apparently thought he had nothing left to give Mādhavi and immediately offered the anklets; when he proposed Madurai, she secretly left with him. This, critics say, reveals lack of worldly skill.

Yet her immediate departure has a reasonable explanation. A scene from *Maṇimēkalai* helps by partial analogy.

### An Evening Scene of Swans

A male and female swan played in a lotus pond. At evening, a lotus closed around the female and hid her. The male tore open the petals, rescued her, and climbed with her onto the high frond of a tall coconut tree:

> When the lotus enclosed the female
> with whom the male swan had played till weariness,
> he tore the flower covering apart,
> took his mate, and climbed the high frond
> of a lofty, great coconut palm.  
> *(123–126)*

They left because pond and lotus had become threatening. They did not settle on a low shrub but sought distance from the water, choosing the tallest kind of tree, then the upward-reaching high frond. The poem does not say they “sat”; it says they “climbed,” preserving upward movement.

Kaṇṇaki acted like those swans. She wanted to take her husband far from Mādhavi, the threat to her household. If they remained in Pukār, the old spirit might climb the drumstick tree again—that is, Kōvalaṉ might return to Mādhavi. A proverb says, “One who has tasted food and one who has tasted woman will not remain quiet.” Her fear was natural. She also left secretly because others might prevent the journey.

Therefore her refusal to quarrel or resist cannot simply be called a defect. She had once let the fortress fall; would she be deceived again? She could not foresee what happened in Madurai. That was accidental, and she was not responsible for nature’s command.

It remains regrettable that she could not stop Kōvalaṉ at the beginning. Perhaps she tried greatly and he remained obstinate and unreformed. Who can say otherwise?
