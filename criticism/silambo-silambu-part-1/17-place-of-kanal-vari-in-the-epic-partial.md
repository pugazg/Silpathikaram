## The Turning Point

The canto called *Kāṉal Vari* is a turning point in *Cilappatikāram*. Māṭalaṉ the Brahmin tells Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ himself that it was the *Kāṉal Vari* song that finally pressed down the crowned heads of the northern kings Kanaka and Vijaya by making them carry the black stone intended for Kaṇṇaki’s image. Had this song not existed, there would have been no occasion for either the Madurai Kāṇḍam or the Vañji Kāṇḍam.

*Kāṉal* means a grove by the seashore. *Vari* means a particular kind of song. The songs sung by Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi while seated in the seashore grove are therefore called *Kāṉal Vari*.

The Indra festival was celebrated at Pukār. As usual, Mādhavi gave an elaborate performance of dance and song, and many people watched with delight. Kōvalaṉ could not tolerate it. Once Mādhavi had become exclusively his, he resented her dancing for the pleasure of everyone. This is a trait found in some men: a man may desire any woman he likes, but his own wife must not so much as raise her eyes toward another man. The parents of one prospective bridegroom went to see a girl. He is said to have asked, “Has some other prospective bridegroom already come and looked at her? If so, I do not want her.” Kōvalaṉ seems to have been that bridegroom’s elder brother in spirit. Yet Mādhavi, though born in the courtesan community, was faithful.

Realizing that Kōvalaṉ had become displeased with her, Mādhavi skilfully cooled his anger. Do women need to be taught how? The full-moon season arrived, and the townspeople went to bathe and celebrate by the sea. Mādhavi and Kōvalaṉ too went to the shore. Their very manner of travel naturally foreshadowed separation: Kōvalaṉ rode a horse-like mule, while Mādhavi travelled in a carriage.

In the coastal grove, beneath the shade of a *puṉṉai* tree, they enclosed a space on fresh sand with curtains so that it resembled a private room. A couch was placed inside, and Mādhavi and Kōvalaṉ sat upon it.

Mādhavi took the yāḻ from Vayantamālai’s hand. It had no defect in its resonator, stem, pegs, or strings. Through the eight methods beginning with *paṇṇal* and *parivaṭṭaṇai*, she sounded and examined it. As her fingers moved over the strings, she listened closely to determine whether the eight musical operations, including expansion and shaping, were properly aligned. She did not command Kōvalaṉ to play. Instead, as though merely asking him to begin and establish the rhythm for her, she placed the yāḻ in his hand.

Kōvalaṉ naturally began to perform *Kāṉal Vari*. But the songs appeared to contain signs suggesting that he had a particular woman in mind. He may have inserted such a suggestion only in mischief or play. The substance of the songs is as follows.

## 1. River Songs—“Live Long, Kāviri!”

He first sang: “O Kāviri! Even if the Cōḻa unites with the Ganges, you do not quarrel with him; that is chastity—live long! Even if the Cōḻa joins Kumari, you do not quarrel; that is chastity—live long! Your movement with fresh flood and great sound is the Cōḻa’s prosperity—live long, Kāviri!” The source provides the first two of the three songs corresponding to these ideas:

> Though the lord of the white umbrella garlanded by the Moon,
> upholding the straight sceptre upon his crowned head,
> unites himself with the Ganges,
> do not quarrel—live long, Kāviri!
>
> O carp-eyed one, though he unites with the Ganges,
> your refusal to quarrel has taught me
> that this is the great chastity of noble women.
> Live long, Kāviri!  
> *(Song 2)*

> Though the lord of the enduring white umbrella,
> upholding the unbending straight sceptre,
> unites himself with the virgin Kumari,
> do not quarrel—live long, Kāviri!
>
> O carp-eyed one, though he unites with Kumari,
> your refusal to quarrel has taught me
> that this is the enduring great chastity of women.
> Live long, Kāviri!  
> *(Song 3)*
