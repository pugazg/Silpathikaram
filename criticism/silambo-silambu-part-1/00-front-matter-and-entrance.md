# Publisher’s Note

Dr. Sundara Shanmuganar has offered Tamil readers, in the form of this book, the fruit of his many years of research into the captivating savour of *Cilappatikāram*.

Through this work we can appreciate with particular clarity the epic artistry and poetic skill of Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ, the fitting construction of his characters, and the brisk movement of his story.

This outstanding critical study of *Cilappatikāram*, the epic of the three branches of Tamil, has itself been created almost like a new work of literature.

Because the book is written in prose, everyone can read and understand it. Professor Sundara Shanmuganar cites passages from *Cilappatikāram* throughout and communicates the essence of the epic to the public in Tamil that is pleasant, simple, and touched with humour.

Readers of this fine study of *Cilappatikāram* will certainly feel the desire to read and enjoy the whole epic.

Under thirty-one headings, from “The Epic of the Three Crowned Kings” to “Solutions to Certain Problems,” the author explains the profound substance of the epic with relish.

Besides citing many works in support of his interpretations, the author establishes his arguments by supplying numerous incidents drawn from worldly experience.

Dr. Sundara Shanmuganar, who has given us this book, deserves the praise and appreciation of Tamil people.

# Entrance

## *Cilappatikāram*

Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ, who belonged to the period following the Cankam age and was attached to the Jain faith, heard the story of Kaṇṇaki from Cāttaṉār, the grain merchant of Madurai. Since Kaṇṇaki’s anklet holds the central place in that history, he composed a book connected with the anklet and named it *Cilappatikāram*.

Just as *irumpu* + *pātai* becomes *iruppuppātai* (“railway”) and *karumpu* + *vil* becomes *karuppuvil* (“sugarcane bow”), *cilampu* + *atikāram* becomes *Cilappatikāram*. *Atikāram* is that which grows or develops continuously. The *Piṅkala Nikaṇṭu* also gives “book” as one meaning of the word *atikāram*. Thus the work received the title *Cilappatikāram*, “the book concerning the anklet.” It is also known in shortened form as *Cilampu*.

This is one of the Five Great Epics of Tamil. Among them, the twin epics *Cilappatikāram* and *Maṇimēkalai* deal with histories that took place in Tamil Nadu. Of those two, *Cilappatikāram* is foremost. Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ built a temple of stone for Kaṇṇaki; his younger brother Iḷaṅkō built this temple of words.

## *Silambō Silambu!*

Subramania Bharati praised the epic as “the *Cilappatikāram* that seizes the heart.” It possesses beauty of sound and the power to intensify feeling.

This work, a revolution of womanhood, begins in the mode of happiness but finally ends in tragedy. Even so, through portions such as the Canto of Benediction, Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ supplies a somewhat artificial auspicious conclusion.

Some passages spoken in the author’s own voice employ a rarefied style, whereas the words placed in the mouths of the characters move in styles suited to them. It is a dramatic epic—need anything more be said about the flavour of its language?

Because events belonging to different times are placed wherever the circumstances require them, one may be tempted to say that *Cilampu* contains invented fictions. Yet, compared with many other epics, its false inventions are few. It is difficult to write an epic with no imaginative element at all. A work written entirely without imagination would not be an epic but a history book. This, however, is an epic.

Though *Cilampu* is an epic of ordinary citizens, it lacks nothing in literary beauty. A separate book ought to be written about the arts within it.

## How This Book Took Shape

Many critical books and essays on *Cilappatikāram* had already appeared. Why, then, did I write this book so late?

Many years ago, while studying for the Tamil M.A. examination, I filled two notebooks with notes. Later, because I lay bedridden for many years with a brain tumour, and because those notebooks remained hidden in a place where I could not see them, I was unable to turn the notes into a book. Recently, while searching for something else, I happened to find the notebooks in a bundle of papers lying on a loft. I shortened the notes to some extent, added a few new ideas, and made this book from them. One should not allow notes to go to waste, should one?

Although I studied for the Tamil Vidwan and Tamil M.A. examinations, and although I taught the epic in college, only now do I feel the inward satisfaction of having studied *Cilappatikāram* with at least some degree of method. *Cilappatikāram* is a vast ocean difficult to cross. Only a few pearls from it are introduced in this book. I am also conscious of the limits of space.

## Criticism

Some of my critical views may differ from those of others. For the Tamil M.A. examination, I chose literary criticism as my optional subject. I have read several books on criticism in both Tamil and English. No one can define conclusively, “This alone is criticism; criticism must be performed only in this way.” Each person’s principles may differ. My own criticism too may therefore take a form of its own.

The book has been given the shortened title *Tittikkum Tiṟaṉāyvu*. I ask readers to expand this as “criticism that makes [the work] taste sweet.” In other words, it means criticism that makes *Cilappatikāram* delightful. I have tried, to the extent of my knowledge and ability, to make *Cilappatikāram* taste sweet. That is all.

It may also be understood as an inquiry into Iḷaṅkō’s skill in making *Cilappatikāram* delightful.

To support my views, I have supplied a number of literary quotations, common expressions, and incidents from life. I have also included some phrases and anecdotes for humour. I ask readers not to regard them as crude. Does one not need an occasional change of flavour?

## Repetition of What Has Already Been Said

An idea connected with several people or several matters may recur in the sections dealing with each person or matter. According to the principle, “Repeating what has been said is no fault when it brings forth another meaning,” I ask readers kindly to forgive such repetitions.

I have done what I could after passing the age of seventy while continuing to bear the troubles associated with a brain tumour. Please accept it.
