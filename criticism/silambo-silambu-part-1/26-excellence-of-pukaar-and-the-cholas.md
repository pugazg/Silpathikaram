# 26. The Excellence of Pukār and the Cōḻas

At the very beginning of the work—in its tenth line—Iḷaṅkō praises the city:

> “Let us praise flower-filled Pukār! Let us praise flower-filled Pukār!”

Pukār is the shortened alternative name of Kāvirippūmpaṭṭiṉam. Today, saying “I am going to the city” may mean going to Chennai. In that period, saying “I am going to the city” may similarly have meant going to Pukār.

## Iḷaṅkō’s Disappointment

Iḷaṅkō expresses the confidence that Potiyil, the Himalaya, and Pukār would remain forever without decline. Pukār, he says, possessed a distinctive excellence and such abundant opportunities and comforts that its ancient inhabitants never needed to migrate. Since wise people of complete learning and experience inhabited all three places, they would speak only of their stable endurance, never of their destruction:

> “Whether Potiyil or the Himalaya,
> or Pukār of incomparable excellence,
> enriched by ancient clans who know no migration—
> the exalted, who have completely mastered truth
> through learning and understanding,
> say only that these stand without trembling;
> they never speak of their decline.” (1:14–19)

Contrary to Iḷaṅkō’s faith, however, Pukār was swallowed by the sea. The land on which the city stood may have projected farther into the sea than the land on either side. When a cyclone caused the sea to surge, mountain-like waves may have risen and engulfed Pukār. Its fate would have resembled the sea’s swallowing of Dhanushkodi in 1952.

It would have been better had Iḷaṅkō written only:

> “Whether Potiyil or the Himalaya,
> or Pukār of incomparable excellence...”

The great disappointment comes from his additional claim that Pukār was inhabited by ancient clans who did not know migration.

There is reason to believe that Pukār’s old inhabitants did migrate. Nāṭṭukkōṭṭai Chettiars live in Pudukkottai and Ramanathapuram districts, and their region is often called Chettinad. Some Chettiar houses there are built with the ground floor raised upon a very high plinth.

In 1962, another Chettiar visited our house with the distinguished Tamil professor Dr S. Meyyappan of Annamalai University. He too became my friend, though I no longer remember his name. I asked him why houses in his region were built with their first floor raised on such a high foundation. He explained:

“Our people came from Kāvirippūmpaṭṭiṉam, Pukār. The sea destroyed Pukār, and even in the part that survived, household possessions were soaked and ruined. Disliking a coastal region haunted by fear of water, they moved to the present elevated country where there was no such danger. Still fearing that floods might come, they built their houses on high foundations approached by many steps.”

I do not know what other Chettiars think of this explanation. Whatever the truth, Iḷaṅkō’s line about “Pukār where ancient clans knew no migration” has become a source of disappointment. Yet Iḷaṅkō cannot be held responsible for nature’s divine play.

Pukār is said to have possessed pleasure and fame comparable to both the heavenly world and the Nāga country:

> “Pukār, where enduring fame and expansive delight flourish,
> like the great city of the Nāgas and the Nāga land itself.” (1:21–22)

The city was crowded with merchants of such wealth that kings desired it. It possessed the resources to receive the whole world as guests and provide whatever they needed. Goods from many countries accumulated by ship, pack animal, and other means, making the city resemble a gathering of many lands.

In Pukār, filled with heaps of wealth piled in countless crores, trade continued after evening in the merchant streets. Lamps shone before sellers of colours, sandal paste, flowers, crafted goods, sweet cakes, fish, and other wares. The lighthouse, the lamps on the fishermen’s boats, the lights used by foreigners, and the lamps carried by guards all poured out radiance.

Pukār contained many shrines and precincts: the Karpaka shrine, the White-Elephant shrine, the Nāga shrine, the Sun shrine, the Spear shrine, the Vajra shrine, the Nigaṇṭha shrine, the Moon shrine, and others.

The city was divided into two great quarters. The eastern half along the shore was Maruvūrppākkam; the western half was Paṭṭiṉappākkam.

Maruvūrppākkam contained ornament mansions and warehouses; dwellings of *mlecchas* and other foreigners; streets of those who sold cosmetic paste, lime powder, sandal paste, flowers, garlands, and eaglewood; streets of artisans who fashioned objects from many metals and kinds of wood; tailors; leather workers; flower-stringers; a street of bards who sang with many instruments; and streets of people engaged in small and disreputable occupations. These details, with the relevant verse, have also been discussed under the chapter “Commerce.”

Paṭṭiṉappākkam contained streets occupied by the king, ministers, soldiers, Brahmins, merchants, court eulogists, Māgadha bards, physicians, astrologers, pearl-stringers, conch-cutters, timekeepers, military commanders, actors, courtesans, comic performers, and festival-drum players. The royal palace district lay in this quarter.

When one considers these two distinct quarters, one is tempted to think that division exists everywhere and in everything.

Between them lay the day-market, just as a battlefield lies between the camps of two hostile kings. There stood the shrine and sacrificial altar of the spirit who had aided the Cōḻa king Mucukunta. Sacrifices, dances, and songs were performed at the altar. Other institutions and events in Pukār included the following.

## The Altar of Human Sacrifice

From the time of Mucukunta Cōḻa, warriors are said to have offered their own lives. The warriors of Maruvūrppākkam and those of Paṭṭiṉappākkam competed with one another, blessing the king with victory and cutting off their own heads as sacrifice. We may infer that suicide troops existed even then.

A still more severe form of human sacrifice appears in the *Kaliṅkattupparaṇi*. Warriors cut their heads off at the base of the neck and place them in the hands of the goddess Koṟṟavai. The severed head praises her, while the headless trunk stands worshipping her:

> “They sever the long head at the base of the neck;
> they place the severed head in the goddess’s hand;
> the offered head praises victorious Koṟṟavai;
> the diminished body stands with hands joined in worship.”
> (*The Singing of the Temple*, 15)

The human sacrifice in the *Kaliṅkattupparaṇi* seems even more terrible than that in *Cilappatikāram*.

A pavilion in one part of Pukār contained the pearl canopy, debate hall, and ornamental gateway that Karikāla had obtained from defeated enemies.

**The Veḷḷiṭai Hall:** A place where merchants’ bundles were stored. A person who stole one was made to carry it around the city.

**The Ilañci Hall:** The sick were cured by bathing there.

**The Neṭuṅkal Hall:** Those driven mad by poison or medicine, those who had swallowed poison, and those possessed by spirits were cured by circumambulating this hall. Its stone emitted light.

**The Spirit Square:** A spirit there seized and devoured false ascetics, unchaste people, evil ministers, those who desired another’s spouse, and false witnesses.

## The Hall of the Image

This hall contained an image that shed tears silently whenever the king’s sceptre bent from justice or judges abandoned impartiality.

During the Indra festival, sacred fires were maintained in the temples of many gods, and festivities were held for the Thirty-Three and other deities. Jain and Buddhist establishments conducted special events. Musicians of many traditions performed. Royal functionaries joined together to install Indra ceremonially and conduct the festival.

During the celebrations, pleasure-seekers such as Kōvalaṉ roamed the streets in delight. Numerous public women moved among them. Their breasts brushed against the chests of men in the crowd, and the painted cosmetic designs upon the women’s breasts stained the men. Fearing that their wives would suspect and scold them if they returned home with such marks, the men went home accompanied by guests. After all, guests are included among those who can mediate and end a wife’s estrangement.

Tirunīlakaṇṭa Nāyaṉār suffered a similar “accident.” Perhaps he did not go home with a guest. His wife suspected him and declared, “Do not touch me.” From then onward he would not even turn to look at bodies draped in women’s saris. Paṭṭiṉattār kept him in mind when he sang, “I am not able, through a woman’s vow, to renounce youth.”

Iḷaṅkō created this scene for literary flavour. Deliberate bodily brushing in a crowd might occur, but matters would hardly become so serious that every man required a guest as protection.

The excellence of the Cōḻa lineage is as worthy of praise as that of Pukār. When the goddess Madurāpati addressed Kaṇṇaki, she recalled the Cōḻa king Cibi, who cut flesh from his own body for a dove, and Manu Nīti Cōḻa, who drove his chariot over his son to render justice to a cow whose calf had been killed.

Kaṇṇaki herself narrated the celebrated histories of chaste women of the Cōḻa country.

The epic records several Cōḻa victories: because he had no enemies in the south or west, Karikāla marched northward, won victories, struck the Himalaya with his goad, and engraved the tiger emblem there.

It also magnifies other glories of the dynasty—Mucukunta Cōḻa’s aid to Indra and Toṭittōṭ Cempiyaṉ’s destruction of the suspended fort, among many others.

A Tamil maxim says that women’s chastity can be protected and flourish only when a king rules justly with an upright sceptre. Kaṇṇaki, by the firmness of her chastity, is said to have assigned the fame she attained to the Cōḻa king:

> “Unless mighty kings administer justice,
> chastity cannot flourish among women of great renown—
> so declared the ancient, gracious Tamil wisdom.
> Because she was the Pattini whom the world worships and praises,
> she bestowed that glory upon the kings
> whose heads are adorned with the *ātti* garland.” (28:207–211)

The *Maṇimēkalai* expresses the same principle:

> “The austerity of ascetics and the chastity of women
> cannot exist when the ruler’s protection does not.” (23:208–209)

Thus the excellence of Pukār and the greatness of the Cōḻa lineage receive their proper place in *Cilappatikāram*.
