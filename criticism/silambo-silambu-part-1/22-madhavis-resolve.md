# 22. Mādhavi’s Resolve

After Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki, Mādhavi holds the next place in *Cilappatikāram*. Drama gives its principal characters supporting characters, and Mādhavi is formally such a figure. Yet Kōvalaṉ appears to spend more time with her than with Kaṇṇaki. In the first part of the epic, Kaṇṇaki almost becomes secondary while Mādhavi occupies her place. If the goldsmith is Kōvalaṉ’s villain, Mādhavi is Kaṇṇaki’s antagonist; speaking frankly, one may even call her an antagonist who damaged Kōvalaṉ’s good life.

Through dance, music, and beauty she attracted him. She moved through the epic as a queen of art, performed before the king, won the *talaikkōl* distinction, and received a green-gold garland valued at 1,008 *kalañcus*.

Yet like a red lotus rising from mud, Mādhavi was born to Cittirāpati, a courtesan of a degraded profession, but chose Kōvalaṉ alone as husband to the end. After his death she refused her mother’s demand that she enter hereditary courtesanship.

Cittirāpati had placed the dance staff in her daughter’s hand at five, trained her for seven years, and made her debut before the king at twelve. Mādhavi buried her mother’s dream and laboured to establish in society the higher principle that the degrading profession should end.

With unrivalled dance, song, and bodily brilliance, she gave Kōvalaṉ quarrel and union and delighted him. But when her public performance at the Indra festival entranced everyone, resentment appeared in him like fire hidden beneath ash.

At the seashore he sang *vari* songs playfully, suggesting that he desired another woman. Mādhavi answered in the same playful mode, suggesting desire for another man. Kōvalaṉ mistook play for fact, calculated crudely that “a courtesan is only a courtesan,” and abandoned her.

That very day she sent a letter through Vayantamālai asking him to return. He refused even to receive it and closed the Mādhavi chapter as though it could never reopen. That night on the moonlit terrace she suffered unspeakably. The yāḻ and the music she attempted seemed to oppose her and became confused.

She sent another letter through the Brahmin Kōcikaṉ, asking him to find Kōvalaṉ on the forest road and bring him back. Kōvalaṉ had become fruit beyond reach.

Mādhavi had lived like a respectable wife with unfailing love for him. She later made their uncarved-sculpture daughter Maṇimēkalai renounce while still a tender fruit, and renounced herself, giving dignity to genuine asceticism.

## Mādhavi’s Defects

Mādhavi did not accept Kōvalaṉ because she was overcome by his beauty or virtue. She obtained him through a transaction. The green-gem garland worth 1,008 *kalañcus* was publicly offered in the market with the condition that its purchaser might attain her. The poor, innocent, lust-driven Kōvalaṉ paid the price, bought the garland, and only then reached Mādhavi.

He entered her house still carrying the purchased garland and is said to have remained there. This cannot mean that he never again visited the family business or the house where Kaṇṇaki lived. He must have returned repeatedly for wealth, ignored the household’s advice, and carried more property back. When he finally returned empty-handed, Kaṇṇaki apparently assumed that poverty had driven him home and said, “There are anklets; take them.”

This implies that she had already handed over and lost one ornament after another. Kōvalaṉ sees her faded body and laments:

> Seeing your withered body and its suffering,
> I—sporting with the deceitful woman
> whose nature joined falsehood—
> lost the mountain of vast wealth supplied by my lineage;
> poverty now gives me shame.  
> *(68–71)*

If such wealth was lost, the garland bought for 1,008 *kalañcus* was surely lost with it.

Could all the wealth have disappeared through charity alone? It was more likely consumed by their ostentatious life. At Maṇimēkalai’s naming ceremony, Mādhavi and Kōvalaṉ are said to have poured a rain of red gold in gifts. Whose household gold did Mādhavi rain down? Some argue that the singular “deceitful woman” in Kōvalaṉ’s confession does not refer to Mādhavi. Whom else could it mean?

Could Mādhavi have been unaware that Kōvalaṉ’s abandonment of Kaṇṇaki was wrong and caused pain to his wife, parents, and others? If she truly believed he should return to his wife, would she have sent Vayantamālai after he left, said, “Though he has not come this evening, we shall see him in the morning,” and later sent Kōcikaṉ?

Thus she bears some responsibility for Kōvalaṉ’s neglect of trade, separation from Kaṇṇaki, and extravagant life.

Yet impartiality requires another angle. If a man destroys himself through drink, is it the liquor’s fault? If a woman eats poison and dies, is it the poison’s fault? The drinker and poison-eater are responsible. Likewise, if Kōvalaṉ was ruined through Mādhavi, the primary fault is Kōvalaṉ’s. Still, as liquor and poison are harmful, Mādhavi may be called unsuitable—from the standpoint of Kaṇṇaki, the family, and even Kōvalaṉ himself.

## Mādhavi’s Merits

We cannot leave her condemned without recognizing her good qualities. The fullness she later attained concealed the deficiency of her earlier life.

Paṭṭiṉattār sings of a wife who lifts her sleeping husband’s hand aside, leaves the house for another man, returns before dawn, and resumes sleep; women born in respectable families may thus pass from hand to hand, though such cases are rare. Conversely, some women born in good families draw a man from his wife yet live with him alone as a faithful spouse.

Mādhavi’s position was different. She was the daughter of Cittirāpati’s courtesan line, whose women did not “sell” the body permanently but rented it for a time. Nevertheless, once Kōvalaṉ came to her, she lived with him as a faithful woman and bore his child, even though she did not send him back to his wife. When a playful song turned serious and caused his departure, she did not follow hereditary custom and seek another man. She repeatedly tried to restore their household. She grieved continuously. On hearing of his death, she became a severe renunciant and made her young daughter do the same. This is her great distinction.

## Was Mādhavi Born in the Courtesan Community?

Some deny it; others affirm it.

Those who deny it cite the line:

> The broad-shouldered maiden, undiminished in birth,
> joined to conduct not inferior to the excellence
> of the celestial women of unsurpassed greatness.  
> *(3:4–6)*

They argue that “undiminished in birth” cannot describe a woman of a degraded courtesan lineage. They add that she did not explicitly demand money from Kōvalaṉ; she remained with him; bore his child and lived as a family woman; received royal honour; twice sent letters after his departure; and shaved her head and renounced after his death. They distinguish the hereditary court-artists who danced at royal festivals from ordinary prostitutes.

Those who affirm her courtesan birth reply that “undiminished in birth” belongs to the myth of Urvaśī. While Urvaśī danced before Agastya in Indra’s court, she and Indra’s son Jayanta looked at each other with desire, lost rhythm, and were cursed to be born on earth. Mādhavi is linked to that celestial lineage; the praise concerns the mythic origin of the artistic clan, not social respectability.

The public sale of the garland is strong evidence. The king fixes a daily price of 1,008 *kalañcus*. A companion stands in the market announcing that the purchaser of the green garland may obtain the creeper-like woman. Such procedure belongs to the courtesan institution, however refined the artistic status.

Ancient Tamil usage calls public women “flower-price women,” those who receive payment daily for union. *Cilappatikāram* itself uses the expression (5:51).

Māṭalaṉ later tells Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ that Mādhavi said to Cittirāpati:

> Hearing it, the maiden Mādhavi told her good mother,
> “I shall follow the good path.
> Let Maṇimēkalai never assume the courtesan’s form
> that inflicts vast suffering.”  
> *(27:103–106)*

“I shall follow the good path” means that she will not follow the hereditary profession, and she forbids it for Maṇimēkalai.

A parallel in *Maṇimēkalai* concerns the merchant Cātuvaṉ, a second version of Kōvalaṉ. He abandons his wife Ātirai, joins a courtesan, eats her food, gives away vast wealth, and loses everything through dice and gambling. When he becomes poor, the courtesan drives him away and displays another paying man:

> Cātuvaṉ, husband of Ātirai, became unworthy,
> left his ornamented wife, and went away.
> A courtesan fed him from her hand;
> through dice and gambling he gave away great wealth.
> When the wealth was ruined and his kin suffered,
> the courtesan who had cherished him displayed another’s favour
> and shook him from her hand as a man without money.  
> *(16:3–10)*

The nature of the institution is clear.

Several *Cilappatikāram* passages should be recalled: Kōvalaṉ says he lost his family’s mountain of wealth “sporting with the deceitful woman”; he wandered “with loud-voiced minstrels and city courtesans”; he calls himself one who departed from the path and brought a flower-bodied woman to suffering; and he laments his life among empty talkers and fresh courtesans.

In a respectable household, a husband may jokingly say, “I saw a beautiful woman and shall love her,” but the wife will rarely answer, even in play, “I saw a handsome man and shall love him.” This one-sided morality still exists. Yet in *Kāṉal Vari*, Mādhavi answers Kōvalaṉ’s suggestion of another woman with a suggestion of another man. The author argues that a conventional family woman would not sing so; a woman raised in courtesan culture possessed a different freedom.

When Kōcikaṉ delivers Mādhavi’s letter, Kōvalaṉ reads it and thinks, “She is without evil” (13:94). This cannot mean she is physically well, for she sends the letter in distress. Nor can it simply mean she has never committed any fault, since he believed her extravagance and supposed love for another had caused his departure. The likely meaning is: though born into a community where women pass from hand to hand, she did not choose another after he left; she remained faithful and sent for him. In that sense she was “without evil.” He then says, “The fault is mine” (13:95), admitting that he wrongly suspected her. His readiness to absolve the woman who helped bury his marriage also reveals the old limitation of his woman-seeking mind.

Further evidence appears in *Maṇimēkalai*. When Cittirāpati asks Queen Irācamātēvi to release Maṇimēkalai, the queen says:

> “Drink, falsehood, lust, killing,
> and theft of the heart—these the wise renounce.
> Fearing as pollution your leaderless life
> that takes these as its chief principles,
> the flower-creeper came here;
> she will not return with you to your house.”  
> *(24:77–81)*

U. Vē. Cāminātaiyar glosses “leaderless life” as the lowest profession of prostitution.

A forest deity disguised as Vayantamālai also tells Kōvalaṉ that Mādhavi lamented the courtesan’s life:

> Whether exalted, learned in texts,
> or capable of discerning every category,
> people reject it as a disease and cast it away:
> the courtesan’s life has reached the lowest state.  
> *(11:180–183)*

Though spoken by a deceptive deity, the statement is placed in the epic by Iḷaṅkō and identifies the social class.

The word *tēvaṭiyār* originally meant a servant of the deity, one involved in temple worship. Over time it came to denote women engaged in courtesanship. In a temple known to me, such a woman once sang daily and danced with an instrument before the decorated deity during festivals. The street where they lived was called Tēvaṭiyār Street. The practice has now ended. Most families formerly connected with the profession have reformed into respectable, chaste households. Mādhavi’s transformation was similar.

I once wrote a story, *A Treasure Hard to Find*. A courtesan named Cittirātēvi forces her daughter Kōtai toward the profession. Kōtai resolves to accept the first man as husband; the wealthy Cētunātaṉ deceives her after she becomes pregnant. Their child Kalā is born. When the mother again presses Kōtai into the profession, Kōtai commits suicide. The grandmother raises Kalā and tries to initiate her, but Kalā refuses, marries, and lives chastely. Mādhavi embodies both Kōtai’s and Kalā’s reform. Her personal conduct may be blameless in the end, but the maternal lineage is nevertheless a courtesan lineage.

Her severe renunciation probably arose from guilt as well as grief. She may have reflected: because of me their household broke; they crossed a difficult road to Madurai; Kōvalaṉ, the Pāṇḍya king and queen, Kaṇṇaki, many goldsmiths, Kōvalaṉ’s mother, and Kaṇṇaki’s mother died; Madurai burned. Feeling herself a cause, she renounced and prevented her daughter from entering the hereditary occupation.

Renunciation was also a weapon protecting a widowed woman of courtesan birth from the vulture-eyes of low men. Her firmness deserves admiration.

## Mādhavi’s Religion

Even among relatives, one family may be Śaiva and another Vaiṣṇava. Nammāḻvār, a Vēḷāḷa, was Vaiṣṇava; Appar, also a Vēḷāḷa, was Śaiva. Religious difference across communities is therefore unsurprising. In the age when Jainism and Buddhism flourished, relatives and members of the same or different social groups followed either faith.

In *Cilappatikāram*, Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki travel with the Jain nun Kauṇti and worship Jain Cāraṇas. In *Maṇimēkalai*, Mādhavi and Maṇimēkalai explicitly become Buddhist renunciants and receive moral teaching from the Buddhist master Aṟavaṇa Aṭikaḷ. Mādhavi’s chosen religion was therefore Buddhism.
