# 4. Common Ownership of the Divine

The world speaks of common ownership in economics. It may speak of common ownership in many other matters too. But to speak of and practise common ownership in theology and religion is a great revolution. Tirumūlar said, “There is one clan and one God” (2104). Some shout themselves hoarse that, just as all rivers merge in the sea, all religious doctrines end in the idea of one God and therefore religious conflict is unnecessary. But has this principle succeeded?

It is astonishing and painful that every major religion practised in the world contains several, sometimes many, divisions. When the image of one religion’s deity is carried in procession through a street, followers of another religion may shut and bolt their street doors. In the face of that practical reality, what more need be said?

With this background, let us turn to Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ’s *Cilappatikāram*. We may call Iḷaṅkō a great advocate of common ownership. Just as he made the work an epic common to the three crowned kings, he made it an epic common to all deities. Though born in a royal lineage, he embraced renunciation; is this not the highest qualification of one who has renounced? Let us examine, canto by canto, what he says concerning the divine.

## The Prologue

The prologue mentions the Silver Hall in Madurai where Śiva, whose matted hair bears the koṉṟai garland, is enthroned:

> “In the public hall of the matted-haired One crowned with lovely koṉṟai,  
> I lay at midnight in the Silver Hall.” (40–41)

Śiva is said to possess five halls: the Golden Hall, Silver Hall, Ruby Hall, Copper Hall, and Painted Hall. Chidambaram is the Golden Hall; Madurai the Silver Hall; Tiruvalangadu the Ruby Hall; Tirunelveli the Copper Hall; and Courtallam in Tirunelveli district the Painted Hall. One of these five, the Silver Hall of Madurai, is named in the prologue.

## The Canto of Domestic Virtue

Kōvalaṉ praises Kaṇṇaki’s beauty. He says that Murukaṉ of the six faces split his long spear lengthwise and gave the two halves as her eyes. This follows the convention of comparing women’s eyes to spears:

> “Was it not because the six-faced One,  
> though possessing no ordinary mode of birth,  
> has the nature of being seen in two forms,  
> that he split one long, radiant spear  
> and gave it as the two rain-cloud eyes  
> with red outer corners in your face?” (49–52)

The *Cīvakacintāmaṇi*, in the section on Patumaiyār, similarly says that Patumaiyār’s eyes resembled the two portions of Murukaṉ’s sharp spear:

> “She whose eyes were the two halves  
> of Murukaṉ’s uplifted, keen spear.” (126)

Thus Murukaṉ appears in the Canto of Domestic Virtue.

## The Canto in Which the City Celebrates Indra’s Festival

A festival was held for Indra at Kāvirippūmpaṭṭinam. At that time special worship was also conducted in the temples of Śiva and other gods:

> “The temple of the Great One whose body is unborn;  
> the splendid temple of the six-faced red-speared god;  
> the temple of the strong one whose body is white as a bright conch;  
> the temple of the Tall One of blue body;  
> the temple of the king beneath the garlanded white parasol …  
> and, in another quarter, festivals rich and distinct  
> for many different gods.” (169–173, 178)

Iḷaṅkō’s identification of Śiva as “the Great One whose body is unborn” is apt. Texts say that Śiva performed many sacred sports while appearing in human bodies. Yet he was not born to parents like others; he suddenly assumed human form and performed those acts.

Among the ninety-six kinds of minor Tamil literature is *Piḷḷaittamil*, “Tamil on the Divine Child.” Such a work imagines a deity, great person, or benefactor as a small child and composes ten sections of ten poems each, making one hundred poems. *Piḷḷaittamil* works have been sung for many gods. Śiva alone has no such work. Since he was not born as a child, no one composed a *Piḷḷaittamil* on him. In keeping with this tradition, Iḷaṅkō calls Śiva “the Great One whose body is unborn.”

The six-faced red-speared god is Murukaṉ. The strong god whose body is white as a conch is Baladēva. The tall blue-bodied one is Tirumāl. The king beneath the garlanded white parasol is Indra.

Calling Indra alone “king” follows Tolkāppiyar’s tradition. In the *Akattiṇaiyiyal*, Tolkāppiyar names the deities of the mullai, kuṟiñci, marutam, and neytal landscapes:

> “The forest world where Māyōṉ dwells;  
> the dark-mountain world where Cēyōṉ dwells;  
> the sweet-water world where the King dwells;  
> the great-sand world where Varuṇaṉ dwells—  
> these may be spoken of, in the stated order,  
> as mullai, kuṟiñci, marutam, and neytal.” (5)

The first is mullai, belonging to Tirumāl; the second kuṟiñci, belonging to Murukaṉ; the third marutam, belonging to Indra; and the fourth neytal, belonging to Varuṇaṉ.

Why is Indra alone called “king”? The Sanskrit word *Indra* is understood to mean king or leader. Thus Narēndra is leader of people; Surēndra, leader of gods; Rājēndra, king of kings; Gajēndra, leader of elephants; and so on. Tradition holds that there is a celestial world inhabited by many gods, of whom Indra is chief, hence Dēvēndra. Iḷaṅkō therefore follows Tolkāppiyar’s usage in calling him “king.”

## The Canto of Seeing the Country

### The Shrine of the Jewel-Coloured Lord

After separating from Mādhavi, Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki secretly left Pukār before dawn. On their way, they circled the temple of blue-jewel-coloured Tirumāl, who rests in mystic sleep upon the serpent couch:

> “They passed after circling the shrine  
> of the Jewel-Coloured Lord,  
> who reclines in mystic sleep  
> upon the splendid serpent.” (9–10)

### Buddhist Monasteries

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki also passed, in reverence, the seven Buddhist monasteries built by Indra. There, sky-roaming ascetics came and proclaimed the gracious words of the Buddha beneath the Bodhi tree:

> “They passed the seven monasteries of Indra,  
> where wanderers of the air came and proclaimed  
> the sacred words of the Righteous One  
> beneath the splendid shade of the green-leaved Bodhi,  
> whose five great branches rose high.” (11–14)

### The Platform of Arukaṉ

Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki circled and worshipped the shining stone platform of Arukaṉ, erected by Jain lay devotees:

> “They worshipped and circled the radiant stone platform  
> raised together by the world’s ascetics.” (24–25)

### Names of Arukaṉ

On the road, Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki joined Kavunti Aṭikaḷ, a Jain woman ascetic. Jain wanderers praised Arukaṉ, the Jain deity, by the following names:

> “The Knower, the Righteous One, the One beyond the bounds of knowledge;  
> the Concentrated One, Jina-Indra, the Perfected One, the Blessed One;  
> First of Dharma, Leader, Dharma itself;  
> Substance, Pure One, Ancient One, Poet;  
> Supreme Jina, God, Lord of the auspicious path of liberation;  
> the Highest, the Virtuous, the Light in the world beyond;  
> the Principle, the Saint, the Wanderer, the Cause;  
> the Accomplished One, the Great One, the Noble, the Radiant Light;  
> Lord, Teacher, Possessor of natural virtue, our King;  
> the One of unfailing fame, the great sovereign of virtues;  
> Śaṅkara, Īśa, Self-born, Four-faced;  
> the One who produced the scriptures, Arukaṉ, sage of grace;  
> Master of melody, Possessor of eight qualities, Supreme Reality beyond;  
> Celestial One, First of the Vedas …” (176–189)

So many names are given to Arukaṉ. Among them, “Blessed One,” “Lord,” and “Possessor of eight qualities” also appear in the invocation to God in the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*. The names “the Highest,” “Śaṅkara,” “Īśa,” and “Four-faced” ordinarily designate Śiva and Brahmā. Since Jainism separated from Hinduism, Śiva, Tirumāl, and Brahmā also appear within its vocabulary.

Pavaṇanti, the Jain monk who composed the grammar *Naṉṉūl*, invokes the Four-faced One at the opening of its Book of Letters:

> “Worshipping the Four-faced One  
> seated in the lovely shade  
> of the flower-laden aśoka,  
> I shall clearly expound letters.”

At the opening of the Book of Words he invokes Acyuta, Tirumāl:

> “Worshipping the feet of Acyuta  
> beneath the full-moon triple parasol  
> that shades the three worlds,  
> I shall proclaim words.”

The extant *Naṉṉūl* has no Book of Meaning. Some say Pavaṇanti composed one but it was lost. If that is true, perhaps he invoked Śiva at its beginning.

Among Arukaṉ’s names is also “Lord of the *śivagati*.” *Śivagati* means liberation. It is noteworthy that Jains too use this expression for final release.

Kavunti Aṭikaḷ later praises Arukaṉ by further names: the One who subdued the three; the One who conquered Desire; the One who conquered the five; the One who embraced gracious righteousness; Arukaṉ the righteous; the Knower; the One who walked upon the flower; and the Lord of endless bliss. “The One who walked upon the flower” recalls the phrase “the One who went upon the flower” in the invocation of the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*.

Because *Cilappatikāram* uses names such as Blessed One, Lord, Possessor of eight qualities, the One who walked on the flower, Conqueror of the five, One who subdued the five gateways of sense, and Righteous One or Ocean of righteousness, some claim that Tiruvaḷḷuvar was a Jain. Let that debate rest. Let us call Tiruvaḷḷuvar common to every religion and every faith; let us read the *Tirukkuṟaḷ* as the common scripture.

## The Canto of Seeing the Forest

### Worship of Arukaṉ

At the opening of this section, Arukaṉ is worshipped beneath the aśoka tree:

> “They worshipped the Knower of primordial form,  
> seated in the rich shade of the aśoka  
> whose clusters hang low.” (3–4)

### The Sacred Appearance of Tirumāl

After Kōvalaṉ, Kaṇṇaki, and Kavunti passed Uṟaiyūr and rested in a young grove, a Brahmin from Māṅkāṭu arrived. Kōvalaṉ asked who he was and where he came from. The Brahmin replied:

“My village is Māṅkāṭu, and I am a Brahmin. I worshipped Araṅkaṉ, who rests in mystic sleep on the island in the Kāviri. I also saw and worshipped the sacred form of dark Tirumāl upon Tiruvēṅkaṭam. He wore golden cloth and a jewelled necklace and held discus and white conch in his two hands, like a black cloud bearing lightning and rainbow, while at evening the sun shone on one side and the white moon on the other.” The poem says:

> “I came because my heart was troubled, saying,  
> ‘Show my eyes the form of the Lord of Fortune’s breast  
> reclining on the great island  
> amid the broad-waved Kāviri;  
> and the standing form of the red-eyed Tall One  
> high upon the summit of swelling-watered Vēṅkaṭam,  
> where sun and moon shine on either side;  
> like a good dark cloud wearing lightning as a garment  
> and the radiant rainbow as an ornament;  
> holding in lotus hands  
> the hostile-destroying discus and milk-white conch;  
> wearing upon his chest the beautiful radiant necklace;  
> appearing resplendent in flower-gold cloth.’  
> I am from rain-rich Māṅkāṭu.” (39–53)

Iḷaṅkō’s description of Tirumāl at Tiruvēṅkaṭam recalls parallel passages in other literature. Let us compare them in the method of literary comparison.

In *Maṇimēkalai*, in the Canto in which the goddess Maṇimēkalā appears, Cāttaṉār imagines the maiden Pukār wearing a white earring on one side and a gold earring on the other: the moon shines in the east and the setting sun in the west.

> “Pukār, the flower-creeper surpassing poets,  
> wore the aged moon in the eastern quarter  
> and the falling rays in the western quarter  
> as a white silver earring and a golden earring …”

A similar idea appears in *Paripāṭal*: Tirumāl holds in one hand the discus like the orb of the sun and in the other the white conch like the orb of the white moon:

> “In hands bearing discus and conch,  
> like the two distinct orbs  
> adorning the two skies in their proper season …” (13:7–9)

The phrase “I came because my heart was troubled, saying, ‘Show my eyes’” is especially delightful: the Māṅkāṭu Brahmin’s eyes themselves tormented his mind with the desire to see and worship Tirumāl at Śrīraṅgam and Tiruvēṅkaṭam.

The Brahmin further points out a mountain on the road. One must worship the Tall One, Tirumāl, who dwells upon that lofty mountain, place his sacred feet in one’s heart, and circle the mountain three times:

> “If you worship the Exalted One on the lofty mountain,  
> place his red feet in your thought,  
> and circle the mountain three times …” (105–107)

Tirumāl is called “the Exalted One.” Because he assumed the cosmic Trivikrama form to measure the world, he is traditionally called both the Tall One and the Exalted One. The passage also points to the people’s custom of circling a temple, mountain, or other sacred object three times.

### Koṟṟavai

The three travellers reached a shrine of Koṟṟavai. She is described as possessing an eye in her forehead, as a goddess worshipped by the celestials, as the lady of heaven, and as Aiyai:

> “They reached the shrine of Aiyai—  
> the maiden with an eye upon her brow,  
> the goddess of the celestials,  
> the faultlessly glorious lady of the heavenly land.” (214–216)

## The Hunter’s Song

The Hunter’s Song gives Koṟṟavai many names and attributes:

> “O brow that wears the moon’s white crescent!  
> O coral-mouthed one with bright sword-like teeth,  
> whose sleepless eye opens in the split forehead!  
> O dark-throated one who drank poison!  
> O fierce one who strung a serpent as bowstring  
> and bent the long mountain as a bow!  
> O breasted one girdled with a perforated-fanged serpent!  
> O bearer of the trident in your bangle-wearing hand!  
> O spirit who clothes yourself in elephant hide  
> and wears tiger skin as a girdle!  
> O Koṟṟavai of the victorious sharp sword,  
> whose small feet lament with anklet and warrior’s ring!  
> O maiden standing upon the head  
> of the demon whose two forms met in one body!  
> O Amari, Kumari, Gauri, Samari,  
> Trident-bearer, Nīli, younger sister of Māl,  
> Aiyai, red one, fierce-sworded broad-handed one,  
> maiden of the leaping stag, green-bangled maiden,  
> maiden of the subtle arts, maiden of rare beauty!” (54–71)

The crescent-bearing brow, forehead eye, poison-darkened throat, bending of Mount Mēru as a bow, trident, elephant hide, and tiger skin are all Śiva’s attributes. Their application to Koṟṟavai suggests that Koṟṟavai is identified with Śivai, Umā, the consort of Śiva. It is astonishing that Iḷaṅkō remembered and supplied so many names.

### Koṉṟai and Basil

Koṟṟavai is said to have worn a garland in which koṉṟai flowers and holy basil were woven together and to have danced in maiden form, protecting the gods while the demons perished:

> “Wearing upon her shoulders  
> a dense flower-garland woven together  
> of koṉṟai and basil,  
> she danced in maiden form  
> while demons withered and gods were saved.” (Song 10)

Koṉṟai belongs to Śiva and basil to Tirumāl. By joining them in one garland, the poem expresses a common vision free of Śaiva–Vaiṣṇava division. The popular saying shines here: “Hari and Śiva are one; dust fills the mouth of one who does not know it.”

The same view appears at the end of the Hunter’s Song. Koṟṟavai is said to have drunk the poison no one else would consume and granted grace to the gods. She is also said to have broken the twin maruta trees created through the plot of her maternal uncle Kaṃsa and to have kicked and destroyed the rolling cart sent by him:

> “You drank the uneaten poison and remained, granting grace.” (22:1)
>
> “Walking between the maruta trees,  
> kick down in grace the rolling cart  
> contrived by your maternal uncle.” (22:3–4)

Śiva drank the poison, but the deed is here attributed to Koṟṟavai. Breaking the maruta trees and kicking the cart belong to Kaṇṇaṉ, the divine incarnation of Tirumāl, yet these too are transferred to Koṟṟavai. Once again we see a common vision without Śaiva–Vaiṣṇava difference.

For comparison, consider a few statements from other works.

### The *Kampa Rāmāyaṇam*

Śiva and Tirumāl, the One appearing as two, each possessed a bow. To discover whose bow was stronger, the Four-faced Brahmā caused them to fight. The story appears in the Paraśurāma section of the Book of Childhood:

> “The Four-faced One set the two—  
> who are the One primordial source  
> higher than all—upon their fierce, strong bows  
> and stirred up a crowded battle.” (28)

We may also compare two verses of Pūtat Āḻvār’s *Third Tiruvantāti* in the *Divya Prabandham*:

> “His names are Hara and Nārāyaṇa;  
> his mounts are bull and bird;  
> his scriptures are revelation; his temples mountain and water;  
> his works destruction and protection;  
> his weapons spear and discus;  
> his forms fire and dark cloud—yet he is one.” (5)

> “The holy One of the golden body and braided matted hair,  
> and the Tall Māl who once measured the world—  
> though they ever move as two bodies,  
> each is forever present within the body of the other.” (98)

## The Canto of Taking the Stone

### On Crown and Shoulder

When Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ set out on his northern campaign, he placed upon his crown the garland of sacred feet given at Śiva’s temple. Later he wore upon his shoulder the garland given at Tirumāl’s temple:

> “As some stood praising with the remnant offering  
> of the One who reclines in mystic sleep  
> in the golden-roofed hall,  
> the king had already placed upon his jewelled crown  
> the beautiful red feet of the red-matted god  
> who concealed the clear water.  
> Therefore he received the other offering  
> and bore it upon his handsome, jewelled shoulder  
> as he proceeded in majesty.” (62–67)

The passage shows that both Śiva and Tirumāl had temples and that Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, having first placed the Śiva temple’s gift upon his crown, wore Tirumāl’s gift upon his shoulder. It is therefore clear that he did not practise religious discrimination.

The Hunter’s Song of this work explains the glory of Koṟṟavai; the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai* the excellence of Tirumāl; and the Mountain *Kuravai* the greatness of Murukaṉ.

Thus *Cilappatikāram* stands as a common temple of words in which every deity is enthroned.

## Religions within the Family

After the deaths of Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki, Mādhavi and Maṇimēkalai embraced Buddhism. Of the fathers of the pair, Kōvalaṉ’s father Mācāttuvāṉ became a Buddhist, while Kaṇṇaki’s father Māṉāykaṉ became a Jain. Religion therefore did not destroy kinship. Even today one may see one family among relatives following Śaivism and another Vaiṣṇavism.

Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ worked very carefully without allowing himself to be trapped in sectarianism. Cāttaṉār, author of *Maṇimēkalai*, openly propagated Buddhism. If asked which religion Iḷaṅkō gave first place to, one may answer Jainism: he propagated it through the Jain wanderers and Kavunti. He is therefore personally regarded as Jain-affiliated. His elder brother Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, however, is described in several places as Śaiva-affiliated, though he too gave space to every religion.

Cāttaṉār was a private individual and could therefore propagate Buddhism openly. Iḷaṅkō belonged to the royal family and was the younger brother of the reigning Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ. He therefore left among the people a principle of religious common ownership.
