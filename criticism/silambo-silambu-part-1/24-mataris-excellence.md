# 24. Mātari’s Excellence

Iḷaṅkō introduces Mātari midway through the story, in the “Canto of Refuge.” She was a woman of the cowherd community, a devotee of Tirumāl—Kṛṣṇa—and also a worshipper of a minor deity. She possessed the privilege of supplying dairy produce to the royal palace. Her daughter was named Aiyai.

When Kauṇti, Kōvalaṉ, and Kaṇṇaki were staying on the outskirts of Madurai, Mātari passed that way after offering milk to the minor deity Iyakki. On seeing her, Kauṇti reasoned: “She appears to belong to the cowherd community. There is no cruel or deceitful occupation in the life of cowherds, who protect cattle and give their produce to others. She therefore will do no harm. She is an elderly woman of experience and seems upright and compassionate. It will be safe to entrust Kaṇṇaki to her.” The poem says:

> “After offering milk to fair-eyed Iyakki
> in the ancient city’s outer quarter,
> the virtuous woman returned—
> Mātari, the senior woman of the cowherds.
> Seeing Kauṇti Aṭikaḷ, she bowed at her feet.
> ‘The life of cowherds, who guard the cows
> and distribute their yield, holds no cruelty.
> This aged woman is without evil,
> upright and compassionate.
> There is no fault in lodging the maiden
> with Mātari,’ Kauṇti thought.” (116–124)

People offer to a deity whatever blessing or wealth is most abundant in their own lives. A cowherd woman lacks no milk. Since Mātari possessed the good fortune of milk in abundance, she offered milk. Being cultured, she touched Kauṇti’s feet and worshipped her.

Iḷaṅkō calls her *mutumakaḷ*, “senior woman,” in two places: “the senior woman of the cowherds” and “the faultless senior woman.” In the first, the word means a woman of consequence—an important person—in the cowherd community. In the second, it means an elderly woman innocent of wrongdoing, to whom a person may safely be entrusted.

The moment Mātari bowed at her feet, Kauṇti concluded that she was upright and compassionate. Could Kaṇṇaki be placed in her house? Cowherds know no treachery: their work is not deceitful; they protect their cattle from sickness and distress and sustain them with good fodder. There could therefore be no error in entrusting Kaṇṇaki to them. One should not count against them the fact that some milk-sellers mix water into milk. Not everyone does it. Mixing another substance might cause illness; water causes none. However much water is added, milk remains white—such is milk’s great blessing! Through Kauṇti’s reflections, Iḷaṅkō thus introduces Mātari’s excellence.

## Mātari’s Wealth

The phrase “senior woman of the cowherds” itself suggests that Mātari possessed considerable wealth and influence. When Kōvalaṉ later left her house for Madurai’s market street, the “Canto of the Place of Killing” describes him as “leaving the house of the cowherds of many cattle” (*pallāṉ kōvalar illam nīṅki*, line 98). *Pal āṉ* means “many cows”: Mātari’s household owned numerous cattle.

In ancient times the person who owned many cattle was regarded as wealthy. Indeed, the Tamil word *māṭu*, “cattle,” also means wealth. Many literary examples could be given, but one *Tirukkuṟaḷ* is sufficient:

> “Learning is the imperishable and distinguished wealth of a person;
> all other possessions are not wealth.” (400)

Here *māṭu* means wealth. In Kannada, too, *dhana* may mean cattle and wealth. The Latin word *pecunia* likewise has an etymological connection with livestock and property. The abundance of cattle was therefore once the measure of prosperity. The expression “cowherds of many cattle” reveals the wealth of Mātari’s household. Mātari also led Kaṇṇaki to her house surrounded by cowherd men and women; this too indicates her standing.

Kauṇti entrusted Kaṇṇaki, not Kōvalaṉ, to Mātari. There is no need to speak separately of granting refuge to a man: Kōvalaṉ was included in the refuge extended to Kaṇṇaki.

## Mātari’s Hospitality

Having accepted Kaṇṇaki as one under her protection, Mātari did not lodge her in the main household among her own people. She placed the couple in a small, well-guarded house roofed with timber, freshly renewed with red-earth plaster. She had Kaṇṇaki bathed in fresh water and told her: “My daughter Aiyai will remain as your companion and help you. Since Kauṇti has brought you to a good place, your husband too need have no anxiety.”

Because Kōvalaṉ observed the vows of a Jain layman, Mātari instructed the women, including “the young woman related through the husband,” to provide without delay good vessels in which the meal could be prepared during daylight:

> “Since the lord observes the vows of a Jain householder,
> with the young woman related through the husband,
> quickly provide good vessels suited
> for cooking the meal that is prepared by day.” (18–21)

The phrase *nāttūṇ naṅkai* means the husband’s sister—here Aiyai. It is a worldly custom to address a newly arrived man as an elder brother to a woman of the household. Kōvalaṉ therefore becomes Aiyai’s brother; Kaṇṇaki becomes her sister-in-law; and to Kaṇṇaki, Aiyai becomes the husband’s sister. The creation of such relationships is a civilised practice.

“Aṭikaḷ,” “the lord,” refers to Kōvalaṉ. *Cāvaka nōṉpikaḷ* are laypeople who observe religious vows while living in the household. Kōvalaṉ is referred to in the respectful plural, just as one might say “Kōvalaṉ, they.” Kaṇṇaki is not included in this phrase because she herself is the person about to cook.

The “food prepared by day” is literally the food made in daylight. What connection exists between a Jain lay-observer and cooking during the day? Kōvalaṉ was a Jain. Jains do not light lamps at night, lest moths fall into the flame and die; to maintain the vow against killing, they eat before darkness descends.

There is a village called Nallāṟṟūr about ten miles west of Puducherry. People in that village once ate before lamps were lit, while there was still daylight. People of Cuddalore used to tease that a guest who reached Nallāṟṟūr after dark would find it difficult to obtain a meal. This is not a present custom; it must belong to an earlier period. Was it not in the Cuddalore region that Nāvukkaracar once joined Jainism? Nallāṟṟūr falls within the Cuddalore electoral area. This practice may therefore have existed there when Jainism flourished. The village is also distinguished by the tomb of Sivappirakāca Aṭikaḷ, the “treasury of imagination” who composed many works.

My suggestion that the meal was cooked by day because Kōvalaṉ was a Jain is only an inference. Those who do not accept it may leave it aside. The distinction between *nāḷ aṅkāṭi*, “day bazaar,” and *allaṅkāṭi*, “night bazaar,” confirms that *nāḷ* may signify daytime.

The command to provide “good vessels” is also noteworthy. However many utensils women possess, they are rarely satisfied. Though their houses may contain aluminium, brass, copper, stainless steel, and silver vessels of many kinds, the sight of earthen pots and jars arranged by a potter may make them exclaim, “Ah! What beautiful things!” Mātari’s house may likewise have contained many vessels. “Good vessels” means new, superior utensils not previously used by the household.

“Give them without delay” shows, on one hand, that the guests had arrived in great hunger and, on the other, that the food had to be cooked and eaten before nightfall. Rice, vegetables, milk, ghee, curd, and other ingredients were then supplied:

> “Together with the cooking vessels they gave her:
> the clustered, mature jackfruit of the noble tree
> that bears without visible blossom;
> striped cucumber, green pomegranate,
> mango fruit and sweet plantain;
> fine *cāli* rice and the products of their milk—
> saying, ‘Bangle-armed lady, receive these.’” (23–28)

Thus they gave cooking vessels, superior *cāli* rice, milk, and milk products such as ghee and curd, together with a mature jackfruit, cucumber, green pomegranate, mangoes, and plantains. A meal containing jackfruit, plantain, mango, and rice is called “the three fruits and rice.” All three fruits were provided to Kaṇṇaki.

*Kōḷip pākaḷ* denotes jackfruit. The “fruit” in “clustered mature fruit” refers to the stage at which the jackfruit has matured but not fully ripened. Before its bulbs become sweet, the fruit is chopped repeatedly with a heavy knife and cooked as a vegetable; in this stage it is called *kottukkāy*.

A fine thought lies hidden in the expression “the jackfruit of the noble tree that bears without flowering.” N. Mu. Vēṅkaṭacāmi Nāṭṭār explains:

> “The clustered and mature fruit of the jack tree, which bears without flowering, is said to possess a noble nature because, like noble people who give without being asked, it fruits without first displaying flowers. *Kōḷi* is a tree that fruits without flowering; *pākaḷ* is the jack tree.”

A verse in the *Ciṟupañcamūlam* expresses a related idea:

> “There are trees that fruit without flowering;
> those who know goodness and master books
> are old in wisdom without ageing.” (22)

Just as a tree appears to fruit without flowering, those who understand goodness and are learned in books are respected as elders even when young in age. The *Tivākara Nikaṇṭu* also defines the term:

> “*Kōḷi* is a cool tree that fruits without flowering.” (4:80)

Fig, sacred fig, banyan, pineapple, and jack are commonly said to fruit without flowers. What does this mean? When a person who rarely visits finally appears, people say, “Your coming is like the flowering of the fig.” They assume that fig and similar trees bear fruit without blossom. In truth, fig, sacred fig, pineapple, banyan, and jack all have flowers. Many blossoms gather together, are enclosed by outer tissue, and take a rounded form; pollination occurs within, and the cluster develops into a fruit. Each apparent fruit must therefore be understood as an aggregation of many flowers. Among such *kōḷi* forms, jackfruit is foremost in both size and taste. The *Perumpāṇāṟṟuppaṭai* says:

> “Among the *kōḷi* trees with rich and tender branches,
> the jackfruit is proclaimed supreme in fruit.” (407–408)

This is also known through ordinary experience. Hence Iḷaṅkō’s phrase, “the jackfruit of the noble tree that bears without flowering.”

## A Sight Too Full for the Eyes

As Kaṇṇaki served the meal and Kōvalaṉ ate, the intimacy between them became a sight too wonderful for Mātari and her daughter Aiyai to contain with their eyes. They spoke joyfully to one another: “Could this Kōvalaṉ be Kṛṣṇa, flower-dark in colour, whom Yaśodā bore in the northern cowherd settlement? Could this Kaṇṇaki be Nappiṉṉai, the radiant woman of our own community who, beside the river Yamunā, removed Kṛṣṇa’s distress and married him? How can we adequately wonder at and delight in this sight?”

> “Is this noble man eating the good nectar
> the fresh-flower-coloured one whom Yaśodā bore
> in the cowherd settlement?
> And is this many-bangled woman the lamp of our ancient clan,
> who beside the Yamunā relieved the sorrow
> of the pure jewel-coloured one?
> Aiyai and her mother, filled with wonder, said:
> ‘Their sight here is too much for our eyes to hold.’” (46–53)

At certain moments, people seem to interpret circumstances through the forms of their own community and the deity they worship. In the *Vēṭṭuva Vari*, Kaṇṇaki is metaphorically praised in forms suited to hunters. Here the cowherd women Mātari and Aiyai see Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki in the forms of Kṛṣṇa and Nappiṉṉai, figures belonging to their own community.

A comparable scene occurs in Cēkkiḻār’s *Periyapurāṇam*. When Paravai Nācciyār first sees Cuntarar in Tiruvārūr, she wonders who he can be:

> “Is he Murukaṉ appearing before me?
> Or Love himself in surpassing radiance?
> Or a celestial warrior with garlanded chest?
> Or one who has received the true grace
> of the Lord with lightning-red matted hair?
> Who is this man who has transformed my mind?” (290)

Paravai wonders whether he is Murukaṉ, the god of love, a celestial, or a devotee of Śiva. That verse reflects a Śaiva religious setting. In the same way, Mātari and Aiyai imagine according to their community and faith. “The noble man” is Kōvalaṉ; the “many-bangled woman” is Kaṇṇaki; the “fresh-flower-coloured” and “pure jewel-coloured” one is Kṛṣṇa; the “lamp” is Nappiṉṉai. Perhaps women naturally enjoy watching such scenes!

## The Story of Nappiṉṉai

A cowherd king named Kumpakaṉ lived near Mithilā. His daughter was Nappiṉṉai. Seven demons came as wild bulls and caused trouble. Kumpakaṉ announced that he would give his daughter in marriage to whoever subdued and killed the bulls. Kṛṣṇa did so and married Nappiṉṉai. This occurred on the bank of the Yamunā. Mātari and Aiyai were overwhelmed with delight because Kaṇṇaki seemed to them like this Nappiṉṉai.

## Difference of Community

Kaṇṇaki must have been utterly exhausted when Mātari brought her home under protection. In such a condition, should the work of cooking have been left to her? Should not Mātari and Aiyai have prepared and served the meal? Why was Kaṇṇaki herself made to cook?

What explanation can be offered except that the cowherd community was regarded as low and the merchant community as high, and that members of a high community would not eat food prepared by those deemed lower? Even when cowherds achieved rare and great things, people of the time refused to attribute the achievement to them and instead claimed that someone else had done it. Those called high-caste possessed the arrogance of asking, “Are we to accept that a cowherd did this?” Consider several examples.

Tirumūlar, who belonged to the cowherd community, composed the *Tirumantiram* of three thousand remarkable verses. But the truth was obscured. The story says that a cowherd named Mūlaṉ fell dead while tending cattle; the cows cried. A yogi travelling from Kailāsa took pity, projected his life into Mūlaṉ’s body, hid his own body, and led the cattle home. When he returned, Śiva had destroyed the yogi’s original body because he was destined to remain there and serve. Living thereafter in the cowherd Mūlaṉ’s body, the yogi composed the three thousand verses. Is this true? No; it is imagination. The history was changed so that the greatness of a cowherd’s intellectual power would not be acknowledged.

Another cowherd boy who tended cattle defeated Avvaiyār in wit by asking whether she wanted “roasted fruit” or “unroasted fruit.” Unwilling to assign this distinction to the cowherd community, high-caste tellers transformed the story and claimed that Murukaṉ had appeared in the form of the boy.

Kālidāsa, the Sanskrit poet, is one of the world’s great poets. He belonged to a pastoral community and had tended goats and cattle. Yet a story was constructed that Kālī wrote something upon his tongue and thereby made him a great poet. Others produced the Himalayan-sized lie that Kālidāsa was a Brahmin who happened to herd animals. Brahmins do not tend goats. Perhaps those high-caste storytellers babbled in memory of the historical claim that Aryans entered India through the Khyber Pass driving sheep and cattle!

Now an atomic bomb is about to fall here. Kṛṣṇa himself, said to have grown up in the northern cowherd settlement, belonged to the cowherd community. The story says that the child born to Vasudēva and Dēvakī was carried to the house of Nandagōpa and Yaśodā in the cowherd settlement. Vasudēva left the male child Kṛṣṇa there and carried away the female child lying beside Yaśodā. Would Yaśodā or the women who attended the birth not search for the missing girl? Would they not wonder how a male child had appeared when a girl had been born? To “adjust” the inconsistency, the story says that the goddess Māyā had come as the girl. This must therefore be imagination.

Kṛṣṇa lived wholly within a pastoral setting and married Nappiṉṉai of the cowherd community. Historians describe Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma as kings of the Yādava—that is, pastoral—community. A direct piece of evidence is Iḷaṅkō’s phrase in *Cilappatikāram*, “whom Yaśodā bore in the cowherd settlement.” Those who cannot accept this may reject it. The thought is offered for scholarly examination, not as a final and absolute conclusion.

## The Cowherd Women’s Kuravai

After Kōvalaṉ left for the market street, many evil omens appeared in the cowherd settlement. Mātari described them to Aiyai and then arranged for the cowherd women to perform a *kuravai* dance. Tirumāl is praised throughout this canto.

## Mātari’s Vaiṣṇavism

Mātari followed Vaiṣṇava belief. After the *kuravai*, she took flowers, fragrant incense, sandal paste, and a garland and went to worship Tirumāl in his temple on the bank of the Vaigai:

> “The senior cowherd woman, graceful from the dance,
> took flowers, incense, prepared sandal paste, and garlands;
> she went down to the bathing-place
> to worship the feet of great Tirumāl
> beside the long-flowing Vaigai.” (*Tuṉpa Mālai*, 1–5)

Although Mātari worshipped the minor deity Iyakki, she clearly held Vaiṣṇava convictions on a larger scale. Is not Tirumāl the god of the pastoral *mullai* landscape? Does not the *Akattiṇaiyiyal* of the *Tolkāppiyam* say, “the forest world inhabited by Māyōṉ” (sūtra 5)?

Iḷaṅkō’s statement that Mātari was reborn in her next life as the daughter of a Vaiṣṇava family creates a delightful sense of wonder.

When news arrived that Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki had died, Kōvalaṉ’s mother and Kaṇṇaki’s mother gave up their lives. Mātari, having lost the person entrusted to her protection, entered fire and died. Of these three, Kōvalaṉ’s mother and Kaṇṇaki’s mother were reborn as twin girls in the womb of the wife of a merchant named Araṭṭaṉ Ceṭṭi in Vañji. Mātari was reborn in a family serving Tirumāl, who reclines in divine sleep upon the serpent couch at Tiruvaṉantapuram.

The first two, being members of the merchant community in their former births, were reborn as daughters of a merchant. Mātari, having been born among cowherds who served Tirumāl, having worshipped him, and having performed the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*, was reborn in a Vaiṣṇava family.

Thus Mātari shines as an excellent and radiant character in *Cilappatikāram*.
