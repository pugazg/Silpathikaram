# 29. Notable and Delightful Details

Let us now examine, canto by canto, a selection of the notable and delightful details found in *Cilappatikāram*.

## The Song of Auspicious Blessing

### The Wedding Invitation

Half a century ago, most families did not print invitations when a wedding took place. As a boy, I saw people go from house to house, offer betel leaf and areca nut, and invite others orally. Even relatives living in other towns were visited in person and invited with betel and areca.

Today, through an abundance of money, invitations are printed at great expense. There may be one invitation from both families, another in the name of the women, and so on. Some even announce the wedding in newspapers and ask readers to treat the advertisement itself as an invitation. The amount now spent on an invitation for a wealthy wedding might once have paid for my grandfather’s entire marriage.

In ancient Pukār, the families of Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ were both extremely wealthy. They therefore seated women upon elephants and sent them through every street of Pukār to announce the marriage. This was an affair of great houses. Even men today may fear an elephant; it is striking that women then rode upon one to make the announcement:

> “They seated ornamented women upon the backs of elephants
> and gave the marriage to the great city.” (1:43–44)

The expression “gave the marriage to the city” is particularly beautiful and delightful.

Today, invitations printed by the bride’s family generally put the bride’s name first and the groom’s second; the groom’s family reverses the order. Iḷaṅkō, composing the equivalent of a wedding invitation in *Cilappatikāram*, places Kaṇṇaki before Kōvalaṉ. Was Iḷaṅkō a member of the bride’s family? Of course not.

The title *Cilappatikāram*—“The Account of the Anklet”—arises from Kaṇṇaki’s anklet. The heroine occupies a more indispensable place in the epic than the hero. Perhaps this is why Kaṇṇaki receives first position.

Or perhaps her name precedes her husband’s by the same principle found in names such as Umāpati, Umāmahēśvaraṉ, Ambikāpati, Mīṉāṭcicuntaraṉ, Vaḷḷināyakaṉ, Vaḷḷimaṇāḷaṉ, Citti Vināyakar, Lakṣmīkāntaṉ, Lakṣmīnārāyaṇaṉ, Rādhākṛṣṇaṉ, Sītārāmaṉ, and Jānakīrāmaṉ, where a woman’s name introduces the man’s. If Iḷaṅkō appeared before us now, we could ask him the exact reason. Perhaps it was simply the convention of his time. Or, as a Cēra from a region later associated with matrilineal inheritance, did he give women first place?

## The Canto Celebrating the Evening

### Jasmine Grown in the House

A flower-spread bed is described as:

> “A couch of many flowers where jasmine grown in the house
> and the flowering *mallikai* had opened.” (4:27–28)

In the “Canto of Lodging in the Outskirts,” a garland of *mādhavi*, *mallikai*, and jasmine is likewise called:

> “A spreading garland of *mādhavi*, *mallikai*,
> and jasmine grown in the house.” (13:120–121)

Chaste women are said to have cultivated jasmine in their homes. The expressions “jasmine grown in the house” and “jasmine grown in the dwelling” indicate this. The same idea appears in the *Kuṟuntokai*:

> “Her many-dark tresses smell
> of the jasmine at the house-front.” (19:4–5)

in the *Naṟṟiṇai*:

> “In the middle of the house, jasmine buds open in season.” (115:6)

in the *Akanāṉūṟu*:

> “Pointing to the great branch of the household jasmine.” (23:12)

and in the *Peruṅkatai*:

> “Jasmine rising from the house mingled with *mallikai*
> and spread a great fragrance.” (1:33:73–74)

Many other works contain the same association. Drawing on this tradition, Oṭṭakkūttar even says in the *Takkayākapparani* that Umā herself cultivated jasmine. Its hundred thousand branches climbed into the sky and brushed the moon; the deer-mark upon the moon, fearing that the plant belonged to the goddess, did not graze upon it:

> “The jasmine that the sharp-horned goddess never released
> rose into the sky with a hundred thousand branches;
> its cool shoots touched the moon’s horn,
> and the fawn there did not graze them,
> thinking them forbidden.” (75)

Because jasmine was grown in the houses of chaste women, the *Āciriyanikaṇṭu* gives “chastity” as one of its names (137). Many other literary works likewise connect jasmine and chastity.

### The Whiteness of Fame

It is a literary convention to describe fame as white. In the lines:

> “The white crescent appeared in the evening sky
> as a silver lamp ruling the kingdom of stars.” (4:23–26)

commentators also interpret *viḷakkam*, “radiance,” as fame. Fame is represented as silver-white.

Parimēlaḻakar and others give the meaning “fame” to *viḷakkam* in the *Tirukkuṟaḷ*:

> “If the grievous disease called enmity departs,
> it will yield unfailing and imperishable radiance.” (853)

Kampaṉ’s *Rāmāyaṇa* says that white banners spread like fame and like the white waves of a celestial river:

> “The great army’s banners rolled in the wind like fame
> and spread without bound like the white waves
> of the river of the sky.” (5.9.7)

The moon’s white light also seemed as though Rāma’s fame had entered and wandered there:

> “It resembled the splendour
> of that man’s fame entering and roaming.” (5.1.65)

Kampaṉ uses the idea elsewhere too. Sivappirakācar writes in the *Pirapuliṅkalīlai* that white Mount Kailāsa appeared like the accumulated fame of Śiva:

> “The prosperous hill of Kailāsa,
> where the generous lord’s white fame had gathered.” (2.4)

### Kōvalaṉ’s Absence

Because Kōvalaṉ had left Kaṇṇaki, her feet lost their anklets; her waist lost its girdle; her breasts lost the writing of red cosmetic paste; her ears lost their heavy earrings; all her limbs lost ornaments other than the auspicious marriage-thread; her face lost its fine perspiration; her eyes lost collyrium; her forehead lost its mark; her white, radiant smile lost Kōvalaṉ; and her dark hair forgot its oil:

> “Her lovely little red feet were without their ornamental anklets;
> the girdle left her soft-clothed waist;
> she drew no red paste upon the courtyard of her breasts;
> she rejoiced in no ornament except the auspicious one;
> abandoning her heavy earrings, her ears hung bare;
> fine perspiration fled her moon-bright face;
> her long red-fish eyes forgot their collyrium;
> her coral-radiant forehead lost its mark;
> her white shining smile lost Kōvalaṉ;
> her dark hair forgot the adornment of oil—
> Kaṇṇaki, whose heart had lost all means of action.” (4:47–57)

This is delightful because Iḷaṅkō does not merely say she did not wear each item; he says that each part had “lost” something. He also varies the words—left, departed, fled, forgot, lost—within one meaning. Because there was no union with her husband, her face lost the perspiration of intimacy.

A description proceeding from the feet upward is called *pādādi kēśam* in Sanskrit; one proceeding from hair downward is *kēśādi pādam*. In Tamil we may simply say “from foot to head” and “from head to foot.” Here Iḷaṅkō follows the foot-to-head order, beginning with the feet losing the anklet and ending with the hair forgetting oil. The method is particularly appropriate to a work centred upon an anklet: “the anklet departed” rightly receives first place.

I have interpreted “the white shining smile lost Kōvalaṉ” to mean that the smile itself lost him. Commentators instead say that Kōvalaṉ lost Kaṇṇaki’s smile. That does not fit the pattern of the surrounding lines and destroys the refinement of the conception. Her smile appeared only before Kōvalaṉ. Since he was absent, the smile itself no longer existed; it had lost Kōvalaṉ and had no work to perform. When Kōvalaṉ later returns after leaving Mādhavi, the poem explicitly says Kaṇṇaki showed him a smiling face:

> “She displayed a face with a graceful, auspicious smile.” (9:72)

This passage also helps resolve a debated question. In the marriage canto, Aṭiyārkkunallār glosses “the auspicious ornament arose throughout the broad city” as “the auspicious decoration appeared everywhere.” One hesitates to criticise Aṭiyārkkunallār—whose very name means “good to all devotees”—but the explanation is unclear. Some interpret it as the marriage-thread being carried in procession through the city. Whether Kōvalaṉ actually tied a *tāli* around Kaṇṇaki’s neck is disputed. Some use Aṭiyārkkunallār’s commentary to argue that he did not; others use another commentary to say he did. The latter is more fitting. The internal evidence is the line already quoted:

> “She rejoiced in no ornament except the auspicious one.” (4:50)

She wore no ornament other than the *tāli*, which by nature remained upon her.

A second internal proof occurs in the “Canto Establishing Domestic Virtue,” where Kōvalaṉ praises Kaṇṇaki:

> “O garlanded one of fragrant flowers,
> when the stainless auspicious ornament itself adorns you,
> why have those who praise your beauty
> obtained the right to place other ornaments upon you?” (2:62–64)

This too makes clear that the *tāli* had been tied.

In the “Canto in Which the City Celebrated the Indra Festival,” Iḷaṅkō’s imaginative description of public women moving through the streets during the festival is rich in flavour. One must read the poem itself; elaborating it here would make this chapter too long.

## The Canto of Seeing the Country

Farmers’ labour sustains both the households of those who beg and the victory of the kings who protect. Since agriculture becomes possible through the wealth of the Kāviri, the farmers are honoured as “children of the Kāviri”:

> “The children of the maiden Kāviri of spreading waters,
> who through cultivation sustain
> the households of petitioners
> and the victory of protectors.” (10:148–150)

The conception of the Kāviri protecting her agricultural children like a mother has been discussed more fully under another heading.

## The Canto of Seeing the Forest

### The Command of the Eyes

The Brahmin of Māṅkāṭu says he set out because his eyes commanded him to show them Śrīraṅgam and Tiruvēṅkaṭam:

> “The reclining form of him whose chest is occupied by the goddess,
> and the standing form of the red-eyed Tall One—
> my heart was troubled as my eyes demanded,
> ‘Show them to us!’
> So I came from Māṅkāṭu in the Kuṭaku hills.” (11:40, 51–53)

The personification of the eyes insisting that they be shown the sights is charming. A comparable imagination occurs in the novel *Teyvikat Tirumaṇam*:

> “But the young man could not answer his eyes. At first they reminded him that they wished to see her; he ignored them. They continued to plead that he give them an opportunity; he dismissed them. At last they became obstinate that they must see her—and they did.”

The Māṅkāṭu Brahmin spent money to show his eyes Śrīraṅgam and Tiruvēṅkaṭam. A related saying of Shakespeare comes to mind: “Make the hand poor and the eye rich.” To make the hand poor is to spend the money in it; to make the eye rich is to see many sights, take pleasure, and gain general knowledge. Through the Māṅkāṭu Brahmin, Iḷaṅkō indirectly urges us to travel and gain knowledge of the world.

### The Orb Worshipped by the World

The sun, which opens the lotus, appeared and awakened Ceḻiyaṉ’s Madurai:

> “The orb worshipped by the world, which opened the flower’s enclosure,
> awakened lofty Kūṭal from sleep.” (14:4–6)

The sun is called “the orb worshipped by the world” because the world worships it and many planets surround it. In the “Canto of the Water Consecration” it is called “the red-rayed god of morning” (27:137). Sun worship exists in many places. Iḷaṅkō himself says, “Let us praise the sun.” There are temples to the sun and a village named Sūriyanār Kōyil. Egypt too possessed sun temples.

Nakkīrar begins the *Tirumurukāṟṟuppaṭai* by praising:

> “The much-praised sun that moves on its rightward course,
> making the world rejoice.” (1–2)

People appeal to the sun as a witness for justice: “There goes Sūriya Nārāyaṇaṉ burning across the sky—he will question you.” The sun is also the mother from whom our earth was born.

## The Canto of the Legal Plea

### Mistress of Speech

Hanumān is called “master of speech” because of the verbal skill with which he briefly introduced himself to Rāma and Lakṣmaṇa. When the Pāṇḍya asks, “Who are you, vine-like woman?”, Kaṇṇaki becomes a mistress of speech.

She answers in compact form: “My city is Pukār, ruled by Cibi, who removed a dove’s suffering, and by Manu Nīti Cōḻa, whose heart was burned by a cow’s tears and who killed his precious son beneath his chariot wheel. I am the wife of Kōvalaṉ, son of the great merchant Mācāttuvāṉ of that city—Kōvalaṉ, who came to your Madurai to sell my anklet and was killed by you. My name is Kaṇṇaki.” May we not call the woman who narrates her whole history so concisely a mistress of speech?

In the “Canto of the Garland of Vows,” Kaṇṇaki recounts the histories of seven chaste women and declares that she was born in the Pukār that gave birth to them. The passage deserves to be read and enjoyed.

## The Canto of the Vision

### Yama’s Astonishment

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ alone fought five hundred Aryan kings. Yama is said to have watched the battle in astonishment, his terrible eyes wide open without blinking:

> “Cruel-eyed Death watched it with eyes opened wide.” (25:64)

This is a piece of literary delight.

When Pāṇḍya’s queen Kōpperuntēvi saw her husband die, she immediately gave up her own life, as though using it to search for his:

> “As though taking her own life to seek his life,
> the great queen too died at that very moment.” (25:85)

This too is literary personification.

### Political Strategy

Villavaṉ Kōtai advised Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ to send advance messengers to the northern kings announcing his expedition. But Aḻumpil Vēḷ said that no messengers were necessary: spies of all the world’s rulers would already be present in Vañji. If the announcement were made by public drum within the city, the spies would hear it and send the news home. This is a delightful piece of political strategy.

### Loss of the Shoulder-Companion

The following proclamation was drummed through Vañji under royal order:

“O kings of the north! Since the Cēra has already claimed the Himalaya, you owe him tribute. Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ now comes north to obtain a stone for Kaṇṇaki. Pay tribute when he passes. Otherwise, renounce your wives, your companions at the shoulder, and live as ascetics.”

> “Live in the renunciation that abandons the shoulder-companion.” (25:190)

The fuller passage says:

> “If you come before him bearing the tribute due,
> live after hearing the great report
> of the fierce war that felled the sea’s *kaṭampu*,
> and the vast report of the bow engraved upon the mountain cleft.
> If you will not listen,
> live in the renunciation that abandons the shoulder-companion.” (25:186–195)

The Cēras had crossed the sea to fell the *kaṭampu* tree and had reached the Himalaya to engrave their bow. The passage carries the refined implication: if you do not fear them, flee beyond the sea or beyond the Himalaya and live there.

“The companion at the shoulder” means the wife. Compare Valluvar’s title for the wife: “companion of life.”

## The Canto of Taking the Stone

### The Mountain’s Back Bends

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ travelled through the Nīla Mountains with a vast army. Under the multitude of troops, “the mountain’s back bent” (26:82). This is literary delight. Only living beings normally possess backs that bend; the action is transferred to the inanimate mountain.

According to the *Taṇṭiyalaṅkāram*:

> “Joining an action belonging to its proper object
> to another object that resembles it
> is called *samādhi*.” (25)

In Tamil, this figure may be called “the ornament that joins a comparable action.”

### The Enemies’ Disguises

Except for Kanaka, Vijaya, and others taken captive in Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s northern war, many enemies escaped in disguise. Some wore matted hair; some dressed in ochre; some smeared sacred ash; some carried a Jain monk’s seat and peacock-feather whisk; some posed as singing bards; some bore many instruments upon their shoulders like actors. Casting away their swords, they ran to whatever places they wished, disguised as practitioners of arts they happened to know. In modern language, perhaps they “withdrew successfully”:

> “Some wore matted hair and garments; some were smeared with ash;
> some became great ascetics with seats and peacock feathers;
> some became singing bards, some actors
> with many instruments upon their shoulders.
> Abandoning their uplifted swords,
> in the skilled forms of chosen disguises,
> they scattered wherever they wished.” (26:225–230)

The passage is rich in humour. Similar scenes occur in Kampaṉ’s *Rāmāyaṇa* and Cayaṅkoṇṭār’s *Kaliṅkattupparaṇi*.

In the “Slaying of Akṣa Kumāra” in the *Sundara Kāṇḍa*, Kampaṉ humorously describes demons escaping Hanumān in many disguises. Some became fish and entered the sea; some took the form of cattle and grazed along the road; some became carrion birds and pecked at corpses; some became Brahmins; some took female form and parted their hair; some begged Hanumān for refuge; some stood motionless reciting Viṣṇu’s names like devotees. When wives and relatives embraced them weeping, some pushed them away and cried, “We are not your kin—we are gods who came to witness the war!” Others claimed to be human, became bees in groves, lay as though unconscious, broke their demonic fangs to resemble human teeth, or darkened their fiery hair:

> “Some became fish and reached the sea;
> some, as cattle, grazed along the roads;
> some took the form of flesh-eating birds;
> some became men of the four Vedas;
> some became young doe-eyed women
> and stood before him parting their hair;
> some cried, ‘Lord, we seek your refuge!’
> and some who stood there called upon Hari.” (40)

> “Whenever wives and close kin embraced them,
> some cried, ‘We are not your relatives;
> we are gods who came here!’ and went away;
> some shouted that they were human;
> some became bees in mandāra-filled groves;
> some feigned bewilderment;
> some broke their fangs,
> and some darkened hair that shone like fire.” (41)

In the “Singing of the War” in the *Kaliṅkattupparaṇi*, Kaliṅga warriors fleeing the Cōḻa army ran through thorny scrub. The thorns tore away their clothes and hair, so, now naked and shaven like Jain monks, they escaped by claiming not to be soldiers but ascetics (63). Some folded their bowstrings into sacred threads and said they were Brahmins travelling to bathe in the Gaṅgā (64). Some wrapped themselves in blood-soaked banners like ochre robes, shaved their heads, and claimed to be Buddhist monks (65). Others beat elephant bells as cymbals and said they were not Kaliṅgas but Telugu bards (66).

The enemies of the Cōḻa and Cēra were human, so the imagination remained within limits. Those who fought Hanumān were demons, allowing Kampaṉ’s imagination to exceed all bounds.

Literary comparison suggests one conclusion: Kampaṉ and Cayaṅkoṇṭār, both later than *Cilappatikāram*, may have poured Iḷaṅkō’s earlier conception into their own works. The scenes may of course have arisen naturally in each poet’s imagination, but it remains reasonable to regard the latter two as extracts from the flavour of *Cilappatikāram*.

## The Canto of the Water Consecration

### Eighteen as the Duration of War

The war of gods and demons lasted eighteen years; the war of Rāma and Rāvaṇa lasted eighteen months; the Bhārata war lasted eighteen days; Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s war defeating Kanaka and Vijaya lasted only eighteen *nāḻikai*:

> “Count the total of lives consumed as twice nine:
> years, months, days, and time-units—
> bring them together and reckon them in this watery world.” (27:8–10)

Nine doubled is eighteen. Thus: eighteen years for the divine war, eighteen months for the Rāmāyaṇa war, eighteen days for the Bhārata war, and eighteen *nāḻikai* for the Cēra’s victory. Two and a half *nāḻikai* make roughly one hour, so the Cēra won in about seven and a quarter hours.

### Ascending the Crown

After narrating Kaṇṇaki’s history to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, Māṭalaṉ concludes:

> “O king of the Kuṭavar...
> she ascended the jewelled crowns of the northern kings.” (27:64–65)

Kanaka and Vijaya carried upon their crowned heads the stone from which Kaṇṇaki’s image would be made. Māṭalaṉ expresses this elegantly as Kaṇṇaki herself ascending their crowns. Even before the statue existed, certainty of purpose transformed the stone into Kaṇṇaki.

If one thinks “stone,” Kaṇṇaki disappears; if one thinks “Kaṇṇaki,” the stone disappears. The temple images we see today are no longer perceived as stone but appear as gods.

Tirumūlar’s *Tirumantiram* expresses a comparable idea through a wooden elephant. If one thinks of it as an elephant, awareness of wood disappears; if one thinks of it as wood, the elephant disappears:

> “The great rutting elephant concealed the wood;
> within the wood the great rutting elephant disappeared.” (2290)

The proverb “When we see the dog, we do not see the stone; when we see the stone, we do not see the dog” conveys the same point. Some houses place a stone dog in the courtyard to deceive thieves.

### One Anklet and a Hand

When the gatekeeper announced Kaṇṇaki’s arrival to the Pāṇḍya, he said, “She has in her hand one anklet wrought in gold” (20:42). The emotional refinement lies in the reversals: an anklet meant for the foot is held in the hand, and of a matched pair she possesses only one.

If every line of *Cilappatikāram* is examined closely, countless such special details emerge.

### The Weapon of Tears

Some people use tears as a weapon to obtain what they need. Nature gives this weapon to children. Women in families sometimes shoot it as well; so do certain poor people and petitioners.

Beyond satisfying needs, tears may also act as a weapon that destroys the power of the cruel. Valluvar says under “Tyrannical Rule”:

> “The tears shed by those who suffer and cannot endure
> are the army that wears away the tyrant’s wealth.” (555)

Tears may even become a weapon that kills. Was it not Kaṇṇaki’s tears that killed Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ? The “Canto of Blessing” proclaims:

> “Long live the king of kings who gave his life to be slain
> by the tears of the woman who suffered through ancient karma.” (13:1–2)

The phrase does not say merely that he “lost” his life but that he “gave” it, a refinement that raises the Pāṇḍya into a king of kings.

The *Naṟuntokai* emphasises that tears can become a killing sword:

> “The tears shed in anguish by those
> who lost their case when Manu’s law was violated,
> even if the three gods guard the offender according to rule,
> become a sword that cuts through generation after generation.” (75)

Such tears bring great disgrace even upon descendants. Their power is immense.

> “The tears of the poor are equal to a sharpened sword.”

Many such delightful and significant details occur in the epic. Let us consider one more group.

## The Two Great Kuravais

*Cilappatikāram* contains two great *kuravai* dance-sections: the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai* and the Hill-People’s *Kuravai*. The former may be called a miniature epic of Tirumāl and the latter a miniature epic of Murukaṉ. Both excel in the art of making a large subject shine through compression. One wonders where and from whom Iḷaṅkō learned to write such passages.

### 1. The Cowherd Women’s Kuravai

#### The Sevens

Because many evil omens appeared in their settlement, Mātari told Aiyai that performing a *kuravai* would prevent misfortune. Seven bulls had been raised in the pen, one for each of seven maidens. Mātari described which girl would belong to the man who subdued each bull:

1. The maiden wearing a honey-filled flower garland desires the man who leaps upon the black bull without fearing its rage.
2. The shoulders of the gold-bangled woman belong to the man who mounts and subdues the bull marked upon the forehead.
3. The maiden with jasmine in her hair belongs to the man who masters the powerful young bull.
4. The shoulders of the vine-like woman belong to the man who subdues the white bull covered in tiny speckles.
5. The breasts of the good vine-like maiden belong to the man who subdues the white bull with golden spots.
6. The woman with *koṉṟai* flowers in her hair belongs to the man who mounts the victorious young bull.
7. The maiden wearing a fresh *pūvai* flower belongs to the man who embraces and masters the pure white bull.

The verses read:

> “This honey-flower-garlanded woman desires
> the man who leaps upon the raging black bull without fear.”

> “The shoulders of this gold-bangled maiden belong
> to the man who subdues the forehead-marked bull.”

> “This jasmine-flower-haired woman belongs
> to the man who rides the mighty young bull.”

> “The shoulders of this vine-like woman belong
> to the man who subdues the finely speckled white bull.”

> “The soft breasts of this good vine-like woman belong
> to the man who subdues the gold-speckled white bull.”

> “This *koṉṟai*-flower-haired woman belongs
> to the man who rides the victorious young bull.”

> “This fresh-*pūvai*-flower woman belongs
> to the man who subdues the pure white bull.”

After saying this, Mātari gave the seven maidens new names in addition to their old ones: Kural, Tuttam, Kaikkiḷai, Uḻai, Iḷi, Viḷari, and Tāram:

> “Then, looking upon her daughter, she said:
> ‘These are the seven young maidens
> who raised the bulls of the pen for mounting.’
> Placing them in the ancient order,
> the senior cowherd woman created and assigned names:
> from the central Kural in order—Kural, Tuttam,
> Kaikkiḷai, Uḻai, Iḷi, Viḷari, and Tāram—
> names desired for the women of spreading flower-like hair.”

“To create and name” means inventing new names for them.

Some researchers identify these ancient Tamil seven notes with today’s *sa, ri, ga, ma, pa, dha, ni*—Ṣaḍja, Ṛṣabha, Gāndhāra, Madhyama, Pañcama, Dhaivata, and Niṣāda. Vipulānanda Aṭikaḷ, however, says in the *Yāḻ Nūl* that no exact one-to-one equation can be made. The matter requires research.

#### “Shall We Not Hear It, Friend?”

My desire urges me to give three passages from the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai* without explanation so that they may simply be enjoyed:

> “If Māyavaṉ, who used a calf as a club to bring down the fruit,
> should come today among our cattle,
> shall we not hear from his lips
> the sweet flute of *koṉṟai*, friend?”

> “If Māyavaṉ, who used the serpent as rope to churn the sea,
> should come here among our cattle,
> shall we not hear from his lips
> the sweet flute of water-lily, friend?”

> “If Māyavaṉ, who broke the *kuruntu* trees
> on the slopes of the lovely pasture,
> should come within our boundary among the cattle,
> shall we not hear from his lips
> the sweet flute of jasmine, friend?”

These deserve to be sung and savoured repeatedly. The cowherd women singing them while performing the *kuravai* is like a rain of honey falling upon a pavilion of sugar.

Three lines under “Praise in the Third Person” are also noteworthy:

> “What kind of ears are ears that have not heard the warrior’s glory?
> What kind of ears are ears that have not heard Tirumāl’s glory?”

> “What kind of eyes are eyes that have not seen the dark one?
> What kind of eyes are the eyes of those
> who blink while seeing him?”

> “What kind of tongue is a tongue that does not praise
> the one who walked as messenger for the five Pāṇḍavas?
> What kind of tongue is a tongue that does not say, ‘Nārāyaṇa’?”

Out of overflowing love, the ear must hear Tirumāl’s greatness, the eye must see his form, and the tongue must praise him.

The poet does not stop with attacking eyes that fail to see Tirumāl. He attacks even eyes that blink while seeing him: they must gaze continuously without closing at all. This is a fine conceit.

Since the cowherds were Vaiṣṇavas who worshipped Tirumāl, these passages belong naturally in their *kuravai*. Iḷaṅkō’s greatness lies in becoming the appropriate actor in each setting.

For literary comparison, consider Tirunāvukkaracar’s commands to his bodily members:

> “O head, bow!”
> “O eyes, behold!”
> “O ears, listen!”
> “O nose, murmur!”
> “O mouth, praise!”
> “O heart, remember!”
> “O hands, join and worship!”
> “What use is the body otherwise?”
> “What use are the feet otherwise?”

In a Tēvāram hymn he assigns duties to nine organs among the senses of knowledge, organs of action, and inner faculty.

Iḷaṅkō names only the duties of ear, eye, and tongue because he is not speaking in his own authorial voice. He measures his words according to the situation of the cowherd women. Nāvukkaracar, addressing his own organs, could issue commands generously and without thrift to many of them.

The Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai* deserves to be sung repeatedly and enjoyed.

### 2. The Hill-People’s Kuravai

#### “People of the Little Clan! People of the Little Clan!”

The hill people saw Kaṇṇaki standing beneath a *vēṅkai* tree in their mountains and then ascending to the heavenly world. Since they believed they had no other deity like her, they called upon their clan to worship her. The passage is delightful and needs no explanation:

> “People of the little clan! People of the little clan!
> Take a deity for yourselves, people of the little clan!
> Beneath the good shade of the fragrant-branched *vēṅkai*,
> on the lower slope of the mountain where the waterfall
> descends in many colours,
> take a deity for yourselves, people of the little clan!
> Beat the *toṇṭakam* drum; beat the little drum;
> sound the curved horn; ring the fierce bell;
> sing the *kuṟiñci* mode; raise fragrant smoke;
> make flower-offerings; establish the protective gateway;
> praise her again and again; scatter mingled flowers,
> so that for the lady who lost one breast
> the great mountain may never sleep
> but pour forth prosperity.”

The instruments and forms of worship follow one upon another, delighting the reader.

#### “This Is Laughable!”

A heroine loves a man, but because marriage is delayed she wastes away. Her mother, ignorant of the true cause, assumes that a deity has seized and frightened the girl. Believing the deity to be Murukaṉ, she arranges a *vēlaṉ veṟiyāṭṭu*, a possession-ritual. She worships Murukaṉ and summons a possessed priest called the *vēlaṉ* to diagnose and cure the illness. In Tamil love poetics this is the conventional situation called “the *vēlaṉ*’s frenzy.”

The heroine says to her friend: “My mother thinks the illness caused by my lover was caused by Murukaṉ and has sent for the frenzied *vēlaṉ*. This is laughable, friend!”

She elaborates four times. The priest who comes to cure the illness caused by her mountain-lover is ignorant because he does not know its true source. If Murukaṉ, destroyer of Krauñca Mountain, possesses that priest, Murukaṉ too must be foolish. If the son of Śiva comes upon a priest trying to cure an illness caused by the fragrant mountain-man, the god is even more foolish than the priest. If Murukaṉ of the *kaṭampu* garland comes to cure the burning illness given by the hero’s chest, he is the most thoroughly foolish of all.

The verses say:

> “Good straight-bangled friend, this is laughable!
> My mother does not know that the cool-hill man
> where pepper grows caused my illness;
> she calls it the work of the flowering *kaṭampu* lord,
> desires the frenzy, and says, ‘Let the *vēlaṉ* come!’”

> “Fine-bangled friend, this is laughable!
> The *vēlaṉ* who comes to cure the illness of the great mountain-man
> is a fool; and if he comes,
> the one who destroyed Krauñca Mountain is a fool beyond him.”

> “Good close-bangled friend, this is laughable!
> The *vēlaṉ* who comes to cure the illness caused
> by the fragrant mountain-man is a fool;
> and if the son of the lord beneath the banyan comes upon him,
> that god is more foolish still.”

> “Good straight-ornamented friend, this is laughable!
> The *vēlaṉ* who comes to cure the burning illness
> given by the mountain-man’s chest is a fool;
> and if our dark-*kaṭampu*-garlanded god comes,
> he is more foolish than the *vēlaṉ* himself.”

Iḷaṅkō appears to have mastered Tamil love grammar completely.

The heroine then says they will turn the ritual to their own purpose:

1. If Murukaṉ comes upon his blue peacock with Vaḷḷi, they will ask him to grant marriage with her mountain-lover.
2. If the son of the mountain goddess comes, they will worship him and Vaḷḷi, the maiden of their hill community, and beg him to prevent a marriage with a stranger and grant union with the beloved.
3. They will ask that the marriage with the lover be publicly known.
4. They will worship Murukaṉ and Vaḷḷi, “our own clan’s daughter,” and ask the god to reject the erroneous marriage and grant the right one.

The verses say:

> “If, in the fierce ritual-ground where the *vēlaṉ* comes in frenzy,
> the son of the lord beneath the banyan arrives
> upon the blue bird with the straight-ornamented woman,
> we shall ask for the marriage-ornament
> with the lord of the great mountain.”

> “O moon-browed maiden, we worshipped the flower-like sacred feet
> of the mountain people’s daughter
> and the son of the lord of good Mount Kailāsa:
> ‘End the alien marriage; grant marriage with him.’”

> “O moon-browed maiden, we worshipped the paired feet
> of the exalted god, son of the mountain goddess,
> and the daughter of the hill people dwelling in our ancestral mountain:
> ‘May she obtain marriage with him for all to know.’”

> “She is a daughter of the hill clan—our own clan’s daughter.
> O six-faced one, we worship your paired feet with hers:
> ‘Let the man who touches your sacred feet at the ford
> receive the good marriage; release us from the mistaken one.’”

The refined appeal is that Murukaṉ’s wife Vaḷḷi belongs to their own hunter community; this kinship is used to draw and persuade the god. Many such flavours may be enjoyed in the Hill-People’s *Kuravai*.

In both great *kuravais*, a common dramatic structure appears: the second line of a stanza is repeated as the third. One example from each follows.

**Cowherd Women’s Kuravai**

> “He entered the three waters and felled the deathless *kaṭampu*;
> the king of kings, the Cēra, ruler of prosperous Vañji;
> the king of kings, the Cēra, ruler of prosperous Vañji—
> they say he lifted his sugarcane-like shoulders and churned the sea.”

**Hill-People’s Kuravai**

> “We see no fault with which to reproach him;
> the fresh flood that came touching the stone of his mountain—
> the fresh flood that came touching the stone of his mountain—
> if other women bathe in it, friend, our heart will suffer.”

I remember with pleasure reading in *The Art of Play Writing* that immediate repetition of the same word or phrase is a device that adds dramatic flavour.

Thus *Cilappatikāram* contains many notable and delightful details.
