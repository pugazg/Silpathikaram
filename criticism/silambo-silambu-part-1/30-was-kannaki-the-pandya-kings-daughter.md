# 30. Was Kaṇṇaki the Pāṇḍya King’s Daughter?

## The Canto of Blessing

The women of Vañji sing of Kaṇṇaki as the Pāṇḍya king’s daughter:

> “Women of Vañji! Women of Vañji’s middle streets!
> Soft-footed companions of the king with the unfailing spear,
> come—all of you, come!
> Let us sing of the woman who destroyed Kūṭal with her breast
> and defeated the king with her red anklet;
> come, let us sing of the southern king’s daughter!
> Our king’s tongue of justice proclaimed,
> ‘Pāṇḍyas do not live when the just sceptre bends,’
> and she, the green-bangled maiden, reached this land;
> come, let us sing of the Pāṇḍya’s daughter!” (29:11)

The meaning is that all the women of Vañji are summoned to sing of Kaṇṇaki: she who burned Madurai with her breast, she who was the Pāṇḍya’s daughter, and she who reached the Cēra land after their king praised the Pāṇḍyas as rulers who would not remain alive when the sceptre departed from justice.

The women sing further: “Because the Cēra built Kaṇṇaki a temple, restored her to new life, and made her a deity through a new divine birth, we women of Vañji called her the Cēra king’s daughter. But Kaṇṇaki, now a goddess, declared in an audible voice, ‘I am the Pāṇḍya’s daughter.’ Let us praise our Cēra! Let the divine daughter Kaṇṇaki praise the Pāṇḍya!”

> “We said, ‘She is the daughter of our celestial Cēra king’;
> but the vine-like woman said,
> ‘I am the daughter begotten by the lord of the Vaigai.’
> Let us praise the celestial Cēra;
> let the divine daughter praise the king of the Vaigai.” (29:12)

Kaṇṇaki was the daughter of Mānāykaṉ, a merchant of Pukār. We must therefore untangle the difficulty created by calling her the Pāṇḍya’s daughter. What new complication is this? We need the help of an older folk story.

## Was Kaṇṇaki Kālī?

An old story says that Kālī came as Kaṇṇaki. I had thought about this even in my youth. On 11 May 1949, under the auspices of the Tiruvalluvar Association of Tiruchirappalli, I delivered a lecture lasting two and three-quarter hours at a choultry in the fruit-market street on the subject “What Is Chastity?” in relation to *Cilappatikāram*. On the fourteenth of the same month, at the Murukaṉ temple in Vaṇṭippāḷaiyam, Cuddalore, I spoke for two and a quarter hours on “Was Kaṇṇaki Kālī?” Many of the ideas expressed in one town naturally appeared again in the other.

My conclusion was that Kaṇṇaki was not Kālī but an ordinary human woman like others; Kālī was not born in her form. The elder who presided over the meeting rejected my conclusion in his closing speech and insisted that Kālī herself came as Kaṇṇaki. I would not yield and rose again to assert my view. He would not yield either; nor did I. Somehow the meeting ended. There had been no such opposition in Tiruchirappalli.

A performance detail about Kōvalaṉ that I saw or heard in youth ran as follows: Mādhavi placed a garland around his neck, and he could not remove it. I remember one line of the associated song:

> “The garland that fell upon my neck cannot be removed—
> maiden, whose trick is this?”

Children, imitating adults, would playfully mispronounce and sing it; I too have sung it. We also sang the line attributed to Kaṇṇaki in her plea before the Pāṇḍya:

> “O blameworthy Pāṇḍya, repay blame for blame!”

Several related lines were discussed earlier under “Omens.”

## Summary of the Old Story

The complete folk tale may be compressed still further. A Pāṇḍya king without children prayed intensely to Kālī to grant a child in his queen’s womb. Kālī herself was born as the baby, with a garland upon her chest and anklets upon her feet.

The king summoned astrologers to ask the child’s future. They warned that she would bring destruction upon him and Madurai and advised him to remove her. The king placed the infant in a box and had it set afloat far away in the Kāviri. A childless merchant of Pukār found the box on the riverbank, rejoiced at the baby within, named her Kaṇṇaki, and raised her.

When she came of age, her foster-father married her to Kōvalaṉ of the same city. A courtesan named Mādhavi of Tirukkaṭaiyūr placed a garland upon Kōvalaṉ’s neck, and he could not remove it. He remained with her. When the time came that Mādhavi rejected him, he returned to Kaṇṇaki. They travelled to Madurai, where Kōvalaṉ was killed. Kaṇṇaki, who was Kālī, destroyed the Pāṇḍya and Madurai.

This is the folk story in extreme abbreviation. What we should take from it is only that it supplies evidence for the claim that Kaṇṇaki was the Pāṇḍya’s daughter. We need not accept that she was Kālī; that is not true.

What evidence enabled Iḷaṅkō to make both the women of Vañji and Kaṇṇaki herself call her the Pāṇḍya’s daughter? If he drew upon this folk story, why does the wedding canto call her the daughter of Mānāykaṉ of Pukār? If the folk tale preserves an actual event, we can find peace only by assuming that Iḷaṅkō called her simply “daughter” without specifying “foster-daughter.” Since he may have known that this story was performed in the country before he wrote the epic, he may have inserted the phrase “Pāṇḍya’s daughter” at a suitable point. Kaṇṇaki may also have called herself his daughter out of compassion because he died on account of her anklet.

Long stories that later appear in literature were probably often performed in the country before being written down. Such drama might have been *vēttiyal*, court theatre performed before kings and other elites, or *potuviyal*, public street theatre performed before ordinary people.

For comparison, consider Homer’s Greek epic the *Iliad*. One view holds that many parts of it were sung by ordinary people in villages of Yugoslavia. Could *Cilappatikāram* have taken shape in a comparable way?

## The One-Breasted Woman

A poem in the *Naṟṟiṇai*, concerning a hero, heroine, and other woman, uses as a simile a woman who cut off one breast:

> “Tirumāvuṇṇi, who cut off one breast
> because the distress caused by an alien man tormented her.” (216:8–9)

Some infer that this Tirumāvuṇṇi, who acted after cutting off a breast because of grief caused by a man, may have been Kaṇṇaki.

The goddess Bhagavati of Koṭuṅkōḷūr is even today called “the one-breasted woman.”

It is therefore natural for epics to arise upon the foundation of stories circulating among the people.

## Pēkaṉ and Kaṇṇaki

A poem by Kapilar in the *Puṟanāṉūṟu* says that the generous Pēkaṉ, famed for covering a peacock with a cloak, had abandoned his wife. She stood at the palace gate and wept until her breasts were wet:

> “O fierce-horsed Pēkaṉ of open-handed generosity...
> Who is this pitiable woman?...
> Unable to restrain the tears she poured forth,
> sobbing until her breast was wet,
> she wept greatly, like a flute lamenting.” (143)

The poem seems to plead: “Who is she? Show her grace.” Her name too is said to have been Kaṇṇaki. Some claim that the story of Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki arose from this account. That is not fitting. That story is one thing; this is another.
