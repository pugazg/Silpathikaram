# 11. Arts in the Epic

It is commonly said that there are sixty-four arts. That is one conventional classification; new arts may arise in every age. Each art has treatises of its own, but descriptions of the arts also appear incidentally in works devoted to other subjects. It is therefore unsurprising that several arts occupy a large place in *Cilappatikāram*, an epic that combines literature, music, drama, and continuous narrative. Music and dance-drama receive even greater attention in it than literary art itself.

The *Canto of the Debut Performance* describes the qualifications of several kinds of teachers and the nature of the theatre. Let us examine them.

## 1. Mādhavi’s Training in Dance

Mādhavi appeared as though she were Urvaśī, sent down from Indra’s world by the curse of the sage Agastya. She lacked nothing in the three accomplishments of dance, song, and beauty. Beginning at the age of five, she trained with the dance-staff for seven years, and at twelve made her debut before the Cōḻa king.

## 2. Qualifications of the Dance Teacher

The dance teacher must possess the following capacities:

1. He must know the conventions of *akakkūttu* and *puṟakkūttu*—the inner and outer dramatic forms—including such paired oppositions as satirical and laudatory performance, courtly and popular performance, and Tamil and Āryan performance.
2. He must coordinate dance with the fourteen components of song.
3. He must know the eleven dance forms beginning with *alliyam*.
4. He must know the twenty-eight expressive configurations appropriate to inner drama.
5. He must know the components of outer drama, beginning with *tēvapāṇi*.
6. He must understand the instrumental patterns appropriate to the different divisions of dance.
7. When presenting inner or outer dance in accordance with song, rhythm, and tempo, he must know the subtle use of single-hand and double-hand gestures.
8. In inner dance, he must prevent single-hand gestures from entering the double-hand system, and double-hand gestures from entering the single-hand system.
9. In outer dance, he must ensure that representational gesture does not intrude during pure dance, and that pure dance does not intrude during representational gesture.
10. He must keep the footwork of *kuravai* dance distinct from that of *vari* dance.

These are among the qualities required of a master of dance.

## 3. Qualifications of the Music Teacher

1. He must know, and be proficient in, yāḻ music, flute music, tonal colour, rhythmic divisions, vocal music, the technique of the *taṇṇumai* drum, and the eleven dance forms.
2. He must set composed forms so that they acquire both musical coherence and emotional flavour (*rasa*).
3. He must know the four modes of movement appropriate to the two classes of song, *centuṟai* and *veṇṭuṟai*, and must preserve the correct music for native words, altered literary words, regional words, and northern loanwords, understanding every musical distinction without fault.
4. He must grasp the poet’s intended meaning, the expressive gesture prescribed by the dance teacher, and the song appropriate to both.

These are among the qualifications of the music teacher.

## 4. Qualifications of the Poet

1. He must be accomplished in all three branches of Tamil—literature, music, and drama—so that the whole Tamil country within its four boundaries may recognize his mastery.
2. He must know the two dramatic traditions, the courtly and the popular.
3. He must know the character of the eleven *paṇs* and compose songs suited to rhythm.
4. He must compose in faultless language.

These are the qualifications of the writer of songs.

## 5. Qualifications of the Taṇṇumai Drummer

1. He must know every dance and the song-types beginning with *paṇṇal*.
2. He must know the prescribed fundamental rhythmic patterns. The number printed in the source at this point appears corrupt.
3. He must understand the phrase-components formed from Tamil letters, excluding northern letters, and all three branches of Tamil.
4. He must know the variants of mode, rhythm, and tempo, together with their merits and defects.
5. He must understand the four categories of diction beginning with native words.
6. He must maintain the form without allowing it to slacken.
7. He must know how to sustain what should be sustained and release what should be released.
8. His playing must accord with the yāḻ, flute, and voice.
9. He must supply what other instruments lack and restrain their excess.
10. He must display beautiful hand technique.

These are the qualities expected of the *taṇṇumai* player—the drummer corresponding broadly to the maddaḷam or mṛdaṅgam performer.

## 6. Qualifications of the Flute Teacher

1. He must know *cittirappuṇarppu* and *vañcanaippuṇarppu*, two specialized methods of musical joining.
2. His knowledge must equal that of the song teacher.
3. He must understand ascent, produced by lifting and closing the finger-holes beginning with the index finger, and descent, produced beginning with the little finger.
4. He must know the 103 modal characteristics and the fourteen *kōvai* arrangements.
5. He must coordinate with the *muḻavu*, *taṇṇumai*, yāḻ, and voice.
6. Of the four movements—*mutal-naṭai*, *vāram*, *kūṭai*, and *tiraḷ*—the first is slow and the last highly compressed. Leaving these aside where necessary, he must be able to render the measured *vāram* song with proper order of word and music.
7. He must sound each letter distinctly and grammatically, without damaging its form.

These are among the qualifications of the flute master.

## 7. Qualifications of the Yāḻ Teacher

1. The fourteen *kōvai* arrangements must shine and accord with the nature of the song.
2. He must arrange the seven *pālai* modes through paired strings.
3. By twelve transformations, he must generate the twelve *pālai* modes.
4. He must combine them suitably within the sixteen *kōvai* arrangements associated with women’s register.
5. He must bring forth all the *pālai* modes in correct order.
6. He must understand the tonal conditions called strength, softness, and balance.
7. He must join the strings without disturbing their arrangement or diminishing the modal character.
8. He must make music with syllables appropriate to the joining.

These are the qualifications of the yāḻ teacher.

## 8. Nature of the Theatre

### I. The Site

1. The theatre must be built on land free from defects.
2. Shrines, monasteries, Brahmin settlements, wells, tanks, and groves should be nearby.
3. The ground must be firm and free of pits, hollows, and deep cavities.
4. Its soil should have a pleasant smell and taste.
5. It must contain no bones, husk, stones, salt, slime, or dust.
6. It should stand in the centre of the town, on a street along which chariots pass.

These are the qualities required of the site.

### II. The Stage

The stage should be seven measuring-rods wide, eight rods long, and one rod high. The measuring rod itself is defined as twenty-four thumb-breadths of a well-proportioned man. There must be two entrances, one for entering and one for leaving. The rod should be made from bamboo cut on a sacred mountain such as Potiyil, with one span between each node.

### III. Stage Arrangement

1. Paintings of the guardian spirits of the four social groups should be displayed for public worship.
2. Lamps should be arranged so that the shadows of the pillars do not fall across the stage.
3. A single-faced curtain (*orumukha eḻini*) should be fixed at the left pillar.
4. A double-faced curtain (*porumukha eḻini*) should be fixed between the two right-side pillars.
5. A concealed descending curtain (*karantuvaral eḻini*) should be installed above.
6. An elaborately painted canopy should cover the stage.
7. Pearl garlands of white, gold, and red—associated respectively with the Moon, Jupiter, and Mars—should hang and sway as strands, loops, and festoons.

These are the arrangements required in the theatre.

## 9. Nature of the Talaikkōl

1. The shaft of a royal umbrella captured from a defeated enemy should become the *talaikkōl*, the ceremonial staff of honour.
2. Nine kinds of gems should be set at its joints, with plates of Jāmbūnada gold between them.
3. A special place must be prepared for the staff in the king’s palace.
4. It must be regarded as Jayanta and worshipped according to sacred ritual.
5. On an auspicious day, water from a holy river must be brought in a golden vessel and used to bathe it.
6. It must be decorated with garlands and other ornaments.
7. With an elephant present, drums sounding, and the king and the Five Great Councils accompanying it, the staff must be conveyed by chariot and elephant. It is placed in the hand of a singer standing in the chariot, and carried in procession around the city.
8. On reaching the theatre, it must be installed facing the stage.
9. Only then may the dance begin.

These are the ceremonies connected with the *talaikkōl*.

## 10. Manner of Performance

1. Before the dance begins, the king, ministers, and other dignitaries must sit in their assigned places.
2. The performing area must be apportioned by rods: three rods for the central dance area, one for the dancers, one for the singers, one for the intervening space, and one for the instrumentalists.
3. Mādhavi mounted the stage with her right foot first and stood by the double-faced curtain at the right pillar.
4. The senior women of the chorus stood beside the left pillar, near the single-faced curtain.
5. They first sang prayers to the deity, asking that evil depart and good arise. Both the one-beat and two-beat *vāram* invocations could be sung.
6. At the close of the invocation, all the instruments sounded.
7. The yāḻ followed the flute; the voice and *taṇṇumai* followed the yāḻ; the pot-drum followed the *taṇṇumai*; and the instrument called *iṭakkai* joined the *muḻavu*.
8. Like a hawk and its shadow, all instruments had to move in perfect correspondence. Instrument, song, and dance likewise had to agree.
9. Within this arrangement, Mādhavi danced the eleven forms according to the dramatic treatise, without error.
10. Without disturbing the measure of the *pālai* mode, she fitted words and music to each dramatic form so that song, percussion, and dance became one.
11. She performed all the regional dances in both the slow opening movement and the measured *vāram* movement.
12. She then completed the *Vaiśākha* posture.
13. Appearing like a golden creeper, she followed the treatise exactly, making gesture and emotion visible and never departing from the fourteen prescribed dramatic components.
14. Having performed triumphantly, she received the title marked by the *talaikkōl* and became an acknowledged debutante. The king rewarded her with a green garland valued at 1,008 *kalañcus* of gold.

This gives us the general nature of the performance.

So far we have briefly examined some of the artistic practices described in the *Canto of the Debut Performance*. A full explanation would grow enormously. Let us now glance very briefly at artistic details in other cantos.

## Arts in Other Cantos

### Kāṉal Vari

Mādhavi inspected the yāḻ, tested it by ear, plucked it to examine its tone, and then handed it to Kōvalaṉ. After he played, she played in turn.

### The Summer Canto

After Kōvalaṉ left her, Mādhavi sang the *Mēṟcempālai* mode by voice on the moonlit terrace, but the music faltered under emotion. She then took the *cakōṭa yāḻ* and played the modes called inner-state *marutam*, outer-state *marutam*, near-state *marutam*, and expanded *marutam*; again the music became confused by grief.

### The Canto of Resting in the Outlying Settlement

Kōvalaṉ played the yāḻ with the bards. His handling of the *ceṅkōṭṭu yāḻ* is described with particular distinction. Of the four principal types of yāḻ, he took the *ceṅkōṭṭu* and firmly fastened two of its six components, the *tantirikaram* and the *tivu*, before playing.

### The Canto of Seeing the City

Madurai possessed “two great streets of those skilled in eight times eight arts”—that is, streets of people accomplished in the sixty-four arts. It also had specialists in the arts associated with the nine gems.

### The Canto of Explanation

Astronomical knowledge existed in that age. It had been foretold that Madurai and its kingship would perish by fire on a Friday in the dark fortnight of the month of Āṭi, when the star Kārttikai coincided with the eighth lunar day:

> In the month of Āṭi, in the great dark fortnight,
> on the eighth day in the fiery asterism,
> on a Friday, the bright flame would consume it.  
> *(23:133–135)*

This passage belongs to the art of astronomy.

### The Canto of the Water Consecration

Here we encounter astrology. One evening, while Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ stood on the bank of the Ganges, he saw the crescent moon. At once the astrologer said that thirty-two months had passed since they left Vañji. Astronomy and astrology are closely related arts.

### The Canto of the Memorial Stone

After his victory, Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ offered his warriors a feast of yāḻ music performed by women minstrels. They embraced the small yāḻ with its curved stem, harmonious strings, and resonating body, and sang the *Cempālai* mode with *kural* as its base, then *Paṭumalaippālai* with *tuttam* as base, followed by *Cevvaḻippālai* and the lovely, sweet *Kuṟiñci* mode. This was a banquet for the ear.

When Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ sat with his queen Vēṇmāḷ on the moonlit palace terrace, a skilled actor named Cākkaiyaṉ delighted them by performing the dance called *Koṭṭic Cētam* or *Koṭukoṭṭi*. This dance was believed to have been performed by Śiva. Its marvel was that Śiva danced while holding Umā on the left half of his body: her anklets and shoulder ornaments did not tremble, her girdle made no sound, her breasts did not move, her earrings did not sway, and her hair did not loosen. Cākkaiyaṉ reproduced that feat.

When I think deeply about all these arts, my head spins. Remembering that I myself possess mastery in none of them makes fear and shame compete within me. When even understanding these subtleties is difficult, where is the question of mastering them? Some such artists still live today. They should be honoured properly. To those concerned only with filling their stomachs, and to money-making machines—human robots—what difference is there between art and an eggplant? Along with creating and developing new arts, people have a duty to protect the old arts from extinction.
