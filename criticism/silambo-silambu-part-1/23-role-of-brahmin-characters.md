# 23. The Role of the Brahmin Characters

## Brahmins

Four Brahmin characters appear in *Cilappatikāram*: one woman, Tēvanti, and three men—the Brahmin of Māṅkāṭu, Kōcikaṉ, and Māṭalaṉ.

A speaker who noticed this arrangement once criticised Iḷaṅkō for giving a great deal of room in his work to Aryanism—that is, to Brahmins. This criticism calls for an answer.

Members of the Brahmin community become hurt and angry when anyone calls them *pārppāṉ* or *pārppanar*. Those opposed to Brahminism say *pārppāṉ*; servants of Aryanism say “Brahmin”; self-styled moderates alternate between the two according to convenience. These people, caught between both positions, are pragmatists who know how to survive.

In the classical corpus, including the *Tolkāppiyam*, the words *pārppanar*, *pārppār*, *pārppāṉ*, and *pārppani* occur, scattered across more than seventy-five places. Not all the people so designated were Aryan Brahmins. Tamil Brahmins—Tamil *antaṇar*—existed in Tamilakam from ancient times. Shall we examine firm evidence for this?

In the chapter on the combination of the shortened *u* in the *Eḻuttatikāram* of the *Tolkāppiyam*, the following occurs within sūtra 77, beginning “uyirum puḷḷiyum”:

> “In the two domains of the higher and the non-rational classes,
> compounds of quality reveal all five genders.” (77:4–5)

The Brahmin commentator Nacciṉārkkiṉiyar explains it thus:

> “Consider such forms as *karum-pārppāṉ*, *karum-pārppani*, *karum-pārppār*, *karuṅ-kutirai*, and *karuṅ-kutiraikaḷ*. They respectively mean: a Brahmin man who is dark, a Brahmin woman who is dark, Brahmin people who are dark, a horse that is dark, and horses that are dark. Thus a compounded qualitative noun conveys all five genders.”

Those qualified here by the word “dark” are the *antaṇar* of Tamil Nadu. Those who conduct worship in Śaiva temples under the title *kurukkaḷ* are called Ādi-Śaiva Brahmins.

There is internal evidence within *Cilappatikāram* itself, in the “Canto of the Exposition,” that Tamil Brahmins existed:

> “Behold the firm and mighty Cēra with the long spear,
> who granted a heavenly dwelling to the Tamil Vedic Brahmin.” (23:63–64)

The expression “Tamil Vedic Brahmin” deserves notice here.

Among the four Śaiva preceptors, it is generally accepted that Cuntarar belonged to the Ādi-Śaiva Brahmins who performed temple worship. Nāvukkaracar was a Vēḷāḷa. The other two, Campantar and Māṇikkavācakar, were Tamil *antaṇar*. However many Vedic rites may have been attached to Campantar in the *Periyapurāṇam*, he was a Tamil *antaṇar*; Māṇikkavācakar was likewise. Do Brahmins descended from the Aryan line bear names such as Campantar, Ñāṉacampantar, Māṇikkavācakar, or Tiruvātavūrar? To my knowledge, they do not, though perhaps a very few may be found somewhere. Members of the Ādi-Śaiva Tamil *antaṇar* community called *kurukkaḷ* do sometimes bear the name Campantaṉ. Brahmins will not even turn toward the name Tirunāvukkaracar. Appūti Aṭikaḷ, who belonged to the *kurukkaḷ* lineage, named everything in his household—his possessions, his children, his water-shed, and all other rational and non-rational things—Tirunāvukkaracar.

It also appears that in ancient times Brahmins acted as companions and helpers to Tamils.

In the *Kaṟpiyal* section of the *Poruḷatikāram* of the *Tolkāppiyam*, the forms of speech proper to a Brahmin companion are given:

> “To speak of the state of love and of the state of the chariot;
> to reveal the hero’s unspoken intention;
> to interpret omens connected with cattle;
> to urge departure and to restrain departure—
> these and similar acts belong to the Brahmin.” (36)

The same section lists the intermediaries who may go from the hero to end the heroine’s estrangement:

> “The companion, the mother, the Brahmin, the confidant,
> the bard, the woman singer, the younger men, the guests,
> the actors, the *viṟali* dancers, the wise, and witnesses—
> these are said to be the properly constituted mediators.” (52)

One may recall here the traditional account in the *Periyapurāṇam* that an *antaṇar*—said to have been Śiva himself—went twice as messenger from Cuntarar to end Paravai Nācciyār’s estrangement.

A sūtra in the *Akattiṇaiyiyal* section of the *Nampi Akapporuḷ*, together with its old commentary, may also be compared:

> “To set forth the impermanence of youth, body, and wealth;
> to restrain departure and to consent to departure;
> all these and similar acts belong to the Vedic men.” (100)

The commentary says that the sūtra gathers together all the functions of the Brahmin companion among the two kinds of confidants. Everything beginning with youth belongs to him; “and similar acts” includes seeking access and the like.

In *Cilappatikāram*, the Brahmin woman Tēvanti was Kaṇṇaki’s friend. The *antaṇar* Kōcikaṉ knew Mādhavi’s household and therefore went to see her when she grieved after separation from Kōvalaṉ. Through him Mādhavi sent a letter to Kōvalaṉ. One must therefore not condemn all the Brahmins appearing in the epic as Aryan Brahmins.

Some practices followed by Aryan Brahmins and Tamil Brahmins may have resembled one another. One group may have adopted certain practices from the other. Both groups are entitled to the distinguished name *maṟaiyavar*, “people of sacred learning.” Let us now consider, one by one, those who occur in the epic.

## 1. Tēvanti

Although brief references to Tēvanti occur under other headings, we shall examine her somewhat more fully here. Tēvanti was Kaṇṇaki’s Brahmin friend.

A Brahmin woman named Mālati was nursing her co-wife’s child when the milk caught in its throat and the child died. Mālati lay in supplication at the temple of Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ, begging that the child be restored to life. At that moment a demoness called Iṭākiṉi swallowed the corpse. To remove Mālati’s grief, Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ appeared in the form of the child. Mālati returned the child to the co-wife. He grew into a young man, married Tēvanti, lived with her for eight years, and then vanished. The widowed Tēvanti thereafter remained attached to the temple of Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ. This is her story.

Some researchers astrologically divine that Tēvanti and her husband, Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ, had no bodily union. Only if one can believe that a deity came in human form and married Tēvanti can one also believe that the two had no bodily relation. This is simply a form of epic imagination.

One evening Tēvanti came and blessed Kaṇṇaki, saying, “May you regain your husband.” As though foretelling what was to happen later, Kaṇṇaki told Tēvanti of the evil dream she had seen. Tēvanti replied that it was the fruit of an old deed and advised her to bathe in the two pools called Sōmakuṇṭam and Sūriyakuṇṭam and worship at the temple of the god of love, by which she might regain her husband. Kaṇṇaki rejected the advice, saying that immersion in sacred pools and worship of that deity were not fitting for her.

Later, on learning that Kōvalaṉ had been killed, Tēvanti went to Madurai. From there she travelled with Aiyai to Kaṇṇaki’s temple in the Cēra country and lamented. Possessed by a deity, she uttered many revelations. She told the deified Kaṇṇaki that Kaṇṇaki’s mother and Kōvalaṉ’s mother had died, unable to bear their children’s deaths, and that both fathers and Mādhavi had embraced renunciation. She explained Maṇimēkalai’s renunciation to the Cēra king. Before Kaṇṇaki, Tēvanti alternated songs with the nurse and the intimate maid. Once possessed, she caused water to be sprinkled upon three little girls and revealed their previous births: one had been Kaṇṇaki’s mother, another Kōvalaṉ’s mother, and the third Mātari. After death, all three had been reborn as those girls and had come there. Later Kaṇṇaki possessed Tēvanti and made her speak of Iḷaṅkō’s renunciation.

### A Woman Priest

At the end, this Tēvanti was appointed priestess of Kaṇṇaki’s temple:

> “He ordered that the precincts of the Pattini shrine be duly arranged,
> and that the adornment of the daily festival be performed;
> he commanded that flowers, incense, and fragrant offerings
> be conducted by Tēvanti.” (30:151–154)

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ endowed land for the temple’s expenses, arranged for the daily festival worship, and appointed Tēvanti priestess to perform the regular rites—anointing with fragrant paste, offering flowers, and raising sweet-smelling incense.

The appointment was an intelligent and fitting act. Kaṇṇaki’s shrine was a temple of a female deity; Tēvanti had been Kaṇṇaki’s friend; she had long been accustomed to temple life at Pācaṇṭa Cāttaṉ’s shrine; as a widow she had no household responsibility or other occupation; she was a Brahmin woman; and on several occasions she had entered states of divine possession. In a country where even temples of goddesses are served by male priests, making Tēvanti a priestess was a new and revolutionary act.

## 2. The Brahmin of Māṅkāṭu

The epic does not disclose the Māṅkāṭu Brahmin’s personal name. Surely he must have had one. Perhaps he is a Vedic man who needs no name. He came from Māṅkāṭu in the Kuṭaku hill region of the Cēra country and, like Kōvūr Kiḻār or Ālattūr Kiḻār, is identified by his village. He is also identified by community, as in a name such as “the young gold-merchant of Uṟaiyūr.” This shows that people of the time could be known by their village, by their community, or by both. He was a Vaiṣṇava *antaṇar* with a strong desire to travel and see different places.

After crossing Uṟaiyūr, Kauṇti, Kōvalaṉ, and Kaṇṇaki rested in a young grove. The Māṅkāṭu Brahmin arrived there, praising and blessing the Pāṇḍya king and his country. Kōvalaṉ asked him to state his village and identity. The Brahmin replied: “I come from Māṅkāṭu in the Kuṭaku hills. My eyes urged me to set out and travel so that they might behold Araṅkaṉ reclining in divine sleep at Śrīraṅgam and Vēṅkaṭattāṉ enthroned at Tiruvēṅkaṭam.” When Kōvalaṉ asked the road to Madurai, he continued:

“It is pitiable that you have begun this journey in the fierce heat of late summer. If you cross the forest and mountain by this road, you will see three paths branching like Śiva’s trident. If you take the right-hand road, you must cross the Pāṇḍya king’s Little Hill. The middle road is easy, but a bewildering deity dwells along it, and you must escape its delusion.

“If you take the left road, you will reach the dark grove-hill of Viṣṇu. There is a cavern there. To enter it, think upon Viṣṇu’s feet and circumambulate the hill three times. A female deity will appear and question you; if you answer, she will open the entrance. After passing through several gateways, you will reach one with double doors. Another female deity will ask questions. If you answer suitably, she will show you three pools: Puṇṇiya Caravaṇam, Pavakāraṇi, and Iṭṭacitti. By bathing in Puṇṇiya Caravaṇam one learns the fivefold grammar; by bathing in Pavakāraṇi one perceives former births; by bathing in Iṭṭacitti one attains whatever one has desired.” He went on at length in this manner.

On hearing him, the Jain-affiliated Kauṇti rejected the Vaiṣṇava claims. “None of what you describe is necessary,” she said. “The fivefold grammar of which you speak can be learned from the scripture of our Arukaṉ. Former birth may be inferred from the present one. If one lives truthfully, one may attain the desired end without bathing in Iṭṭacitti.” With this reply she dismissed him.

It seems that Iḷaṅkō used the Māṅkāṭu Brahmin to advertise Vaiṣṇavism and then used Kauṇti Aṭikaḷ to brush it aside and promote Jainism.

## 3. Kōcikaṉ

Kōcikaṉ was an *antaṇar*. Because he belonged to the Kōcika lineage, he is also called Kaucikaṉ. His role in *Cilappatikāram* is small: he functions as a postman. Carrying the letter Mādhavi had entrusted to him for Kōvalaṉ, he travelled through the forest searching for him.

One morning Kōvalaṉ had gone beside a water source to perform his bodily necessities. Wearied by travel and unaware that Kōvalaṉ was nearby, Kaucikaṉ saw a withered *mādhavi* creeper and spoke to it in pity. Hearing him pronounce the word “Mādhavi,” Kōvalaṉ approached and asked for news. Kaucikaṉ said:

“Kōvalaṉ, after you left the city, your parents suffered grief beyond words. They sent people in many directions to find you, but without result. Hearing this from Vayantamālai, Mādhavi became overwhelmed with sorrow and lay confined to her bed. When I went to see her, she wrote this letter and asked me to find you and place it in your hands.” He then gave the letter to Kōvalaṉ. That is Kaucikaṉ’s role; he appears as a helper of Mādhavi’s household.

The poetic charm of the passage lies in this: *mādhavi* is also a name for the flowering creeper called *kurukkatti*, and that floral name had been given to the woman Mādhavi. Women are named after flowers—Centāmarai, Tillai, Tuḷaci, Alli, Kumutam, Ceṅkaḻunīr or Ceṅkēṇi, Marukkoḻuntu, and the Sanskrit-derived names Ampujam, Paṅkajam, Carōjā, Vaṉajā, Kamalam, and Mallikā. Some are called Mālati, Caṇpakam, or Kāñcanā, three names associated with flowers. Others bear Puṣpā, the Sanskrit word for flower. Today some women bear the pure Tamil name Malar. On the same basis the woman Mādhavi received the other name of the *kurukkatti* creeper.

Seeing the withered creeper, Kaucikaṉ spoke in sorrow, his heart withering like that of Vaṭalūr Rāmaliṅka Vaḷḷalār whenever he saw a withered crop. On hearing the word “Mādhavi,” Kōvalaṉ made contact with him.

But did Kaucikaṉ truly address the withered *kurukkatti* by the word *Mādhavi*? The matter is doubtful. May we not say that Iḷaṅkō himself created the double meaning and played upon it to polish the epic and heighten its flavour?

## 4. Māṭalaṉ

Māṭalaṉ enters *Cilappatikāram* midway through the work—after the first book, the Pukār Kāṇḍam, within the second book, the Madurai Kāṇḍam—and continues to act until the end of the third, the Vañji Kāṇḍam.

He was foremost among learned *antaṇar*, a native of Talaicceṅkāṭu near Pukār. He had travelled south, bathed in the Kumari river, circumambulated Mount Potiyil, visited Madurai, and finally come to rest in the grove-school where Kauṇti stayed with Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki. Kōvalaṉ saluted him and asked about his journey.

There Māṭalaṉ praised Kōvalaṉ by recounting incidents that established him as “a warrior of compassion,” “a wealth that never departs,” and “a noble protector of the destitute.” The credit for making Kōvalaṉ appear as the epic’s hero belongs to Māṭalaṉ.

“Kōvalaṉ, so far as I know, you have done only good in this birth; yet your coming here with Kaṇṇaki to suffer in this fashion must be the fruit of an evil deed from an earlier birth,” he said, consoling him. Since Māṭalaṉ came from Talaicceṅkāṭu near Pukār, he knew Kōvalaṉ’s good acts well. He must also have known that Kōvalaṉ had left his parents and Kaṇṇaki, become attached to Mādhavi, and lost his wealth. But he did not wish to recall these matters and break Kōvalaṉ’s spirit. Instead, he said, “You have done good,” and offered comfort. Māṭalaṉ was a wise man who acted according to circumstance and knew how to speak. He repeatedly used the doctrine of karma to support what he said. If belief in karma serves one valuable purpose, what can it be other than this capacity to create peace by offering consolation?

Māṭalaṉ and Kauṇti advised Kōvalaṉ that it was no longer proper to remain in Madurai’s outskirts. If the couple entered the city and joined members of their merchant community, they would be warmly received as the son and daughter-in-law of Mācāttuvāṉ. Māṭalaṉ thus also served as a noble guide who pointed toward a means of survival.

He did more than console and advise. He feared blame and possessed a strong sense of responsibility. Māṭalaṉ informed the mothers of Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki that their children had died. Both women, their hearts broken, gave up their lives. Grieving that they had died only because he had brought the news, Māṭalaṉ accepted responsibility and feared the stain of blame. To cleanse it, he travelled north, bathed in the Gaṅgā, and found peace.

After bathing in the Gaṅgā he met Cēraṉ Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ, who had defeated the northern kings and was encamped on the river’s southern bank. Having saluted him, Māṭalaṉ narrated the events in Madurai and other news:

“Mādhavi’s song in the *Kāṉal Vari* crushed the crowned heads of Kanaka and Vijaya. Kōvalaṉ separated from Mādhavi, travelled to Madurai with Kaṇṇaki, was falsely accused of theft, and died. Kaṇṇaki came to the border of your Cēra country and gave up her life. On learning this, Mātari, who had given her refuge, entered fire and died. I went to Pukār and reported these events. Hearing them, Mācāttuvāṉ became a Buddhist renunciant; Mānāykaṉ entered an Ājīvaka establishment and took up renunciation; the mothers of Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki died of broken hearts; Mādhavi and Maṇimēkalai renounced the world. In the Pāṇḍya country Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ and Kōpperuntēvi died. Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ’s younger brother Veṟṟivēl Ceḻiyaṉ sacrificed a thousand goldsmiths and now rules Madurai. The Cōḻa king in Pukār remains well.”

After hearing him, Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ presented Māṭalaṉ with fifty *tulām* of gold, equal to the king’s own bodily weight.

When Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ became enraged on hearing that the Pāṇḍya and Cōḻa kings had belittled his northern victory, Māṭalaṉ calmed him. He taught the impermanence of wealth, youth, and the body, and urged him to cease making war and instead perform a righteous sacrifice.

Māṭalaṉ sprinkled upon three little girls the water given by the divinely possessed Tēvanti. Their former births were revealed: two had been Kōvalaṉ’s mother and Kaṇṇaki’s mother; the third had been Mātari. Māṭalaṉ explained their identities to the Cēra king, after which the king entered the sacrificial hall with him.

When we consider Māṭalaṉ’s many functions, we are reminded of a passage from Shakespeare’s *As You Like It*:

> “All the world’s a stage,
> And all the men and women merely players;
> They have their exits and their entrances;
> And one man in his time plays many parts.”

The whole world is a theatre. Men and women are merely actors. They leave the stage in death and enter it in birth. One person plays many roles during a lifetime.

In keeping with this idea, Māṭalaṉ performs many parts in *Cilappatikāram*. Iḷaṅkō needed such a figure to connect the narrative’s actions and hold its structure together. He created and employed Māṭalaṉ appropriately for that purpose.

At the beginning of this chapter I noted the criticism that Iḷaṅkō had given excessive room in his work to Aryanism and Brahmins. Whether these Brahmin characters were historical participants in Kaṇṇaki’s story or were shaped by Iḷaṅkō in this form, everything becomes intelligible once we remember that Brahmins were suitable for the kinds of narrative functions assigned to them in the epic.

There is also a Brahmin-related story woven into the work that is not directly connected to its central plot—the story of Parācaraṉ, Vārttikaṉ, Takkiṇāmūrtti, and Kārttikai. Parācaraṉ, a Brahmin from the Cōḻa country, approached the Cēra king and received a great quantity of wealth as a gift. He passed this wealth to a little boy named Takkiṇāmūrtti, son of the Brahmin Vārttikaṉ in the Pāṇḍya country. Seeing the child’s exuberant display, people falsely accused his father of having stolen the riches and given them to his son. Vārttikaṉ was imprisoned.

His wife Kārttikai lamented and pleaded. When the Pāṇḍya king later discovered the truth, he not only released Vārttikaṉ but freed the other prisoners as well. This Brahmin story appears in the “Canto of the Exposition.”

It is narrated to Kaṇṇaki by the city-goddess Madurāpati in order to illustrate the greatness of the Pāṇḍya rulers.
