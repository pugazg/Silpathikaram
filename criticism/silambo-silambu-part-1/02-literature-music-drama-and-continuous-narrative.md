# 2. A Continuous Poem Containing Literature, Music, Drama, and Narrative

Because *Cilappatikāram* contains the three Tamil components—*iyal* (literature), *icai* (music), and *nāṭakam* (drama)—and because its subject matter, or story, proceeds continuously, the work is specially described as “a continuous poem of literature, music, drama, and narrative substance.”

The very word drama implies a connected story in which literature and music are combined with acting. Tamil is called *muttamil*, “threefold Tamil,” because it consists of literature, music, and drama. Since drama contains all three, drama alone may in a condensed sense be called *muttamil*. Let us nevertheless examine separately the places in *Cilappatikāram* where these three components appear.

## Literature

*Iyal* is the portion that communicates thought without music or acting. The *āciriyam*, *kali*, and *veṇpā* metres in *Cilampu* belong to this literary component. So do the construction of the story; the counsel and moral instruction spoken by Kavunti, the Jain ascetics, Māṭalaṉ, and others; the many emotional modes; principles of royal justice; Pattini worship; the establishment of the three truths; the vindication of justice; descriptive passages; information about countries, cities, and rulers; and the many lessons taught by the work.

For the category called *toṉmai*—“antiquity joined with explanatory prose”—mentioned by Tolkāppiyar, Nacciṉārkkiṉiyar cites *Cilappatikāram* as an example.

## Music

The Canto of the Dance Debut explains the qualifications of the music-master, the percussion master, the flute-master, and the yāḻ-master. Music also appears in Mādhavi’s performance in the moonlit courtyard in the Canto of Summer; the songs of the *Kāṉal Vari*; Kōvalaṉ’s singing with the minstrels in the Canto of the Halt Outside the City; the music of united lovers; the Hunter’s Song, the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*, and the Mountain *Kuravai*; Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s musical feast for his warriors in the Canto of the Memorial Stone; and, in the Canto of Benediction, the three women’s *ammāṉai* songs for the Cōḻa, ball songs for the Pāṇḍya, swing songs for the Cēra, and pestle songs praising all three dynasties. These and many other passages constitute the musical component.

## Drama

The very presence of literature and music is already a constituent of drama.

The story of this epic has been performed as street theatre, stage drama, and motion-picture drama. The Canto of the Dance Debut describes the construction of a stage. Its characters are well suited to drama. Kōvalaṉ is the hero; Kaṇṇaki is the heroine. Mādhavi functions as the woman who brings harm to Kaṇṇaki, and the goldsmith as the villain who destroys Kōvalaṉ.

Marriage, love, separation, sorrow, anger, courage, elopement, concubinage, Kavunti’s companionship, Mātari’s refuge, false accusation, murder, Kaṇṇaki’s heroic uprising, the burning of Madurai, the deaths of the Pāṇḍya and his queen, the worship of Kaṇṇaki, the renunciation of some characters, the deaths of others, and the Cēra’s war are all visual elements suited to drama.

The epic also possesses the dramatic flavour of preliminary signs that announce beforehand what is to come. Today this is praised as “direction.”

It is a fine dramatic step to praise in advance the integrity and excellence of rule of the very Pāṇḍya who will later have Kōvalaṉ killed. Other dramatic elements include the construction of many sections as alternating dialogue; repetition of the same line; and the insertion of songs and prose explanations at intervals.

The Canto of the Sea-Bath lists eleven dances: *koṭukoṭṭi*, *pāṇṭaraṅkam*, *alliyattokuti*, *mal*, *tuṭi*, *kuṭai*, *kuṭam*, *pēṭu*, *marakkāl*, *pāvai*, and *kaṭaiyam*.

The Canto of Summer names eight modes of dramatic enactment: *kaṇkūṭu vari*, *kāṇ vari*, *uḷ vari*, *puṟa vari*, *kiḷar vari*, *tērcci vari*, *kāṭci vari*, and *eṭuttukōḷ vari*.

Among the five landscapes, the agricultural *marutam* appears in the Canto in which the City Celebrates Indra’s Festival; the seashore *neytal* in the *Kāṉal Vari*; the arid *pālai* in the Hunter’s Song; the pastoral *mullai* in the Cowherd Women’s *Kuravai*; and the mountain *kuṟiñci* in the Mountain *Kuravai*.

## The Dramatic Acts

Forty years ago I read an English book titled *The Art of Play Writing*. As I read it, I found myself wondering: “*Cilappatikāram* is structured according to these very principles. Could someone perhaps have formulated this grammar after looking at *Cilappatikāram*?” Let that question rest.

Shakespeare’s plays have five acts. On that model, *Cilappatikāram* may be divided into five stages:

1. **Beginning:** From the marriage in the Canto of Auspicious Benediction through the *Kāṉal Vari*.
2. **Development:** From the *Kāṉal Vari* through the Canto of Refuge. One might also say that the turning point begins within this development.
3. **Climax:** The Canto of the Killing Ground, the sixteenth of the epic’s thirty cantos.
4. **Reversal:** From Kaṇṇaki’s transformation and the destruction of Madurai through her journey to the Cēra land.
5. **Conclusion:** From the descent of the gods and Kōvalaṉ to take Kaṇṇaki upward, through Kaṇṇaki worship, the Canto of Benediction, and the Canto of the Granted Boon.

Condensed further, these become three divisions: the beginning is the Pukār Book, the middle is the Madurai Book, and the conclusion is the Vañci Book.

In brief, the epic begins as comedy and ends as tragedy. Its tragic ending opens the ceremonial gateway to the Vañci Book.

## Repetition

Repeating exactly what has already been said is normally regarded as the fault called “repetition of what has been said.” Against this stands the grammatical maxim: “Even when what has been said is repeated, it is no fault if it produces another meaning.” I remember reading in *The Art of Play Writing* that repetition is a dramatic device used to create emphasis, correction, and intensity when emotion rises. *Cilappatikāram* possesses this feature.

Its use in the *Kāṉal Vari* is discussed under another heading. Let us now see how Kaṇṇaki employs it.

When Kaṇṇaki learns that her husband has been killed, she says in the Canto of Circling the City:

> “I shall see my beloved husband—this is one thing.  
> If I see my beloved husband,  
> I shall hear from his mouth words free of evil—this is one thing.  
> If I leave without hearing his faultless good words,  
> the southern king has done what causes unbearable grief—this is one thing.” (19:10–14)

And again:

> “Are there women? Are there women—  
> women who bear the suffering that befalls  
> the husbands they have taken?  
> Are there women? Are there women?  
> Are there worthy people? Are there worthy people—  
> worthy people who take up and raise a child that another bore?  
> Are there worthy people? Are there worthy people?  
> Is there a god? Is there a god  
> in Kūṭal, where the king escaped the edge of the sword?  
> Is there a god? Is there a god?” (51–59)

And:

> “She stood; she thought; tears fell from her long carp-like eyes.  
> She stood; she thought; she did not wipe the tears from her long carp-like eyes.” (73–74)

In the Canto of the Lawsuit, Kaṇṇaki addresses the gatekeeper of the Pāṇḍya palace:

> “Gatekeeper! Gatekeeper!  
> Gatekeeper of the ruler who has failed in kingly justice,  
> his wisdom emptied, his heart devoid of discernment!  
> Announce this:  
> ‘A woman stands at the last gate,  
> holding in her hand one of a matched pair of anklets,  
> a woman who has lost her husband.’  
> Announce it! Announce it!” (20:24–29)

The gatekeeper goes to the Pāṇḍya and reports:

> “A woman stands at the last gate,  
> holding in her hand an anklet of golden workmanship,  
> a woman who has lost her husband—  
> a woman who has lost her husband stands at the last gate.” (20:42–44)

Such repetition gives drama a distinctive beauty of sound. All these are dramatic features. Drama needs beauty of sound as well as beauty of word and meaning, does it not?

Moreover, just as an audience learns the events of a play through the speech of its actors, almost all the story in *Cilampu*, apart from descriptions of nature, is arranged so that we learn it through the speech of the characters.

There is another feature. Modern plays and films sometimes reveal events from the past in the middle or at the end of the drama. *Cilampu* contains the same arrangement. For example, Kōvalaṉ’s youthful acts of generosity, compassion, and courage are disclosed through Māṭalaṉ in the fifteenth canto, the Canto of Refuge, shortly before Kōvalaṉ’s death. These are all excellent dramatic devices.

It is therefore entirely fitting that *Cilappatikāram* should be crowned with the jewelled title: “the continuous epic poem of literature, music, drama, and narrative substance.”
