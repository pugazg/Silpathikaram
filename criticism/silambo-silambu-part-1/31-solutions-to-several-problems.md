# 31. Solutions to Several Problems

When a history is written as an epic—especially an ancient epic—we cannot expect events to be recorded year by year, month by month, day by day, and hour by hour as in formal history. An epic may mention only the rising and setting of sun and moon; broad seasons such as summer, rain, and cold; an occasional month; and shorter periods such as dawn, morning, noon, evening, night, and midnight. We must not treat an epic like a diary. With this principle in mind, let us approach *Cilappatikāram* and attempt to resolve several problems.

## 1. The Alleged Chronological Contradiction

Some scholars say that *Cilappatikāram* contains a chronological contradiction. The issue is as follows.

Kauṇti Aṭikaḷ, Kōvalaṉ, and Kaṇṇaki came to stay in the outskirts of Madurai. One morning Kōvalaṉ toured the city and returned to the outskirts, perhaps a little before noon. Kauṇti then entrusted Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ to the cowherd woman Mātari, who happened to pass that way.

Mātari led the couple to her dwelling, bathed and auspiciously adorned Kaṇṇaki, and—because Kōvalaṉ was a Jain lay-observer who ate before darkness—ordered that the materials required for cooking be supplied quickly. Kaṇṇaki prepared the meal, fed Kōvalaṉ, and gave him betel and areca nut.

After eating, Kōvalaṉ lamented his earlier wrongs to Kaṇṇaki, embraced her, concealed his tears, took one anklet, and went to Madurai’s market street to sell it. There he was falsely accused and killed. Some suppose that the death occurred that evening or around seven in the early night. The “Canto of the Place of Killing,” however, gives no explicit hour. Perhaps because he was a Jain layman who ate before darkness and went immediately to the market, some have inferred an evening death.

In the later “Canto of Circling the City,” Iḷaṅkō clearly marks an evening. The sun withdrew its rays and disappeared behind the great mountain; bewildering dusk arrived. At that evening hour, after others showed her the place, Kaṇṇaki saw Kōvalaṉ’s lifeless body. The poem says that in the morning she had taken the flower garland from his hair and worn it in her own; in the evening she embraced his bloodstained corpse and lamented:

> “The red-rayed sun contracted its beams
> and went to hide beyond the great mountain,
> filling the prosperous wide world with darkness.
> As the flower-vine woman cried aloud
> in the dim bewildering evening,
> the city raised a resounding roar.
>
> In the morning she had taken the garland
> from her lord’s dark, bee-thronged hair,
> placed it upon her flowing tresses, and embraced him;
> in the evening she saw him,
> blood from his wounds streaming over his body—
> the unbearable grief of seeing him
> who would never again see her.” (19:31–38)

Cīrāmulu Reṭṭiyār and Mu. Ku. Cakannātarācā argue that Iḷaṅkō makes Kōvalaṉ die in the evening in the “Canto of the Place of Killing” but then says in the “Canto of Circling the City” that Kaṇṇaki embraced him in the morning and saw his corpse that evening. They therefore identify a chronological error in the epic.

### Is There an Error in the Epic?

R. Vīrapattiraṉ, formerly professor of Tamil at the University College of Kerala University, discussed the matter in his book *The Faultless Cilampu*, written against Cakannātarācā’s *A Small Error in Cilampu*.

Vīrapattiraṉ states that Dr Va. Cu. Māṇikkam, Te. Po. Mīṉāṭcicuntaraṉ, and Ma. Po. Civajñāṉam accepted that a chronological contradiction existed. He also rejects Mo. Turai Araṅkaṉār’s claim that Kōvalaṉ’s killing and the *kuravai* dance occurred on the same day.

My own view is that Iḷaṅkō’s statement—that the woman who embraced her husband in the morning saw his corpse that very evening—is perfectly appropriate. It is surprising that Va. Cu. Mā., Te. Po. Mī., and Ma. Po. Ci. accepted the alleged contradiction and then tried to explain it away.

### An Invented Timetable

Vīrapattiraṉ imagines the following schedule:

- Kōvalaṉ returned from touring Madurai at noon on the first day—about fifteen *nāḻikai*, roughly twelve o’clock.
- Mātari took the couple under protection at twenty-two and a half *nāḻikai*, about three in the afternoon.
- Kōvalaṉ ate and left to sell the anklet at twenty-seven and a half *nāḻikai*, around five.
- He was killed at roughly thirty-two and a half *nāḻikai*, about seven in the evening.
- The *kuravai* was performed the next morning between about nine and eleven.
- Mātari bathed in the Vaigai, heard of Kōvalaṉ’s death on the way, and told Kaṇṇaki at noon of the second day.
- Kaṇṇaki saw the body at thirty *nāḻikai*, around six that evening.

### “The Hour of the Departing Sun’s Fading Ray”

Vīrapattiraṉ places Mātari’s taking the couple home at three in the afternoon. But Iḷaṅkō says that she led them away when the sun was setting:

> “With the mature maiden of young white teeth,
> at the hour when the departing sun’s ray was fading,
> as the cow seeking its calf raised its full cry...
> she passed through the entrance and entered her house.”
> (*Canto of Refuge*, 202–204, 218)

“The departing sun,” “the fading ray,” and “the cow seeking its calf” all indicate evening at sunset. That cannot be three in the afternoon. Vīrapattiraṉ’s timetable is clearly only an approximate construction.

He therefore makes Kōvalaṉ die on the first evening and Kaṇṇaki see the corpse on the second evening. The reasons he gives, with my objections, follow.

### Arguments and Replies

**Argument:** When Kōvalaṉ left to sell the anklet, a bull crossed his path as an evil omen. Cattle return from the herd only in the evening; therefore he departed on the first evening.

**Reply:** Might the cattle not have been leaving for pasture in the morning? Moreover, Mātari’s house is called “the house of the cowherds of many cattle”; at least one animal might have been moving between house and street. Near my own house in Puducherry, other people’s cattle can be seen from morning until evening.

**Argument:** The goldsmith came surrounded by many members of his profession. They may all have finished palace work and been returning home in the evening.

**Reply:** Why could they not have been setting out together for work in the morning?

**Argument:** Only at evening would they finish work and return as a group; in the morning they might go individually.

**Reply:** Must they all return as one group even in the evening? Would they not leave work separately for their respective homes? Did they all live in one place?

**Argument:** It is easier to kill someone after dark without others knowing.

**Reply:** Is royal punishment administered only at night? Could it not occur in daylight?

**Argument:** Kōvalaṉ needed money immediately and could tolerate no delay; therefore he left as soon as he ate on the first evening.

**Reply:** Could he not have waited one night and sold the anklet next morning? In a strange city, selling jewellery in daylight would be safer than selling it at night.

**Argument:** Because the killing occurred on the first day, evil omens appeared in the cowherd settlement the next morning.

**Reply:** Most traditions describe evil omens as appearing before the misfortune, not after it.

Vīrapattiraṉ’s claim that Kōvalaṉ went out and was killed on the first evening is therefore not convincing.

Both the anonymous early commentator and Aṭiyārkkunallār say that the killing occurred the next day. Aṭiyārkkunallār divides the “Canto of the Place of Killing” into two days: up to line 21, “Give them without delay,” belongs to the first day; from line 22 onward belongs to the next. After commenting on line 21, he writes: “Now the poet tells what occurred on the following day.”

Aṭiyārkkunallār says that after Mātari instructed the cowherd women to give the materials for cooking, she went to the palace to measure and deliver ghee, and the women did not provide the ingredients immediately but only the next day. Vīrapattiraṉ rightly asks: if so, did Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki fast through the entire first night? Would Mātari have left them like that? His objection here is correct.

The provisions must have been given on the first afternoon, and the couple ate that evening. But Vīrapattiraṉ, who says the killing occurred that first evening and the body was seen the next evening, interprets the opening lines of the quoted stanza to suit his theory. Since *kālai* can mean morning, time, or day, he takes it as “daytime” and says Kōvalaṉ died during the first day. Yet if *kālai* can mean daytime, why must it refer only to the first day and not the second? His argument does not hold.

### Sexual Union in Daylight?

Vīrapattiraṉ advances an even more comic suggestion. He says that after eating on the first evening and before leaving at five to sell the anklet, Kōvalaṉ had sexual intercourse with Kaṇṇaki. In the daze of union, he supposes, the garland upon Kōvalaṉ’s head slipped into Kaṇṇaki’s hair. Their union would therefore have occurred between four and five in the afternoon.

What an oddity! Must they unite in daylight? Might they not have intended to wait until Kōvalaṉ returned from the market and then unite at night? Would a flood carry away the water in the well? There is a proverb: “Like a starved cow leaping at the feeding-trough.” Was Kōvalaṉ such a starved cow? He had immersed himself in the ocean of desire and emerged exhausted.

Were Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki staying in a modern lodge? Today a man may bring an unrelated woman by day, rent a room in a lodge, and complete the affair for which they came. Their situation was nothing of the kind. On the road, a divine woman had come in the form of Vayantamālai and offered Kōvalaṉ an opportunity to be seduced, yet he rejected it. Must such a man unite by day? When both he and Kaṇṇaki were lying in grief, was daylight intercourse their urgent need? It is like a man asking another for a light for his cigarette while that other’s beard is burning. Should one spread a bed for them in daylight amid their misery? Night was available.

### The Times of the Meal and the Killing

Vīrapattiraṉ assigns Kōvalaṉ’s death to seven in the early night of the first day. According to Iḷaṅkō, however, seven would have been the time when he ate.

Mātari took the couple home at “the hour when the departing sun’s ray was fading.” Let us call that six in the evening. The provisions were quickly supplied; Kaṇṇaki cooked and fed Kōvalaṉ. That meal could reasonably have occurred around seven. How can the hour of eating be called the hour of death?

### “Red Eyes”

In the Indra-festival canto, Iḷaṅkō says “Kaṇṇaki’s dark eyes” (line 237); in the “Garland of Sorrow” he says that she “wept until her red eyes reddened” (line 33). Vīrapattiraṉ argues that the eyes were first red because of sexual intercourse and became still redder after Kōvalaṉ’s loss.

If intercourse at about 4:30 on the first afternoon made her eyes red, did that redness remain until the next evening? I do not know anyone in ordinary life whose eyes remain red for twenty-four hours after one act of union. In the “Canto of Seeing the Forest,” Iḷaṅkō describes a woman’s eyes as “rainy eyes with rich corners through which red lines flow” (line 184). Why should Kaṇṇaki’s “red eyes” not similarly mean eyes streaked with red?

Vīrapattiraṉ’s account therefore fails. What is the proper solution to “morning” and “evening”? A solution exists.

### The Proper Solution

The garland did not aim itself and fall into Kaṇṇaki’s hair in the daze of sexual union. Aṭiyārkkunallār and Vēṅkaṭacāmi Nāṭṭār both explain that Kaṇṇaki received it from Kōvalaṉ and wore it. Whether Kōvalaṉ actually gave her the garland, or whether one was even present that morning, is secondary. Could this not be an epic device used by Iḷaṅkō to create tragic feeling—to say that Kaṇṇaki sent her husband out in an auspicious morning setting and saw him as a corpse that evening?

If she embraced her husband in the morning and saw his body in the evening, the killing could have occurred around noon, between those times. Why then invent a death on the previous evening and a viewing on the following evening? Would the corpse have been left there for twenty-four hours? This was an official royal execution; the state would not have allowed the body to remain so long. The auspicious morning is a form of epic foreshadowing.

If he had meant “he died yesterday evening and she saw him this evening,” Iḷaṅkō should have written “yesterday” and “today” rather than “morning” and “evening.” Valluvar uses the explicit terms in:

> “This world possesses the greatness of saying:
> ‘One who existed yesterday does not exist today.’” (336)

### A List of Faults

After eating on the first evening, Kōvalaṉ lists his former failings to Kaṇṇaki. Among them he says:

> “I failed to obey the commands of my two aged elders.” (16:67)

That means that in Pukār his parents repeatedly rebuked and advised him, but he ignored them and did not reform. Iḷaṅkō had not previously narrated those parental admonitions. From this later line, we infer for ourselves that they occurred. In the same way, from the statement that one who left in the morning became a corpse by evening, we should infer that he was not killed on the first evening but around noon the next day.

### Epic Flavour

Why does Iḷaṅkō write continuously that, after the first evening’s meal and exchange of grief, Kōvalaṉ went to sell the anklet and was killed? He wishes the reader to feel the tragedy that the man who said, “I shall take one of your little-foot anklets, exchange it, and return,” never returned.

Had he written, “They ate in the evening, slept at night, and on the following morning Kōvalaṉ left to sell the anklet,” the epic flavour would have weakened and the urgency of pathos been blunted. Hence his narrative continuity.

### A Dramatic Device

This method must also be remembered as a dramatic device. Cinema often inserts into the middle of a scene something that happened earlier—either as a flashback or as an account told to others. A dream may similarly show in advance what will happen later.

The same principle applies to the dramatic epic *Cilappatikāram*. The fact revealed in the nineteenth canto—the “Canto of Circling the City”—that the killing occurred between morning and evening is anticipated in the sixteenth canto, the “Canto of the Place of Killing.” Like a dream that shows the future early, the event of the following day is narrated continuously after the events of the first evening. The structure should therefore be accepted as dramatic epic, not treated as a dated historical diary.

### A View to Be Rejected

Some give yet another account: the Pāṇḍya went to end the queen’s estrangement in the evening or early night; the goldsmith inflamed him and caused Kōvalaṉ to be killed; the king was then seated with the queen upon a bed when Kaṇṇaki came and pleaded her case; king and queen immediately fainted and died one after the other.

They cite the line:

> “He sat upon the *amaḷi* borne by lions.” (20:22)

They say *amaḷi* means a bed. But they ignore “borne by lions.” A seat supported by lions is the royal throne—a lion-seat—not a bed. This is therefore a view to be rejected, not accepted.

What should be learned from the discussion is this: Cīrāmulu Reṭṭiyār was wrong to identify a chronological error in *Cilappatikāram*; Cakannātarācā’s book *A Small Error in Cilampu* was unnecessary; and although Vīrapattiraṉ rightly argued that no chronological contradiction existed, the method he used to establish his conclusion was not appropriate.

Nevertheless, his effort through *The Faultless Cilampu* to uphold the epic’s excellence and Iḷaṅkō’s fame deserves praise. My thanks and respects to him.

## 2. Why Did Kōvalaṉ Go to Madurai?

The next problem concerns the reason for Kōvalaṉ’s journey to Madurai. Some say he feared that Mādhavi would somehow enchant him and draw him back; if he remained in Pukār, he might return to her, so he decided to leave immediately for Madurai. This is unconvincing.

Some modern film actresses stop acting after marriage. Some continue acting while living with their husbands. Some change husbands after several years of marriage. To which of these three types did Mādhavi most resemble?

For a time she behaved like the second. Even while living domestically with Kōvalaṉ, she performed song and dance at Pukār’s Indra festival and delighted everyone. Kōvalaṉ disliked this. The wound in his mind was then intensified, like oil poured upon fire, by her *Kāṉal Vari* song, which appeared to imply that she held another man in her heart. The truth is that Kōvalaṉ, deeply hurt, left Mādhavi and went to Kaṇṇaki.

### Water at the Bank and the Mirage

I remember a song from a film seen in youth, in which P. U. Chinnappa played Kōvalaṉ. As Kōvalaṉ walks away from Mādhavi toward Kaṇṇaki, he sings:

> “Like a deer that seeks a mirage
> while water lies beside the bank.”

The water beside the bank is Kaṇṇaki; the mirage is Mādhavi. The Kōvalaṉ of *Cilappatikāram* was in the same condition.

After he left, Mādhavi sent a letter through Vayantamālai asking him to return. He refused. On the road to Madurai, a divine woman took Vayantamālai’s form and tried to seduce him; he was not deceived. Mādhavi sent another letter through Kaucikaṉ; he still did not return. He therefore did not flee for Madurai in fear that Mādhavi would enchant him again. He went back to Kaṇṇaki with a firm mind.

Kaṇṇaki said, “There are anklets; take them.” From this we understand that she had already given away her other ornaments. Yet Kōvalaṉ did not take the anklet and return to Mādhavi as before. Having lost the wealth of the household, he was ashamed to face his parents and feared the ridicule of the townspeople. He therefore resolved to go to Madurai, earn wealth, and return. Without informing anyone, he took Kaṇṇaki and departed in darkness.

That is the true reason for his journey. Iḷaṅkō, like many others, developed the habit of blaming everything upon karma. Kōvalaṉ’s killing in Madurai was an unexpected and accidental event.

## 3. Were Gaṅgā and Kanni Vayantamālai?

The third problem concerns Kōvalaṉ and Vayantamālai.

### A Whole Book

The scholar Pāvalar Maṇi Ā. Paḻani wrote a book titled *Kāṉal Vari or a Line of Tears?* Its entire argument is that Kōvalaṉ had a sexual relationship with Vayantamālai, Mādhavi’s companion. One wonders whether a whole book was needed to say this.

The first two songs of the *Kāṉal Vari* have been subjected to extraordinary strain:

> “O Kāviri, live long! Though the king beneath the white umbrella,
> whose garland is the moon, wields his red sceptre
> and unites with Gaṅgā, do not grieve.
> O fish-eyed one, I know that your not grieving
> though he unites with Gaṅgā
> is the great chastity of women. Live long, Kāviri!”

> “O Kāviri, live long! Though the king beneath the enduring white umbrella,
> wielding the unbending red sceptre,
> unites with Kanni, do not grieve.
> O fish-eyed one, I know that your not grieving
> though he unites with Kanni
> is the great chastity of enduring women. Live long, Kāviri!”

The songs say that even if the Cōḻa king joins Gaṅgā in the north and Kanni—the Kumari river—in the south, Kāviri will neither grieve nor become estranged.

The other songs in the canto concern themes of Tamil love poetics and were discussed under “The Place of the Kāṉal Vari in the Epic.”

Te. Po. Mīṉāṭcicuntaraṉ says that Kōvalaṉ sang the first two songs with Kaṇṇaki in his mind: even if he joined Mādhavi or other women, Kaṇṇaki would not become estranged. It is also said that he wished to teach Mādhavi Kaṇṇaki’s lofty character.

But if Kaṇṇaki is metaphorically Kāviri and the songs say that she will not grieve even if Kōvalaṉ joins other women, they should be sung before Kaṇṇaki. What use is singing them before Mādhavi? Will the message ever reach Kaṇṇaki? Must her excellence be explained to Mādhavi by declaring, “I may unite with whomever I please”?

### A New Discovery

Ā. Paḻani offers a new interpretation of Kāviri, Gaṅgā, and Kanni. He says Kōvalaṉ also had sexual relations with Vayantamālai, which Mādhavi disliked. The meaning of Kōvalaṉ’s song, he argues, was: “Just as Kāviri does not become estranged when the Cōḻa joins Gaṅgā and Kanni, you, Mādhavi, should not become estranged when I unite with Vayantamālai.”

Other scholars have made related claims. Ku. Tirumēṉi writes in *Kōvalaṉ* that he lived without limits and had relations with people like Vayantamālai; this, he says, is why the forest deity assumed Vayantamālai’s form and offered a plausible reason for approaching him. S. Rāmakiruṭṭiṇaṉ writes in *Iḷaṅkō’s Characterisation* that Iḷaṅkō makes us infer an erotic relationship between Kōvalaṉ and Vayantamālai.

Drawing upon these views, Ā. Paḻani identifies Vayantamālai with both Gaṅgā and Kanni. His reasons, and my objections, follow.

### Arguments and Replies

**Reason 1:** Kōvalaṉ was intensely lustful, went astray in youth, spent time among courtesans, did not spare even Mādhavi’s attendants, and lingered in the courtesans’ streets while touring Madurai. The epic says:

> “Kōvalaṉ, accustomed to wandering
> with flute-mouthed bards and city courtesans.” (5:200–201)

and:

> “I joined the deceitful woman of corrupt conduct,
> lost the mountain of great wealth inherited from my lineage,
> and have brought upon myself the shame of poverty.” (9:69–71)

A man with such habits could have had relations with Vayantamālai.

**Reply:** Even if Kōvalaṉ indulged in love’s games, how can one say that he sang specifically with Vayantamālai in mind? Might he not have thought of other women, or sung generally without referring to anyone? Does imagining that he spent a long time in the courtesans’ streets establish his appetite? His “horoscope” was already known before that.

**Reason 2:** On the road to Madurai, a forest deity desiring Kōvalaṉ came in Vayantamālai’s form. She chose that form because Kōvalaṉ and Vayantamālai had an intimate relationship and she believed he would not reject her.

**Reply:** When a stranger seeks access to a distinguished person, it is common to introduce oneself by naming a relative, friend, or associate of that person and explaining the connection; sometimes one brings the known intermediary along. The divine woman’s attempt to approach Kōvalaṉ in the form of someone familiar is no more than this ordinary practice.

Even if Kōvalaṉ knew many women, the epic’s audience knows Vayantamālai by name. Iḷaṅkō may have selected her form because it gave the narrative recognisable flavour. The divine woman would know that if she came as Mādhavi—who had renounced him—Kōvalaṉ would reject her, and if she came as a completely unknown woman he might not dare unite with her.

**Reason 3:** The verse announcing the divine woman says:

> “The deity dwelling in the forest came through desire,
> thinking, ‘He will grant himself through the love he has cherished,’
> and appeared in the form of Vayantamālai.” (11:171–173)

The word *nayanta*, “cherished,” is a past participle. It proves, according to the argument, that Kōvalaṉ had previously desired and loved Vayantamālai; therefore she is the woman indicated in the *Kāṉal Vari*.

**Reply:** The early commentator glosses the phrase generally as “because he possesses cherished love.” Aṭiyārkkunallār says “through the love cherished for Mādhavi,” and Vēṅkaṭacāmi Nāṭṭār gives the same sense. None of these three glosses is necessary here.

Why not understand that the divine woman thought: “Kōvalaṉ will receive me lovingly—and indeed with deeply cherished love”? *Nayanta* can intensify and confirm the quality of the love she expects. It need not mean that he will accept her because he previously loved Vayantamālai.

The scene may simply be one among the epic’s many mythological inventions. If one accepts other divine episodes, why reject this one? It therefore does not follow that Kōvalaṉ sang with Vayantamālai in his heart.

### The Proper Solution

Vayantamālai is Mādhavi’s companion. In *Maṇimēkalai*, Cāttaṉār says that Mādhavi’s mother Cittirāpati sent Vayantamālai to Mādhavi. She also appears to have served as an attendant. Mādhavi’s sending a letter to Kōvalaṉ through her is well known.

If Mādhavi disliked a relationship between Kōvalaṉ and Vayantamālai, she could simply have removed or dismissed Vayantamālai. There was no need to allow matters to reach the point where Kōvalaṉ could sing, “As Kāviri does not become estranged when the Cōḻa joins Gaṅgā and Kanni, you, Mādhavi, must not become estranged when I unite with Vayantamālai.” Driving Vayantamālai away was not a task requiring an axe; it was as easy as pinching something off with a fingernail. The *Kāṉal Vari* therefore contains no place at all for Vayantamālai.

### A Habit of Men

Whom, then, did Kōvalaṉ hold in mind while singing? No one. He sang merely to tease Mādhavi, play a mischievous joke, and lightly threaten her. Men have such a habit. Consider one example.

A friend told his wife: “When I ride my scooter and see beautiful women, my scooter slows down.” He said it only to provoke and tease her playfully. Did his wife reply in equal fashion, “When I walk in the street and see handsome men, my legs slow down”? No, not at all. Many such examples could be given. Women of respectable households have not, until now, generally spoken like that—what the future holds, who knows?

Mādhavi, however, answered in rivalry by singing as though she too held another man in her heart, thereby displaying the inherited temperament of her courtesan community.

Her song was not true either. Her pure mind is evident from her renunciation after Kōvalaṉ’s death. We should therefore rest with the conclusion that the *Kāṉal Vari* heightened the epic’s flavour and, through an accidental chain of events, became a natural cause that eventually placed the Pattini stone upon the crowned heads of Kanaka and Vijaya.

The more deeply one examines *Cilappatikāram*, the more its epic flavour becomes sweet.

**O anklet, anklet that seizes the heart!**
