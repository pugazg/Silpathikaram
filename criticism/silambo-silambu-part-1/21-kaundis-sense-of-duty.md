# 21. Kauṇti Aṭikaḷ’s Sense of Duty

## A Companion

“Do not travel a long road without a companion,” says Ativīrarāma Pāṇḍiyaṉ’s *Veṟṟivēṟkai* (78). Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki secretly left Pukār in darkness and had to travel roughly thirty *kātams*—about three hundred miles—to Madurai. Their rare and invaluable companion was Kauṇti Aṭikaḷ.

## The Title *Aṭikaḷ*

Kauṇti was a Jain woman ascetic who lived at a place called Kauṇti’s monastery, one *kāvatam* west of Pukār. It was customary to call exalted men or women *Aṭikaḷ*. Though Kōvalaṉ had behaved badly, Kaṇṇaki regarded him as exalted and addressed him, “Aṭikaḷ, eat the ambrosia” (*Canto of the Killing Ground*, 43).

## Historical Fact or Literary Invention?

Was Kauṇti’s companionship a historical event that could truly have occurred, or a character invented by Iḷaṅkō to make the narrative move conveniently? The couple had barely crossed Pukār and the Kāviri and travelled one *kāvatam* when they met a guide who remained with them all the way to Madurai. Some may suspect that it looks too neatly arranged. One scholar has said that Iḷaṅkō made the companion a woman for Kaṇṇaki’s sake and an ascetic for Kōvalaṉ’s. Did he fear that, if the female Kauṇti were not a renunciant, Kōvalaṉ—who had acquired a “taste for women”—might try his hand with her too?

Yet the event is believable. Long-distance travellers naturally converse and join company with someone they meet on the road. The couple passed the place where Kauṇti stayed. She asked who they were, where they were going, and why. Kōvalaṉ told her. At first she objected: their aim was unsound, and the delicate woman could not cross the harsh forest road. Kōvalaṉ would not yield. Kauṇti then said, “Go, if you must. You say you are travelling to Madurai. I too have long wished to go there, hear the works of the great, and worship the Arhat. I shall come.” Thus all three proceeded together. We may reasonably believe that this occurred rather than being wholly imagined.

## Fitness of the Journey

A husband and wife alone on a very long road are not secure; a honeymoon is not the situation in question. To speak plainly, when a woman must withdraw along the road to relieve herself, Tamil custom does not consider a man the appropriate attendant; another woman should accompany her. Kauṇti met this need. Likewise, when a husband must go away briefly, he should leave his wife with a woman. *Cilampu* says that Kōvalaṉ left Kaṇṇaki with Kauṇti once to drink water and once to attend to his morning needs. Kauṇti was old and dignified enough for Kōvalaṉ to bow at her feet. The journey of the three was therefore appropriate, useful, and safe.

## Kauṇti’s Anger

While they rested in a grove, a degraded man and a courtesan mocked Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki. When Kauṇti said, “They are my children,” the pair asked whether brother and sister could become husband and wife. Enraged, Kauṇti cursed them into jackals. At the couple’s request, she later limited the curse and allowed their restoration.

Even if we do not literally believe in transformation into jackals, we may accept that Kauṇti became angry and fiercely rebuked them.

Some critics argue that such anger is incompatible with renunciation and that Kauṇti was not a true ascetic. Yet her title *Aṭikaḷ* is attached precisely because she was one. A solution may be found by reflecting on the *Tirukkuṟaḷ* chapter “The Greatness of Renunciants”:

> The anger of those who have climbed and stand
> upon the mountain called virtue
> is difficult to withstand even for a moment.  
> *(29)*

## Kauṇti’s Refutation

A Brahmin from Māṅkāṭu explained beliefs of his own tradition. Kauṇti replied by stating the Jain position and rejecting his claims. This shows that she possessed skill in argument.

## Kauṇti’s Persuasive Speech

> The world will swiftly listen and act
> if it obtains people capable of speaking
> in orderly and pleasing fashion.  
> (*Tirukkuṟaḷ* 648)

In the *Canto of Seeing the City*, Kauṇti speaks persuasively and consoles Kōvalaṉ. Bowing to her, he says: “I departed from right conduct, lost my wealth, and brought Kaṇṇaki in suffering to a strange city. Let her remain under the protection of your sacred feet while I enter Madurai, find members of my merchant community, and arrange our future. Will this burden you?”

Kauṇti answers: “Kōvalaṉ, all this is the fruit of karma. The great have made the mouth a drum and the tongue its beating-stick and proclaimed endless moral instruction. The learned perform good acts; those who slip from life’s path suffer the pain of separation, union, and Kāma. All is karma. Why grieve over what has passed? Many have suffered like you. Rāma searched the forest in anguish after losing Sītā. Naḷa gambled away his kingdom, entered the forest with his wife, and abandoned her there at night. Both suffered separation. You still have your wife beside you. Compared with Rāma and Naḷa, you are greatly fortunate. Do not grieve.” Thus she skilfully turned the comparison toward consolation.

Her persuasion appears again when Mātari, returning from an offering of milk to the local goddess, worships Kauṇti in the settlement outside Madurai. Kauṇti wants to send Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki into the city before dark but worries who will take them and where they will stay. She recognizes that Mātari, a cowherd woman whose harmless occupation is protecting cattle, is fit to receive persons seeking refuge.

She says: “Mātari, these two belong to the merchant community. Their names are Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki. Kōvalaṉ’s father Mā Cāttuvāṉ is immensely wealthy in Pukār and renowned throughout the country. The merchants of Madurai know him. If they learn that this is his son, they will rejoice as though finding a rare treasure, welcome the couple, and install them in a fine mansion. Until they reach the merchant quarter, I give them into your protection, woman of the pastoral community.”

> Mātari, listen. If the merchants of his clan
> hear the name of this maiden’s husband’s father,
> they will welcome them like people who have found rare treasure,
> receive him with the broad dark-eyed woman,
> and install them in a fragrant house.
> Until they enter the home of those possessors of great wealth,
> I have given them as refuge to you, woman of the herding clan.  
> *(125–130)*

Kauṇti’s choice is excellent. Her manner of naming Mā Cāttuvāṉ contains subtleties. Instead of pointing to Kōvalaṉ and saying “his father,” she points through Kaṇṇaki: “this woman’s husband’s father.” This indirectly affirms Kaṇṇaki’s primacy as heroine. Yet saying simply “this woman’s father-in-law” might diminish Kōvalaṉ, so the longer phrasing also introduces them explicitly as husband and wife.

In some families a child gains honour from the father and must be introduced as “so-and-so’s son.” In others a father gains honour from the child. Kōvalaṉ’s case is pitiable: he is identified both as Mā Cāttuvāṉ’s son and Kaṇṇaki’s husband. The couplets on a father making his son foremost in an assembly (67) and a son causing people to ask what penance his father performed to beget him (70) have little work here.

Kauṇti also creates prestige: “Do not think lightly of them. Once Madurai’s merchants know that Mā Cāttuvāṉ’s son and daughter-in-law have arrived, they will snatch them away in eagerness. I entrust such high-born people to you, a pastoral woman without considering high and low; this is a great opportunity for you.”

She further wins Mātari’s heart: “Bathe Kaṇṇaki, mark her forehead, adorn her with flowers and clean clothing, and become her companions, guard, and mother. Her feet have never touched earth, yet for her husband she has crossed the road in anguish. We have seen no deity other than this woman of chastity. Do you not know that in a land where such women live, rain does not fail, prosperity does not diminish, and the king’s victory and just sceptre do not falter?”

> Other than this deity who has undertaken
> the burden of chastity, we have seen no splendid god.
> In a country where chaste women live,
> the sky does not lie, prosperity does not fail,
> and the victory of the long-land’s kings does not decay.  
> *(143–147)*

Science cannot accept that rain and prosperity depend on chaste women. Kauṇti may be using hyperbole to win Mātari, like Vaḷḷuvar’s saying that rain falls when the husband-worshipping wife commands it. The epic’s introductory prose also makes the surprising claim.

## The Merit of Protecting Refugees

Kauṇti gives Mātari an example. In a grove near Kāvirippūmpaṭṭiṉam, Cāraṇa ascetics taught lay Jains. A celestial being appeared with one hand still resembling a black-fingered monkey’s. Asked for his history, an ascetic explained:

The wife of Cāyalaṉ, bearer of the title Eṭṭi, fed every visitor. One day she served an ascetic. A starving monkey ate his leftovers and drank the washing water, then gazed longingly at him. Moved by compassion, he entrusted it to her as though it were a child. She protected it well and, after its death, made gifts in its name. The monkey was reborn as the son of King Uttara Kauttaṉ in Vāraṇavāci of the Central Country. The prince made many gifts, died at thirty-two, and became the celestial being now present. Through the merit of protecting the monkey, Cāyalaṉ and his wife attained liberation.

“Understand from this the greatness of guarding one who seeks refuge. Now take Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ quickly to your house,” Kauṇti concludes.

Her association with them was not a fleeting “railway friendship.” Her responsible effort to protect companions met on the road deserves the highest praise.

## Kauṇti’s Religious Position

Kauṇti’s deity is the Arhat; she belongs to Jainism. One may say that Iḷaṅkō uses her to convey Jain teachings. Several internal signs support this.

When she learns their destination, she says that she has long wished to go to fault-free Madurai in the good southern Tamil land, hear the moral teaching of great Cāraṇas who have abandoned violent doctrine, and praise the Omniscient Arhat:

> Because I have long held a united desire
> to go to fault-free Madurai in the good southern Tamil country,
> hear the moral teaching of stainless learned ones
> who have abandoned doctrines of violence,
> and praise the Omniscient One,
> I too shall come.  
> *(Canto of Seeing the Country, 56–60)*

She carries a begging bowl, sling at the shoulder, and peacock-feather whisk:

> Kauṇti Aiyai took the faultless begging bowl,
> the sling upon her shoulder, and the peacock-feather whisk.  
> *(98–99)*

These are customary Jain ascetic implements.

When Cāraṇa ascetics teach in a grove, all three fall at their feet. The ascetic says that worshipping the Arhat ends rebirth and recites many names: Omniscient, Righteous, Beyond the Boundary of Knowledge, Self-controlled, Jina, Perfected One, Blessed One, First of Dharma, Leader, Dharma, Reality, Pure One, Ancient One, Scholar, Victor over Passion, Deity, Lord of the Auspicious Path, Supreme, Virtuous, Light Beyond, Knower of Truth, Holy One, Cāraṇa, Cause, Siddha, Great One, Noble One, Radiant Light, Lord, Teacher, Natural Virtue, Our King, Faultless Fame, Great King of Virtues, Śaṅkara, Īśa, Self-born, Four-faced, Source of the Scriptures, Arhat, Sage of Grace, Master of Music, Eight-qualitied One, Beginningless Ancient Reality, Celestial One, and First of the Veda.

Hearing this, Kauṇti raises joined hands above her head and vows: her ears will hear no speech but the Arhat’s; her tongue utter no names but his; her eyes see no feet but his; she will fall before no other; her hands join for no other; her head bear no other feet; her mind receive no other sacred utterance. Her Jain devotion is clear. She later repeats the Cāraṇa teaching to others.

When the Brahmin of Māṅkāṭu tells them that bathing in the pools Puṇṇiya Caravaṇam, Pavakāraṇi, and Iṭṭacitti grants various benefits, Kauṇti refutes him: one may know all needed truth from the Arhat’s ethical texts without entering Puṇṇiya Caravaṇam; one need not immerse in Pavakāraṇi to know former births; and the benefits of Iṭṭacitti can be gained by righteous living and compassionate protection of living beings.

Even when entrusting Kaṇṇaki to Mātari, she uses a story connected with Jain Cāraṇas.

## A Simple Difficulty

A minor contradiction is easily resolved. Kauṇti declares that body, senses, mind, and speech belong in worship only to the Arhat, yet calls Kaṇṇaki “the splendid deity” and says she has seen no other. The Arhat is an unseen deity; Kaṇṇaki is a visibly present one. The situation also matters: Kauṇti must exalt the woman she entrusts if Mātari is to receive her wholeheartedly. After many days of travel at perhaps one *kāvatam* a day, Kauṇti knew Kaṇṇaki’s character. Moreover, Iḷaṅkō, writing after Kaṇṇaki had been enshrined, places through Kauṇti’s mouth a hint of the divinity to come.

In ordinary speech, a fervent Vaiṣṇava or Śaiva may still say of a noble person, “He is a god,” or “That mother is a goddess.” This usage too should be remembered.

Finally, when Kauṇti learned of Kōvalaṉ’s death, she fasted until she died. Some still act so. She chose it as a renunciant; people today need not imitate her.
