## Locked dramatic controls

1. Madurai begins as an organised, maintained and desirable capital whose visible competence intensifies its later justice failure.
2. The gate, moat, wall, flags, guards, transport and registration systems function before the state’s procedural collapse.
3. Kaṇṇaki enters with an injured body and with the unopened anklet pair under her control.
4. Kōvalaṉ can read markets and craft systems, but he has no local merchant network, witness or protected introduction.
5. River, market, performance, ritual, animal, artisan and palace-service systems form one connected urban economy.
6. Kauṇṭi prioritises Kaṇṇaki’s body and shelter over Kōvalaṉ’s urgency to reach the market.
7. Mādhari’s household is a functioning pastoral institution, not a poverty symbol or generic maternal sanctuary.
8. Kauṇṭi’s handover is a practical transfer from ascetic route protection to household care.
9. Kaṇṇaki’s travel injury is cleaned and rested but not erased.
10. Kaṇṇaki remains materially and verbally authoritative about ownership and the anklet sale.
11. Cattle and dairy irregularities are first tested through ordinary work knowledge before becoming shared ritual concern.
12. The Kuravai is communal repair through coordinated performance, not decorative interruption or certain prophecy.
13. The Kuravai continues through market, accusation and execution and breaks only when the death news reaches `SCN-MDU-010`.
14. Kaṇṇaki gives one anklet to Kōvalaṉ for sale while retaining the other; both remain unopened.
15. Kōvalaṉ is the sale agent; Kaṇṇaki remains the owner.
16. Kōvalaṉ’s market vulnerability comes from missing local protection, not ignorance of commerce or craft.
17. The goldsmith converts specialised expertise and palace-linked credibility into false classification.
18. Other artisans are not implicated collectively.
19. “Thief” reaches the state before owner, sale purpose, witness, paired object, contents or place and time of theft are examined.
20. The royal order moves faster than inquiry and predetermines the officers’ action.
21. Kōvalaṉ attempts to state his name, Kaṇṇaki’s ownership, the paired anklet and the sale purpose, but the procedure does not receive his speech.
22. Kōvalaṉ’s death is wrongful state violence, not deserved karmic punishment for earlier relational failures.
23. The sale anklet enters state custody intact; its gem contents remain concealed until court.
24. Mādhavi’s flower-letter remains a separately tracked object on Kōvalaṉ’s body.
25. The death report breaks the Kuravai through failed response-song, rhythm and movement rather than through generic shock montage.
26. Kaṇṇaki’s first questions concern speech, witness, object inspection and authority; grief becomes political without disappearing.
27. The surviving anklet changes from household wealth into a visible civic claim while remaining unopened.
28. The sun/sky/city voice may dispute the theft label, but it cannot replace the later material test.
29. Madurai’s citizens respond unevenly through fear, sympathy, craft knowledge, withdrawal and witness; they do not become an instant organised protest.
30. Kōvalaṉ’s body remains the material result of failed royal procedure and must not be aestheticised as heroic martyrdom.
31. Kaṇṇaki’s grief retains the whole conflicted person she lost; defending him against the theft charge does not erase harm done to her or Mādhavi.
32. Missing answers about theft location, witnesses, the queen’s anklet, object opening, ownership and order expose the procedural failure.
33. Kaṇṇaki leaves the body temporarily because the sovereign must answer; this is chosen legal action, not emotional recovery.
34. The surviving anklet goes with Kaṇṇaki toward the palace; the sale anklet remains intact in state custody.
35. `SCN-MDU-014` hands directly into the queen’s warning dream before the palace gate receives Kaṇṇaki’s claim.
