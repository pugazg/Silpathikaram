## Locked dramatic controls

1. The Road begins as a direct continuation of Kaṇṇaki’s first uncertain step outside Pukār.
2. Terrain removes social confidence through feet, load, water and pace rather than generic danger spectacle.
3. Kāviri country is a working irrigation and agrarian system, not effortless pastoral background.
4. Distance is measured through halts, water, shade and bodily recovery.
5. Kaṇṇaki’s foot pressure develops gradually into redness, swelling, a small open injury and reduced walking capacity.
6. Kauṇṭi’s care is practical, observant and consent-aware; she does not become an omniscient oracle.
7. River-island teaching deepens ethical context but cannot excuse Madurai’s institutional failure as deserved fate.
8. River-crossing technology remains `RESEARCH_HOLD`; bodily exposure and loss of protection are locked.
9. Kaṇṇaki retains control of the anklet through the journey, and its contents remain concealed.
10. Route knowledge is oral, ecological, political and sacred rather than a modern neutral map.
11. Pālai is produced by season and water loss; it is not a permanent sand desert.
12. The cave route is a genuine sacred alternative and is not exposed as fraud.
13. Kauṇṭi listens before refusing and chooses the direct road because its risks can be more responsibly managed.
14. The forest deity uses Vasantamālai’s familiar form, but Mādhavi is not converted into a supernatural threat.
15. Kōvalaṉ’s cultural and musical knowledge helps him recognise and break the forest illusion; the exact protective mantra remains under research control.
16. Heat and Kaṇṇaki’s injured feet—not adventure pacing—force the halt at Aiyai’s enclosure.
17. The hunter community is shown through food, storage, tools, family life, care and ritual organisation rather than menace or exoticism.
18. Sālini’s recognition anticipates Kaṇṇaki’s future significance but does not replace her later material proof in Madurai.
19. Korravai/Aiyai practice retains source-specific religious identity and is not automatically assimilated into later standardised goddess imagery.
20. Moonlit travel expresses genuine trust in Pāṇḍya road order; the state is not visually pre-condemned before its procedural failure.
21. The outer religious settlement provides rule-based temporary safety and remains distinct from Mādhari’s later household refuge.
22. Kausika’s arrival now carries Pukār’s unresolved consequences and Mādhavi’s material letter into `SCN-ROAD-015`.
