# Relationship and Authority Architecture

## Purpose

This document maps how agency passes between characters and institutions. It prevents the adaptation from treating relationships only as romance or family connection. Across the epic, people exchange property, care, artistic meaning, accusation, evidence, testimony, authority and memory.

## Five narrative flows

### 1. Care flow

**Kauṇṭi → Kaṇṇaki → Mādhari → Dēvanti and later memory bearers**

Care changes form as the story moves:

- route protection and ethical decision;
- household shelter and food;
- ritual companionship;
- preservation of personal memory inside public worship.

Care repeatedly fails because institutions or circumstances exceed what individuals can protect against. That failure must not erase the labour already performed.

### 2. Desire, art and misreading flow

**Kōvalaṉ ↔ Mādhavi → letter → Kōvalaṉ’s correction**

Their relationship is mediated by:

- patronage and economic access;
- shared musical capacity;
- coded song;
- interpretive suspicion;
- material written communication.

The adaptation must preserve the difference between Mādhavi’s performed meaning, Kōvalaṉ’s interpretation and what the audience can know.

### 3. Accusation and evidence flow

**Goldsmith → royal authority → Kōvalaṉ’s execution → Kaṇṇaki → anklet proof → king’s self-indictment**

The decisive transformation is from untested expert assertion to publicly tested material evidence. The surviving anklet carries property history, marital trust, economic hope and legal proof.

### 4. Testimony flow

**Hill women and Kuravar → Sāttanār → Iḷaṅkō Vēṇmāḷ and Senguttuvan → Māṭalan → wider memory community**

No single narrator owns the whole story. Testimony travels through people with different access:

- local witness;
- narrative synthesis;
- policy judgement;
- mobile report of dispersed consequences;
- ritual lament and public remembrance.

### 5. Authority and memorialisation flow

**Kaṇṇaki’s proven claim → local sacred recognition → royal proposal → expedition and stone → ritual specialists and craftspeople → recurring Pattini worship**

The state enters late. It does not create Kaṇṇaki’s truth. It provides scale, durability, political framing and transregional circulation.

## Central relationship rules

### Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ

Marriage includes injury, shared history, property and attempted repair. Her defence of his innocence in the theft charge must not erase his harm to her or Mādhavi.

### Kaṇṇaki and Mādhavi

They need not meet directly. Parallel labour, waiting, material loss and social judgement can produce a powerful relationship architecture without invented confrontation.

### Kaṇṇaki and Kauṇṭi

Their bond grows through walking, protection, silence and bodily attention. Kauṇṭi recognises vulnerability before institutions recognise truth.

### Kaṇṇaki and the Pāṇḍya court

She enters with almost no institutional power but possesses the decisive object and knowledge. The court has rank, guards and procedure but has already failed its central duty.

### Kaṇṇaki and Senguttuvan

They do not form a conventional interpersonal relationship. He relates to testimony, memory and sacred status produced after her direct civic action. His monument must not replace her voice.

## Authority types

| Authority type | Principal holders | Dramatic test |
|---|---|---|
| Household and material knowledge | Kaṇṇaki, Mādhari | Can practical knowledge protect life and property? |
| Artistic authority | Mādhavi, teachers, musicians, Kōvalaṉ as participant | Can coded performance sustain shared meaning? |
| Ascetic and ethical authority | Kauṇṭi | Can disciplined judgement guide action without controlling fate? |
| Craft authority | Goldsmith and artisan systems | What happens when expertise is trusted without verification? |
| Royal authority | Pāṇḍya king, Senguttuvan, queens and councils | Does sovereignty investigate, listen and accept limits? |
| Ritual authority | Sālini’s community, Dēvanti, Madurāpati, shrine specialists | How does sacred explanation interact with human responsibility? |
| Testimonial authority | Kuravar, Sāttanār, Māṭalan, women memory bearers | Who witnessed, who synthesised and who transmits? |

## Scene-design requirements

1. Every major scene should identify which authority is active and whether it is trusted, contested or transformed.
2. Objects must move between relationships with visible changes in meaning.
3. Information must have a traceable carrier; avoid unexplained omniscience.
4. Care scenes must alter survival, access or vulnerability.
5. Testimony scenes must change policy, memory or public judgement.
6. Royal scenes must show institutional machinery, not only personal emotion.
7. Sacred causation must not erase material evidence or political responsibility.
