# Kaṇṇaki — Performance Dossier

## Core dramatic premise

Kaṇṇaki is not a passive ideal who suddenly becomes an avenging goddess. Her force accumulates through household knowledge, restraint, material sacrifice, travel, bodily exhaustion, grief, failed authority and her exact knowledge of the anklet.

**Performance arc:** contained attention → chosen endurance → public evidentiary speech → catastrophic moral command → silence transformed into memory.

## Character movement by world

### Pukār — controlled interior life

**[TEXT]** Kaṇṇaki belongs to a prosperous merchant household, enters a publicly celebrated marriage and later receives an independent domestic sphere.

**[INTERPRETATION]** Her stillness should read as trained social control and observation, not emptiness. In group scenes, she registers labour, money, kin expectations and Kōvalaṉ’s emotional distance before speaking about them.

**[CAUTION]** Do not invent a modern romantic courtship or make her silently omniscient about Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi.

### The road — endurance becomes visible

**[TEXT]** She chooses to accompany Kōvalaṉ, travels before dawn, crosses agrarian and forest routes, and suffers bodily strain.

**Performance:**

- Early journey: protected feet, controlled breathing, unfamiliarity with rough ground.
- Mid-journey: shortened stride, dust, heat, swelling and increasing dependence on practical pauses.
- Before Madurai: she is physically diminished but more decisive in gaze and response.

**[CAUTION]** Fatigue must not be used to make her weak-minded or childlike.

### Madurai — private knowledge becomes public law

Kaṇṇaki’s court power rests on exact, material knowledge. She knows what her anklet contains and forces the state to test its assumption.

**Voice progression:**

1. Low-volume domestic speech.
2. Sparse road dialogue shaped by fatigue.
3. Broken breath and repetition during grief.
4. Projected gate demand.
5. Controlled legal statement before the king.
6. Expansive public address after proof.
7. Reduced speech after destruction.

The strongest court performance is not continuous shouting. The gem’s impact and the king’s collapse should follow a line delivered with enough control that the object—not volume—completes her argument.

### Hill country and Vañci — others carry her story

**[TEXT]** Hill people first recognise and ritualise her; Kuravar transmit testimony; Sāttanār narrates the case; the queen proposes Pattini honour; specialists later construct the shrine.

**[INTERPRETATION]** After Madurai, Kaṇṇaki’s screen presence may become increasingly fragmentary—body, silhouette, remembered speech, anklet sound, testimony—because public memory is now being made by communities and rulers around her.

**[CAUTION]** Do not let Senguttuvan’s monument displace Kaṇṇaki as the source of the moral event.

## Performance grammar

### Gaze

- Pukār: observes before answering.
- Road: measures terrain and Kōvalaṉ’s condition.
- Madurai streets: seeks witnesses, not sympathy.
- Court: fixes attention on the king and the anklet.
- After proof: gaze widens from one ruler to the city’s moral order.

### Hands and objects

- Household objects: competent, economical handling.
- Anklet offered as capital: deliberate transfer, not impulsive sacrifice.
- Remaining anklet after execution: held as property, body-remnant and evidence.
- Court breaking: a prepared evidentiary act.

### Costume and body states

1. **Merchant-household Kaṇṇaki:** quality textiles and ornaments, worn without exhibitionist excess.
2. **Separated Kaṇṇaki:** subtraction of ornament and cosmetic preparation.
3. **Traveller Kaṇṇaki:** repeated garments, dust, damaged feet, reduced jewellery.
4. **Mādhari refuge:** temporary cleansing and domestic restoration.
5. **Widow-claimant:** disordered hair, dust, tears and one surviving anklet.
6. **Post-Madurai:** avoid polished goddess iconography until community ritual begins to create it.

## Relationship rules

### With Kōvalaṉ

Their relationship contains history, injury, material interdependence and a last attempt at repair. Her decision to travel does not erase abandonment; her defence of his innocence does not require idealising all his choices.

### With Mādhavi

Kaṇṇaki and Mādhavi should not be reduced to jealous rivals. The epic gives them different institutions, forms of labour and modes of suffering. Their cinematic relationship can be shaped through parallel action without inventing direct confrontation.

### With Kauṇṭi and Mādhari

Kauṇṭi protects the journey ethically and spatially; Mādhari restores temporary household shelter. These women form a chain of care that makes Kaṇṇaki’s later isolation more severe.

## Forbidden simplifications

- passive wife who has no agency until Kōvalaṉ dies;
- flawless supernatural being from her first appearance;
- revenge heroine who burns civilians indiscriminately;
- courtroom speaker whose power comes only from rage;
- symbol of chastity detached from property, evidence and failed investigation;
- sacred icon whose state-sponsored monument erases hill-community recognition.

## Locked adaptation decisions

- Her knowledge of the anklet must be established before Madurai.
- Travel must visibly transform her body.
- The court sequence must preserve gem-versus-pearl material proof.
- The fire sequence must retain the stated protected categories.
- Local hill recognition must precede Cēra monumentalisation.

## Open interpretive decisions

- The exact degree of prior awareness she has about Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi.
- Whether the warning dream is shown naturalistically, symbolically or through mixed performance form.
- How literally the post-Madurai ascent is visualised.
- How often the adult Kaṇṇaki is physically shown after testimony begins to replace direct presence.
