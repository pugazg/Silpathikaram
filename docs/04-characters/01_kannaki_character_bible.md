# Kaṇṇaki Character Bible

## Performance and screenwriting edition 0.1

**Status:** Working edition. Exact Tamil wording, social practice, chronology, costume and supernatural representation remain subject to review.

---

## 1. Dramatic identity

### One-line statement

Kaṇṇaki is a merchant-house woman whose disciplined private endurance becomes public evidentiary speech when the state kills her husband without investigation.

### Core movement

**Household formation → estrangement → chosen solidarity → dispossession → witness → accusation → destructive judgement → sacred memory**

### Central contradiction

She is celebrated for steadfastness, yet the story’s decisive act requires her to reject obedience to failed authority and make her anger visible to an entire city.

### Deeper need

To establish that fidelity does not mean silence before injustice and that private suffering can carry a claim against public power.

---

## 2. Evidence discipline

### [TEXT]

The committed translation presents Kaṇṇaki as:

- born to a wealthy sea-merchant lineage in Pukār;
- introduced with an explicit literary age statement;
- married to Kōvalaṉ;
- owner of the anklet taken toward Madurai for sale;
- the person who proves the royal error;
- the woman whose judgement destroys Madurai;
- the figure received by hill people and later memorialised.

### [CAUTION]

The character bible does not establish:

- a modern diagnosis;
- a historically exact numerical age for screen representation;
- that patience equals approval of Kōvalaṉ's conduct;
- that chastity means sexual ownership by society;
- that every later Pattini/Kannaki tradition can be projected into the epic's first narrative layer;
- or that supernatural power removes fear, fatigue, grief or moral ambiguity.

---

## 3. Casting and life stage

Use an adult performer capable of representing:

- early household reserve;
- emotional intelligence without constant dialogue;
- long-distance travel and physical depletion;
- grief that changes bodily organisation;
- exact, controlled public accusation;
- anger without loss of thought;
- and a final movement into sacred distance without becoming an empty icon.

### Actor capacities

- active silence;
- precise handling of props and jewellery;
- sustained vocal authority in the lawsuit sequence;
- credible intimacy and estrangement;
- physical endurance;
- ability to move between private and civic scale;
- emotional continuity through supernatural material.

---

## 4. Personality architecture

### Primary qualities

**Observant**  
She retains details, words, objects and changes in another person's conduct.

**Disciplined**  
Her restraint is an action, not the absence of feeling.

**Capable of chosen loyalty**  
Her journey with Kōvalaṉ must not be staged as automatic obedience. The film must locate deliberation, risk and agency in the decision.

**Materially intelligent**  
She understands household wealth, ornaments and what remains after loss. The anklet is not a decorative token to her.

**Legally exact under pressure**  
In Madurai, she transforms grief into a claim that can be tested through an object.

**Morally dangerous when institutions fail**  
Her truth exceeds the court's capacity to contain it.

### Shadow qualities for performance reconstruction

**[INTERPRETATION] Accumulated silence**  
She may delay speech until the pressure behind it becomes difficult for others to survive.

**[INTERPRETATION] Narrowed field under grief**  
After Kōvalaṉ's death, the distinction between guilty authority and inhabited city becomes a central ethical tension.

**[INTERPRETATION] Identity bound to repair**  
She may believe that staying, accompanying and preserving the anklet can repair a relationship already damaged beyond return.

### Prohibited simplifications

Do not play her as:

- a passive wife waiting for the plot;
- a flawless saint from her first scene;
- a jealous rival whose purpose is to defeat Mādhavi;
- an emotionless emblem of chastity;
- a superhero enjoying destruction;
- a modern courtroom lawyer using contemporary rhetoric;
- or a goddess whose human grief never matters.

## 5. Emotional operating system

### Love

Her love for Kōvalaṉ can include memory, anger, habit, bodily familiarity, duty, compassion and the wish to recover a shared life. It must not be reduced to unconditional endorsement.

### Anger

Early anger may appear through economy:

- refusal to decorate speech;
- controlled handling of objects;
- silence that denies reassurance;
- questions that expose evasion;
- maintaining distance.

After the execution, anger becomes public and evidentiary before it becomes destructive.

### Grief

Grief should develop through distinct stages:

1. inability to accept the report;
2. bodily recognition of death;
3. need to know how authority acted;
4. movement through public space;
5. conversion of loss into accusation;
6. collapse of ordinary social relation;
7. exhaustion after judgement.

### Shame and public gaze

She belongs to a known merchant world and travels after household collapse. Public gaze may carry pity, judgement, curiosity or disbelief. She should not be written as socially invisible before Madurai.

### Justice

Justice for her is first concrete: Kōvalaṉ did not steal the queen's anklet. Her proof is material. Wider moral and supernatural consequence follows the proof; it must not replace it.

---

## 6. Knowledge arc

### Pukār

She knows household relations, property, reputation and Kōvalaṉ's history more deeply than the audience initially does.

### Road

She learns the vulnerability created by distance, heat, dependence and loss of local standing.

### Madurai before the execution

She must trust intermediaries and a city she does not know. Her physical separation from Kōvalaṉ creates the information gap that makes the killing possible.

### After the execution

She reconstructs events quickly enough to identify the court as the site of truth. Her knowledge becomes public performance.

### After the burning

The divine explanation gives a larger causal frame but does not undo what she knows about false accusation and failed government.

### Hill country

Others now interpret her. The performance should preserve the difference between Kaṇṇaki's own exhaustion and the community's recognition of divinity.

---

## 7. Voice and dialogue

### Baseline voice

Precise, economical and socially aware.

### Voice arc

1. **Early household:** measured; meaning carried by what she does not elaborate.
2. **Reconciliation and departure:** direct questions, practical language, limited promises.
3. **Road:** shorter breath, shared decisions, attention to immediate conditions.
4. **News of death:** fragmented recognition becoming exact inquiry.
5. **City and court:** increasing public amplitude without generic declamation.
6. **After judgement:** speech stripped of social accommodation.
7. **Hill country:** minimal, exhausted self-account.

### Dialogue prohibitions

Do not give her:

- contemporary empowerment slogans;
- constant aphorisms;
- romantic forgiveness speeches that erase conflict;
- legal vocabulary unsupported by the world;
- enjoyment of civilian suffering;
- or certainty about metaphysical causation before the divine explanation.

---

## 8. Body and movement

### Pukār

- controlled household posture;
- trained stillness rather than dancerly display;
- familiar movement among stored goods and ornaments;
- bodily awareness of Kōvalaṉ before verbal reconciliation.

### Road

- reduced jewellery and changing load;
- altered gait and foot condition;
- care for the anklet as weight and remaining resource;
- fatigue that cannot be reset between scenes.

### Madurai

- waiting and constrained movement before news;
- destabilised body on encountering death;
- deliberate reorganisation before entering public space;
- court movement governed by evidence and refusal to submit.

### Burning and aftermath

The body is not a clean VFX source. Breath, heat, injury, ash and depletion must remain perceptible. Sacred transformation should not erase the human cost already carried.

---

## 9. Costume and material continuity

### Pukār household phase

- merchant-house quality without using later bridal costume as default;
- ornament set must establish the paired anklets clearly but unobtrusively;
- changes in dress should register household rupture.

### Journey

- practical reduction and reconfiguration;
- dust, sweat, repair and repeated use;
- anklet wrapping must be consistent and secure;
- no instant generic ascetic styling.

### Madurai

- she enters as a vulnerable traveller, not a pre-formed goddess;
- after bereavement, alterations should arise from action and damage rather than a symbolic costume change alone.

### Hill country

Her appearance must preserve continuity with the burned city. The local community's sacred interpretation should not require an unexplained new divine costume.

---

## 10. Relationship map

### Kōvalaṉ

Love, injury, shared history, anger, chosen companionship and final injustice. The performance must allow contradictory feelings at once.

### Mādhavi

Kaṇṇaki and Mādhavi must not be organised as a simplistic good-wife/bad-woman pair. Even where they do not share extensive direct scenes, editing and production structure should reveal how both are constrained by Kōvalaṉ's choices and by different social institutions.

### Kauṇṭi

Potential moral witness, practical companion and spiritual interlocutor. Avoid making Kauṇṭi a device who orders Kaṇṇaki's every choice.

### The Pāṇḍya king

He first exists as distant authority; then as the person whose order killed Kōvalaṉ; finally as a ruler confronted by proof. Kaṇṇaki's speech must address both individual and office.

### The people of Madurai

They are not one guilty body. The adaptation must decide how Kaṇṇaki perceives the relation between ruler, city and inhabitants.

### Hill community

They grant a form of reception that the failed court could not. Their recognition also begins the process by which her life is no longer solely her own story.

---

## 11. Scene objectives

### Reconciliation scene

Objective: determine whether Kōvalaṉ is speaking truthfully and whether a shared future remains possible.

### Departure

Objective: convert remaining property and loyalty into a deliberate risk.

### Waiting in Madurai

Objective: maintain trust under dependence while sensing that control has been lost.

### Encounter with Kōvalaṉ's body

Objective: know what happened and resist social pressure to collapse.

### City-circling movement

Objective: make the injustice publicly audible and reach the source of authority.

### Lawsuit

Objective: force the court to test its claim against the paired material evidence.

### Burning

Objective: act from a state in which ordinary legal repair has become impossible; the production must preserve ethical tension rather than announce uncomplicated victory.

### Hill-country encounter

Objective: state the minimum truth of what happened while others construct sacred meaning around her.
