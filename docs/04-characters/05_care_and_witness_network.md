# Care and Witness Network — Mādhari, Dēvanti and Sālini

## Purpose

These characters form institutions of care, ritual interpretation and memory around Kaṇṇaki. They are not decorative helpers. Each changes what protection, recognition or testimony is possible.

## Mādhari — pastoral refuge and transferred care

### Textual function

- **[TEXT]** Kauṇṭi entrusts Kaṇṇaki to Mādhari when ascetic companionship is no longer sufficient protection.
- **[TEXT]** Mādhari offers a functioning household: food, women’s care, local reputation and pastoral shelter.
- **[CROSS]** Later Vañci testimony reconnects Mādhari’s loss of refuge and death to the Madurai catastrophe.

**Evidence:** HWT-0098, HWT-0099, HWT-0187, HWT-0195.

### Performance rule

Mādhari should assess need before expressing affection. She notices dust, damaged feet, hunger, clothing and the social danger of an unprotected young woman. Hospitality is organised labour, not instant sentiment.

### Relationship function

- Kauṇṭi → Mādhari: deliberate transfer from ascetic protection to household protection.
- Mādhari → Kaṇṇaki: temporary restoration of domestic safety immediately before its destruction.

### Caution

Do not reduce Mādhari to rustic comic relief, an uncomplicated foster mother or a symbol of timeless village goodness. Her care operates within a specific pastoral community and carries real responsibility.

## Dēvanti — ritual intermediary and memory bearer

### Textual function

- **[TEXT]** In Pukār Canto 9, Dēvanti visits Kaṇṇaki, offers blessing and proposes a ritual route for recovering union; Kaṇṇaki refuses it as unfitting.
- **[TEXT]** In Vañci, Dēvanti arrives as one of the women who carries Kaṇṇaki’s earlier life into the Pattini shrine.
- **[TEXT]** She becomes a medium through whom past lives, grief and divine speech enter the final ritual world.

**Evidence:** HWT-0054 as sacred-network context; HWT-0195 and HWT-0196 for Vañci memory and cult participation. The early Dēvanti episode must also retain its direct canto anchor in `pukaar_kaandam.md`, Canto 9.

### Performance rule

Dēvanti’s ritual confidence should coexist with social familiarity. She is neither an alien priestess nor a casual friend. Her speech can be warm, practised and institutionally grounded.

### Relationship function

- Dēvanti → Kaṇṇaki: offers one possible ritual response that Kaṇṇaki actively rejects.
- Dēvanti → Pattini shrine: carries personal memory into public worship.
- Divine speech → Dēvanti: transforms her body into a contested channel of revelation.

### Caution

Do not make her an omniscient oracle, comic believer or exposition device. Kaṇṇaki’s refusal must remain a legitimate act of judgement, not proof that Dēvanti is foolish.

## Sālini — possessed speaker within a hunter community

### Textual function

- **[TEXT]** Sālini’s possession identifies an unpaid ritual obligation affecting the hunter settlement.
- **[TEXT]** Her speech recognises Kaṇṇaki as an exceptional southern woman before Madurai publicly knows her.
- **[TEXT]** The recognition occurs inside a full Korravai ritual system of debt, weapons, animals, music, offerings and communal performance.

**Evidence:** HWT-0077, HWT-0078, HWT-0079, HWT-0080.

### Performance rule

Possession must emerge from community rhythm, breath, sound, obligation and collective attention. Sālini’s ordinary body and social position should be established before the altered state.

### Relationship function

- Hunter community → Sālini: gives ritual context and authority to the possessed utterance.
- Sālini → Kaṇṇaki: anticipatory recognition without making Kaṇṇaki already conscious of divine status.

### Caution

Do not stage the sequence as exotic frenzy detached from social organisation. Sālini is not a free-floating prophet, and her utterance must not replace the later legal proof in Madurai.

## Network-wide adaptation rules

1. Care must be shown as labour, shelter, food, route choice and social protection.
2. Ritual recognition must not be confused with legal evidence.
3. Women’s institutions must remain distinct: ascetic, pastoral, domestic, performative and cultic.
4. Later sacred memory must not erase the earlier vulnerability of the living women.
5. Dēvanti and Sālini must be differentiated in voice, ritual setting and authority.
