# Pukār Visual World Dossier

## Design thesis

Pukār is a city of circulation: river and sea, imported and local goods, merchant wealth, artistic labour, festival crowds and private households are linked through movement.

The city must feel fully functional before Kōvalaṉ and Kaṇṇaki leave it. Its later absence should register as the loss of a lived economic and emotional world, not merely a change of location.

## Source control

- **Primary anchors:** HWT-0002–HWT-0060
- **Scene architecture:** `SCN-PUK-002`–`SCN-PUK-024`
- **Status:** mixed `LOCKED_SOURCE`, `HISTORICAL_RESEARCH` and `DEVELOPMENT`

## 1. City structure

### River–sea system

- **LOCKED_SOURCE:** Pukār is organised through maritime exchange, river movement, waterfront activity and coastal performance space.
- **Visual interpretation:** Water is repeatedly visible or audible through boats, wet goods, river mouths, beach wind, salt, ropes and changing light.
- **Production implication:** Harbour, riverfront, inland merchant streets and beach zones must connect through credible working routes.
- **CAUTION:** Do not build a generic stone capital detached from water and trade.

### Commercial differentiation

- **LOCKED_SOURCE:** Merchant households, specialised goods, market circulation and public exchange define the city.
- **Visual interpretation:** Different streets should be legible by goods, storage methods, labour rhythms, smell and sound rather than modern signage.
- **HISTORICAL_RESEARCH:** Warehousing, weighing, ledgers, coin or barter practice, imported containers and maritime loading systems.
- **CAUTION:** Avoid treating abundance as indiscriminate gold decoration.

## 2. Merchant household world

- **Primary anchors:** HWT-0029–HWT-0035
- **LOCKED_SOURCE:** Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ belong to established merchant families and begin married life in an independently functioning household.
- **Visual interpretation:** The house should support receiving guests, food preparation, storage, textile care, valuables and domestic ritual without resembling a royal palace.
- **Production implication:** Build zones for public reception, household work, private rooms and secure property storage.
- **DEVELOPMENT:** Wealth is shown through quality, scale of provisioning and skilled objects—not constant display.

## 3. Mādhavi’s artistic institution

- **Primary anchors:** HWT-0036–HWT-0041
- **LOCKED_SOURCE:** Training, stage construction, consecration, musicianship, dance repertory, costume preparation and royal recognition are institutional processes.
- **Visual interpretation:** Rehearsal rooms, instrument maintenance, costume storage and teacher–performer hierarchies must exist before the debut performance.
- **Production implication:** The performance complex requires backstage access, musician positions, audience hierarchy and safe movement geometry.
- **CAUTION:** Mādhavi’s world must never appear as a decorative pleasure quarter without discipline, labour and expertise.

## 4. Indra festival city

- **Primary anchors:** HWT-0002–HWT-0024
- **LOCKED_SOURCE:** The festival activates streets, civic forums, sacred sites, performance systems and multiple communities.
- **Visual interpretation:** Festival design should reveal the city’s geography by moving through it rather than concentrating everything in one temple courtyard.
- **Production implication:** Reusable banners, temporary structures, processional routes, crowd-control systems, lamps, food distribution and performance stations.
- **CAUTION:** Keep the Indra festival source-specific and historically situated. Do not automatically assimilate it to later standardised Hindu festival imagery.

## 5. Beach and Kāṉal Vari world

- **Primary anchors:** HWT-0025–HWT-0028, HWT-0045–HWT-0049
- **LOCKED_SOURCE:** Dawn, shoreline ecology, pavilion intimacy, yāḻ performance and sung landscape carry the emotional rupture.
- **Visual interpretation:** The beach is socially occupied and sensorially dense, but the pavilion creates temporary acoustic intimacy.
- **Production implication:** Wind continuity, sand movement, textile control, water horizon, instrument recording and changing dawn light are essential.
- **CAUTION:** Do not isolate the songs from the working coast or turn misunderstanding into a purely private dialogue scene.

## 6. Material and colour logic

### Working palette

- river silt, wet timber, rope fibre and salt at the harbour;
- dyed textiles, flower colour, polished wood and metal in artistic and merchant spaces;
- oil-lamp warmth in interiors;
- pale dawn, sea glare and wind-shifted fabrics at the beach.

These are **DEVELOPMENT** directions, not claims that the source specifies a fixed colour script.

## 7. Social differentiation

Costume, spatial access and object handling should distinguish:

- merchant households;
- artists and teachers;
- sailors and port labourers;
- civic and ritual specialists;
- festival crowds;
- palace-linked authority.

Difference must come from work, access, materials and behaviour—not caricature.

## 8. Required research queue

1. Early historic Tamil coastal settlement and harbour archaeology.
2. Boat, rope, sail, container and loading technologies.
3. Merchant-house storage and reception architecture.
4. Early Tamil performance-stage terminology and construction.
5. Yāḻ variants and performance posture.
6. Textile dyes, flower use, metalwork and jewellery manufacture.
7. Festival crowd logistics without importing later temple-car conventions.

## 9. Visual continuity locks

- Pukār must feel abundant before loss.
- The anklet begins as secured household wealth.
- Mādhavi’s costumes change through professional performance logic.
- Kōvalaṉ’s material condition visibly contracts before departure.
- Kaṇṇaki’s travel clothing must emerge from the same household world rather than appear as a new symbolic costume.
