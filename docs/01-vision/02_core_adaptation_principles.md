# Core Adaptation Principles

## Source-governed edition 0.1

**Project:** *Silapathikaram* source-traceable cinematic adaptation archive  
**Status:** Binding foundation for research and development  
**Primary source:** The original Tamil text of all three kāṇṭams—Pukār, Madurai and Vañji.

---

## 1. Primary-source rule

The original Tamil text of *Silapathikaram* is the controlling source for every major narrative, character, visual, musical, philosophical and production decision.

The working source set is:

1. **Pukār Kāṇṭam** — cantos 1–10;
2. **Madurai Kāṇṭam** — cantos 11–23;
3. **Vañji Kāṇṭam** — cantos 24–30, together with the concluding sections.

English translations, traditional commentaries, modern criticism, historical research and comparative texts may clarify the Tamil text, but they must not silently replace it.

Every later evidence record must identify:

- kāṇṭam;
- canto;
- Tamil passage or line group;
- evidence type;
- verification status;
- and any interpretive change made for cinema.

Where editions differ, the disagreement must be recorded rather than hidden.

---

## 2. Preserve the epic’s declared threefold moral design

The prologue states that the work will establish three truths:

> **“அரசியல் பிழைத்தோர்க்கு அறம்கூற்று ஆவதூஉம்  
> உரைசால் பத்தினிக்கு உயர்ந்தோர் ஏத்தலும்  
> ஊழ்வினை உருத்துவந்து ஊட்டும் என்பதூஉம்”**

The adaptation must preserve all three:

1. **Failed government is answerable to moral law.**
2. **Kaṇṇaki’s truth and steadfastness receive public recognition.**
3. **Past action and causation shape events.**

No one principle may erase the others. In particular:

- fate must not excuse administrative failure;
- political failure must not erase personal responsibility;
- praise of Kaṇṇaki must not reduce her to a passive symbol;
- and supernatural causation must not replace visible human choices.

---

## 3. Treat the three kāṇṭams as one indivisible causal work

The prologue calls the epic **“முடிகெழு வேந்தர் மூவர்க்கும் உரியது”**—a work belonging to all three crowned rulers.

Therefore:

- Pukār is not merely a romantic prelude;
- Madurai is not the whole story by itself;
- Vañji is not a detachable ceremonial epilogue.

The film must preserve the movement:

**household and city → art and estrangement → loss and migration → failed justice → public truth → destruction → communal recognition → royal memorialisation.**

Condensation is permitted, but the causal dependence of the three kāṇṭams must remain legible.

---

## 4. Preserve the epic’s mixed dramatic form

*Silapathikaram* identifies itself as **“உரையிடை இட்ட பாட்டுடைச் செய்யுள்”**—a continuous composition in which prose, verse and song are interwoven.

The cinematic adaptation must therefore treat the following as narrative forms, not decoration:

- spoken narrative;
- lyric verse;
- staged performance;
- dance;
- instrumental music;
- communal song;
- ritual speech;
- lament;
- legal argument;
- landscape description;
- and public proclamation.

A song or dance may reveal character, create misunderstanding, transmit political memory, change a relationship or move the plot. It must not be inserted merely to interrupt the drama.

---

## 5. Keep the anklet as the material and evidentiary spine

The anklet is simultaneously:

- intimate property;
- remaining household wealth;
- a saleable object;
- a falsely identified royal ornament;
- the cause of Kōvalaṉ’s execution;
- legal evidence in Kaṇṇaki’s case;
- a sound-and-material distinction between gems and pearls;
- and the object that gives the epic its name.

The Madurai hearing turns on the Tamil text’s physical proof:

> **“என்காற் பொற்சிலம்பு மணியுடை அரியே”**  
> contrasted with the queen’s pearl-filled anklet.

When Kaṇṇaki breaks the anklet, the gem itself establishes the truth.

Production, screenplay, sound and continuity teams must maintain an auditable anklet dossier covering:

- the pair of anklets;
- ownership;
- material construction;
- contents;
- sound;
- handling;
- transfer;
- mistaken identification;
- breakage;
- and evidentiary meaning.

The anklet must never become a generic magical object.

---

## 6. Build Pukār as a functioning world, not a backdrop

The Tamil Pukār Kāṇṭam constructs the city through:

- merchant households;
- maritime and riverine life;
- markets and wealth;
- artisans and labour;
- public festivals;
- courtesan and performance institutions;
- music and dance training;
- religious and civic spaces;
- fishing communities;
- streets, gardens, shore and harbour;
- and differences between public celebration and private grief.

The adaptation must show that personal choices occur inside an economic and cultural system.

Kōvalaṉ’s loss of wealth, Mādhavi’s artistic profession and Kaṇṇaki’s household position must be connected to this material city. Pukār’s beauty cannot be separated from the labour and institutions that sustain it.

---

## 7. Let art cause action

The *Kāṉal Vari* demonstrates that performance is not secondary to plot. Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi sing through literary voices, interpret each other’s songs, misread intention and separate.

Therefore:

- performance must carry point of view;
- poetic indirection must remain dramatically active;
- music may reveal what ordinary speech conceals;
- artistic excellence must coexist with emotional error;
- and audience interpretation must be allowed to remain complex.

Mādhavi must not be reduced to a seductress, and Kōvalaṉ must not be presented as a man mechanically enchanted by spectacle. Their bond, conflict and separation must arise through art, status, desire, pride, interpretation and incompatible obligations.

---

## 8. Preserve character complexity and uneven knowledge

No principal character begins with complete moral or factual knowledge.

### Kaṇṇaki

She must not be reduced to silent obedience followed by sudden supernatural rage. Her observation, endurance, property, speech, legal reasoning, grief, anger and transformation form a continuous arc.

### Kōvalaṉ

He must not be reduced either to an innocent saint or to a simple betrayer. His desire, artistic attachment, financial loss, misjudgment, return, shame, hope and vulnerability remain dramatically connected.

### Mādhavi

She must be treated as a trained artist with labour, reputation, intelligence, emotional agency, economic position and obligations—not as the moral opposite of Kaṇṇaki.

### Rulers and institutions

Royal authority must be shown as dependent on information, procedure, officials and judgment. A king’s personal virtue cannot repair a decision made without inquiry.

Interpretive psychology may be developed only when it does not contradict the Tamil text and is labelled as interpretation.

---

## 9. Make journey, landscape and bodily cost part of causation

The movement from Pukār to Madurai is not a transition montage. The Tamil text gives sustained attention to:

- changing ecological regions;
- heat, thirst and fatigue;
- roads and route choices;
- cultivated land and wilderness;
- settlements and social groups;
- sacred places;
- hospitality and danger;
- and the body under travel.

Landscape must influence pace, behaviour, costume condition, access to food and water, emotional state and social encounter.

The five-fold Tamil landscape tradition may inform design and sound, but it must emerge from the specific Tamil passages rather than from a generic textbook diagram.

---

## 10. Centre Madurai on failed investigation and public proof

Kaṇṇaki addresses the king as:

> **“தேரா மன்னா”** — king who failed to examine.

Kōvalaṉ is killed before a proper inquiry. The political catastrophe begins with:

- the goldsmith’s self-protecting accusation;
- official acceptance of an untested claim;
- the king’s order;
- enforcement without verification;
- and the silencing of the accused.

The court sequence must therefore be staged as a conflict between authority and evidence, not merely as an emotional confrontation.

Kaṇṇaki’s identity statement, the history of the anklet, the distinction between gem and pearl, the production of the royal anklet and the breaking of her anklet must unfold with procedural clarity.

The adaptation must not invent a fair trial before the execution or soften the state’s responsibility.

---

## 11. Portray Kaṇṇaki’s anger without turning destruction into spectacle alone

The burning of Madurai is sacred, moral, political and catastrophic. It must not be filmed as uncomplicated triumph.

The Tamil text explicitly distinguishes those to be spared:

> **“பார்ப்பார் அறவோர் பசு பத்தினிப் பெண்டிர்  
> மூத்தோர் குழவி”**

and directs the fire toward those associated with wrongdoing.

The adaptation must preserve:

- Kaṇṇaki’s grief and accusation;
- the city’s institutional guilt;
- the stated limits placed upon the fire;
- the suffering of an inhabited city;
- the difference between justice and vengeance;
- and the irreversible consequence of failed rule.

Visual scale must never erase individual lives, animals, homes, workers and neighbourhoods.

---

## 12. Preserve plurality of communities, beliefs and voices

The Tamil text contains royal, mercantile, ascetic, Brahmanical, Jain, local, pastoral, hunting, fishing, artistic and hill-community worlds.

The adaptation must not flatten this plurality into:

- one uniform religious identity;
- a modern sectarian conflict;
- one architectural style;
- one spoken register;
- or one social viewpoint.

Distinct communities require source-based treatment of:

- language and address;
- dress and ornament;
- occupation;
- ritual;
- music;
- food;
- space;
- authority;
- and relationship to the land.

No later religious label or iconographic system may be projected backward without evidence.

---

## 13. Give communities agency in Kaṇṇaki’s transformation into a deity

In Vañji Kāṇṭam, the hill people encounter Kaṇṇaki, hear her account and first declare:

> **“தெய்வங் கொள்ளுமின் சிறுகுடி யீரே”**

Their recognition, song, drum, horn, incense, flower offering and protective ritual are not background folklore. They are the first communal act of sacred memory in the kāṇṭam.

Therefore:

- Kaṇṇaki’s deification must not begin only with a king;
- hill communities must not be reduced to picturesque witnesses;
- their ritual intelligence and collective decision must be shown;
- and local sacred recognition must precede royal monumentalisation.

---

## 14. Treat Vañji as the politics of memory

The Cēra sequence transforms testimony into a state project through:

- hearing the hill people;
- deciding to honour Kaṇṇaki;
- selecting and obtaining stone;
- military action and royal prestige;
- water consecration;
- memorial installation;
- ritual recognition;
- and the circulation of her story across kingdoms.

The adaptation must hold together two truths:

1. the memorial preserves and enlarges Kaṇṇaki’s public significance;
2. royal power also organises, frames and claims memory.

Vañji must therefore examine commemoration, legitimacy, interstate power, sacred authority and the conversion of lived suffering into enduring political culture.

---

## 15. Preserve unresolved tension between moral law, fate and human responsibility

The Tamil text permits several explanatory systems to coexist:

- **அறம்** — moral order;
- **அரசியல் பிழை** — failure of rule;
- **ஊழ்வினை** — ripened past action;
- personal choice;
- testimony and evidence;
- divine agency;
- communal memory;
- and royal action.

The film must not force these into one simple answer.

No character may use fate to cancel accountability. No modern rationalisation may erase the epic’s sacred dimension. No supernatural explanation may make human suffering emotionally weightless.

The audience should be able to experience the tension rather than receive a single explanatory lecture.

---

## 16. Cinema may transform form, but not falsify source

The adaptation may:

- condense repetition;
- combine minor functions carefully;
- reorder within a clearly justified dramatic unit;
- create visual transitions;
- develop controlled subtext;
- and render interior experience through cinematic means.

It may not:

- reverse the outcome of a Tamil-source event;
- invent evidence that changes moral responsibility;
- erase a kāṇṭam because it is difficult to film;
- convert song and ritual into decorative montage;
- modernise a character’s ideology without labelling the interpretation;
- or present a disputed reconstruction as textual fact.

Every substantive departure must enter the adaptation decision log with its Tamil source, rationale, risk and required review.

---

## Binding summary

All future work must satisfy five tests:

1. **Tamil-source test** — Can the choice be traced to an original Tamil passage or explicitly labelled interpretation?
2. **Three-kāṇṭam test** — Does it preserve the causal unity of Pukār, Madurai and Vañji?
3. **Moral-design test** — Does it retain political accountability, Kaṇṇaki’s public truth and the operation of causation without allowing one to erase the others?
4. **Dramatic-form test** — Does it respect the epic’s integration of speech, song, performance, landscape, ritual and legal argument?
5. **Cinematic-honesty test** — Does the change create effective cinema without falsely claiming that invention is present in the Tamil text?

These principles govern all subsequent research dossiers, evidence registers, character bibles, scene design, screenplay drafts, storyboards, production design and scholarly review.
