# Historical Timeline and Political Context

## Source-governed production-research edition 0.1

**Primary authority:** the original Tamil text of the Pukār, Madurai and Vañji kāṇṭams.  
**External support:** archaeology, inscriptions and official excavation publications.  
**Status:** historical framework for research, screenplay, performance and production design—not a claim that the epic can be assigned to one exact calendar year.

---

## 1. Chronology rule: use a layered historical world

*Cilappatikāram* must not be marketed or designed as though every event, institution, object and religious practice belongs to one securely proven year.

The adaptation will work with four chronological layers.

### Layer A — inherited cultural continuities

The epic preserves practices and images that may draw upon long continuities extending from Iron Age and megalithic societies into the early historic period:

- memorial practice;
- warrior prestige;
- clan and lineage identities;
- ecological knowledge;
- exchange between plains, coast and hills;
- portable ritual, music and oral performance.

**Production rule:** these continuities may inform social depth, but no archaeological object should be labelled automatically as a direct illustration of the poem.

### Layer B — early historic Tamilakam

The strongest external comparison belongs broadly to the centuries from approximately the third century BCE through the third or fourth century CE. This period includes:

- Tamil-Brahmi writing on caves, pottery, coins, seals and rings;
- maritime exchange across the Indian Ocean;
- urban and craft settlements;
- Chola, Pāṇḍya, Cēra and chiefly political formations;
- Jain ascetic establishments alongside other sacred traditions;
- imported and locally produced prestige goods;
- merchant mobility and long-distance routes.

This is a **research envelope**, not the declared date of the story.

### Layer C — literary composition and redaction

The date and formation of *Cilappatikāram* remain debated. The poem may preserve earlier social memories while also reflecting later literary organisation, theological development and retrospective political imagination.

**Production rule:** do not use a disputed composition date as though it fixes every costume, building or institution in the film.

### Layer D — cinematic reconstruction

The production world should be a disciplined composite:

- every major element begins with the Tamil text;
- external evidence defines what is historically possible or probable;
- unresolved gaps are labelled **[INTERPRETATION]**;
- later medieval visual forms are excluded unless independently justified;
- a single exact production year will not be advertised.

---

## 2. The epic’s political map

The prologue calls the work **“முடிகெழு வேந்தர் மூவர்க்கும் உரியது”**—belonging to the three crowned rulers.

This establishes a political world organised around the Cōḻa, Pāṇḍya and Cēra realms, but the adaptation must not portray them as three modern nation-states with fixed borders, uniform administrations and continuous territorial control.

The working political model is:

- royal capitals and ceremonial centres;
- ports, markets and revenue-producing settlements;
- local chiefs and lineage authorities;
- merchant households and mobile trading groups;
- craft specialists;
- military retinues and officers;
- agrarian and pastoral communities;
- ascetic establishments and religious institutions;
- hill communities with their own ritual and social authority.

Political power is therefore **networked, personal, redistributive and performative**. It is made visible through protection, tribute, gifts, festivals, justice, military capacity, public abundance and the ruler’s reputation.

---

## 3. Kingship: the straight sceptre and its failure

The Tamil text repeatedly imagines good rule through the **செங்கோல்**—the straight or just sceptre.

Kingship is associated with:

- protection of subjects;
- correct hearing and judgement;
- prosperity and food supply;
- rain and ecological order;
- generosity and public ritual;
- military defence;
- the ruler’s personal accountability for wrongful state action.

The Madurai catastrophe begins when accusation is converted into execution without investigation. The text states that the previously unbending sceptre has bent.

### Adaptation consequence

The Pāṇḍya king must not be played merely as an individually cruel tyrant. The central failure is more disturbing:

1. a court-connected specialist makes a claim;
2. authority accepts identification without testing it;
3. a royal command moves rapidly through officers and armed men;
4. Kōvalaṉ is killed;
5. Kaṇṇaki introduces material evidence only after the state has made correction impossible.

The tragedy demonstrates that a political order praising justice can still destroy an innocent person when procedure collapses.

## 4. Pukār: Chola port, household wealth and civic performance

### [TEXT]

Pukār is presented as:

- a Chola city at the meeting of Kāviri wealth and the sea;
- a centre of merchants and movable riches;
- a city of distinct streets, occupations and populations;
- a performance capital in which Mādhavi’s debut is a civic event;
- a festival city containing multiple sacred institutions;
- a place where local residents and people arriving through maritime exchange coexist.

The text’s Pukār is not background scenery. Its economy enables the marriage, wealth, patronage, artistic life and eventual loss that drive the first kāṇṭam.

### [HISTORY]

The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology identifies Poompuhar at the Kāviri–sea confluence and reports excavations at Kizharveli and Dharmakulam. Brick walls, wooden poles and a water-oriented structure have been interpreted as possible wharf remains, with the reported structure placed around the fourth century CE.

Underwater investigations have also recorded offshore structural remains, while the Department’s marine museum preserves finds from multiple periods.

### [CAUTION]

- The excavated wharf does not prove the exact harbour described by the poem.
- Offshore remains belong to more than one chronological horizon.
- Roman or Mediterranean material does not establish a foreign colony.
- Later Poompuhar museum objects must not all be placed into one epic-era set.

### Production model

Pukār should operate through visible systems:

- river craft and seagoing vessels;
- loading, storage, weighing and brokerage;
- market flags and occupational streets;
- imported goods beside inland produce;
- merchants, sailors, interpreters and labourers;
- rehearsal spaces and performance specialists;
- wealthy households whose assets include jewellery, textiles, grain, credit and social obligation.

## 5. Indian Ocean trade without a “Roman-city” simplification

### External evidence band

Official excavation summaries from Alagankulam, Karur, Kodumanal and Poompuhar document combinations of:

- amphora fragments;
- rouletted ware;
- coins;
- beads and semi-precious stones;
- Tamil-Brahmi inscriptions on pottery;
- craft debris and iron objects;
- brick structures and water systems.

Alagankulam, near the Vaigai and the sea, has produced Mediterranean-region pottery, Tamil-Brahmi sherds, beads and coins. Karur has produced Tamil-Brahmi pottery alongside amphorae and rouletted ware. Kodumanal demonstrates intensive bead and iron-working traditions within inland exchange networks.

### Historical interpretation

These finds support a world connected through maritime and inland trade. They do not mean that all imported goods moved directly from Rome to one Tamil port, nor that every rouletted sherd was imported.

### Production rule

Foreign connection should be expressed through:

- goods, measures, packaging and currencies;
- unfamiliar languages and mediation;
- different clothing and seafaring practices only when supported;
- mixed markets rather than a segregated “Roman quarter” invented for spectacle;
- inland routes linking ports to craft and political centres.

Trade must remain a Tamil social system engaging with a wider ocean, not a foreign civilisation arriving to animate an otherwise static world.

## 6. Madurai and the Pāṇḍya political landscape

### [TEXT]

Madurai is represented as:

- an old and prestigious Pāṇḍya capital;
- a city of markets, craftsmen, neighbourhoods and sacred spaces;
- a court whose authority can reach quickly into the street;
- a city where royal failure has consequences for the entire civic body.

### [HISTORY]

The Vaigai region contains multiple early historic evidence zones rather than one excavated model of the epic’s Madurai.

- Mangulam inscriptions near Madurai mention a Pāṇḍya name, ascetic beds and mercantile connections.
- The Mangulam/Ovamalai landscape documents Jain ascetic presence and patronage.
- Keeladi, southeast of Madurai near the Vaigai, has produced brick structures, floors, ring wells, writing, craft objects, beads, weaving tools and evidence of an extensive settlement.
- Alagankulam demonstrates that the Vaigai system also connected inland political and agrarian zones with maritime trade.

### [CAUTION]

Keeladi is not the palace or street plan of *Cilappatikāram*’s Madurai. It is comparative evidence for urbanism, craft, water management, literacy and material life in the broader Vaigai region.

### Production model

Madurai should be built as an interdependent civic system:

- outer approach and pastoral refuge;
- occupational and market streets;
- goldsmithing and elite ornament circulation;
- guards and message routes;
- palace thresholds and controlled access;
- water points, residences, animals and religious institutions;
- densely inhabited spaces that make the fire a human catastrophe.

---

## 7. The Cēra world, Vañji and Karur

### [TEXT]

The Vañji kāṇṭam presents a Cēra political world of:

- royal mobility;
- hill tribute and knowledge;
- ministers, learned advisers, envoys and military forces;
- interstate rivalry and prestige;
- stone acquisition and transport;
- water consecration;
- monumental installation;
- ritual and diplomatic circulation of Kaṇṇaki’s memory.

### [HISTORY]

Karur is an important comparative centre for the Cēra world. Official excavation summaries report Tamil-Brahmi sherds, amphorae, rouletted ware, precious stones, coins, bricks and iron objects. The nearby Pugalur inscriptions record three generations of Cēra rulers and a donation associated with a Jain ascetic.

### [CAUTION]

The identification of Vañji with Karur is historically influential but must remain an argued hypothesis within the project. The poem’s political geography, literary Vañji, excavated Karur and later traditions must not be collapsed silently into one exact map.

### Production model

The Cēra world should be differentiated from Pukār and Madurai through:

- river and inland-route connectivity;
- access to western hills and high-value forest products;
- mobile royal camps and tribute encounters;
- craft and exchange networks;
- political theatre surrounding the memorial expedition;
- the conversion of oral testimony into state-sponsored sacred memory.

## 8. Religion: plurality rather than a single official faith

The three kāṇṭams contain or evoke:

- Indra festival traditions;
- Śiva, Murukaṉ, Viṣṇu and Balarāma-related worship;
- local and landscape deities;
- possession and protective rites;
- Jain ascetics and ethical discourse;
- Brahmanical ritual specialists;
- hill-community worship;
- divine appearances and the eventual Pattini cult.

External inscriptional evidence from Mangulam, Pugalur, Jambai and other Tamil-Brahmi sites confirms that ascetic establishments, rulers, merchants and donors interacted across the early historic landscape.

### Production rules

- Do not give every sacred space a later monumental temple form.
- Distinguish household worship, street shrines, groves, caves, temporary festival structures and royal ritual.
- Do not portray Jain, Brahmanical and local traditions as sealed communities that never interact.
- Do not flatten all deities into one generic “Hindu” visual language.
- Treat conversion of Kaṇṇaki into a deity as a developing historical process: experience → testimony → local ritual → royal memorial → wider circulation.

---

## 9. Writing, language and public communication

Tamil-Brahmi inscriptions are found across Tamil Nadu on cave surfaces, pottery, coins, rings and other objects. Official archaeological summaries broadly date the script from the third century BCE into the early centuries CE.

The evidence supports:

- personal names and ownership marks;
- donations and patronage;
- merchant and craft contexts;
- political names and titles;
- movement of writing through trade and religious networks.

It does **not** by itself prove universal literacy.

### Production model

Writing may appear selectively on:

- goods and containers;
- seals, rings or ownership objects;
- administrative or commercial markers;
- donations and sacred sites;
- court or messenger materials where justified.

Public communication should still rely heavily on speech, heralds, drums, flags, witnesses, reputation and embodied performance.

---

## 10. Social order and labour

The historical world must be populated by working systems, not only royal and heroic characters.

Research and production must account for:

- maritime and river labour;
- agricultural workers and pastoralists;
- goldsmiths, bead makers, iron workers, potters and textile workers;
- dancers, musicians, teachers and stage workers;
- merchants, brokers, carriers and guards;
- ascetics and ritual specialists;
- domestic labour within wealthy households;
- forest and hill products entering royal and commercial exchange.

The epic’s luxury is possible because of labour. Production design must reveal the work behind pearls, gems, textiles, ships, festivals, palaces and memorial stone.

## 11. Memory as political action

The final kāṇṭam shows that history is not preserved automatically.

Kaṇṇaki’s memory moves through stages:

1. she experiences and names the catastrophe;
2. hill people witness and interpret her transformation;
3. testimony reaches the Cēra court;
4. the king chooses to institutionalise remembrance;
5. stone, water, military prestige and ritual create a durable public form;
6. rulers and communities participate in the circulation of the new sacred memory.

The memorial project therefore contains both devotion and politics.

### Adaptation consequence

The film must show that royal commemoration can:

- preserve truth;
- enlarge a victim’s public significance;
- create shared ritual;
- establish legitimacy;
- and also frame the story through state power.

Vañji is consequently not an epilogue. It is the epic’s investigation of who controls memory after political catastrophe.

## 12. Working historical timeline

| Evidence horizon | Approximate range | Relevance to adaptation | Restriction |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Iron Age/megalithic continuities | before and into early historic centuries | memorial practices, material continuities, routes and warrior culture | no direct one-to-one illustration of the epic |
| Early Tamil-Brahmi horizon | c. third century BCE onward | names, donations, merchants, rulers, ascetic sites and selective literacy | does not imply mass literacy or one uniform script practice |
| Early historic urban and trade horizon | c. third century BCE–third/fourth century CE | ports, craft centres, Vaigai settlements, Karur, imported goods and inland routes | evidence comes from different sites and dates |

## 14. Questions requiring specialist review

Before the historical world is locked, commission review on:

- the dating and textual formation of *Cilappatikāram*;
- Chola, Pāṇḍya and Cēra political structures;
- the Pukār/Poompuhar shoreline and harbour evidence;
- the relationship between literary Vañji, Karur and other proposed locations;
- ancient routes between the Kāviri, Vaigai and western regions;
- imported pottery and Indian Ocean exchange terminology;
- early Tamil jewellery and anklet manufacture;
- Jain, Brahmanical, local and emerging Pattini traditions;
- the limits of using Keeladi as comparative evidence for Madurai;
- memorial stone, image installation and water-consecration practice.

Until these reviews are complete, historical claims remain graded as **verified**, **probable**, **plausible**, **disputed** or **cinematic interpretation**.
