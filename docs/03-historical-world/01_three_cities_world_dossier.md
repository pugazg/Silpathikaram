# The Three Cities: Historical-World Dossier

## Working production-research edition 0.1

*Cilappatikāram* moves through three royal worlds—Pukār, Madurai and Vañji—and through roads, forests, pastoral settlements and hill country that cannot be treated as empty space between cities.

This dossier separates:

- **textual world** — what the epic presents;
- **historical hypothesis** — what responsible research may support;
- **cinematic construction** — what production must choose when evidence is incomplete.

---

## 1. Period-control rules

### [CAUTION] No single exact production year

The poem, its redaction, the traditions it preserves and the historical institutions it depicts cannot be collapsed automatically into one precisely dated year. Publicity must not advertise an exact date until literary and historical review supports it.

### [CAUTION] No automatic medieval Chola look

The name “Chola” must not produce a visual world dominated by later imperial stone temples, towering gopurams, bronze styles or court fashions unless a specific element is independently supported.

### [INTERPRETATION] Early-historic material palette

Until specialist review, the working visual language should privilege:

- timber, brick, plaster, tile and thatch;
- courtyards, streets, market frontage and working waterfronts;
- textiles and jewellery differentiated by labour, status, region and occasion;
- portable ritual and performance structures;
- objects that show repair, handling, storage and transport.

---

## 2. Pukār

### Textual functions

Pukār is:

- a celebrated Chola city associated with the Kāviri and the sea;
- a world of merchant wealth and generosity;
- the household setting of Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ;
- a performance capital in which Mādhavi’s training and debut matter publicly;
- a festival city;
- a place where maritime abundance and emotional isolation coexist.

### Production systems to research

#### Waterfront and trade

- river-sea traffic;
- loading, storage, weighing, customs and brokerage;
- inland goods moving toward the port;
- ship maintenance and harbour labour;
- multilingual visitors and commercial mediation, if supported by cross-texts and history.

#### Merchant households

- wealth stored as movable goods, textiles, metal, grain, jewellery and credit;
- kinship and household staff;
- wedding transfers and women’s property;
- the difference between visible luxury and liquid resources.

#### Performance economy

- teachers, musicians, dancers, poets, patrons and officials;
- rehearsal and bodily discipline;
- payment, prestige and household dependence;
- stage construction, audience arrangement and civic display.

#### Festival city

The Indra festival must be researched as civic, ritual, commercial and performative infrastructure. It cannot become a generic temple procession assembled from later visual memory.

### Cinematic identity

Pukār should feel horizontal, porous and mobile: sea wind, river moisture, goods, crowds, music, open courtyards and changing light. Its abundance must be attractive enough that departure registers as material and emotional exile.

## 3. The road and changing landscapes

### Textual functions

The journey south:

- removes merchant protection;
- creates dependence upon local knowledge and hospitality;
- changes the couple’s bodily condition;
- moves through religious and ecological plurality;
- turns the anklet from household wealth into emergency capital.

The poem’s landscapes are not fixed scenic categories. The Madurai book describes mullai and kuṟiñci losing their ordinary nature under severe season and taking the form of pālai.

### Production rules

- Show heat, distance, water access, insects, footwear, carrying and rest.
- Track changes in fabric, hair, ornaments and skin without using dirt as a shorthand for moral seriousness.
- Do not make rural and forest communities timeless or culturally uniform.
- Treat route knowledge as expertise.
- Make sacred geography legible without presenting every narrated wonder as a tourist landmark.

---

## 4. Madurai

### Textual functions

Madurai is:

- the confident capital of the Pāṇḍya;
- a market and craft city;
- a place of refuge and vulnerability;
- the site where accusation passes rapidly into royal violence;
- the court in which material evidence overturns authority;
- an inhabited city destroyed after institutional failure.

### Systems that must be visible

#### Market and craft

- goldsmithing and specialist knowledge of ornaments;
- valuation, sale and recognition of elite goods;
- communication between market actors and authority;
- the goldsmith’s access to the court or its officers.

#### Government and law

Research must distinguish:

- accusation;
- identification;
- arrest or seizure;
- royal order;
- guards’ actions;
- execution;
- court access;
- evidentiary demonstration;
- royal responsibility.

The production should not invent modern legal procedure, but it must show the exact institutional shortcuts that make the injustice possible.

#### Neighbourhood life

The burning must affect differentiated people and places:

- households;
- markets;
- animals;
- workers;
- religious spaces;
- water access;
- people attempting rescue or flight.

Spectacle without inhabited consequence would betray the epic’s political force.

### Cinematic identity

Madurai should feel denser, more enclosed and administratively vertical than Pukār. Court, market and neighbourhood must appear connected before the disaster.

---

## 5. Hill country

### Textual functions

The hill people encounter Kaṇṇaki beneath the vēṅkai tree, hear her account, witness celestial signs and receive her as a deity of their community.

### Production rules

- The hill community is not a picturesque chorus.
- Their questions, recognition, music, instruments, worship and protective boundary-making are actions.
- Do not map literary Kuravar identity automatically onto one modern community without specialist consultation.
- Research mountain subsistence, exchange, settlement, movement, instruments, flowers and ritual practice separately.

### Cinematic identity

The sequence should shift scale from urban catastrophe to local witness. Kaṇṇaki’s transformation becomes socially real because people interpret and act upon what they see.

---

## 6. Vañji and the Chera world

### Textual functions

The Chera movement concerns:

- reception of Kaṇṇaki’s story;
- Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s royal decision;
- interstate prestige and northern expedition;
- selection and transport of stone;
- water consecration;
- memorial installation and worship.

### Research priorities

- the poem’s Chera geography and routes;
- court structure and royal mobility;
- war and diplomatic language;
- stone selection, quarrying, transport and consecration;
- memorial stones and hero/ancestor traditions, without assuming identity;
- how a ruler turns another kingdom’s catastrophe into public memory.

### Cinematic identity

Vañji should not look like a repeated Madurai. Its political and ecological identity must emerge from routes, materials, court practice and the memorial project.

---

## 7. Continuity objects

Production design must track:

### The paired anklets

- maker and materials;
- exterior similarity;
- interior contents;
- ownership and custody;
- sound under different movement;
- wrapping, carrying, sale and breaking;
- visual legibility without prematurely revealing the proof.

### Clothing and ornaments

- wedding and household status;
- Mādhavi’s professional performance materials;
- wealth loss;
- travel adaptation;
- Kaṇṇaki’s altered body and dress after bereavement;
- regional and institutional differences.

### Food and water

Food, hospitality and water access should mark movement from abundance through vulnerability. Exact dishes remain a research question.

---

## 8. Evidence still required

Before production design is locked, obtain specialist review of:

- Poompuhar archaeology and coastal change;
- early historic urban and domestic architecture;
- harbour and ship reconstruction;
- Madurai archaeology and settlement history;
- Chera political geography;
- textiles, dyes and jewellery technology;
- instruments, music and performance space;
- weapons and royal insignia;
- foodways and agricultural seasonality;
- ritual practice and religious plurality;
- fire behaviour in proposed built materials.

All reconstructions remain provisional until linked to the evidence register.
