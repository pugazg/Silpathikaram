# Historical World of *Silapathikaram*

## Three cities, roads, hill country and the making of public memory

**Edition:** Production-research edition 1.0  
**Project:** *Silapathikaram* source-traceable cinematic adaptation archive  
**Status:** Foundational world bible for research, screenplay, character performance, storyboards, production design and scholarly review  
**Controlling source:** The original Tamil text of the Pukār, Madurai and Vañji kāṇṭams  

---

## 1. Purpose

This dossier reconstructs the historical world required by the epic’s complete dramatic movement:

**Pukār → departure and road → Madurai → burning and aftermath → hill-country recognition → Vañji and royal memorialisation.**

It is not a claim that every element described in the poem belonged to one securely dated historical year. It is a controlled research framework that separates:

- **[TEXT]** — directly stated or materially implied by the original Tamil epic;
- **[CROSS]** — supported by another early Tamil literary source after verification;
- **[HISTORY]** — supported by archaeology, inscriptions, numismatics, archaeobotany or responsible historical scholarship;
- **[INTERPRETATION]** — a cinematic reconstruction required where the evidence is incomplete;
- **[CAUTION]** — an attractive but unverified identification, anachronism or simplification that must not be presented as fact.

The original Tamil text governs the adaptation. External evidence can clarify materials, institutions, technologies and environments, but it cannot silently overwrite the epic.

## 3. Period-control rules

### 3.1 No single exact production year

**[CAUTION]** The date of the poem, the date of its redaction, the older traditions it preserves and the historical periods represented by its institutions cannot be collapsed automatically into one year.

Until a specialist chronology review is complete, production language should use formulations such as:

- “the early historic Tamil world”;
- “the world remembered by *Silapathikaram*”;
- “the age of the three crowned Tamil polities”;
- “Kāvirippūmpattinam when river and sea sustained a major commercial city.”

Publicity must not advertise a precise year as settled fact.

### 3.2 No automatic medieval visual language

**[CAUTION]** “Chola,” “Pāṇḍya” and “Cēra” must not trigger a default later-medieval look.

Do not use the following without element-specific evidence:

- imperial-Chola stone-temple complexes;
- towering later gopurams as the normal city skyline;
- later bronze-icon conventions as universal sacred imagery;
- later court costume and military uniform systems;
- modern Bharatanāṭyam staging as a direct reconstruction of Mādhavi’s performance;
- a single pan-Indian palace style applied to all three kingdoms.

### 3.3 Working early-historic material palette

**[HISTORY + INTERPRETATION]** Until specialist review, the visual world should privilege:

- brick, timber, clay, lime plaster, tile, thatch and textiles;
- post-supported and courtyard structures;
- working waterfronts and river edges;
- movable wealth, storage vessels, baskets, bales and metal objects;
- differentiated roads, market fronts, compounds and public enclosures;
- portable ritual and performance structures;
- buildings that show repair, weathering, drainage and labour.

Keeladi provides comparative evidence for brick structures, clay floors, wooden-post roofing systems, roof tiles, ring wells, craft objects, beads and writing. Poompuhar provides comparative coastal and wharf-related evidence. These are evidence bands, not ready-made sets for the poem’s exact cities.

## 4. The epic’s world map

The film requires six connected spatial systems rather than three isolated capital-city sets.

### 4.1 Kāviri–sea system

- river flow and delta agriculture;
- inland goods moving toward Pukār;
- maritime vessels and coastal exchange;
- fishermen, sailors, port labourers, brokers and merchants;
- changing shoreline, sand, salt, moisture and sea wind.

### 4.2 Pukār metropolitan system

- Maruvūrppākkam: port-facing, commercial, craft and migrant world;
- Paṭṭiṉappākkam: royal, elite, institutional and residential world;
- Nāḷaṅkāṭi/day market and civic meeting spaces between or linking the quarters;
- festival routes, performance spaces, sacred sites and the beach.

### 4.3 Southward road system

- city gate and departure before dawn;
- Kāviri-region movement;
- agrarian zones, groves, water bodies and settlements;
- heat-altered landscapes;
- hunter territory and ritual space;
- ascetic shelters and competing route knowledge;
- gradual loss of household protection and bodily ease.

### 4.4 Vaigai–Madurai system

- outer cultivation and groves;
- religious and charitable establishments outside or near the city;
- walls, gates and controlled entry;
- market, craft, residential and sacred zones;
- royal palace, officials, guards and execution route;
- cowherd settlement and refuge network;
- differentiated neighbourhoods affected by fire.

### 4.5 Hill-country system

- small settlement rather than metropolitan centre;
- waterfall, forest, vēṅkai shade and cultivated millet zone;
- music, horn, drums, bells, smoke, flowers and boundary-making;
- mountain products and exchange with the Cēra state;
- community recognition preceding royal appropriation.

### 4.6 Vañji–memorial-state system

- Cēra royal residence and mobile court;
- tribute forecourt and mountain-route reception;
- council, astrologer, ministers, military leadership and administrative specialists;
- public announcement by drum;
- army assembly and northward campaign world;
- riverbank consecration and return;
- Pattini precinct, image-making, protective boundary, offerings and recurring festival.

**[CAUTION]** The exact geographic identification of Vañji remains debated. Production must distinguish the poem’s textual Vañji, historical Cēra centres such as Karur and the final cinematic geography.

# PART I — PUKĀR

## 5. Dramatic function of Pukār

Pukār is not only the place from which the protagonists depart. It establishes everything that can later be lost:

- marital household and kinship protection;
- movable wealth and commercial reputation;
- artistic prestige and patronage;
- urban abundance;
- civic and sacred institutions;
- river–sea openness;
- social visibility and anonymity existing together.

The city must be attractive, complex and fully inhabited. The audience must understand why departure is exile, not a simple change of location.

---

## 6. Tamil-source architecture of Pukār

### Canto 1 — city, household and political blessing

**[TEXT]** The opening praise binds moon, sun, rain, Kāviri country and Pūmpukār to royal order and abundance. Kaṇṇaki and Kōvalaṉ are introduced through wealthy merchant families, and their wedding becomes a city-scale public event.

Production consequences:

- merchant families possess civic standing, not merely private riches;
- marriage mobilises elephants, drums, conches, parasols, floral labour, ritual specialists and public display;
- jewellery, textiles, pavilions and household wealth must be shown as products of organised labour;
- Kaṇṇaki’s later anklet is connected to this world of family property and marriage wealth.

### Canto 2 — merchant domesticity

**[TEXT]** The household world includes upper-storey living, decorated interiors, beds, windows, floral scent, hospitality, attendants and the social expectation that the couple establish an independent domestic life.

Production consequences:

- do not build the house as an empty palace;
- include storage, service routes, kitchens, water, textiles, household staff and guest functions;
- distinguish ceremonial rooms, intimate rooms and working domestic zones;
- show wealth as maintained by people and systems.

### Canto 3 — the performance institution

**[TEXT]** Mādhavi’s debut follows years of disciplined training and requires dance, song, teachers, musicians, poetic knowledge, rhythm, gesture systems, stage preparation, royal attendance and reward.

Production consequences:

- Mādhavi is a highly trained professional artist;
- the stage is an institution, not a decorative platform;
- teachers, accompanists, instrument maintenance, rehearsal, costume, makeup and patronage must be visible;
- the king’s reward links artistic prestige to political authority and economic value;
- audience hierarchy and access require separate research.

### Canto 4 — Pukār by time of day

**[TEXT]** Evening and night are socially differentiated. Lovers, separated women, music, domestic activity, guards, animals, drums, conches and dawn all create an urban clock.

Production consequences:

- Pukār needs a 24-hour sound and light plan;
- silence must vary by quarter and status;
- night protection, lamps, patrols and gate control should be researched;
- Kaṇṇaki’s and Mādhavi’s emotional worlds must coexist within the same city night.

### Canto 5 — city quarters, market and Indra festival

**[TEXT]** The poem presents a highly differentiated city through Maruvūrppākkam, Paṭṭiṉappākkam, the day market, foreign merchants, craft and commodity streets, civic spaces, shrines, banners, pavilions, public announcement and festival mobilisation.

This canto must become the principal source for the Pukār production map.

### Canto 6 — festival culmination and beach world

**[TEXT]** The festival extends toward the coast. The beach is lit by different occupational groups and commercial activities. Temporary structures, crowds, food sellers, craftspeople, performers and sea bathing transform the shore.

Production consequences:

- the beach must be a labouring and commercial space before becoming a pleasure space;
- lamps should identify occupation, stall, vessel and route—not merely decorate the frame;
- crowd control, temporary shelters, waste, water and return routes require design;
- the transition from civic festival to the intimate Kāṉal Vari must be spatially legible.

### Cantos 7–9 — artistic intimacy, misunderstanding and urban communication

**[TEXT]** Song, written message, messenger, seasonal change and interpretive misunderstanding drive the separation between Kōvalaṉ and Mādhavi.

Production consequences:

- literacy and message-carrying require material treatment;
- songs are acts of communication whose meanings can be contested;
- the city’s artistic network continues after Kōvalaṉ withdraws;
- Mādhavi’s residence, companions and professional world must not disappear when the romantic relationship breaks.

### Canto 10 — departure through the city’s sacred geography

**[TEXT]** The couple leave before dawn through a long gate and pass sacred establishments and river-country spaces.

Production consequences:

- the exit must show city edge, gates and a transition from urban to agrarian space;
- departure should contrast stored urban wealth with what can actually be carried;
- the anklet becomes portable survival capital;
- animals, footwear, bundles, food and water must be continuity-tracked.

## 7. Pukār production zones

The following zones are required for storyboarding. Their exact relationship remains a production hypothesis until a full canto-level spatial concordance is completed.

### PUK-Z01 — Kāviri approach and river traffic

Functions:

- inland agricultural and craft goods arriving;
- ferries, small boats and larger cargo transfer;
- water collection and bathing;
- river moisture entering the city’s sensory identity.

### PUK-Z02 — working waterfront and wharf

Functions:

- mooring, unloading, ropes, poles, gangways and storage;
- port labour, guards, brokers and ship repair;
- movement between watercraft and land transport;
- customs or inspection only where supported and explicitly labelled.

External comparison:

- Poompuhar excavations identify brick walls, wooden poles and a possible wharf context;
- underwater work confirms the value and danger of coastal-change research.

Limitation:

- the excavated feature is not proof of the poem’s exact harbour layout.

### PUK-Z03 — migrant and overseas merchant quarter

**[TEXT + CROSS + HISTORY]** The city includes people arriving by ships and a cosmopolitan commercial population.

Design requirements:

- varied languages and mediation without caricature;
- lodgings, warehouses, eating spaces and brokerage;
- imported containers mixed with local repacking systems;
- cultural difference expressed through material practice, not generic “foreign” costume.

### PUK-Z04 — Maruvūrppākkam commodity streets

Research categories:

- colour, lime, sandal, flowers, incense and perfume;
- textiles;
- gems and beads;
- grain and prepared food;
- fish, meat, salt and betel;
- rope, leather, wood, metal and other specialist crafts.

Production rule:

Every commodity street must include supply, measurement, storage, waste and labour—not only finished goods.

### PUK-Z05 — craft workshops

Required systems:

- metal and jewellery work;
- bead and gem handling;
- textile production and finishing;
- woodworking and vehicle repair;
- food preparation;
- lamp, oil and fuel supply;
- instrument manufacture and maintenance.

Kodumanal may provide comparative evidence for beads, iron, craft production and exchange. It must not be copied as Pukār.

### PUK-Z06 — Nāḷaṅkāṭi/day market and civic interface

Functions:

- exchange between the two major quarters;
- public announcement;
- festival mobilisation;
- protective and civic ritual;
- temporary crowd density.

Research questions:

- operating hours;
- stall permanence;
- dispute handling;
- measurement and weights;
- sanitation and animal movement;
- relationship to the poem’s five public forums.

### PUK-Z07 — Paṭṭiṉappākkam royal and elite axis

Functions:

- royal visibility;
- official residences and service routes;
- wealthy merchants and high-status households;
- ceremonial processions;
- controlled access to court and performance.

**[CAUTION]** Do not reconstruct a later imperial palace complex without evidence.

### PUK-Z08 — merchant residential compounds

Required differentiation:

- Kaṇṇaki’s natal household;
- Kōvalaṉ’s family network;
- the couple’s independent home;
- storage and accounting areas;
- women’s property and ornaments;
- hospitality and household service.

### PUK-Z09 — performance district and stage

Required spaces:

- rehearsal room;
- instrument storage;
- costume and ornament preparation;
- teachers and accompanists;
- public stage;
- royal and general audience zones;
- backstage entry and waiting;
- Mādhavi’s residence and professional household.

### PUK-Z10 — sacred and civic institutions

Must be mapped from the Tamil text before final design:

- shrines and temples;
- protective deity spaces;
- the five public forums;
- ascetic or monastic establishments;
- festival pavilions and offering sites.

### PUK-Z11 — Indra festival route

The festival must be treated as:

- civic calendar;
- political performance;
- religious observance;
- commercial season;
- performance programme;
- public-order operation;

### PUK-Z12 — beach, dunes and night-light system

Required layers:

- fishermen and vessels;

## 8. Pukār visual and sensory identity

### Built form

- horizontal and porous;
- timber–brick–plaster mix;
- arcades, yards, platforms and awnings;
- visible storage and goods movement;
- roofs and drainage suited to coastal rain and heat.

### Light

- bright river and sea glare;
- filtered domestic courtyards;
- oil lamps differentiated by use;
- festival illumination without electric-like uniformity;
- dawn and night as major narrative states.

### Sound

- water, ropes, hulls and harbour work;
- bargaining and multilingual speech;
- craft hammering and grinding;
- yāḻ, flute, percussion, song and rehearsal;
- drums, conches and official announcement;
- birds, animals and late-night guards.

### Production warning

Pukār must not be a clean fantasy port. It should show prosperity, crowding, smells, waste, labour, risk, beauty and social inequality in the same frame.

# PART II — THE ROAD AND TRANSITIONAL WORLDS

## 9. Dramatic function of the road

The road removes the protections built in Pukār:

- family reputation;
- household stores;
- servants and transport;
- commercial contacts;
- urban anonymity;
- access to familiar institutions.

It converts the anklet from ceremonial property into emergency capital and makes route knowledge, water, hospitality and bodily endurance decisive.

---

## 10. Route systems in the Tamil text

### 10.1 Departure before dawn

**[TEXT]** The city is still partly asleep when the couple leave. The departure should be quiet, logistically difficult and emotionally unresolved.

### 10.2 Kāviri and island/river environments

The route includes river-country religious and settlement spaces. Water is simultaneously:

- transport barrier;
- source of food and relief;
- sacred geography;
- marker of regional abundance.

### 10.3 Competing route knowledge

**[TEXT]** The Madurai book presents route descriptions, dangerous paths, sacred alternatives and debate over what kind of knowledge should guide travellers.

Production rule:

Do not use a single empty road. Build a network of choices shaped by season, safety, belief and local expertise.

### 10.4 Seasonal transformation into pālai

**[TEXT]** Mullai and kuṟiñci can lose their ordinary condition under severe heat and assume pālai form.

Production consequences:

- landscapes must be dynamic, not textbook-colour zones;
- heat changes water, vegetation, animal behaviour and human speed;
- costume deterioration must be physical, not moral symbolism;
- shade and water become dramatic assets.

### 10.5 Agricultural abundance

**[TEXT]** Rice, sugarcane, millets, turmeric, plantain, areca, coconut, mango and jackfruit appear in route description.

Production consequences:

- show field labour, irrigation, harvesting or storage appropriate to the selected season;
- do not display all crops simultaneously without agricultural review;
- exact plant identification requires botanical verification.

### 10.6 Hunter community and Korravai ritual

**[TEXT]** The hunter sequence includes debt to the deity, possession, costume transformation, weapons, instruments, offerings and communal performance.

Production rule:

The community cannot be reduced to a “tribal spectacle.” Ritual, subsistence, danger, social memory and humour must be internally coherent.

### 10.7 Ascetics, shelters and sacred plurality

The route includes Jain-associated ascetics, Brahmanical sacred narratives, local deities and other religious presences.

External comparison:

- Mangulam and surrounding Tamil-Brahmi cave inscriptions support an early historical landscape in which ascetic establishments, donors, rulers and merchants intersected;
- this supports the plausibility of plural sacred routes but not the exact itinerary of the characters.

---

# PART III — MADURAI

## 12. Dramatic function of Madurai

Madurai is a functioning capital before it becomes a catastrophe.

It must be capable of:

- attracting migrants and merchants;
- valuing and selling elite goods;
- sustaining sacred and charitable institutions;
- controlling gates and streets;
- carrying information to the king;
- deploying guards;
- executing royal orders;
- admitting a public accusation;
- experiencing differentiated destruction.

The city’s tragedy is that its institutions work quickly but not justly.

---

## 13. Tamil-source architecture of Madurai

### Canto 11 — approach and Pāṇḍya country

**[TEXT]** The route describes the political praise of the Pāṇḍya realm, ecological transformation, agricultural resources, religious geography and the choices facing travellers.

### Canto 12 — hunter ritual world

This canto broadens the social geography before the capital. It also creates a precedent for women, ritual speech and divine possession carrying public authority outside royal institutions.

### Canto 13 — outer settlement and refuge

**[TEXT]** The travellers halt in the city’s outer environment. The outskirts must include groves, agriculture, religious or charitable shelter and people who mediate access to the city.

### Canto 14 — seeing and hearing Madurai

**[TEXT]** Madurai wakes through sacred and royal sound: temples or sacred establishments, ascetic institutions, palace, conches, drums and morning activity. Kōvalaṉ enters a city differentiated by streets, professions, markets and elite goods.

This canto must become the principal source for the Madurai production map.

### Canto 15 — asylum, hospitality and the cowherd world

**[TEXT]** Kaṇṇaki is entrusted to a protective household outside the elite merchant system. Food preparation, hospitality, cattle and women’s labour become central.

Production consequences:

- refuge is an institution of trust, not a temporary plot convenience;
- the cowherd settlement requires its own material and musical identity;
- Kaṇṇaki’s domestic actions here must contrast with her Pukār household.

### Canto 16 — market expertise, false accusation and state violence

**[TEXT]** The goldsmith has specialised knowledge, prior guilt and access to authority. The royal order passes to city guards, and Kōvalaṉ is killed before evidence is examined.

Production consequences:

- map the communication chain precisely;
- distinguish palace guard, city watch and execution personnel if the Tamil vocabulary and specialist review support it;
- show how status and expert reputation influence belief;
- do not invent a modern trial that would weaken the poem’s critique.

### Canto 17 — cowherd performance and threatened normal life

**[TEXT]** The Āycciyar Kuravai places music, cattle, devotion, women’s sociality and performance beside the unseen catastrophe.

Production consequence:

The sequence should establish a living community whose ordinary rhythms will soon be broken.

### Cantos 18–19 — grief becomes public movement

**[TEXT]** News travels, Kaṇṇaki encounters the body, speaks before citizens and moves around the city. Urban space becomes witness.

Production consequences:

- design the route from refuge to death site to city streets and palace gate;
- crowds must have differentiated access to information;
- Kaṇṇaki’s body, voice and anklet become public evidence before the court scene.

### Canto 20 — palace access and evidentiary confrontation

**[TEXT]** The gatekeeper, king, queen, court and anklet proof form an institutional sequence. The king’s authority collapses when the material evidence is revealed.

Production consequences:

- the court must be spatially clear enough for the audience to understand access, hierarchy and shock;
- the anklet’s breaking and contents must be visible without earlier spoiling the distinction;
- the queen’s presence and knowledge of her anklet matter;
- the gatekeeper is an active procedural figure.

### Cantos 21–22 — vow and burning

**[TEXT]** Kaṇṇaki’s anger moves from judgement to destruction. The fire is directed with stated exemptions and unfolds through an inhabited city.

Production consequences:

- no uncomplicated revenge spectacle;
- preserve the moral problem of collective consequence;
- map protected and unprotected groups from the Tamil text;
- show fire moving through actual materials, streets, animals, water systems and human decisions.

### Canto 23 — city deity, causation and aftermath

**[TEXT]** The city’s sacred identity speaks after institutional collapse. Past action is explained without undoing the king’s present failure.

Production consequence:

The supernatural layer must coexist with visible political accountability.

## 14. Madurai production zones

### MAD-Z01 — Vaigai/outer agrarian ring

- fields, ponds, groves and food supply;
- cattle and pastoral movement;
- routes by which travellers approach;
- city dependence on surrounding labour.

External comparison:

- Keeladi and its Vaigai-basin setting provide comparative evidence for early historic settlement, brick structures, roofing, wells, craft objects, writing and domestic material culture;
- Keeladi is not “the Madurai of the epic.”

### MAD-Z02 — outer religious and charitable settlement

- ascetics and teachers;
- travellers’ lodging;
- groves and water;
- social mediation before city entry.

External comparison:

- Mangulam/Ovamalai inscriptions and caves support a landscape of ascetic establishments, donors, merchants and Pāṇḍya-linked authority.

### MAD-Z03 — moat, wall and gate system

Research requirements:

- wall and gate vocabulary in the original Tamil;
- bridge or crossing assumptions;
- animal and cart entry;
- guard posts;
- night closure;
- relationship between outer settlement and city.

**[CAUTION]** Do not create a massive later stone fortification by default.

### MAD-Z04 — sacred dawn axis

The city wakes through multiple institutions rather than one dominant temple.

Required differentiation:

- Śiva-associated establishment;
- Viṣṇu-associated establishment;
- Balarāma-associated establishment;
- Murukaṉ-associated establishment;
- ascetic/ethical institution;
- royal palace.

Production rule:

Religious plurality must be heard and seen without forcing all institutions into later standard temple architecture.

### MAD-Z05 — general market and equipment street

The Tamil text’s city inventory includes vehicles, weapons, armour, ceremonial goods, tools, metal objects, perfumes and other goods.

Required systems:

- storage and display;
- repair and manufacture;
- bargaining and valuation;
- carts and animal congestion;
- official purchases and elite demand.

### MAD-Z06 — gold, gem and jewellery market

Required distinctions:

- raw material;
- alloy or recognised gold categories in the text;
- gems, pearls and beads;
- finished ornaments;
- specialist testing and valuation;
- secure storage;
- routes connecting artisans to court clients.

External comparisons:

- Kodumanal supports comparative research on bead production, iron and exchange;
- Karur and Alagankulam provide comparative evidence for trade goods, Mediterranean ceramics, coins and early historic commerce;
- none proves the exact Madurai market plan.

### MAD-Z07 — goldsmith workshop and dwelling

Must include:

- tools and work surfaces;
- secure storage and concealment possibilities;
- household or apprentice structure;
- access to palace networks;
- the difference between genuine expertise and corrupt testimony.

### MAD-Z08 — cowherd settlement and refuge house

Required systems:

- cattle enclosures;
- milk processing and vessels;
- cooking and hospitality;
- women’s work and social network;
- performance space for the kuravai;
- routes to the city and execution ground.

### MAD-Z09 — royal approach and gatekeeper station

Functions:

- petition control;
- official messaging;
- security;
- transition from public street to royal interior;
- later entry of Kaṇṇaki carrying the anklet.

### MAD-Z10 — palace and court

Required spaces:

- royal audience area;
- queen’s movement and residence link;
- officials and attendants;
- storage or retrieval route for the queen’s anklet if represented;
- clear sightlines for the evidence scene;
- no unnecessary monumental excess that weakens the human scale of judgement.

### MAD-Z11 — guard route and execution ground

Must be mapped as an institutional chain:

**goldsmith’s accusation → palace communication → royal order → guards → identification → killing → abandoned body/news circulation.**

Every transition requires a source or an explicit adaptation decision.

### MAD-Z12 — differentiated neighbourhoods

Before the fire, establish:

- elite and ordinary households;
- markets and workshops;
- sacred establishments;
- cattle and animals;
- water points;
- old people, children and workers;
- streets of different width and material.

### MAD-Z13 — fire geography and evacuation network

Research requirements:

- likely fuel loads in timber, thatch, textile, oil and stored goods;
- walls and roofs that slow or accelerate spread;
- water access and carrying vessels;
- animal release;
- crowd movement and blocked streets;
- sacred/supernatural direction of the fire in the poem;
- stated exemptions and their visual legibility.

A fire-behaviour specialist must review the final destruction plan.

- denser and more administratively layered than Pukār;
- controlled gates and inward movement;
- market streets connected to royal authority;
- sacred establishments embedded in urban sound;
- differentiated outer and inner zones.

### Light

- dawn entering a waking capital;

- guard commands;
- Kaṇṇaki’s voice moving from street to gate to court;

# PART IV — HILL COUNTRY

## 16. Dramatic function of the hill community

The hill community performs a decisive historical and theological act:

1. they encounter Kaṇṇaki;
2. they ask who she is;
3. they hear her account in her own words;
4. they witness signs;
5. they interpret the event;
6. they establish worship through collective action;
7. they carry testimony toward royal power.

Kaṇṇaki’s sacred status is therefore not created first by a king.

---

## 17. Hill-country systems in the Tamil text

### Settlement and ecology

- small settlement;
- waterfall and pool;
- flowering vēṅkai;
- mountain slopes;
- millet cultivation;
- forest and gathered products.

### Ritual system

- toṇṭakam and small drum;
- curved horn and bell;
- kuṟiñci mode;
- fragrant smoke;
- flower offering;
- protective boundary;
- repeated praise and communal dance.

### Social intelligence

The community distinguishes human lover from deity, jokes, debates illness and ritual, recognises Kaṇṇaki’s exceptional state and acts institutionally.

Production rule:

Do not present the hill people as anonymous mystics, comic primitives or picturesque extras.

### Exchange with the Cēra state

**[TEXT]** Mountain people bring ivory, aromatic woods, honey, sandal, pigments, spices, fruits, roots and animals before the king.

Production consequences:

- tribute is also a map of mountain ecology and extraction;
- every product requires a chain of gathering, carrying and value;
- animal presentation must be ethically reviewed and may require non-literal cinematic treatment;
- mountain–court relations include hierarchy, exchange and communication.

# PART V — VAÑJI AND THE CĒRA MEMORIAL STATE

## 19. Dramatic function of Vañji

Vañji transforms catastrophe into durable public memory.

The sequence is not an epilogue attached after the “real story.” It investigates:

- how testimony reaches a ruler;
- how kings compare standards of justice;
- how a queen and learned advisers influence commemoration;
- how sacred memory becomes military and interstate policy;
- how material, labour and ritual create a monument;
- how the state preserves and also frames Kaṇṇaki’s meaning.

---

## 20. Tamil-source architecture of the Cēra world

### Canto 25 — mountain tour, tribute and decision

**[TEXT]** Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ and the queen move into the mountain landscape. The soundscape includes waterfalls, kuravai, ritual song, field calls, elephants, watchmen and army noise. Mountain communities bring diverse goods and report Kaṇṇaki’s story. Sāttaṉār explains the Pukār–Madurai catastrophe. The king, queen and learned advisers consider the response and the stone source.

Production consequences:

- the court is mobile and receives information outside a static throne room;
- the queen is an active participant in deciding how Kaṇṇaki should be honoured;
- tribute, oral testimony and literary narration meet in one political space;
- stone choice is a statement of scale and prestige.

### Canto 26 — council, announcement and campaign preparation

**[TEXT]** The king sits with teacher/adviser, astrologer, ministers and military leaders. Public drum announcement, army organisation, officials and ceremony convert the vow into state action.

Production consequences:

- distinguish counsel, calculation, military command and ritual legitimation;
- show clerical/accounting labour behind mobilisation;
- the army requires food, animals, equipment, routes and camp systems;
- public announcement communicates policy beyond the palace.

### Canto 27 — campaign, riverbank and water consecration

**[TEXT]** The stone is obtained in the north, carried in relation to defeated rulers, brought to the Ganges and ritually treated. The army, camp, honours and return form a large logistical sequence.

Production consequences:

- the northern expedition may contain epic magnification and must not be marketed as documentary history;
- military triumph and sacred purpose should remain morally entangled;
- water consecration requires specialists, containers, access, security and ceremonial order;
- the stone’s custody must be continuity-tracked from selection to installation.

### Canto 28 — image, precinct and recurring worship

**[TEXT]** Priests, teacher, astrologer and skilled craftspeople participate. The image is completed, ornamented and installed. Flower offering, protective boundary, sacrifice/ritual and recurring festival are ordered.

Production consequences:

- the monument is a collective work of artisans, ritual specialists and administrators;
- craft stages must be visible rather than replaced by a magical reveal;
- the Pattini precinct requires access, procession, offering, lighting and maintenance plans;
- local hill recognition and state cult must remain distinguishable.

### Cantos 29–30 — circulation, praise and continuing sacred authority

**[TEXT]** Women’s songs, multi-kingdom praise, visitors and boons place Kaṇṇaki within a wider Tamil and transregional memory network.

Production consequence:

The final world should feel politically enlarged but emotionally connected to the woman who spoke beneath the vēṅkai and before the Pāṇḍya court.

# PART VI — CROSS-CUTTING WORLD SYSTEMS

## 23. Kingship and government

The three polities must not be visually interchangeable.

### Chola/Pukār

Emphasis:

- prosperity;
- commercial and festival order;
- royal patronage of performance;
- civic display.

### Pāṇḍya/Madurai

Emphasis:

- capital authority;
- rapid command;
- market–court connection;
- catastrophic failure to investigate;
- ruler’s personal accountability for the bent sceptre.

### Cēra/Vañji

Emphasis:

- reception of testimony;
- counsel and vow;
- military projection;
- transformation of memory into monument and cult.

Production rule:

Royal power must be shown through people, information, logistics and consequences—not only crowns, thrones and armies.

## 27. Religion and sacred plurality

The world contains multiple overlapping sacred systems:

- Jain-associated ascetics and ethical teaching;

- Korravai ritual;
- local and guardian deities;
- possession and omen;
- city deity;

- royal Pattini installation.

# PART VII — EXTERNAL EVIDENCE FRAMEWORK

## 38. Archaeological comparison sites

### Poompuhar

Useful for:

- Kāviri–sea setting;
- coastal change;
- brick and wooden structural evidence;
- possible wharf context;
- underwater archaeology questions.

Not proof of:

- exact Maruvūrppākkam or Paṭṭiṉappākkam layout;
- the poem’s festival route;
- a single destruction/submergence event matching the epic.

### Keeladi

Useful for:

- comparative Vaigai-basin urban material culture;
- brick structures and roofing;
- wells;
- craft and domestic objects;
- inscribed pottery and writing.

Not proof of:

- the exact palace, streets or court of epic Madurai.

### Mangulam/Ovamalai and nearby cave sites

Useful for:

- Tamil-Brahmi inscriptions;
- ascetic beds and establishments;
- donor, ruler and merchant connections;
- sacred landscapes around the Madurai region.

Not proof of:

- Kauṇṭi’s exact route or institution.

### Alagankulam and Korkai

Useful for:

- Pāṇḍya-region maritime exchange;
- ceramics, beads, bricks, coins and Tamil-Brahmi evidence;
- connecting inland political centres with coastal trade.

Not proof of:

- goods present in every Madurai street at the same date.

### Kodumanal

Useful for:

- beads, carnelian, iron and craft production;
- inland exchange networks;
- labour-intensive material culture.

Not proof of:

- Pukār or Madurai workshop layouts.

### Karur and Pugalur

Useful for:

- Cēra political and commercial context;
- Tamil-Brahmi references to Cēra lineages;
- trade goods, coins, amphorae, rouletted ware and craft materials;
- Jain-associated donation context.

Not proof of:

- uncontested identification of every textual Vañji location;
- the exact Pattini precinct described by the poem.
