# Scene Architecture — Source-Governed Method

## Purpose

This layer converts the Tamil-source concordance and Character Bible into screenplay-ready sequence architecture. It is not screenplay prose. It defines what each scene must accomplish, what evidence supports it, which character action carries it, and what production systems are required.

## Scene-card fields

Every scene card must record:

1. **Scene ID and sequence order**
2. **Narrative block and canto**
3. **Location and time condition**
4. **HWT source IDs**
5. **Principal characters or institutions**
6. **Source action** — what the epic directly gives
7. **Dramatic function** — why the scene is necessary in the adaptation
8. **Scene turn** — what changes by the end
9. **Performance focus** — playable behaviour rather than explanation
10. **Production requirements** — world, costume, props, music, movement, VFX or crowd systems
11. **Transition out** — how the next scene is causally or formally reached
12. **Interpretive limit** — what cinema must not claim as Tamil-source fact

## Adaptation-form labels

- **SOURCE_DIRECT** — one substantially continuous source passage supplies the scene.
- **SOURCE_COMPOSITE** — several verified passages are combined without reversing chronology or outcome.
- **PARALLEL_SOURCE** — separately sourced actions are intercut to expose contrast or causation.
- **CONTROLLED_BRIDGE** — limited connective action is invented only to move between verified events; it may not create a new motive, outcome or historical claim.
- **MIXED_PERFORMANCE** — prose, verse, music, dance, ritual or landscape must jointly carry the scene.

## Lock states

- **LOCKED_SOURCE** — scene purpose and outcome are fixed by the Tamil text.
- **LOCKED_ADAPTATION** — a cinematic arrangement is adopted because it preserves the source more clearly.
- **DEVELOPMENT** — source basis is secure but staging, duration or point of view remains open.
- **RESEARCH_HOLD** — production depiction requires additional historical, material or ritual research.

## Binding rules

1. Every scene must cite at least one HWT record.
2. A scene may combine records, but it may not silently combine incompatible locations, times or witnesses.
3. Character motivation must remain separated into **textual action**, **cross-canto inference** and **cinematic interpretation**.
4. Songs, dances, laments, ritual acts, objects and landscapes are dramatic actions, not detachable spectacle.
5. The anklet must remain property, capital, accusation object, material proof and title-bearing memory.
6. Pukār must function as a city and economy before it becomes a lost home.
7. Travel must visibly alter bodies and relationships.
8. Madurai must fail through lack of investigation, not merely through a villain’s trick or impersonal fate.
9. The fire must preserve the text’s moral limits and protected categories.
10. Vañci must show local recognition before royal monumentalisation.
11. Controlled bridges must be marked in the machine-readable matrix.
12. No scene may convert uncertainty into asserted historical fact.

## Scene test

Before a card is locked, answer:

- **Source test:** Can every outcome be traced to HWT evidence?
- **Character test:** Which action, object, silence, song or decision carries the scene?
- **Turn test:** What changes that prevents the sequence from remaining static illustration?
- **World test:** Which social, economic, sacred or political system becomes visible?
- **Production test:** Can departments identify what must be researched and built?
- **Honesty test:** Are invented transitions and interpretations explicitly labelled?

## First release

The initial sequence covers Pukār from the epic’s moral contract and merchant marriage through Mādhavi’s performance world, the Kāṉal Vari rupture, the anklet recovery plan and the pre-dawn departure that leads to Kauṇṭi.
