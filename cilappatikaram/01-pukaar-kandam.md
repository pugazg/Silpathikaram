# Prologue (*Patikam*)

*Metre: Iṇaikkuṟaḷ Āciriyappā*

To Iḷaṅkō Aṭikaḷ—the Cēra prince of Kuṭakkō,  
who had renounced kingship and dwelt within the monastery at Kuṇavāyil—  
the mountain Kuravars came together and said:

“Beneath the rich, cool shade  
of a golden-flowered vēṅkai tree,  
a great and sacred chaste woman stood, having lost one breast. **(1–5)**

The attendants of the king of the immortals came there,  
showed her the husband she loved,  
and, before our very eyes, took her with him  
from the earthly realm into the world of heaven.  
It seemed a wonder. Graciously tell us what it means.”

Then Sāttaṉ, master of lucid Tamil, who was seated beside him, said: **(6–10)**

“I know what happened; I shall relate it.

In Pukār, the ancient city of the Cōḻa  
whose garland is the ātti flower,  
a city of unfading greatness,  
there lived a merchant named Kōvalaṉ.  
Through his life with a courtesan trained for the theatre, **(11–15)**

he lost his rare and precious wealth.  
Wishing to sell a finely wrought anklet  
belonging to his wife Kaṇṇaki,  
he entered great and lofty Madurai,  
the celebrated city of the Pāṇḍya famed in song. **(16–20)**

As he walked through the street of the great royal market,  
a goldsmith saw the anklet in his hand.  
He went away saying, ‘Except for the anklet of the great queen,  
there is no ornament here that can match this one.’  
Then, seeing in it the anklet he himself had formerly stolen, **(21–25)**

he declared that it had been found in another thief’s hand.  
Because the time had come for past deeds to yield their fruit,  
the Pāṇḍya who wore the fresh-blossomed neem did not investigate.  
He summoned his fierce guards and ordered:  
‘Kill that thief and bring the anklet here.’ **(26–30)**

Kōvalaṉ was slain at the execution ground.  
His wife, unable to endure the sight of what had happened,  
shed tears from her long eyes.  
Because she was a faithful wife, the Pāṇḍya met destruction.  
She twisted off the breast from her pearl-garlanded chest **(31–35)**

and fed enduring Kūṭal—Madurai—to towering flame.  
She is the chaste woman praised by multitudes.”

“You said it was the hour when deeds bear fruit.  
What deed of theirs bore this fruit?  
Valiant one, tell us.”

“In ancient Madurai of unshaken glory, **(36–40)**

at midnight I lay in the Silver Hall  
within the public temple of the Lord  
whose matted hair is adorned with koṉṟai flowers.  
Before that heroic, grief-stricken wife,  
the great tutelary goddess of Madurai appeared and said: **(41–45)**

‘Woman whose breast produced the rage of boiling fire,  
an ancient deed has now ripened and ended for you both.  
In a former birth, the wife of a merchant named Saṅkamaṉ,  
in renowned and bountiful Siṅkapuram,  
laid a curse upon you and your bracelet-wearing husband. **(46–50)**

Because that curse has now bound you,  
O woman of long, resonant hair,  
a full fourteen days must pass;  
then you will see your bridegroom  
not in mortal form, but only in the form of the celestials.’

I myself heard this faultless declaration.” **(51–54)**

“Then let us establish, in a poem interwoven with song,  
three truths:  
that righteous law becomes the god of death to rulers who fail in government;  
that the great praise a chaste wife of renown;  
and that past action ripens, takes form, and compels its result.  
Since the anklet entwined by fate is the cause,  
let the work be named *Cilappatikāram*—*The Tale of the Anklet*.” **(55–60)**

“It belongs equally to the three crowned kings.  
Revered Aṭikaḷ, you yourself must graciously compose it,” Sāttaṉār said.

It would contain:

*The Auspicious Benediction*;  
*The Canto in Which the Elders Established Domestic Virtue*;  
*The Canto of the Dance Debut of Mādhavi, the Maiden Skilled in Performance*; **(61–65)**

*The Canto Celebrating the Evening*;  
*The Canto in Which the City Took Up Indra’s Festival*;  
*The Canto of Bathing in the Sea*;  
the petal-opening *Kāṉal Vari*;  
*The Canto in Which Mādhavi Lamented When Summer Arrived and Settled*; **(66–70)**

*The Canto in Which the Ominous Dream Was Explained*;  
*The Canto of Seeing the Country*, framed as a question;  
*The Canto of Seeing the Forest*;  
*The Hunters’ Vari*;  
*The Canto of Resting in the Outlying Settlement with the Garlanded Woman*; **(71–75)**

*The Canto of Seeing the Resounding City*;  
*The Canto of Refuge for the Noble Woman*;  
*The Canto of the Execution Ground*;  
*The Cowherd Women’s Kuravai*;  
*The Garland of Suffering*, after hearing the fiery news; **(76–80)**

*The City-Circling Vari*, which made noon itself tremble;  
*The Canto of the Lawsuit before the renowned king*;  
*The Garland of the Vow*;  
*The Canto of Burning*;  
*The Canto of Explanation*, in which the great goddess appeared; **(81–85)**

*The Hill-Women’s Kuravai* of garlanded maidens;  
and, together with all these, the five cantos—  
*The Vision*, *Taking the Stone*, *The Water Consecration*, *The Memorial Stone*,  
*The Benediction*, and *The Granting of the Boon*.

Thus Aṭikaḷ, famed for learned exposition, composed  
a continuous poem in which prose is interwoven with song,  
and Sāttaṉ, the grain merchant of Madurai, heard it.  
This is the traditional form of the prologue that distinguishes the divisions of the work. **(86–90)**

# Pukār Kāṇḍam

# 1. The Auspicious Benediction

## Cintiyal Veṇpā stanzas

Let us praise the Moon! Let us praise the Moon!  
For, like the cool white parasol above the head  
of the Cōḻa whose garland is heavy with honey,  
it gives its grace to this beautiful world.

Let us praise the Sun! Let us praise the Sun!  
For, like the discus of the lord of the Kāviri land,  
it circles golden-crested Meru. **(1–6)**

Let us praise the great rain! Let us praise the great rain!  
For, like the king’s generosity to the world  
encircled by the fearsome sea,  
it stands above and pours itself down.

Let us praise flower-filled Pukār! Let us praise flower-filled Pukār!  
For, together with the dynasty of its king,  
its fame rises, spreads, and flows  
through the world encircled by swelling waters. **(7–12)**

## Mayaṅkicai Koccakak Kalippā

There—

Whether at Potiyil or at the Himalaya,  
ancient peoples who do not know migration from their settlements flourish;  
and in Pukār too, a city of incomparable distinction,  
the learned, who have mastered truth and completed all inquiry,  
say only that its people stand without trembling—  
never that they have declined. **(13–19)**

Therefore,

in that city of Pukār, whose enduring fame and delights  
rival the vast city and realm of the Nāgas,  
there was a maiden, the flowering branch of the lineage  
of the great sea-merchant Māṉāykaṉ,  
whose generous hand was equal to a rain-cloud.  
She was twelve years old. **(20–24)**

Women worshipped and praised her, saying:  
“She is the famed embodiment of Lakṣmī seated upon the lotus;  
her steadfastness is that of the flawless northern star.”  
Endowed with manifest and abundant virtues,  
the beloved maiden’s enduring name was Kaṇṇaki. **(25–29)**

There also lived Māśāttuvāṉ,  
rich beyond all others among the unique citizens  
protected by the great lord who ruled the whole broad earth,  
a man who made arriving wealth satisfy the needs of others.  
His son, heir to both kinds of treasure, was sixteen years old. **(30–34)**

His fame spread across the earth.  
Moon-faced maidens with voices sweet as melody  
praised him among their companions,  
calling him Murukaṉ himself when they saw him;  
and women lovingly celebrated his gracious beauty.  
His name was Kōvalaṉ. **(35–39)**

The two great families rejoiced  
to see the wedding ornaments of the pair  
on one magnificent day.  
They seated jewel-adorned women upon the necks of elephants  
and announced the marriage throughout the great city. **(40–44)**

Then drums proclaimed it; auspicious instruments thundered;  
conches sounded in due order.  
The white parasol rose as though a king were setting forth,  
and the auspicious procession moved through the wide city. **(45–49)**

Beneath a blue canopy,  
under a pearl-flowered wedding pavilion  
whose diamond-studded pillars bore hanging garlands,  
Kōvalaṉ and the maiden of the constancy of the star Rohiṇī—  
while the moon moved in the sky beside the constellation of the Chariot—  
were guided by an aged brahmin along the Vedic rite  
and circled the sacred fire.  
What austerity had the eyes of those who saw them performed to earn such a sight? **(50–53)**

Women came bearing perfumes and flowers, their bodies radiant;  
some spoke blessings, some sang, some cast sidelong glances;  
some bore sandal paste and incense, some wore swaying garlands;  
some had young uplifted breasts, some carried powdered lime;  
some held lamps and ritual vessels, some spread germinating grain;  
some carried rows of sprout-filled pots, all smiling like opening buds. **(54–59)**

Like bright, fragrant flowering vines,  
their hair spread wide with blossoms.  
“May they never part in love;  
may their embracing hands never loosen;  
may all evil depart,” they prayed,  
scattering small flowers in benediction. **(60–64)**

They seated upon the auspicious bridal couch  
the woman who was like Arundhatī in the beautiful world.

And they prayed that the fierce-speared Cōḻa,  
whose tiger emblem was planted on this side of the Himalaya  
with the golden-crested mountain on the farther side as its companion,  
might roll his peerless wheel of sovereignty everywhere. **(65–68)**

# 2. The Canto Establishing the Virtue of Domestic Life

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

In the prosperous great city,  
rich in merchants and in royal splendour praised by speech,  
wealth never failed to supply all needs  
even if the whole roaring-sea world should gather there.  
Rare goods from hospitable foreign lands, **(1–5)**

and vast commodities heaped together,  
were brought and amassed by ship and caravan.  
There lived wealthy families whose noble line had never declined—  
people who had completed the rare austerity that wins such fortune,  
seeming like the blessed inhabitants of Uttarakuru. **(6–10)**

The lotus-eyed bride and her beloved lord  
sat upon a gem-legged couch fashioned as though by Maya,  
on the middle floor of a lofty mansion.

The fragrance of field-flowers—red water-lily, āmpal,  
full-blown kuvalai, bud-sheathed lotus humming with bees— **(11–15)**

mingled with the scent of neighbouring garden flowers:  
the great white unfolding petals of screw-pine,  
garlanded mādhavi creeper, and champak grove.  
Bees, after choosing and drinking their pollen,  
entered the curls beside the women’s bright faces, **(16–20)**

then wandered seeking the proper season.  
Through the little openings of patterned windows,  
set like rows of gems in the evening mansion,  
the fragrant south wind entered together with the bees.  
The lovers saw it, rejoiced, and grew deeper in love. **(21–25)**

They climbed to the open terrace of the tiered mansion  
where Summer sat enthroned with flower-arrows of fragrance.  
On a sweet flower-bed left for bees to drink,  
where sugarcane and creepers were painted upon broad supports,  
they seemed like the Sun and Moon together, **(26–30)**

whose rays illuminate the whole ancient ocean-girt world.  
As bees opened their mouths and the long moonlight spread,  
a broad garland of white-petalled jasmine  
and a full-chain of red water-lilies slipped from order.  
Necklace and garland mingled helplessly together. **(31–35)**

Looking upon her sacred face with love that could not end,  
Kōvalaṉ spoke this boundless praise:

“The infant Moon, though the gods praise  
the rare beauty of its perfect crescent,  
is truly your own sibling; therefore the Great One  
has fittingly given it to become your radiant brow. **(36–41)**

Because it is the custom to give weapons  
to those who excel in battle against their foes,  
the bodiless god of love has given you  
his single great sugarcane bow  
as your pair of dark, curved eyebrows. **(42–45)**

Because you appeared before the deathless nectar,  
the king of the gods gave you his divine guardian weapon  
to stand between them as your waist.

And did the six-faced Lord—though he had no right  
to grant even one share—give the single long, radiant spear  
seen at the end of his form as two rain-filled, red-cornered eyes in your face? **(46–52)**

The peacock of great dark plumes and jewel-like colour,  
defeated by the beauty of your complexion, withdrew into the cool forest;  
the swan, overcome by your gentle, noble gait,  
retreated into flower-thick fields of good water. **(53–56)**

Only the little green parrot deserves pity:  
though flute, yāḻ, and nectar were mixed together  
and defeated by your childish speech,  
it has learned to dwell beside you  
and will not leave your flower-soft hand,  
O maiden of graceful gait. **(57–61)**

O fragrant-flower garland!  
Why have those who adorn your beauty  
given you any ornament besides  
your own stainless auspicious loveliness?  
Why, beyond a few flowers in your abundant dark hair,  
have they added a garland whose blossoms open to light? **(62–66)**

Why, beyond fragrant smoke of nāṉam and fine akil,  
have they brought musk-sandal paste?  
Why, beyond the painted *toyyil* between your sacred breasts,  
have they added a single strand of pearls? **(67–70)**

Why have they adorned you with these,  
making pearl-sweat bud upon the moon of your face  
and troubling your slender waist?

O flawless gold! O right-spiralled pearl!  
O fragrance without blemish! O sugarcane! O honey!  
O rarest image of beauty! O medicine for my precious life!  
O noble, innocent daughter of the great merchant clan! **(71–76)**

Shall I call you a jewel not born within a mountain?  
Shall I call you nectar not born among the waves?  
Shall I call you music not born within the yāḻ?  
O maiden of long, dark, flowing hair—what shall I call you?” **(77–80)**

Thus he praised her with many inexhaustible words.  
Exulting beside the maiden with swaying flower-clusters,  
the garlanded one passed his days in delight.

Some years went by. Then, so that Kaṇṇaki—  
the great-natured lady whose hair sounded with hanging garlands— **(81–85)**

might be seen to flourish in irreproachable companionship,  
in the service of righteousness,  
in the generous household life that welcomes guests,  
and in the dignity of a distinctive prosperity,  
their rightful kin established the two of them  
in a separate home of their own. **(86–90)**

## Veṇpā

They joined hands and became one,  
as though two fragrant substances had mingled completely;  
as a loving husband and wife,  
they attained every pleasure whose name does not perish—  
yet stood upon the earth like those who had understood  
that nothing in it endures.

# 3. The Canto of the Dance Debut

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

By the grace of the sacred sage of the divine great mountain,  
the heavenly women of astonishing excellence—  
who, together with Indra’s son, had received a curse  
and were freed from it through the consecration of the *talaikkōl*—  
were born on earth without loss of their distinction. **(1–5)**

Among them was the broad-shouldered maiden Mādhavi,  
whose curling hair loosened pollen.  
For seven years she trained so that not one of the three—  
dance, song, and beauty—should be deficient;  
and when she reached the age of twelve, **(6–10)**

she desired to display them before the king of the sea-girt realm.

She knew the grammar of the two kinds of theatre,  
joined the many forms of dance to their proper conventions,  
and clearly mastered, according to rule,  
the eleven dances, their songs, and their percussion. **(11–15)**

When dance, song, melodic movement, and rhythmic measure  
were to be joined and demonstrated,  
she understood the several uses of *piṇṭi*, *piṇaiyal*,  
*eḻirkai*, and *toḻirkai*.  
When a dance was performed, she knew how to remove from a *vāram* sequence  
the hand-gesture proper to *kūṭai*, **(16–20)**

and from a *kūṭai* sequence the hand-gesture proper to *vāram*;  
how to keep *piṇṭi* gesture out of pure dance,  
and dance-gesture out of *piṇṭi*;  
and how to perform *kuravai* and *vari* without confusing them.  
She trained with a master fitted to teach dance. **(21–25)**

Her music-master set song to fitting music  
with yāḻ, flute, metrical pattern, voice,  
low-toned *taṇṇumai* drum, and dance;  
he directed the proper themes for both *vari* and dance,  
and maintained the classical sound of the cultivated tradition. **(26–30)**

He had an intelligence that understood without fault  
every sound of that cultivated tradition.  
When the poet’s intention, the arrangement of dance,  
and the sectional song were to be demonstrated,  
he unfolded them through blameless learning. **(31–35)**

The poet-composer too belonged to an unchanging lineage.  
He knew all Tamil so that the whole Tamil land  
within the roaring ocean might recognise his mastery.  
He observed well the two dramatic systems—  
*vēttiyal*, for kings, and *potuviyal*, for the public. **(36–40)**

He understood the music-master’s altered melodic setting,  
and fixed the poem to it without violating tradition.  
He knew the words of reproach composed by rivals,  
yet was a good poet whose tongue never faltered. **(41–44)**

The master of the *taṇṇumai* knew, without confusion,  
dance, song, music, Tamil,  
mode, melodic style, rhythmic measure, *muṭam*,  
and the classical tradition.  
He enriched the *kūṭai* tempo without defect,  
and carefully restrained the *vāram* tempo. **(45–49)**

In the *vāram* he had thus controlled,  
he listened for the concord of yāḻ, flute, and resonant voice;  
knowing precisely the signs of sharp-nailed hand-technique,  
he joined them without allowing excess in either expansion or restraint,  
and executed the patterned stroke without distortion. **(50–55)**

The flute-player understood how to combine,  
according to the stated rules, both *cittira* and *vañcaṉai* technique.  
Without confusion he produced the four *varttanai* movements,  
and could hear whether the two strings, *kural* and *iḷi*,  
were raised in exact consonance. **(56–60)**

Knowing the beat-pattern marked by the mode-tuned drum,  
he remained in accord with the *taṇṇumai* master.  
Setting the coloured tonal ground upon the yāḻ,  
he expanded what had emerged naturally in the singer’s melody,  
followed what was yet to come, **(61–65)**

moved it delightfully, and brought it into musical order.  
He developed the *vāram* tempo without harm  
and played every syllable of the twofold tonal ground  
without deviation. Such was the flute-master. **(66–69)**

To establish the seven *pālai* modes  
within the fourteen-stringed, well-ordered system,  
the yāḻ-master took the strong portion of *tāram*,  
the soft portion of *kural*,  
and made *kaikkiḷai* arise from the true branch-string. **(70–74)**

He removed the portion belonging to *kaikkiḷai*,  
gave the unfailing *tāram* to *viḷari*,  
and followed the progression of related strings.  
When a branch perceived the fading of its own branch, it joined her;  
the other notes too entered by the same kinship. **(75–79)**

With *uḻai* and *iḷi* above and *kaikkiḷai* below,  
the ancient *cempālai* mode arose.  
Making the end the beginning, without departing  
from the order in which each note properly emerged,  
he produced *paṭumalai*, *cevvāḻi*, and the rare *pālai* modes. **(80–84)**

After *kural* had changed by rightful relation into another *kural*,  
the sequence shifted again in the same proper order.  
Beginning with *iḷi*, the pleasing tonal relations appeared;  
*viḷari* extended above *kōṭi* as *cem-pālai*.  
From the long-established musical order, he brought together  
all paired strings that possessed mutual consonance. **(85–90)**

On the yāḻ, the *pālai* weakened by movement to the left;  
on the flute, *kōṭi* weakened by movement to the right.  
With a learned master who joined strength, softness, and equilibrium  
so that all three shone,  
they prepared the performance ground  
without departing from the nature prescribed by treatises. **(91–95)**

They chose a faultless site upon the earth.  
From a tall bamboo grown on a sacred high mountain,  
whose joints stood one span apart,  
they made the measuring rod for the stage.  
By the canonical rule, the rod measured twenty-four finger-breadths. **(96–100)**

The stage was seven rods wide, eight rods long,  
and one rod high.  
The space between the upper beam and the stage-planks  
was four rods,  
and it shone with two proper entrances. **(101–105)**

Upon the completed stage, worshippers might bow and praise.  
Images of the guardian spirits were painted and placed above.  
Fine lamps were raised so that the shadows of the pillars fell outward.  
They arranged the one-faced curtain, the two-faced curtain,  
and the concealed-entry curtain. **(106–110)**

From the painted canopy they hung  
pearl garlands renowned in song, loops, and festoons.  
In that rare and skilfully prepared theatre,  
they took a strong shaft from the victorious white parasol  
captured by a great-famed king from a defeated ruler. **(111–115)**

They set nine gems at every joint,  
and covered the spaces with plates of refined Jāmbūnada gold.  
In the palace of the white-parasol king,  
they worshipped it as Jayanta, son of Indra,  
and consecrated it as the revered *talaikkōl*. **(116–120)**

They bathed it in holy water carried in a golden vessel,  
then adorned it with a garland.  
On an auspicious day, officers wearing golden ornaments  
received it from the broad hand of the royal elephant-rider  
and praised it. Drums sounded and many instruments roared. **(121–125)**

The king and the Five Great Councils  
circled in chariots and presented it beneath the royal parasol.  
After the procession through the city,  
they entered the theatre and placed it in front.  
The musicians stood in proper order  
according to the unbroken seating convention. **(126–130)**

Mādhavi stepped first with her right foot, mounted the stage,  
and, as custom required, reached the right-hand pillar.  
The *tōriya* women, heirs to the ancient convention,  
had entered by the same rule and stood beside the left pillar.  
To display the splendour of the ordered performance  
and remove every defect, they sang the two *vāram* songs in sequence. **(131–136)**

At the close of the song, all the assembled instruments began.  
The yāḻ followed the flute;  
the *taṇṇumai* followed the yāḻ;  
the *muḻavu* followed the *taṇṇumai*;  
and the *āmantrikai* sounded together with the *muḻavu*. **(137–142)**

With no interval between *āmantrikai* and the other instruments,  
they formed a rhythmic circle containing two strokes.  
They completed eleven such measured circles.  
Without deviating from the received sequence,  
the ensemble subsided with the final *antara-k-koṭṭu*. **(143–147)**

Then, avoiding excess, they set out the *vakkāṇam*.  
Upon the *pālai* mode established in its divisions,  
they clearly understood the measure in which one of four was omitted:  
measuring three and striking one,  
they carried the *kūṭai* movement through five circles. **(148–152)**

After the incoming *vāram* had followed and blended,  
they completed the patterns of six and four in that order.  
As in the stated principle of five,  
they again expanded the same sequence. **(153–156)**

Mādhavi, like a golden flowering creeper shaped by perfect art,  
held faithfully to the laws of dance and displayed them all.  
Therefore the protecting king,  
without departing from the custom due to the garlanded maiden,  
granted her the *talaikkōl* and raised her to the foremost rank of performers. **(157–161)**

By prescribed custom she received, for each performance,  
one thousand and eight *kalañcu* of gold.  
The garland, valued at that sum—  
one hundred and ten counted in groups and completed at eight—  
was declared worth that splendid green-gold price. **(162–165)**

“Whoever buys this garland shall be a fitting lord for our creeper,” they said.  
They gave it to a hunchbacked woman with deer-like eyes  
and stationed her in a street frequented by the city’s wealthy young men,  
as though she were offering it for sale. **(166–169)**

Kōvalaṉ bought the garland of Mādhavi, whose long eyes were like great flowers.  
He entered the pleasure-house with the hunchback  
and, united with Mādhavi, spent the night bewildered and overcome.  
His desire would not release him.  
He forgot his own faultless and noble home. **(170–175)**

## Veṇpā

Numbers and letters; the five divisions of literary Tamil;  
the four great modes; and the eleven dances founded upon mode—  
gold-braceleted Mādhavi of flower-filled Pukār  
brought them all upon the stage  
and carried them through by the power of her voice.

# 4. The Canto Celebrating the Evening

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

“I do not see the mighty bearer of the peerless wheel,  
who spread his wide rays and ruled the whole world.  
Where is the beautiful lord Moon,  
who unfolds lovely moonlight in the fair sky?”

So the earth-maiden, clothed in wave-filled seas,  
grew pale in all her directional faces;  
tears filled her red-lotus eyes,  
and dew trembled over her whole body. **(1–7)**

At that grievous hour, as she wandered bereft of her sovereign,  
even established citizens placed their hands upon their heads.  
Evening seized one portion of the land  
along with bands of lawless subjects—  
like a visiting minor king who occupies a realm  
when the victorious monarch and his army are absent. **(8–12)**

Those abandoned by their low-voiced companions  
entered solitary sorrow;  
those united with their lovers  
entered festive delight.

Cowherds placed their mouths to tender flutes  
made from budding jasmine creepers. **(13–16)**

Six-legged bees cast off their small vexation;  
the young south wind scattered the fragrance of wrapped buds through the streets.  
Women with bright bangles raised jewel-lamps.  
Thus evening came and settled over the wealthy ancient city. **(17–20)**

Though young, the crescent Moon belonged to the line  
of southern kings famed for crushing enemy rulers.  
It appeared in the evening sky,  
drove away the petty force of sorrowful dusk,  
spread milk-white rays without swerving from its nature,  
and ruled the starry realm beneath its silver lamp. **(21–26)**

In chambers where domestic jasmine and mallikai opened  
over many-flowered beds,  
red-coral girdles rose across shapely waists,  
and fine-cloth waist-belts trembled with exertion.  
Upon long moonlit terraces,  
lovers exchanged union and feigned quarrel. **(27–32)**

Mādhavi adorned herself to meet Kōvalaṉ  
with a heart eager for love.

And throughout the city,  
white camphor from the western quarter mingled  
with dark akil wood from the east;  
sandal born in the southern mountain was rubbed  
upon broad stone born in the northern mountain. **(33–38)**

Thick lotus petals, pollen-heavy blossoms,  
lovely kuvalai and great red water-lily flowers,  
green-leaf garlands, heavy necklaces,  
and particles of fragrant powder were mixed together  
and spread upon rich flower-beds. **(39–43)**

Many lovers, overcome by the gentle breeze,  
rested within the chests of their beloved men;  
blue-lily-eyed women entered joyful sleep.

But Kaṇṇaki was otherwise.  
She removed the jewelled anklets from her lovely red feet;  
the girdle left her soft-clothed waist. **(44–49)**

She drew no vermilion design upon the courtyard of her breasts.  
She delighted in no ornament beyond the sacred marriage ornament.  
She cast off her curved earrings, and her ears hung bare.  
Small beads of sweat spread upon her moon-bright face. **(50–53)**

Her long red-carp eyes forgot their collyrium.  
Her coral-bright brow lost its tilakam.  
Her white radiant smile had lost Kōvalaṉ.  
Her dark, kohl-black hair forgot its dressing oil.  
Kaṇṇaki’s heart was helpless. **(54–57)**

Other women separated from their lovers  
breathed like birds beside a blowing furnace  
and withdrew into themselves.  
They did not enter the summer chamber,  
but shut the little windows of the winter room. **(58–61)**

Sandal garlands from Malaya and jewel-pearl necklaces  
did not touch their blossoming breasts.  
They did not lie upon flower-beds  
of bowl-shaped kuvalai and cool red water-lily. **(62–65)**

They stretched themselves upon paired cushions  
stuffed with the soft down of mating swans,  
yet gained no proper sleep.  
In times of quarrel with their wealthy lords,  
they had once thrown away the central nose-jewel  
and driven the outer earring from place; **(66–69)**

now their once-unshaken hearts were shaken.  
Their long eyes, rising and turning toward the side,  
poured pearls of lament.

Until dawn—when the soft-walking swan moved upon the good-water pond;  
when the āmpal breathed fragrance;  
when cool, pollen-rich lotus mouths opened red  
beneath dark, sand-like hair; **(70–74)**

when tune-voiced bees sang the mode of pain;  
when kuvalai eye-flowers opened to sight;  
when bird-mouth drums and the sharp-mouthed conches  
of spotted-feathered elephants sounded in due succession;  
and when the sleeping city beside the mighty waters awoke— **(75–79)**

until night gave way to day,  
the young god of love did not sleep  
through midnight or daylight.  
Bearing fragrant flower-arrows and a sugarcane bow,  
and carrying his victorious makara-banner,  
he wandered everywhere.  
Thus the city’s guard was exceedingly strong. **(80–84)**

## Veṇpā

To those united, the night spread cool shade;  
to those apart, it burned like heat.  
Like the protecting king’s white parasol,  
the moon of the sky unfolded its light  
over both Mādhavi, joined with her lover,  
and Kaṇṇaki, left alone,  
and made the flowers open.

# 5. The Canto in Which the City Took Up Indra’s Festival

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

The earth-maiden’s garment was the wave-filled sea;  
mountains were her breasts, the great rivers upon them her necklaces,  
and rain-clouds her hair.  
Across the vast expanse of her eyes,  
the darkness that conceals pleasure withdrew.  
The Sun appeared upon the crest of the lofty eastern mountain **(1–5)**

and spread blossoming rays of brilliant light that made the world shine.

In Maruvūr-pākkam stood open-terraced houses, broad warehouses,  
and mansions where deer-eyed lovers dwelt;  
the quarter of Yavana foreigners—men ignorant of useful Tamil—  
whose strange speech checked passers-by near the harbour mouth; **(6–10)**

and the glittering waterfront where migrants from countries  
whose wealth arrived by ship lived mingled together.

There were streets where merchants moved selling  
colours, lime, cool fragrant sandal paste,  
flowers, incense, and blended perfumes; **(11–15)**

quarters of subtle-handed weavers  
who made patterned fabrics from silk, wool, and cotton thread;  
and wide streets where wealth lay bewilderingly abundant—  
fine cloth, coral, necklaces, akil wood,  
spotless pearls, gems, gold, and heaps of rare jewels—  
with no one knowing the limit of their measure. **(16–21)**

There was the grain street, where grain of every recognised variety  
was piled beside goods divided by class.  
There were sellers of rice-cakes, cooked foods, and toddy;  
fishermen who sold fish; dealers in white salt;  
butcher-cooks, perfume vendors, and traders in many kinds of fat;  
and places crowded with oilmongers and meat-sellers. **(22–27)**

There were workers in bronze vessels and makers of copperware;  
carpenters who shaped wood and black-handed iron-smiths;  
painters who worked for the eye and modellers who built in clay;  
goldsmiths and makers of fine ornaments;  
tailors and craftsmen who stitched leather. **(28–32)**

People multiplied faultless crafts  
on cloth-panels and woven mats;  
great hereditary *pāṇar*, rare to obtain,  
played without error the seven notes beginning with *kural*  
upon flute, yāḻ, and voice, and demonstrated every melodic path. **(33–37)**

Maruvūr-pākkam shone without blemish  
with many other workers skilled in small handicrafts  
and in the trades of others.

In Paṭṭinap-pākkam were the royal avenue,  
the chariot-banner street, the market-platform street, **(38–41)**

and mansion-lined quarters of great merchant clans;  
the dwellings of Vedic scholars;  
households of hereditary farmers, eminent in conduct;  
physicians learned in the science of life; astrologers who calculated time;  
and many specialised communities that understood the divisions of knowledge. **(42–45)**

There were gem-piercers skilled in setting sacred stones,  
and bracelet-cutters in a broad, great street;  
bards, genealogists, panegyrists,  
timekeepers, auspicious performers,  
palace courtesans, dancing women, **(46–50)**

flower-selling maidens and female attendants;  
practised musicians and players of many instruments;  
jesters and other groups arranged by occupation.  
There were riders of fierce horses, elephant mahouts,  
long-chariot drivers, and stern-eyed warriors, **(51–55)**

all quartered in a broad barracks surrounding the outer area.

Thus Paṭṭinap-pākkam, praised in song,  
was filled with eminent people of exalted distinction.

Between the two great quarters—  
like the contested ground between two mighty kings— **(56–60)**

stood the day-market, beneath a grove of closely planted trees  
whose trunks were tied to form shop-fronts.  
The cries of sellers and buyers  
remained there without interruption.

When the full-moon day of the month of Cittirai arrived, **(61–64)**

at the richly built altar of the guardian spirit  
who had come by command of Indra, king of the gods,  
to remove whatever danger threatened the victorious-speared king,  
they poured boiled pulse, sesame cakes, fatty meat mixed with rice,  
flowers, incense, and *poṅkal*. **(65–69)**

Women of the *tuṇaṅkai* and *kuravai* dances  
entered divine possession and danced.  
Elderly women, speaking with the power of the goddess  
though clothed in women’s form, raised a chant:

“May hunger, disease, and enmity depart  
from both realms of the great king;  
may rain and abundance pour forth.” **(70–75)**

After the sound of those women had passed,  
war-loving heroes from Maruvūr  
and battle-equipped men from Paṭṭinap-pākkam  
advanced before the full-sacrifice altar, crying:  
“May what threatens the fierce king be removed!  
May the strength of those who make this offering have no limit!” **(76–80)**

Men with stone-hurling slings,  
with scarred hides bound across their waists,  
and with ranks of many spears touching their bodies,  
roared and entered the ritual ground.  
Black heads with fierce, reddened eyes,  
like warriors who had fought to the last in a terrible battle, **(81–84)**

were set upon the good sacrificial altar:  
“May the victorious king possess triumph!”

With the thunderous cry of the altar that consumed living sacrifice,  
and with hide-eyed war drums,  
they offered the great oblation to heaven. **(85–88)**

Tirumāvaḷavaṉ, fierce in his love of war,  
found no worthy opponent anywhere in the two lands.  
He appointed an auspicious day for sword, parasol, and hide-eyed drum to depart:  
“Let them go and find enemies!  
Let the strong shoulders of mine be known throughout the earth.” **(89–94)**

On that day he set forth toward the meritorious northern quarter.  
With unshaken resolve and overflowing desire,  
he reached the mountainous barrier that seemed to say,  
“This formidable range has turned back your enemy.”  
He engraved his tiger upon the brow of the Himalayan peak  
where the immortals dwell, and turned homeward. **(95–98)**

For him, the king of the good Vajra country, girdled by great waters,  
had paid tribute with a victorious pavilion;  
the sword-edged ruler of fine Magadha  
had given a ceremonial hall after defeat in war;  
and the king of Avanti, rejoicing, had presented  
a lofty and traditional gateway-torana. **(99–104)**

Though fashioned of gold and gems,  
their workmanship followed a tradition unknown even to subtle craftsmen:  
Maya himself had made and given them in ancient times  
in return for aid received by their ancestors,  
and thus their excellence dispelled sorrow.  
Joined together, they formed a rare hall praised by the noble. **(105–110)**

There too stood the *Veḷḷiṭai Maṉṟam*,  
where bales bearing painted letters and the names of foreign merchants  
lay at the shop entrances, free of black-barred gates  
or the guards of their owners;  
for if anyone dared steal, the stolen load would mount upon his head  
and would not come down until he circled the hall—  
a terror that made even inwardly dishonest men tremble. **(111–117)**

There was the *Ilañci Maṉṟam*,  
where hunchbacks, dwarfs, the mute, the deaf,  
and those whose bodies were ravaged by disease  
bathed, walked around the hall,  
and emerged with flawless form and good colour. **(118–121)**

There was the hall of the tall stone whose shadow reached the ground,  
where those crazed by deceit,  
those trembling in anguish after poison,  
those pierced by the cruel fangs of flame-mouthed snakes,  
and those tormented by ankle-eyed spirits  
circled, worshipped, and were freed from suffering. **(122–127)**

There was the *Bhūta Square*.  
Its spirit thundered so loudly that the cry travelled four *kātam*:  
“Those who conceal vice beneath false asceticism;  
women who hide wickedness beneath outward decorum;  
treacherous ministers; those who desire another’s wife;  
false witnesses and slanderers—  
all such people fall into the noose in my hand!”  
Then it struck and devoured them. **(128–134)**

There was the *Pāvai Maṉṟam*.  
If the royal sceptre bent,  
or if the council that proclaimed justice  
twisted the authoritative text and clung to one side,  
the image standing there would shed tears of reproach  
without speaking a word. **(135–138)**

At all five halls, praised by the excellent  
who understood the nature of truth,  
they offered rare sacrifices. **(139–140)**

They raised the fragrant drum of the Vajra shrine  
upon the neck of a caparisoned elephant.  
At the temple where Airāvata, king of white elephants, was manifest,  
they announced the final observance of the festival’s commencement.  
At the shrine of the wish-granting celestial tree,  
they raised the long auspicious flag until it touched the sky. **(141–146)**

Every gateway of the tall mansions shone  
with green-gold platforms on coral-clustered legs,  
inlaid with emerald and diamond;  
with makara arches bearing rows of lustrous pearls  
within the opened mouths of *kimpuri* figures; **(147–151)**

with flawless green-gold toranas engraved with auspicious signs;  
with full ritual pots, shining trays,  
maiden-lamps, green-gold banners,  
white-haired fly-whisks, and fragrant powder.  
All these crowded the streets in fitting order. **(152–156)**

The Five Great Councils and the Eight Great Assemblies,  
royal princes and merchant princes,  
riders of swift horses, troops of elephants,  
and warriors mounted in racing chariots  
gathered there together. **(157–160)**

In the wide royal enclosure where the king’s majesty was exalted,  
they prayed: “May the renowned king possess victory!”

From the great, pollen-rich bathing-place of the cool, fragrant Kāviri,  
they carried sacred water in golden pots—  
one thousand and eight rulers of the earth  
who protected the living beings of the vast world. **(161–166)**

While earth stood amazed and heaven wondered,  
they bathed the king of the gods in the excellent water.

At the temple of the Great One whose body is unborn;  
at the splendid temple of red-speared, six-faced Murukaṉ;  
at the temple of the strong, fair-bodied Baladeva; **(167–171)**

at the temple of the tall, dark-bodied Viṣṇu;  
and at the temple of the garlanded king beneath the white parasol—  
the fire rites of the four Vedas were performed on one side,  
without departing from the truth taught by the ancient First One. **(172–175)**

On another side, the four classes of gods, the eighteen divine hosts,  
and distinct deities manifested in their several forms  
received festivals appropriate to their divisions. **(176–178)**

Elsewhere stood the monasteries of the righteous,  
the institutions that guarded virtue,  
and charitable establishments outside the city walls;  
learned teachers expounded noble conduct there. **(179–181)**

In another place, by a special act of grace,  
the banner-charioted king removed the fetters  
of rulers who had opposed him. **(182–183)**

Elsewhere, performers, instrumental musicians,  
learned masters of mode and yāḻ, singers and *pāṇar*,  
created music of immeasurable excellence. **(184–186)**

Throughout the broad festival ground,  
in streets where the eyes of the drums never slept,  
celebration and joy overflowed.

Mādhavi, wearing heavy earrings,  
had not suffered public reproach through separation from her beloved.  
With Kōvalaṉ she wore intertwined garlands **(187–193)**

of house-grown jasmine, mallikai, mayilai,  
bowl-shaped kuvalai, and red water-lily.  
They attained the intoxicated delight of love  
and rested in a fragrant pleasure-grove  
where beautiful flower-buds breathed perfume. **(194–195)**

By day they enjoyed seats in the day-market;  
they entered the fresh fragrance of flower-filled groves,  
where incense and sandal never faded.  
They delighted in the gracious speech of laughing companions. **(196–199)**

As Kōvalaṉ was accustomed to wander  
with voice-trained *pāṇar* and the city’s courtesans,  
so the Malaya breeze wandered through the streets  
with sweet young Summer and low-mouthed bees. **(200–203)**

Had the Moon itself come down to wander there—  
carrying a dark cloud, abandoning the little hare,  
drawing two black carp for eyes and a kumil-flower for a nose—  
because it feared Rāhu, its serpent enemy in the sky? **(204–207)**

Had a heavenly creeper come—  
a woman nurtured into full form by the makara-bannered god of love—  
after drinking the auspicious dew of ambrosial excellence  
from the water-mouthed Moon spread across the long earth? **(208–211)**

Had Lakṣmī entered this prosperous city  
to display its great wealth to the king of both lands?  
For besides flame-coloured *ilavam* and jasmine,  
dark long kuvalai and kumil flowers too had blossomed there. **(212–215)**

Or did a deceitful lotus wander in an *uḷ-vari* disguise,  
seeking a firm companion? **(216–217)**

Did Death, whose split mouth devours countless lives,  
fear to oppose the king’s just sceptre,  
and therefore—without abandoning its terrible work—  
change from manhood into womanhood,  
shape a smiling face in a modest form,  
and wander as a woman,  
plucking a sweet-speaking, stringed yāḻ? **(218–223)**

So strong was the army of the bodiless Love-god  
that it repelled every hostile force that faced it.

Household women of northern-star chastity,  
who had covered their whole bodies with painted-line adornment,  
received their broad-shouldered husbands as honoured guests  
and dwelt with them in disciplined union. **(224–229)**

Yet if the red-cornered, jewel-petalled kuvalai  
of the women’s radiant faces turned away  
before the visiting feast was complete,  
what medicine could this broad earth provide?  
Thus, at fortunate noon, they trembled helplessly. **(230–234)**

Like red water-lilies that tremble,  
their fragrant inner pollen spilling upward  
and their honey flowing out,  
Kaṇṇaki’s dark eyes and Mādhavi’s red eyes  
hid what filled their hearts, concealed it within,  
and shed water. **(235–238)**

Their eyes twitched according to omen—  
Kaṇṇaki’s on the left, Mādhavi’s on the right—  
during the days of the festival of Indra, king of heaven. **(239–240)**

# 6. The Canto of Bathing in the Sea

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

In a flower-grove dripping honey  
upon the broad flank of the great silver mountain,  
a Vidyādhara hero was resting in love’s hospitality  
with his long, dark-carp-eyed beloved.  
He said:

“In a rich city of the southern region, **(1–5)**

this is the day when Indra’s festival is raised.

A vast and mighty spirit once gathered  
a host of swift and violent demons  
and came against the king whose ringing anklets guarded  
the tiger-bannered capital.  
Defeated, the demons withdrew with darkness thick in their hearts  
and hurled deceit against the city from afar. **(6–10)**

Indra, king of the gods, sent that great spirit—  
which turned their deceit back upon them—  
to the lord of the flawless spear.  
Let us see the place where it remains and consumes sacrifice.

Let us also see the ordered Five Halls,  
which the king obtained from the immortals  
after guarding Amarāvatī, **(11–16)**

entrusted to his people, and established upon earth  
in faultless accordance with their noble excellence.

Let us hear Nārada’s refined song upon the vīṇā  
and the *vāram* song of the *tōriya* women.

Because Urvaśī once withheld her dance  
from filling the ears of the thousand-eyed lord, **(17–21)**

she lost her heavenly auspiciousness  
and was cursed: ‘Let your vīṇā remain upon earth.’  
Let us see the dance of this Mādhavi,  
whose serpent-like waist descends from the lineage  
of that heavenly maiden.” **(22–25)**

“O red-lipped, slender-waisted woman,  
let us worship the lord of the immortals.”

He showed her the Himalayan peak,  
the water-rich Gaṅgā, the city of Uñcai,  
the Vidyādhara forest, Veṅkaṭam hill, **(26–30)**

and the fertile Kāviri country whose abundance could not be contained.  
Then they reached Pukār with its spreading flower-gardens.  
Following the prescribed way, he worshipped and showed her each place,  
for he had come to see the joyful festival of the wealthy ancient city. **(31–34)**

First they heard the mode of Māyōṉ,  
the four modes of the Varuṇa spirits,  
and a song to the moon that travels the sky. **(35–37)**

Then, to display the ordered splendour and remove every defect,  
they saw upon Bhāratī’s stage  
the eleven sacred dances:

When the gods prayed for the burning of the three cities,  
and the command was given to release the great fire-tipped arrow,  
Śiva—Umā forming one half of him—danced the *Koṭukoṭṭi*. **(38–43)**

Before Brahmā of the four directions,  
who stood facing the divine chariot,  
Bhāratī performed the vast *Pāṇṭaraṅkam*. **(44–45)**

To overcome Kaṃsa’s treachery,  
the dark-hued Kṛṣṇa danced the *Alliyam*;  
a second dance was his wrestling after conquering the demon. **(46–48)**

In the midst of the great ocean,  
upon a stage of water-waves,  
Murukaṉ—who overcame Sūrapadma’s might—  
danced the *Tuṭi* before the foe. **(49–51)**

After felling the demons’ weapons  
and plunging them into fear,  
he lowered his parasol and danced the *Kuṭai* before them. **(52–53)**

Viṣṇu, who strode the long earth,  
walked through the streets of Bāṇa’s great city  
and danced the *Kuṭam* with a pot. **(54–55)**

Kāma, his manhood transformed into a woman’s guise,  
danced the *Pēṭi*. **(56–57)**

The enchantress, unable to endure  
the cruel deeds of wrathful demons,  
danced upon wooden stilts—the *Marakkāl*. **(58–59)**

When the demons fled from the fierce array of battle,  
the red goddess Lakṣmī danced the *Pāvai*. **(60–61)**

And in the fields beside the northern gateway,  
Indrāṇī danced the *Kaṭaiyam*. **(62–63)**

The eleven dances—each with its own ornaments, doctrine,  
posture, and sacred iconography preserved without change—  
and the proper section of song for each  
were displayed clearly according to rule. **(64–67)**

“This is Mādhavi, descended from the Mādhavi lineage  
of whom I spoke while we sat in the pollen-loosening flower-grove,”  
the eminent Vidyādhara said to his beloved,  
and they watched in delight. **(68–71)**

Nor were they alone.  
Celestials came secretly, unseen by those in the middle air,  
to behold the festival of the heavenly king.  
They watched the dances, costumes, and ornaments to the end. **(72–74)**

Kōvalaṉ, who had sat in the appearance of a lover’s quarrel,  
rejoiced when Mādhavi prepared herself for him.

She washed her long, fragrant hair  
in good water steeped with ten astringents, five perfumes,  
and thirty-two fragrant herbs. **(75–79)**

She dried her flower-soft hair in incense smoke  
and anointed every section with rich musk paste.

She coloured her lovely little red feet with *alattakam*,  
and fitted fine ornaments upon her delicate toes. **(80–83)**

Upon her feet she placed  
*pariyakam*, anklets, *pāṭakam*, *cataṅkai*, and *ariyakam*. **(84–85)**

She fastened clustered ornaments tightly upon her thighs.  
Around her waist she wore thirty-two strands of lustrous pearls,  
and over them a flower-bright silk garment  
whose colour shone like water. **(86–88)**

She placed a lovely neck-piece upon herself  
and pure gem-studded armlets upon her shoulders. **(89–90)**

Upon her forearms she fitted  
patterned *cūṭakam* set with central gems and diamonds,  
red-gold bangles, *pariyakam*, white conch bangles,  
and many coral bracelets,  
all resting properly above the fine hair of her arms. **(91–94)**

Her soft kāntaḷ-flower fingers were covered  
with a curved ring shaped like the split mouth of a vāḷai fish,  
a red-gold ring set with a bright gem,  
and a bow-shaped diamond ring with an emerald base. **(95–98)**

Inside her beautiful neck she wore, together with a pearl necklace,  
a chain, delicate linked ornaments,  
a jewelled cord, and other intricate works. **(99–101)**

A pure, lovely gem-strand descending from its cord  
was arranged to conceal the small of her back. **(102–103)**

In her ears she wore handsome ornaments  
whose moon-shaped design was set at intervals  
with clustered sapphires. **(104–105)**

Upon her dark, cool hair she arranged in splendour  
the divine *utti*, the rich-water right-turning conch,  
and the continuous head-ornaments *toyyakam* and *pullakam*. **(106–108)**

Having given Kōvalaṉ both union and feigned quarrel,  
she sat within the chamber upon a song-worthy couch. **(109–110)**

When the full-moon festival arrived for the ancient city,  
she wished to go with the great stream of people  
and see the sea-games in the petal-opening shore-grove. **(111–114)**

At dawn, when birds cried from the lotus pond  
and the elephant announced the morning watch,  
and when the silver lamp of Venus cut through deep darkness,  
she adorned herself magnificently beside her garlanded lover. **(115–118)**

The sky-generous Kōvalaṉ mounted an *attiri* mule;  
the deer-eyed Mādhavi mounted a carriage. **(119–120)**

They passed through the platform street  
of mansion-filled quarters and heaps of rich treasure  
piled in countless crores.

There, among flower-decorated lamps and gem-lamps,  
auspicious maidservants scattered aruku grass and paddy  
while their ornaments rang. **(121–125)**

Moving on both sides, they passed  
the gracious abode of Lakṣmī  
and entered the centre of the city avenue,  
which had risen in prosperity through the wealth of the makara-bearing sea. **(126–129)**

They reached the evening-market quarter  
where migrants enriched by maritime trade  
raised flags and cried their wares  
beside the wide waves and white sands of the shore. **(130–133)**

There were lamps of merchants selling colours, sandal, flowers, lime,  
and prepared delicacies;  
lamps illuminating the handicraft of skilled artisans;  
lamps burning in the rice-cake shops of grain-dealers;  
black broad lamps on the stands of food vendors; **(134–138)**

bright lamps in toddy-selling women’s shops;  
lamps of fish-sellers at intervals;  
the harbour beacon along the glittering waterfront;  
lamps upon fishermen’s boats with spreading nets; **(139–142)**

unfailing lamps of people from foreign-speaking countries;  
and lamps of guards protecting great cargoes.  
Beyond counting, all gathered together  
and shone upon both sides of the harbour-mouth  
like stars mingled with lightning. **(143–147)**

The shore-grove, lovely in the *neytal* landscape,  
was enclosed by screw-pine hedges  
and appeared like a *marutam* field-wall  
upon the wide, swelling water,  
like a fragrant lotus blossoming upon the sea. **(148–150)**

Flower-creeper maidens joined their companions in play.  
Rows upon rows presented an unblemished spectacle.

Goods of many mountains and goods of many seas  
mingled in abundance among the moored ships. **(151–154)**

Royal princes and their intimate retinues,  
merchant princes and their varied companions,  
women who danced and women who sang,  
and surrounding curtains hung upon the sides  
all gathered there. **(155–158)**

As on the first day when Karikāla Vaḷavaṉ of sky-touching fame  
entered the cool waters,  
people of different appearance and different clamour  
filled the festival ground with distinction. **(159–162)**

At the mouth of the great Kāviri, which wears away the seashore,  
the unrestrained clamour of the four social orders  
crowded the whole space and sounded together. **(163–165)**

Within a fenced enclosure of petal-flowered screw-pines  
that drove away the smell of the sea,  
upon fresh sand beneath the shade of a puṉṉai tree,  
they surrounded the place with painted curtains  
and raised a canopy. **(166–169)**

Upon a white-legged couch set beneath it,  
Mādhavi took from the hand of Vasantamālai—  
who stood in attendance—  
a well-tuned, straight-stemmed yāḻ,  
and sat beside Kōvalaṉ in accordance with their intimacy. **(170–174)**

## Veṇpā

At evening a jewel-like bee slept  
inside the white petal folded within a screw-pine by the sea.  
At morning, when intoxicating honey breathed from the pollen,  
the clear-hued chariot of the beautiful, fierce-rayed Sun appeared.

# 7. Kāṉal Vari — Songs of the Seashore Grove

## Prose introduction

Mādhavi received the yāḻ with reverence.  
It was beautiful as a dark, wide-eyed bride adorned with flowers  
within a painted picture. Its body, stem, tuning-pegs, and strings  
were free from every defect.

Through the eight preliminary procedures—  
*paṇṇal*, *parivaṭṭaṇai*, *ārāytal*, *taivaral*,  
*kaṇṇiya celavu*, *viḷaiyāṭṭu*, *kaiyūḻ*, and *kuṟumpōkku*—  
she awakened its music.  
She resolved every uncertainty regarding the mode.

Her soft kāntaḷ-flower fingers,  
adorned with rings set upon emerald bases,  
spread over the many strings like a swarm of bees.

She listened with her own ears to each effect  
produced by the eight instrumental techniques  
laid down by musicians—  
*vārtal*, *vaṭittal*, *untal*, *uṟaḻtal*,  
rolling in rhythm, clarifying, plucking,  
and striking upon the resonant tonal ground.

Then, as the attendant asked, “What mode shall come next?”  
Mādhavi extended the yāḻ to Kōvalaṉ.  
Looking toward the Kāviri, he began to play  
the River Songs and the *Kāṉal Vari* melodies  
to gladden Mādhavi’s heart. **(1)**

## River Song

O Kāviri, live long!  
Even if the king beneath the moon-like white parasol,  
upright in his red sceptre,  
unites himself with the Gaṅgā, do not quarrel.

Though he unites himself with the Gaṅgā,  
your not quarrelling, O carp-eyed river,  
has taught me what the great chastity  
of noble women truly is.  
O Kāviri, live long! **(2)**

O Kāviri, live long!  
Even if the lord beneath the enduring white parasol,  
whose red sceptre never bends,  
unites himself with the virgin Kumari, do not quarrel.

Though he unites himself with Kumari,  
your not quarrelling, O carp-eyed river,  
has taught me what the great chastity  
of steadfast women truly is.  
O Kāviri, live long! **(3)**

The cries of farmers, the roar of sluice-gates,  
the sound of breaking waters,  
and the rejoicing of those who bathe in your cool flood  
rise together as you move.  
O Kāviri, live long!

That festive roar accompanying your course  
is the wealth of Vaḷavaṉ,  
whose warriors shout without guarding their mouths.  
O Kāviri, live long! **(4)**

## Linked Song — Opening Link

Before the dark-flower, long-eyed maiden,  
he pointed again and again to the sea-god  
and swore a rare oath—then broke it.  
How were we poor women to know  
that he was without righteousness, my lord?

In our city Pukār,  
seeing white, curved conches and pearls  
shining like the wide-rayed full Moon and the host of stars,  
the āmpal opens its folded bud. **(5)**

He became our lover,  
came behind us through the shore-grove  
bearing a gift in his hand,  
then turned into a stranger while we stood begging.  
How were we to know, my lord?

In our city Pukār,  
bees swing without distinguishing  
between women’s eyes and blue flowers  
that blossom in the paired reflection of moonlit water. **(6)**

A murmuring conch, beaten by ancient surging waves,  
came ashore and crawled over the women’s patterned sand,  
ploughing through and destroying their play.  
It sank there, my lord.

A garlanded maiden gently lifted it with soft fingers.  
The kuvalai flowers of her garland narrowed like little eyes,  
and those who passed could not pass on.  
Pukār is our city. **(7)**

## Unheaded Vari

To erase the track made by a right-spiralled conch  
that crawled ashore and ploughed the sand,  
the abundant puṉṉai sheds its flowers  
and covers the shore-grove with fine pollen.

Will the tender, budding breasts of the town  
cure the unbearable disease lodged in me—  
the disease caused by straight carp-eyes  
in a full-moon radiant face? **(8)**

A maiden stands driving birds away  
from flesh-heavy fish laid out to dry.  
As though that were not enough,  
she carries in her hand a fragrant ñāḻal branch  
while swarms of bees roar around it.

I did not know that a goddess dwelt there  
in the flower-scented shore-grove.  
Had I known, I would never have approached. **(9)**

In the courtyard of the net-fishers’ quarter,  
where nets and market-fish are spread to dry,  
she stands holding a flower in her hand.

Another form has been assumed for the purpose:  
cruel Death itself lives there  
in the shape of long, killing-spear eyes.  
I did not know it in the cool, wave-washed grove.  
Had I known, I would never have approached. **(10)**

## Standing Vari

Is that the Moon you see—  
a face upon which Love has finished drawing  
carp, bow, dark cloud, and all his art?

Is it the Moon? Look!  
It lives in the small village of boatmen,  
as though it had fled the serpent in the lovely sky. **(11)**

Are those stain-bright spear-eyes,  
with bangles clashing as she runs on either side—  
or is it cruel Death? Look!

It is cruel Death itself,  
which has taken the form of a soft, innocent maiden  
in the little settlement of the sea-people. **(12)**

Is this a possessing goddess? Look!  
She drives birds from pale fish set out to dry  
and inflicts an anguish of distress upon all who see her.

It is a goddess indeed—  
one who has taken a woman’s body  
with five intermingling divisions of hair  
in the cool shore-grove thick with aṭumpu creepers. **(13)**

## Broken Vari

Fragrant flowers given by the grove;  
fresh scent spread upon the sand;  
flawless sacred speech;  
young, swelling bamboo-like breasts;  
a face like the full Moon;

the broad tract of beautiful sand;  
fragrant flowers opening their perfume;  
the crowded grove;

a tender budding smile;  
a face like the full Moon;  
the young woman’s paired breasts—

## Landscape Standing Vari

Your brothers enter the sea, kill life, and live.  
You enter the body, kill life, and live as well.

Do not destroy the waist that has entered suffering,  
burdening it with fierce breasts  
whose strength cannot be restrained. **(17)**

Your father kills life with his cruel-eyed net;  
you kill life with the net of your long eyes.

Do not destroy my tender, bending waist,  
which sways and collapses like lightning in rain  
beneath your necklace-bearing breasts. **(18)**

Your brothers take their running boats and seize life;  
you seize life with your curved eyebrows.

Do not destroy this small, soft side  
that wastes away beneath its burden,  
without looking upon the greatness  
or suffering of another. **(19)**

## Another song

Grasping a coral pestle in her hand,  
the red-eyed maiden pounds white pearls.

The red eyes of the woman who pounds white pearls  
are no kuvalai flowers—  
they are cruel, cruel indeed. **(20)**

In the shade of the puṉṉai,  
at the mouth of the flesh-smelling surf,

The red eyes of the woman who walks as swans walk

Death, Death itself. **(21)**

the red-eyed maiden drives birds from the drying fish.

The red eyes of the woman driving birds from the drying fish  
are no white spear—  
they are hotter, hotter still. **(22)**

## Another song

Do not approach her, innocent swan;

Do not approach her, innocent swan;

## Prose transition

When long deer-eyed Mādhavi heard these *Kāṉal Vari* songs,  
she thought: “There is some enduring implication here;  
he has become confused about his own state.”

Feigning delight in union,  
she took the yāḻ in a mood of estrangement.  
As though she too possessed a hidden meaning,  
she began to sing the *Kāṉal Vari* mode  
with a voice fitted naturally to music—  
so that the goddess of the land marvelled  
and the people of the wide earth rejoiced. **(24)**

## River Song

With bees roaring richly beside you,  
and wearing a garment of jewel-like flowers,  
you open your dark carp-eyes, sway, and walk.  
O Kāviri, live long!

All this opening of dark carp-eyes, swaying, and walking  
has taught me that your husband’s perfect red sceptre does not bend.  
O Kāviri, live long! **(25)**

Peacocks dance in flower-filled groves;  
cuckoos willingly sing their music;  
and lovely garlands tremble at your side  
as you move. O Kāviri, live long!

All this movement with beautiful garlands beside you  
has taught me the power  
of your husband’s fearsome spear.  
O Kāviri, live long! **(26)**

Long live his fertile country!  
You are the mother who raises it as her child;  
you never cease the great help  
that sustains it through the ages.  
O Kāviri, live long!

Your unceasing flow of life-preserving aid  
is the grace of the fierce noonday Sun—  
the king who rules the ocean-girt world.  
O Kāviri, live long! **(27)**

## Linked Vari

Though the bright pearl does not equal  
the red mouth and jewel-smile  
of the woman with the sword-bright face,  
you come each day like the deluded god of love, saying,  
“This is the pearl drawn from the curving water,” my lord.

Pukār is our city,  
where the swelling sea gives radiant white pearls,

In the quarter of the fierce fisherfolk,  
how were we poor women to know  
that the bangles of a maiden’s red hand  
would betray the man secretly joined to her, my lord?

Pukār is our city,  
where swans, mistaking the full Moon and stars,  
climb flower-heavy puṉṉai branches,

In that quarter where victorious liquor never fails its drinkers,  
how were we to know that you would bring  
a ceaseless disease upon women  
without remaining with them, my lord?

Pukār is our city,  
where waves erase the children’s sand-play,  
and the fisherfolk scoop up wet sand in their hands  
and cast it at the sea,  
while their wound-wet spears rise toward the Moon. **(30)**

## Landscape Standing Vari

He looked at the spotted crab playing with its mate,  
looked at the cluster-flowered shore-grove,  
looked at me—then departed without awareness,  
the lord of the roaring waters.

O woman of five curling divisions of hair,  
I cannot understand his nature. **(31)**

Have he, his cool compassion, and his deer-drawn chariot  
left without thinking of us?  
Let them go and remain away.

O soft-clustered aṭumpu flowers, O swans—  
we cannot forget the one who has forgotten us. **(32)**

O sweet-honey-mouthed *neytal* flower,  
you suffer and cannot sleep,  
like my eyes that lament through sorrow-sharpening evening.

In the dream that comes to you,  
will you see and learn whether the hard-hearted man  
returns to the shore-grove? **(33)**

Clear-water tide,  
you have destroyed every trace  
left by the wheel of his bird-swift, deer-drawn chariot.  
What shall I do?

You have destroyed them, clear-water tide.

O tide that moves to destroy the track

of the lover who accepted us.  
Live long, sea-tide!

You destroyed his track;

Live long, sea-tide! **(36)**

O lord of the sea-shore

The fresh wounds shot into me

After singing in this manner, the jewelled maiden

Evening, you have settled the sweet *pālai*

## Prayer

With these grief-laden words, spoken in rash resolve  
through the bewildering evening that came by the path of fire,  
we worship your flower-feet, great god of the sea:

“Forgive the false oath sworn in the fragrant, flower-filled grove.” **(51)**

## Closing prose

Kōvalaṉ heard her and thought:

“When I sang the *Kāṉal Vari*,  
she fixed her heart upon another man  
and sang, weaving together many deceptive lies—  
this woman of illusion.”

His past action, fastening itself upon the yāḻ-music,  
had now come forward to assume form and yield its result.  
He loosened his embracing hands  
from the woman whose face was like the full Moon.

“The time here has passed; let us rise,” he said,  
yet did not rise together with her.  
Surrounded by his attendants, Kōvalaṉ departed.

The voices of her companions fell silent  
in the pollen-loosening flower-grove.  
With a helpless heart, Mādhavi entered her carriage.  
Without her lover, she returned to her own house.

May the radiant-sword Cōḻa—  
whose war-elephants make the rulers of the vast world bow—  
spread his moon-white parasol  
across the interior of the ocean-girdled great mountain of the earth. **(52)**

# 8. The Canto of Summer

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

In the cool-water good land of Tamilakam,  
whose limits are Veṅkaṭam—the hill of the Tall One—  
and Kumari’s sea, the ocean of the bracelet-wearing maiden,  
there stood lofty Madurai, glorious Uṟantai,  
clamorous Vañci, and wave-resounding Pukār. **(1–4)**

“In these renowned capitals where kings sit enthroned,  
sweet young Summer has arrived  
as the joyful companion of Māraṉ, the king of love.”

So announced the young south wind,  
born of the great sage upon fertile Potiyil. **(5–9)**

It seemed that the army of the makara-bannered youth  
was assuming faultless battle-array.  
The cuckoo, one warrior in that army  
who dwelt among banner-like grove-creepers,  
spoke his command. **(10–13)**

Mādhavi, long-eyed as a great flower,  
had been unable to reunite with Kōvalaṉ  
after his quarrel during the sea-games  
in the petal-opening shore-grove.

Desiring him, she climbed  
the summer chamber on the sky-high upper storey. **(14–18)**

Because pearls of the southern sea  
and sandal from the southern mountain  
were bound by nature to pay tribute to her,  
she held a flawless plectrum  
in the vermilion court between her breasts. **(19–23)**

She received into her hand the yāḻ of unshaken tradition  
and, bewildered, sang a sweet song.

Of the nine sitting postures,  
she assumed the foremost, well-proportioned posture.  
Her right hand’s *patākai* rested upon the stem;  
the four fingers of her left embraced the *māṭakam*. **(24–28)**

She recognised and removed the hostile defects—  
*cem-pakai*, harsh resonance, hollow vibration, trembling,  
and other faults of tone.  
She strung the faultless fourteen-string sequence  
from *uḻai* at the beginning to *kaikkiḷai* at the end. **(29–32)**

She examined the musical relations  
of the four—consonant, branch, hostile, and friendly—  
and listened at the mouths of *kural* and *iḷi*. **(33–35)**

Following canonical order, in groups of five and seven,  
with *uḻai* as beginning and *uḻai* as end,  
and with *kural* as beginning and *kural* as end,  
she tested the four classes of *marutam*:  
inner, outer, neighbouring, and expanded. **(36–41)**

She completed the three modes of movement in due order  
and, in the clear instrumental technique  
that follows the appointed tonal path,  
the flower-creeper maiden entered a mode beyond the main system. **(42–44)**

She took a mature white petal of screw-pine  
from among champak, mādhavi, tamāla, dark-mugai,  
white mallikai with its root,  
red water-lily of lovely petals, and kattikai flowers  
arranged at the season when one blossom answers another. **(45–49)**

The peerless prince who rules the wide earth  
with the single red sceptre of fragrant flower-arrows  
commands that every face in the world  
except one should bow and worship.  
Mādhavi now turned away that one sacred face. **(50–53)**

Mixing thick red *alattakam* paste,  
she used the full bud of a nearby *pittikai*  
as a writing stylus. **(54–55)**

She wrote:

“The prince Sweet Young Summer,  
who unites all living beings with their joyful mates,  
is not alone in injustice.

The lord Moon, appearing at evening  
with the hare upon his tender flank, is unjust too. **(56–59)**

Though he interrupts the time of those who are united,  
and though those who have parted forget their companions,  
it is no wonder that fragrant flower-arrows take good life.  
Know this.” **(60–63)**

With all sixty-four arts accompanying her thought,  
she unfolded in immature, childlike words  
a letter whose syllables were loosened by solitary love—  
words whose tongue spoke outwardly  
of mode and technique while carrying another meaning within. **(64–67)**

Pale in body and afflicted by spreading evening,  
she called Vasantamālai to come.  
“Take every meaning contained in this pure flower-letter  
and give it to Kōvalaṉ; bring him here,” she said. **(68–71)**

Vasantamālai, long-eyed like a spear and lion,  
took the letter and gave it to Kōvalaṉ in the grain-market street.

Kōvalaṉ replied by recalling the eight dramatic *vari* forms  
through which Mādhavi had enacted love before him. **(72–73)**

First was the *Kaṇ-kūṭu Vari*:  
her sword-bright face had gathered  
tilakam, curls, tiny dark bow-like brows,  
kuvalai eyes, kumil nose, and kovvai lips;  
her intoxicated glance arose of itself through love  
and came to meet him without another’s summons. **(74–77)**

Next was the lovely *Kāṇ Vari*:  
like a rain-cloud burdened and suffering,  
a radiant moon with wandering carp-eyes  
opened a sugar-filled coral mouth,  
showed a smile-ground of young pearls,  
said “Come,” and came—then said “Go,” and went—  
the dark, long-eyed woman. **(78–83)**

Then, lamenting the arrival of evening  
and seeing how the sickness in his mind sharpened her weakness,  
she concealed parrot-like speech, innocent-swan gait,  
and joyous peacock grace.  
She came alone in the costume of a spear-eyed maidservant  
and performed the *Uḷ Vari*, entering in disguise. **(84–89)**

Then came the poor *Puṟa Vari*:  
her anklets lamented and her girdle rang;  
her waist could not bear its ornaments.  
With a loving glance, she saw his weakness  
turn into another condition  
and performed while remaining outside. **(90–93)**

In the *Kiḷar Vari*,  
her garland, hair, pollen-covered curls,  
single strand of pearls, broad sacred breasts,  
and lightning waist appeared together beneath her noble brow.  
Small serving-maids carried replies between them;  
she listened to the double meaning spoken on both sides  
in quarrel-bearing words whose interior contained union.  
Her form weakened, her soft hair loosened,  
and she rose transformed. **(94–101)**

In the *Tērcci Vari*,  
during the time of separation,  
she selected and repeatedly told  
her own suffering to each of his relatives. **(102–104)**

In the *Kāṭci Vari*,  
bewildered within a garland where bees opened the flowers,  
she told her condition to everyone who saw her. **(105–106)**

In the *Eṭuttuk-kōḷ Vari*,  
she repeatedly fainted before them  
and was lifted and restored by those nearby. **(107–108)**

“She is a dancing woman; these are the praised conventions  
that belong to such a bracelet-wearing performer,” Kōvalaṉ said.  
He refused the jewelled flower-letter  
written by the ornamented maiden with the sacred face. **(109–112)**

Vasantamālai’s heart withered at the refusal.  
She hurried to the garlanded woman  
and told her what had happened.

“Though he does not come this evening,  
we shall see him in the morning,” Mādhavi said. **(113–116)**

Yet with a helpless heart  
she could not lie upon the flower-strewn couch.  
Long great-flower-eyed Mādhavi remained awake. **(117–118)**

## Veṇpā

Red lotuses open; honey drips from mango shoots;  
young aśoka branches loosen their petals.  
Though clustered Summer has arrived,  
what will happen today to the heart  
of the woman with strong, beautiful spear-eyes?

## Veṇpā

“By command of the bodiless god,  
all who have quarrelled must reunite,” the cuckoo proclaims.

You who blended with the long mode of Summer—look!  
You have made your soft flower-face suffer  
for the mode of the shore-grove.

# 9. The Canto in Which the Dream Was Explained

*Metre: Kali Veṇpā*

Day faded. Throughout the inner city,  
slender-waisted women scattered jasmine buds and matching paddy,  
then lit jewel-lamps at evening  
and assumed the ornament of night.

Long before that time, Mālati had nursed  
the child of her husband’s other wife. **(1–5)**

The milk choked the infant and it died.  
Mālati grieved:

“The brahmin and his wife will accuse me  
of what I did not do  
and will refuse to accept what I truly say.”

Carrying the child, she went from shrine to shrine:  
the wish-granting tree, the white elephant,  
the bright white serpent deities, the eastern gate, **(6–10)**

Sūrya’s temple, the city guardian, the spear shrine,  
the Vajra shrine, the temple of the outer-city spirit,  
the Nigaṇṭha monastery, and the Moon shrine.

Everywhere she pleaded:  
“O gods, remove the affliction that has come upon us!” **(11–14)**

At last she lay down in sorrow before Pāṣaṇḍa Sāttaṉ.  
A young female spirit appeared and mocked her:

“Faultless-looking girl, the gods do not grant boons  
to those who have performed no austerity.  
This is not a lie; it is the truth.  
Give me the corpse in your hand.” **(15–18)**

She seized it from Mālati  
and went into the hanging darkness  
of the cremation-ground enclosure.  
There Iṭākiṉi, a corpse-eating demoness,  
took the child Mālati had placed in her lap. **(19–22)**

Mālati wailed like a peacock struck by thunder.  
Sāttaṉ said:

“Mother, do not burn in anguish.  
Look—your child lives.”

He became that very child  
beneath the shade of a cuckoo-filled grove. **(23–26)**

Without suspicion, the maiden lifted the magical infant,  
wrapped it in her garment,  
and placed it in the mother’s hands.

The child grew into a student  
who followed the pure Vedic scholar  
and crossed to the far shore of celestial learning. **(27–30)**

After the parents died, Sāttaṉ litigated with the heirs,  
performed every duty owed to father and mother,  
and lived there.

He took as wife a woman named Tēvanti.  
One day he told her,  
“Your flower-like, kohl-lined eyes cannot bear this,” **(31–34)**

revealed his undecaying youthful divine beauty,  
and said, “Come to my shrine.”  
Then he disappeared.

Her radiant husband had gone away saying,  
“I shall visit every sacred bathing-place.”  
Tēvanti continually worshipped at his temple,  
praying that he might be brought back to her. **(35–40)**

Thinking that noble Kaṇṇaki, whose excellence could not fade,  
had suffered a similar lack,  
Tēvanti approached her with compassion.  
She scattered aruku grass, small pūḷai flowers, and paddy,  
and blessed her: “May you regain your husband.” **(41–44)**

“I shall not regain him,” Kaṇṇaki answered.  
“My heart is troubled by a dream.

He took my hand, and we entered a great city.  
In that city the townspeople set upon me  
a word that ought never to have been spoken—  
as though they had placed a scorpion upon me:  
that some evil had befallen Kōvalaṉ. **(45–49)**

Hearing it, I argued before the king.  
Then a disaster came upon the king and the city.  
I shall not describe it.

It was like a crime of fire, O woman of close-set bangles.  
What happened to me, what happened to my companion,  
and the good ending I attained—  
if I tell it, it will seem laughable.” **(50–54)**

“You are not bereft by your mother,” Tēvanti said.  
“In a former birth you failed to complete  
a vow owed for your husband. Let that fault be destroyed.

Where the Kāviri meets and troubles the sea,  
in the broad, petal-opening *neytal* grove,  
there are the Soma pool and the Sūrya pool. **(55–59)**

Women who bathe in those waters  
and worship at Kāma’s shrine  
will enjoy happiness with their husbands in this world  
and be reborn in the land of pleasure.

Let us go and bathe there one day.” **(60–62)**

“That is not honourable,” said the jewelled Kaṇṇaki,  
and remained where she was.

Afterwards a young maid came to the doorway,  
appearing like a long-awaited guardian, and said,  
“Our Kōvalaṉ has come.” **(63–66)**

Kōvalaṉ entered the song-worthy chamber  
and saw the wasting body of his bracelet-wearing wife.

“I danced with a deceitful woman of corrupting conduct,” he said,  
“and lost the mountain of ancestral celestial wealth.  
This poverty has brought me shame.” **(67–71)**

Kaṇṇaki showed her radiant face  
and gave a gentle smile.

“I still have my anklets. Take them,” she said.

“Beginning with this anklet,  
I shall recover the wealth lost with all my goods,” Kōvalaṉ replied. **(72–75)**

“I shall go to flourishing, lofty Madurai.  
O garlanded woman, rise and come with me.”

Thus, as the force of past deeds drove them toward their final result,  
they set out at night  
before the dense rays of dawn had spread their feet. **(76–79)**

## Veṇpā

The dream seen by his beloved  
made the words of dark, long-eyed Mādhavi empty.  
Driven by ancient action toward its final consequence,  
he departed in the night  
before the dense rays of dawn had spread their feet.

# 10. The Canto of Seeing the Country

*Metre: Nilai-maṇḍila Āciriyappā*

In the last watch of night,  
when dawn had not yet opened its eye in the sky,  
and the white Moon had left the fish-bright heavens  
while black darkness still remained,  
their hearts were driven onward by past action. **(1–4)**

They passed through the long outer gateway  
of exceptional beauty carved with antelopes, goats,  
swans of pure feathers, and yak-tail standards. **(5–8)**

They circumambulated the temple of jewel-hued Viṣṇu,  
who rested in subtle sleep upon the ornamented serpent. **(9–10)**

They passed seven Indra monasteries,  
where wandering celestial ascetics proclaimed  
the sacred word of the Enlightened One  
beneath the beautiful shade of the green-leaved bodhi  
whose five great branches rose high. **(11–14)**

Then they reached the Arhat sanctuary of the Five Orders,  
where excellent people had gathered—  
people who had renounced flesh-food, kept unfailing vows,  
understood the end of suffering, restrained themselves,  
and realised the nature of truth. **(15–18)**

At the vast celestial hall,  
where the five junctions met and converged,  
beneath the rich cool shade of a golden-flowered *piṇṭi* tree,  
world-renouncing ascetics had set a shining stone platform,  
believing that Cāraṇa sages might come  
for the water festival or the great chariot festival.  
They bowed and circumambulated it. **(19–25)**

Leaving together through the city’s outer passage,  
which resembled a great river descending from a mountain,  
they passed the city wall near the spring-house,  
where Love, together with lovely Summer,  
and the Malaya breeze paid tribute to the king  
with a pavilion of good trees heaped with many flowers. **(26–31)**

They left the cool broad road clothed in low groves,  
passed through the last gateway at the Kāviri’s mouth,  
turned west, and entered  
a flower-grove on the great northern bank  
of the deep-flowing Kāviri. **(32–35)**

After travelling one *kāvatam*,  
they reached the hermitage of Kavunti  
and rested in a grove of flowering trees.

Kaṇṇaki’s creeper-slender waist bent;  
her feet ached; her fragrant abundant hair was disordered;  
and she breathed short breaths. **(36–39)**

Her thorn-like little teeth shone  
as she asked in an innocent, unripe voice,  
“Where is ancient Madurai?”

Kōvalaṉ laughed gently and said:  
“O woman whose hair contains twenty fragrances,  
it is only five or six *kātam* beyond our broad country.” **(40–44)**

There they saw Kavunti Aiyai,  
seated within her enclosure beside the sweet-spoken woman,  
and bowed at her feet.

“You possess beauty, noble lineage, eminent conduct,  
and the vow never to transgress the sacred word of the Great One. **(45–48)**

Why have you left your last threshold  
and come here like people overtaken by distress?” she asked.

“There is nothing to explain, venerable ascetic,” Kōvalaṉ said.  
“I desire to seek wealth in ancient Madurai.” **(49–51)**

“Her little feet, adorned with *pāṭakam*,  
have never ploughed against rough stones.  
You must cross country separated by forests.  
Who understands how difficult this will be for her tender nature? **(52–54)**

This is not fitting. Abandon the journey here.”

But they did not turn back.

After hearing the righteous words  
of one whose stainless learning had rejected cruel speech,  
Kōvalaṉ praised the Enlightened One. **(55–57)**

“My heart too is fixed upon faultless Madurai  
in the good southern Tamil land,” said Kavunti.  
“I shall also go. Come with me.” **(58–60)**

Kōvalaṉ worshipped and praised Kavunti Aiyai:

“Revered Aṭikaḷ, if you grant us your grace,  
you will have relieved the suffering  
of this bangle-shouldered woman.” **(61–63)**

“Kōvalaṉ, see how many dangers lie  
upon the road you have chosen,” she said. **(64–65)**

“If we travel through the flower-filled groves  
so that her tender nature will not endure the sun’s heat,  
we may meet pits dug for roots  
where the earth has fallen open. **(66–69)**

Champak blossoms shed pollen and conceal such pits  
with a false cover;  
they may bring helpless suffering  
to travellers who fail to notice them. **(70–71)**

Those who step aside to avoid fallen flowers  
may collide with ripe jackfruit  
hanging like ancient enemies. **(72–73)**

Turmeric and ginger mingle in tangled enclosures;  
hard seeds from red-fleshed jackfruit  
may strike and hurt the feet. **(74–75)**

Carp-long-eyed woman’s beloved,  
if we travel beside the fields, other dangers arise.

In a flower-fragrant pond,  
a vāḷai fish seized by a water-dog  
may leap sideways through gleaming, confused rice-plots;  
this woman may be startled. **(76–81)**

Honey from crushed cane may spill  
and mingle with pure pond-water surrounded by bees.  
Overcome by unrestrained thirst and confusion,  
she may scoop it up in her cupped hands and drink. **(82–85)**

Kuvalai flowers plucked by reapers  
may lie upon the path with striped, spotted bees resting in them.  
Exhausted by walking and thirst,  
she may unknowingly place her foot upon them. **(86–89)**

Along the banks of rushing water,  
beautiful crabs and snails may be crushed  
under the unavoidable pressure of her feet.  
If that causes painful harm,  
her descending sorrow will be hard to bear. **(90–94)**

There is no path except through field or grove.  
Guide the dark-haired woman carefully;  
recognise each danger and keep her from approaching it.” **(95–97)**

Kavunti took her spotless alms-bowl,  
the cloth carried upon her shoulder,  
and the peacock-feather whisk in her hand.

“May the deity embodied in the meaning of sacred speech  
be our companion upon the road,” she said.  
They began the blameless journey. **(98–101)**

Even if the dark planet smoked,  
or a smoke-bannered comet appeared,  
or radiant Venus travelled into the southern quarter,  
the rain-clouds, mature with pregnancy,  
poured abundance with fierce thunder  
from heights where the wind struck. **(102–105)**

The Kāviri’s fresh flood,  
carrying rich goods born in the western mountains  
and meeting the wealth of the sea,  
pressed through its estuary mouth  
and roared without rest. **(106–109)**

That sound alone could be heard;  
for the water-lifting devices—the *āmpi*, *kiḻār*,  
resonant *ēṟṟam*, and high-water *piḻā*—  
had no need to sound. **(110–112)**

In the green lotus-groves of wetlands  
beside red-rice fields bordered by sugarcane,  
water-hens, loud-voiced herons, red-legged swans,  
green-legged cranes, forest fowl, water-coloured crows, **(113–116)**

*uhu*, *ūral*, *puḷ*, and *putā* birds  
raised many different cries, spreading like the clamour  
of the battlefront between victorious kings. **(117–119)**

Red-eyed black buffaloes, their unwashed hairy bodies  
sunk in fine mud from unploughed hollows,  
rubbed their dripping backs upon haystacks;  
the loosened stacks spilled abundant feed  
from the young threshing enclosure  
upon the ripened heads of red *kavari* rice. **(120–124)**

Black-handed workers and field labourers  
gathered and shouted together. **(125–126)**

Women of the fields, long-eyed like red carp  
and sparing in speech, had removed fragrant flowers  
and pressed perfumed paste into their hair.  
Mud covered their bangle-wearing shoulders and breasts,  
and they appeared splendid in their mire-played costume. **(127–130)**

Having driven away the heat with strong liquor,  
they sang the guests’ song. **(131)**

Those standing beside the plough  
wore broad garlands woven with shining grain,  
rich aruku creeper and kuvalai flowers;  
they worshipped with joined hands  
as though honouring the lord who owns the earth  
and sang the auspicious plough-song. **(132–135)**

Those who had reaped and heaped the stalks  
drove the threshing bulls  
and sang the measuring song of the great red-rice harvest. **(136–138)**

Clear *kiṇai* drummers, proud in spirit,  
raised the joyful music of earth-resounding drums. **(139–140)**

Hearing all this beside the waters  
of the great riverbank, the travellers’ eager hearts  
did not sink into sorrow. **(141–142)**

With the victory of the mighty king  
whose chariot bears the ploughing-tiger banner,  
fragrant smoke from the brahmins’ fire-offerings  
in flame-bright kitchens that nourish the embryo of rain  
covered every sky-high mansion. **(143–146)**

Together with the auspicious settlements of Vedic scholars,  
which looked like mountains wrapped in cloud,  
they saw towns set throughout the country:  
the homes of the children of the Kāviri maiden;  
communities of those who received alms  
and the triumphant houses of those who sustained them;  
and ancient, valiant villages of cultivators. **(147–151)**

Smoke from boiling sugar-mills spread  
and made the towns appear like mountains  
in the clouded sky. **(152–153)**

They travelled no more than one *kāvatam* at a time,  
staying many days; then came one particular day of journey. **(154–155)**

Upon an island-stage in the river,  
within layered groves of trees  
clothed in troops of monkeys,  
they came to a flower-fragrant place  
where heavenly beings might dwell. **(156–159)**

Revered ascetics of undying fame  
who had left Paṭṭinap-pākkam  
had together established a shining stone platform there.  
Cāraṇa sages appeared upon it  
and proclaimed faultless truth—the Dharma of the Great One. **(160–163)**

“May ancient deeds be broken apart!”

Kavunti recognised them; all three fell at their feet. **(164–165)**

Though the sage knew by the lamp of his mind  
the reason for their coming and its manifest consequence,  
he had removed both attachment and hatred  
and become heroic in detachment; therefore he did not grieve. **(166–169)**

“Kavunti of surpassing excellence, behold:

Powerful action does not cease when told to cease.  
The seed once sown comes forward to meet its fruit;  
when it clings at the appointed hour, it cannot be cast away. **(170–173)**

Bodies and lives do not remain together,  
like flames scattered through vast space by a violent wind. **(174–175)**

The Enlightened One is Knower, Righteous One,  
he who has crossed the boundary of knowledge;  
the Concentrated One, Jina, Siddha, Bhagavān;  
First Lord of Dharma, Leader, Dharma itself; **(176–178)**

Reality, Holy One, Ancient One, Sage;  
Victor over anger, God, Lord of the auspicious path;  
Supreme One, Possessor of virtue, Light beyond; **(179–181)**

Truth, Good One, Cāraṇa, Cause;  
Perfected One, Great One, Excellent One, radiant Light;  
Lord, Teacher, Natural Virtue, our King; **(182–184)**

he of unfailing fame, great king of virtues;  
Śaṅkara, Īśa, Self-born, Four-faced;  
source of the sacred limbs, Arhat, sage of grace; **(185–187)**

master of modes, possessor of eight virtues,  
ancient reality without partiality;  
celestial one, first source of the Vedas, shining light. **(188–189)**

Unless one dwells within the light of the Veda he proclaimed,  
the bundle-house of birth will never open.” **(190–191)**

Hearing the true words of the Cāraṇas,  
Kavunti, foremost in austerity, placed her hands upon her head and vowed:

“My ears shall open to no sacred speech  
except the word of wisdom taught by the One  
who has destroyed the three faults. **(192–195)**

My tongue shall utter nothing  
except the thousand and eight names  
of the One who conquered Desire. **(196–197)**

My eyes shall see nothing,  
even if it comes within reach,  
except the paired feet of the One  
who conquered the five senses. **(198–199)**

This worthless body of mine shall touch nothing upon earth  
except the sacred body of the One  
who wears gracious righteousness. **(200–201)**

My two hands shall join and fold in no direction  
except toward the wise and righteous Arhat. **(202–203)**

My head shall bear no ornament upon its crown  
except the flower-feet of the One  
who walked upon flowers. **(204–205)**

My mind shall not turn aside and recite anything else  
except the lordly word of endless bliss.” **(206–207)**

Hearing her praise his harmonious teaching,  
the assembled ascetics rose into the upper air,  
one cubit above the long earth. **(208–210)**

“May the bonds that produce rebirth be destroyed for Kavunti,” they said.

The three worshipped the sages travelling through the unsupported sky  
and bowed, praying, “May our bondage be cut.”  
Then they continued. **(211–213)**

They crossed at a long bathing-place  
beside a water-decked pavilion  
on the great Kāviri within a rain-dark flower-grove. **(214–215)**

The wife, her husband, and the woman of great austerity  
reached the southern bank at a faultless religious settlement.  
They rested in a flower-grove whose ground was surrounded by blossoms. **(216–218)**

A crude courtesan and a man of empty speech  
entered the pollen-rich grove. **(219–220)**

“They look like Kāma and his goddess. Who are they?  
Let us ask and learn,” they said.

To Kavunti, whose body was thinned by fasting, they asked:  
“Who are these people travelling with you along the river-road?” **(221–224)**

“They are my children. They possess human bodies.  
Move away from us; they have suffered greatly,” Kavunti replied. **(225–226)**

“Can people born from the same womb  
live together as husband and wife?  
Tell us, learned woman,” they mocked. **(227–228)**

Hearing the evil words, Kaṇṇaki covered her ears  
and trembled before her husband. **(229–230)**

“These people have ridiculed my flower-garlanded daughter.  
Become old jackals in a thorn-filled forest,” Kavunti cursed. **(231–233)**

The curse born of her austerity bound them immediately.  
Not understanding what had happened,  
they heard the long howl of small jackals. **(234–235)**

The fragrant-garlanded woman and her noble husband trembled.

“Though they departed from the path and spoke improper words,  
their ignorance must be understood,” they pleaded.  
“Venerable ascetic, tell us when those  
who offended in your sacred presence may be released.” **(236–240)**

“Having received a low birth today through ignorance,  
let them hide beside a nocci grove near Uṟaiyūr.  
After suffering their spreading affliction for twelve months,  
let them regain their former forms,” Kavunti said,  
and set the limit of the curse. **(241–245)**

Then Kavunti Aiyai, the wife, and the husband  
entered the outer defensive grove of Uṟaiyūr—  
the city whose broad-eared elephants  
had crushed opposing armies. **(246–248)**

# Concluding Account of Pukār Kāṇḍam

Among the three crowned kings,  
this book has shown those born in the Cōḻa line,  
whose broad arms shine with bracelets:  
their righteousness, valour, and power;  
the exalted character of their ancient, victorious capital; **(1–4)**

its festival-filled distinction and the coming of celestial beings;  
the population and abundance of food  
in their land of unceasing delight;  
the faultless excellence of their divine Kāviri;  
and the unfailing sky that pours fresh rain. **(5–9)**

It has shown the stage, dance, rhythmic measure, and *vari*;  
the *Bhāratī Virutti* that attained widespread fame;  
the landscape-standing and paired-standing *vari*;  
the ordered yāḻ lying in concord;  
the fourteen *cakōṭa* strings and the central *pālai*; **(10–14)**

formation at *tāram*, the modes known by their tonal positions,  
the city chariot, and the radiant melodic style.  
All these, together with many other subjects,  
appear united within a distinctive single design. **(15–18)**

Thus the Pukār Kāṇḍam,  
which has viewed them all in one comprehensive manner, is complete. **(19–20)**

## Veṇpā

Like the Sun that buds and blossoms in the morning,  
and the Moon that rises in the evening, may Pukār live—  
Pukār, garlanded by fame upon the earth,  
whose surrounding ocean is its moat.

**End of Pukār Kāṇḍam.**
