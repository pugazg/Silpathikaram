# 24. The Kuravai of the Hill People

## Prose-song prelude

“We had driven away sparrows and chased off parrots,  
remained upon the mountain, bathed beneath waterfalls,  
plunged into pools, and were returning weary.  
Then beneath the fragrant shade of a mountain vēṅkai  
we saw you standing like Vaḷḷi, trembling in heart,  
having lost one breast. Who are you? Do not be angry with us.”

She answered:

“When mighty past action came forward to take form,  
destroying fragrant Madurai and its king,  
I lost my husband there and came away.  
I am a woman of cruel fate.”

They bowed in fear, raising their paired, bangle-wearing hands.  
While they stood watching, the celestials rained a long shower of flowers  
and took her away together with her husband.

“None in our clan is a great deity like her.  
People of the little settlement! People of the little settlement!  
Take her as your deity, people of the little settlement!

Beneath the good shade of the fragrant-branched vēṅkai,  
on the lower slope of the cliff where coloured waterfalls shine,  
take her as your deity, people of the little settlement!

Beat the *toṇṭakam* drum; beat the little drum;  
blow the curved horn; ring the harsh bell;  
sing the mountain *kuṟiñci* mode; raise fragrant smoke;  
offer flowers; establish the protective boundary;  
praise her again and again; scatter mingled blossoms—

so that for the maiden who lost one breast  
the great mountain may never sleep  
but pour out its abundance.” **(1)**

## Linking words

“Look there, jewelled woman; now look here.  
The mountain waterfall descends,  
scattering black collyrium earth, red mineral powder,  
and dense vermilion dust;  
it flows with fragrance and bears the beauty of Indra’s bow.  
Let us bathe in it.

Let us bathe, friend; let us bathe, friend—  
in the waterfall of the mist-wrapped grove  
belonging to the man who said, ‘Do not fear,’  
yet gave us no good.” **(2)**

“We see no cause at all to quarrel with him:  
this fresh water touched the rocks of his mountain before it came.  
Yet, friend, my heart cannot bear  
that other women should bathe in the fresh water  
that touched the rocks of his mountain.” **(3)**

“We see no cause at all to quarrel with him:  
this fresh water played over the gold of his mountain before it came.  
Yet, friend, my heart cannot bear  
that other women should bathe before us  
in the fresh water that played over his mountain’s gold.” **(4)**

“We see no cause at all to quarrel with him:  
this fresh water played among the flowers of his mountain before it came.  
Yet, friend, my heart cannot bear  
that other women should bathe above us  
in the fresh water that played among his mountain flowers.” **(5)**

## Song prelude

“Come, women; let us speak.  
If we plunge and bathe in the flawless water  
until our kohl-lined eyes grow red,  
let us take up a *kuravai* and sing one song,  
praising the spear that slew the demon in the mighty sea.” **(6)**

It is the spear in the hand of the Lord  
who never leaves glorious Centil, Ceṅkōṭu, White Hill, or Ērakam—  
the bright-leafed white spear  
that entered the broad, dark ocean long ago  
and struck down the great Sūr. **(7)**

It is the spear borne by the peerless One  
who alone possesses six beautiful faces and twelve hands—  
the white spear with which he fought,  
while the king of the jewel-sky praised him,  
until the power of the demons mounted on peacock-faced forms was destroyed. **(8)**

It is the sacred hand-spear of the child  
who drank milk from the divine breasts of six mothers  
in the flower-filled bed of Saravaṇa—  
the long spear that split the chest  
of the mountain-shaped demon  
and destroyed the hill called Krauñca. **(9)**

## Song prelude

“Woman of fine bangles, this is laughable!  
To cure the illness caused by the lord  
of the cool, pepper-growing mountain,  
my mother—knowing nothing—has declared it the work of the kadamba-garlanded god  
and wishes for a possession-ritual;  
she has said, ‘Let the vēlaṉ priest come.’” **(10)**

“Woman of examined bangles, this is laughable!  
If the vēlaṉ comes to cure the illness  
caused by the lord of the great mountain,  
the vēlaṉ is a fool—  
and more foolish than he is the one  
who destroyed the hill called Krauñca.” **(11)**

“Woman whose bracelets fit close upon your hand, this is laughable!  
The vēlaṉ who comes to cure the illness  
caused by the fragrant mountain is a fool;  
and more foolish still would be  
the son of the Lord beneath the banyan,  
if he himself should come.” **(12)**

“Straight-jewelled woman, it is laughable!  
The vēlaṉ comes to cure the burning illness  
given by the chest of the mountain lord.  
The vēlaṉ is a fool—  
and more foolish than he would be our god  
wearing a dark kadamba garland, if he should come.” **(13)**

## Song prelude

“If the speared Lord should come  
to the fierce ritual ground where the vēlaṉ dances in possession—  
if the son of the Lord beneath the banyan should arrive  
upon the blue bird, beside his straight-jewelled wife—  
let us ask that the lord of the great mountain  
receive his marriage ornaments.” **(14)**

“O son of the Lord of good Mount Kailāsa,  
we, daughters of the mountain people,  
have worshipped your sacred feet—  
feet like blossoms upon a branch.  
Grant that every alien marriage be removed  
and that she receive marriage with him,  
O moon-browed, peacock-natured innocent.” **(15)**

“O son of the Mountain Daughter,  
we, daughters of the Kuravars dwelling in our ancestral mountain,  
have worshipped your paired feet,  
O exalted and enduring god.  
Grant that she enter a marriage with him  
known openly to all.” **(16)**

“The Kurava maiden is our clan’s own daughter.  
Together with her, O six-faced One,  
we have worshipped your paired feet.  
Grant a good marriage to those who touch  
your sacred feet upon the riverbank;  
remove the mistaken marriage.” **(17)**

## Song prelude

“While we sang so, he stood hidden and listened.  
Before the garlanded mountain lord could leave,  
I went forward, worshipped his sacred feet,  
and stood there. Hear what I said, friend; live long!

‘The one who comes into this village for the maiden,  
wearing kadamba flowers and bearing a spear,  
has no six faces, no beautiful peacock,  
no Kurava bride, and no strong shoulders.  
The foolish people of this little settlement  
will not recognise him as the kadamba-adorned god.’” **(18)**

## Song prelude

“When I told him that slander had arisen,  
the flower-crowned mountain lord,  
whose heart had withered at dawn,  
seems to have gone to arrange the marriage.

Let us sing of that one state:  
the celestials and their attendants joined together,  
showed the beloved husband to the worshipped chaste woman—  
the woman who destroyed great Madurai with her breast—  
and gave him back to her.

Come, friend, live long; let us sing!  
Come, friend, live long; let us sing!

Let us praise and sing the woman  
who burned bannered Kūṭal  
when the king departed from justice.

As we praise the woman who carried out the law of fire,  
let us ask that the lord of the great mountain  
receive his marriage ornaments.

Women of chastity praise and worship her—  
she of serpent-like waist, who is present in our green millet field.

Will the celestials, after lifting her serpent-waisted husband  
and giving him back, ever cease speaking of her?

Beneath the fragrant forest vēṅkai stands a maiden  
whom the immortals worship and praise in heavenly life.  
Will she not return from heavenly life  
together with her husband?

Let us praise the woman who will never return.  
May this village gain a special distinction—  
a distinction indeed, a distinction indeed:  
may this village be privileged to witness  
the marriages of its gold-braceleted women.”

Thus we sang the standing song and danced the *kuravai*.  
When our beloved, having seen it, returned into our hands,  
may the king who drank and rejoiced live forever—  
the king of the western people who ruled Kolli  
and the Himalaya upon which he engraved his bow.

# 25. The Canto of the Vision

The Cēra of celestial lineage—  
who had felled the kadamba tree in the great sea,  
and bent the mountain-bow at the Himalaya  
so that the celestials marvelled—  
the sword-bearing Kōtai  
sat in the silver hall at radiant Ila-vañci  
with his queen, Iḷaṅkō Vēṇmāḷ. **(1–5)**

“Let us go see the mountain  
whose waterfalls resound like sleepless drums  
through mist-wrapped groves,” he said.

With bracelet-wearing companions spreading around them,  
they left the courtyard of Vañci. **(6–9)**

The victorious-speared king, desiring to play  
with celestial maidens in richly flowering groves,  
spread out golden-flowered gardens, river reaches,  
shining-water islands, young tree-groves,  
stages, and resting places  
across one hundred and forty *yōjana*. **(10–15)**

He resembled the great elephant  
whose name is borne by the mighty Lord.

Kōṅkam, vēṅkai, hanging-clustered koṉṟai,  
nāgam, tilakam, fragrant kāḻ, and āram trees  
shed flowers so thick that they concealed  
the flowing water beneath them. **(16–19)**

Honey-bees, humming bees, and other swarms sang.  
The great river, crossing the massive mountain  
like a necklace upon the chest of the Tall One,  
met the king at a bank of heaped sand. **(20–23)**

All around sounded:

the Kurava women’s mountain *kuravai* and songs;  
the vēlaṉ’s mode in praise of victorious red-speared Murukaṉ;  
the short millet-pounding song;  
the cries raised from the fields; **(24–26)**

the voices of Kuravars with honey-bright eyes;  
the frightening roar of waterfalls like drums;  
the trumpet of a spotted elephant fighting a tiger;  
the cry of a watchman high upon the noisy summit; **(27–30)**

the shouts of mahouts restraining  
an elephant fallen into a pit;  
and the clamour of the moving army. **(31–32)**

Mountain people came bearing rare goods  
whose measure and limit could not be known.  
Like enemy kings who stand with tribute  
in the wealthy courtyard of Vañci,  
unable to obtain an audience with the royal son, **(33–35)**

they carried:

white elephant tusks and heaps of akil wood;  
yak-tail fly-whisks and pots of honey;  
blocks of sandalwood and lumps of vermilion;  
clusters of black collyrium and beautiful orpiment; **(36–40)**

cardamom creepers and heavy pepper vines;  
kuvai flour and thick-rooted kavalai;  
coconuts and sweet mangoes;  
green-leaf garlands and jackfruits;  
medicinal roots, sugarcane, and flowering creepers; **(41–45)**

rich clusters from tender-stemmed areca palms;  
heavy fruit-clusters from great banana plants;  
lion cubs, tiger cubs with sword-like stripes,  
young rutting elephants, monkey young,  
and broad-footed bear cubs; **(46–50)**

mountain goats, wild cattle, innocent fawns,  
spotless musk-deer, stainless mongooses,  
plumed peacocks, civet kittens,  
forest fowl, and sweet-speaking parrots. **(51–54)**

Bearing these upon their heads, the mountain people said:

“We have been your hereditary servants for seven births.  
May your victory live!

Beneath a forest vēṅkai stood a maiden  
who had lost one breast and entered solitary grief. **(55–58)**

The celestials praised her;  
she was reunited with her husband;  
the celestials praised her again  
and she attained the world of heaven.

Of what country is she? Whose daughter is she?  
Though we live in your land, we do not know.  
May you live for many hundred thousand years!” **(59–63)**

Seeing the long-speared king whose earth rejoiced in him,  
Sāttaṉ, master of cool Tamil,  
who had sat there in delighted wonder,  
spoke with affection:

“Firm-mighted king, hear me tell  
everything that happened to the bright-braceleted woman. **(64–68)**

Because of the anklet made an instrument of evil action,  
misfortune came upon the bangle-wearing maiden’s husband.  
She went before the king with his victorious army,  
carrying the anklet, and argued her case. **(69–72)**

She broke the red anklet before the queen  
and proclaimed her vow—  
that great chaste woman of fivefold hair—  
saying, ‘Know the truth.’ **(73–75)**

The fire that rose from her still-young breast  
burned ancient Madurai, the great city.

The lord of the southerners,  
whose sacred chest rested upon a lion-supported couch,  
could not bear the sorrow of his garlanded queen. **(76–80)**

He seemed to faint and die.  
She stroked his flower-feet,  
but he heard no long word from her lips.  
She did not become confused;  
she could not endure the harsh grief. **(81–83)**

‘I shall go where the king has gone,’ she said,  
and, like one seeking the life  
of the man who had taken her own,  
the great queen too died beside him. **(84–86)**

As though to reveal to the protecting ruler  
that the victorious king’s sceptre had become cruel,  
the maiden did not wander alone in her own country;  
she came into the heart of yours.” **(87–90)**

When Sāttaṉ had spoken without omission, he blessed:

“May your victorious triumph flourish,  
age after age, generation after generation!” **(91–92)**

The Cēra king heard of the fiery fate  
of the lord of the southerners and grieved. He said:

“Before a word that departs from justice  
can reach the hearing of kings like us,  
may the life seated within the body depart—  
this is what a ruler ought to desire. **(93–98)**

The king’s life straightened the sceptre  
that powerful action had bent  
and made it just again.

If the wealth of rain is withheld,  
there is great fear from heaven; **(99–100)**

if living beings suffer through royal error,  
there is still greater fear.

Kings protect their subjects  
while fearing the cruel sceptre.  
To be born into a good ruling house  
is nothing worthy of worship—only suffering.” **(101–104)**

Thus he spoke wisely to the learned poet  
who had courageously come and declared  
the suffering that pressed close upon him. **(105–106)**

Then the king asked his great queen:

“Who deserves greater wonder for noble goodness—  
the woman who went with her husband’s departing life,  
or this jewelled woman who came here with anger?” **(107–110)**

The great queen replied:

“May the woman who died without seeing  
her beloved’s suffering attain great prosperity in heaven.

Let that matter stand. We must worship  
as a goddess of chastity this woman  
who has reached our broad country.” **(111–114)**

The king beneath the garlanded white parasol agreed.  
He looked toward the learned scholars of the texts. They said:

“A stone may be taken from unfailing Potiyil;  
but if one takes it from the vast great Himalaya,  
whose crest bears the bow-emblem,  
it too will become fit for the deity. **(115–119)**

Its ritual water-consecration may properly be performed  
in the great Gaṅgā or the Kāviri.” **(120–121)**

The king answered:

“To take a stone from Potiyil  
and bathe it at the ancient Kāviri’s fore-shore  
is not a deed that brings distinction  
to those born in our long-sword, warrior clan. **(122–125)**

With brahmins whose hair is matted and coarse,  
whose garments never dry,  
whose sacred thread crosses their chests,  
and whose wealth is the three fires,  
I shall go to the great mountain-king  
and obtain a stone on which to fashion the divinity  
of this great chaste woman, perfected in innocent simplicity. **(126–130)**

There I shall also see and wear  
the radiant victory-garland woven of vēṅkai  
and flowering mandāra, touched by moonbeams  
upon a high and broad crest—

and proclaim the *Kāñci* themes:  
the transience shown to those who have passed and those who remain  
by a life that gives no fruit when it departs from the path; **(131–134)**

the *Makaṭpāl Kāñci*, in which an ancient clan’s young maiden  
is offered to the moon-crowned king;  
the long great *Kāñci* in which northern power  
stands opposed to my Vañci in the south; **(135–137)**

and the exalted garland of battle  
woven from vēṅkai and mandāra  
that blossom upon the long crest mingled with moonlight. **(138–140)**

I shall wear the *Kuṭainilai Vañci*,  
the *Koṟṟa Vañci*,  
the *Neṭumā-rāyam Vañci* of established rank,  
the vast *Viyaṉ-peru Vañci* made glorious by victors,  
the unfailing *Peruñcōṟṟu Vañci*,  
and the ever-renowned victorious *Vaḷḷai*. **(141–145)**

Crown the army with sky-high palmyra garlands  
from which frightened enemies have fled.  
Let our forces shine with provisions.

Outside golden Vañci, the flower that never withers,  
we shall wear the Vañci garland  
and clash sword against sword,” he said. **(146–149)**

“May your victory live for many years!”  
Villavaṉ Kōtai said to the king:

“Though kings like you fought against you  
in the red field of Koṅku  
and surrendered enemy banners—tiger-striped and carp-emblemed—  
those banners entered only the ears  
of your directional elephants as ornaments. **(150–155)**

Koṅkaṇas, Kaliṅgas, fierce Karunāṭas,  
Paṅgaḷas, Gaṅgas, many-speared Kaṭṭiyars,  
and northern Aryas met sweet Tamil forces  
in the mountain hunt of your wrath;  
that sight has never left my eyes. **(156–159)**

On the day when you bathed our king’s daughter  
in the fierce flood of the great Gaṅgā,  
you alone stood against a thousand Aryan kings  
in a terrible battle-array;  
even hard-eyed Death opened its eyes and watched. **(160–164)**

If you now intend to make the sea-girt land Tamil country,  
there is no one in the whole ancient-watered world  
who can stand against you.

Our king goes to the great Himalaya  
only for a stone on which to fashion the deity. **(165–169)**

Let letters be sent to every ruler of the northern region:  
‘Take upon your crowned heads  
the splendid bow, carp, and tiger emblems  
of the good southern Tamil land.’” **(170–172)**

“Enemy spies from the cool groves of Nāvalam  
never leave the gateway of our guarded Vañci,”  
said Aḻumpil Vēḷ.  
“Is it not their nature to place such words  
in the ears of elephant-owning kings?” **(173–177)**

The king of an immeasurable army agreed.  
After entering unfading Vañci,  
made illustrious by the assembly  
of the Vañci warriors who never join enemies, **(178–180)**

a proclamation arose:

“Long live our king, great among rulers!  
May he guard the world through age after age!

Our protector goes to the vast Himalaya,  
whose crest bears the bow,  
to take a stone and return. **(181–184)**

All kings of the northern region:  
come before him bearing the tribute due.  
Hear and live by the story of the fierce war  
that felled the sea-kadamba,  
and the great story of the bow engraved upon the mountain cleft. **(185–189)**

If you will not hear,  
live as renunciants, abandoning the companions of your shoulders.” **(190)**

“May the sacred body of the ankleted king live!  
May the vanguard live!”

The proclamation drum was mounted  
upon the neck of the royal war-elephant,  
and its voice rose around the beautiful city. **(191–193)**

# 26. The Canto of Taking the Stone

After the proclamation drum had sounded,  
the royal son mounted the lion-supported ancestral throne.  
The preceptor, chief astrologer, ministers of rare ability,  
and commanders of the army assembled with him,  
praising: “Long live the king of kings!” **(1–5)**

When he asked for the prescribed counsel  
concerning the direction he intended to take,  
the mighty lord beneath the high white parasol  
addressed every warrior in his renowned army:

“The ascetics of the Himalaya have conveyed to us  
words spoken by kings whose lives lack true order. **(6–10)**

If those words are allowed to pass away unanswered,  
they will bring disgrace upon rulers like us.

Unless I take from the crowned heads  
of the northern kings a stone  
on which to fashion the deity,  
may my keen sword return empty. **(11–15)**

And if, in my fierce battle-array  
with close-set anklets, I fail to make enemy kings tremble  
but instead become a sceptre that makes my own prosperous subjects tremble,  
let that shame be mine.” **(16–18)**

The preceptor replied:

“Lord of conquering battle,  
what king can oppose your vow?  
Those who wear the ātti garland,  
and those who wear the flowering neem  
beneath splendid jewelled crowns, are not your enemies. **(19–22)**

The rulers at the Himalayan boundary  
have not insulted you.  
Let your anger become still.” **(23–24)**

Then the chief astrologer rose—  
one trained for twelve years in exact calculation  
and accomplished in the five branches of knowledge.  
He said:

“Fierce-mighted king, may your victory live!  
Every ruler on both sides of the world  
bows to your lotus-red sacred feet. **(25–29)**

This is the auspicious moment.  
Let the army rise toward the direction you intend.” **(30–31)**

The king of unfailing victory heard him and ordered:

“Let sword and parasol move northward.”

The serpent who bears the mighty earth trembled;  
warriors shouted and drums thundered. **(32–35)**

Rows of jewel-lamps displaced the night.  
Among ranks of mingled banners stood the vast army,  
together with the Five Great Councils,  
the Eight Great Assemblies,  
and royal officials appointed over horses and elephants. **(36–40)**

Officers of administration, accountants,  
keepers of justice, and masters of statecraft cried:  
“Long live the ruler of the earth-clad world!” **(41–42)**

The warrior’s sword and garlanded white parasol  
were placed upon the neck of a great rice-eating elephant  
and carried to the outer fortification. **(43–45)**

The king entered the faultless Vañci assembly in due order.  
Great food was distributed to the mighty commanders  
whose rare armies rejoiced in battle. **(46–49)**

The long and eminent sword-bearing king  
wore upon his jewelled crown  
the Vañci flower that had blossomed  
in unwithered Vañci. **(50–52)**

When the morning drum that summons tribute  
from the guardians of the world sounded at the gateway,  
he bowed before the sacred feet of the exalted Lord  
whose vast form contains the world  
and whose long dark crest bears the crescent Moon. **(53–56)**

Adorning those feet with the warrior’s Vañci garland,  
the king—whose own head had never bowed—  
bowed and circumambulated them. **(57–58)**

Fragrant smoke from brahmins’ fire offerings  
troubled the sweet-smelling garland upon his breast.  
He mounted the neck of a battle-joyous elephant. **(59–60)**

“May Kuṭakkō Kuṭṭuvaṉ possess victory!”  
Some stood praising him with the sacred remnant  
received from the Lord resting in subtle sleep  
in the golden hall. **(61–63)**

Because he had placed upon his jewelled crown  
the beautiful red feet of the red-matted-haired god  
who concealed the clear water in his locks,  
he received the remnant and bore it  
upon his jewelled shoulder. **(64–67)**

As he set forth in splendour, theatrical maidens danced  
and sang:

“O victorious king, shine within the *kūṭai* movement!  
Let garlands of victory, *tumpai*, and jewelled palmyra  
rise upon the high forehead of your caparisoned elephant. **(68–71)**

Beneath the white parasol,  
let our bangle-wearing eyes drink the joyful sight  
that steals our pale bracelets away.” **(72–73)**

Magadha bards, panegyrists, and heralds  
pronounced auspicious blessings.  
Elephant warriors, cavalry leaders,  
and keen-sword soldiers praised his sword-arm. **(74–77)**

Like a celestial leaving his city  
to march against demons,  
he left Vañci. **(78–79)**

Commanders and the foremost army  
flowed around him like the edge  
of the white-crested ocean.  
Mountains seemed to bend their backs;  
settled lands seemed to become roads. **(80–83)**

The world-ruling king marched with horses,  
ornamented chariots, and armies,  
and encamped on the long outer slope of Nīlagiri. **(84–85)**

Dancing elephants, chariots, horses,  
and eminent warriors stood under unfailing guard.  
At the camp, when the fierce day-making Sun  
placed his sacred feet upon the earth-maiden, **(86–89)**

mighty men praised the royal footstool.  
The king mounted a great, renowned couch. **(90–91)**

As the moving army’s clamour  
rose in vast and touching sound,  
sages who travelled through the sky descended, saying:

“Let us see the Indra-like ruler  
who governs the broad earth.” **(92–95)**

They appeared in the radiant royal assembly  
with bodies that dazzled like lightning.  
The king rose and worshipped them. **(96–97)**

They said:

“O celestial one born in Vañci,  
made glorious by the grace of the red-matted Lord, hear us.  
We are going to Malaya. **(98–100)**

Because your purpose is to go  
to the great Himalayan abode,  
know that brahmins learned in the rare Vedas live there.  
Great king of the earth, it is your duty to protect them.” **(101–103)**

They blessed him and departed.

“Long live the ruler of the swelling-water world!” cried  
Koṅkaṇa dancers and fierce Karunāṭa performers,  
wearing the fine ornaments of their respective clans. **(104–107)**

With dark curling hair massed like night  
and resonant garlands spread in bewildering profusion,  
long dark-carp-eyed women,  
their young swelling breasts bearing necklaces, sang: **(108–111)**

“Black cuckoos call and swarms of bees make yāḻ-music.  
Bud-opening Summer has arrived,  
yet our lovers have not returned.”  
Thus they performed the women’s *vari* of eminent beauty. **(112–115)**

Kuṭaku performers appeared with bangle-wearing,  
long dark-carp-eyed women, singing the rain-season *kuravai*:

“Bangle-wearing woman, take up your ornaments!  
Do you not see the season? **(116–117)**

Thunder crashes rapidly.  
Has the rain-cloud come,  
or is it the chariot bearing our lover,  
who has completed his appointed deed?” **(118–120)**

Ōvar painters appeared with companions in drooping costume  
and praised:

“May the sword-warrior king,  
having completed his work of battle,  
live through the ages!” **(121–124)**

They praised according to the convention  
shown by the master of theatrical performance.  
The king gave them rare ornaments beyond calculation. **(125–126)**

While the spear-lord whose weapon makes kings tremble remained there,  
there arrived fifty-two theatrical women;  
two hundred and eight ensemble musicians;  
one hundred jesters trained in ninety-six religious systems; **(127–131)**

one hundred great chariots with carved fronts;  
five hundred fierce, rutting elephants;  
ten thousand swift-maned horses;  
and twenty thousand hand-built carts  
from the immeasurable northern country,  
each marked with written signs. **(132–136)**

With them came one thousand cuirassed officers  
headed by Sañjaya, each bearing an honour-title.  
They stood at the gate and announced:

“Red-sceptred king whose bow-banner rises high,  
we are at the entrance.” **(137–140)**

“Let the theatrical women, excellent men,  
ensemble instrumentalists, and Sañjaya come here,”  
the red-sceptred king ordered. **(141–143)**

Sañjaya entered the radiant royal assembly,  
bowed low, and praised the king many times.  
He presented the fifty-two performers  
and the skilled specialists  
who had entered by command, each according to kind. **(144–147)**

Then he said:

“The Hundred Kaṉṉars, who are united with you without difference,  
have said, O victorious king:

‘If the celestial-like lord goes to the northern region  
only for a stone on which to fashion the deity, **(148–151)**

we ourselves shall take a stone from the high Himalaya,  
perform its water-consecration in the swelling Gaṅgā,  
and bring it with our own power.’” **(152–154)**

“Long live the ruler of the swelling-water world,”  
the guardian of the sea-like army replied.

“Kanakaṉ and Vicayaṉ, sons of Bāla Kumāra,  
whose tongues know no restraint,  
have joined visiting kings and declared  
that the power of refined Tamil is unknown. **(155–161)**

Tell the Hundred Kaṉṉars  
that this army now goes as Death against them.  
Have them prepare a great fleet of boats  
to carry us across the great Gaṅgā.” **(162–165)**

After Sañjaya departed, one thousand cuirassed men  
of tireless speech brought heaps of sandalwood,  
deep-water pearls, and tribute sent by southern rulers. **(166–169)**

The royal scribes gave the king’s sealed letters  
to the northern monarchs. **(170–171)**

After those men departed,  
wealthy lords who governed lands  
praised the auspicious strength  
of the enduring ruler of the swelling-water world. **(172–174)**

The king left the camp and journeyed  
to the northern bank of the great Gaṅgā  
upon the fleet of boats received from the Kaṉṉars. **(175–177)**

They met him there and conducted him across their country.  
He entered Uttara, girdled by rising waters,  
advanced into enemy territory, and set his war-camp. **(178–180)**

Before the warrior of an army impossible to withstand came:

Uttaraṉ, Vicittiraṉ, Uruttiraṉ, Pairavaṉ,  
Cittiraṉ, Ciṅkaṉ, Taṉuttaraṉ, and Civētaṉ—  
all the kings of the northern region. **(181–184)**

“We shall see the power of southern Tamil,” they said.

Kanakaṉ and Vicayaṉ, joined in alliance,  
advanced against him with an army  
that covered the earth. **(185–187)**

Like a lion rising for the hunt  
and delighting at the sight  
of a great herd of black elephants as its prey,  
the many-speared kings leapt forward. **(188–190)**

The guardian met their Kāñci-garlanded army.

Cloth banners swallowed the Sun’s rays;  
tight-hide, cruel-sounding drums,  
long white conches, long horns,  
thunder-voiced war drums, and soft deep gongs sounded. **(191–194)**

The roar of instruments that consumed living sacrifice,  
together with hide-eyed drums, shook every direction. **(195–196)**

There were bow-shouldered men with battle-spears in broad hands;  
warriors in stained leather; fierce chariot-drivers;  
white-tusked elephant corps; swift-galloping cavalry. **(197–199)**

The vast dust raised from the earth  
destroyed the very sight of the ground. **(200)**

The tongues of overturned bells  
from battle elephants  
and the swelling voices of conches  
beneath radiant banners ceased trembling  
and drew inward in silence. **(201–203)**

Garlanded companies mingled with garlanded companies.  
Shoulders and heads were cut apart. **(204–205)**

Upon heaps of bow-shouldered warriors’ bodies,  
headless trunks stumbled over thrown corpses.  
Drum-eyed demon women danced to rhythmic beats. **(206–208)**

In fat-thick blood flowing beneath corpse-heaps,  
troops of demon women shook loose their hair and danced. **(209–210)**

The Cēra slew the fierce soldiers of the Aryan kings  
whose charging chariot-armies filled the field,  
and heaped them upon the battleground. **(211–212)**

Carved chariot fronts, necks of rutting elephants,  
and backs of racing horses lay ruined. **(213–214)**

The host of lives that buffalo-mounted Death  
would consume in a whole day  
was revealed upon the Aryan field of war. **(215–217)**

The ankleted king fought in close combat.  
Upon his high dark crest he wore  
a seasonal *tumpai* garland joined with palmyra. **(218–220)**

Kanakaṉ and Vicayaṉ—broad-handed, fierce-speared men  
who had despised rich Tamil through the pride of their keen swords—  
fell into Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s net of wrath  
with fifty great chariot-warriors. **(221–224)**

Others abandoned their swords and fled  
in deceptive forms wherever they could:  
as matted-haired ascetics, naked renunciants,  
ash-smeared devotees, peacock-feather-bearing great fasters,  
singers, instrumentalists, and many-shouldered actors. **(225–230)**

The guardians of armoured elephants trembled.

The king yoked tusked elephants like plough-oxen,  
used his sword as the plough,  
drew the furrow deep, and turned the enemy earth. **(231–233)**

The sword-ploughman blessed the warrior’s field.  
He raised his long bracelet-wearing arm  
and lifted before him the black crowned heads of kings. **(234–236)**

He sang of battles that churned the belly of the sea:  
the war that rose in sea-moated Laṅkā,  
and the battle of the sea-hued Lord’s chariot-city. **(237–239)**

He praised the leader in the *front-chariot kuravai*.  
On the corpse-strewn ground, demons danced  
the *rear-chariot kuravai*. **(240–241)**

Crowned heads became hearthstones;  
skulls became cooking pots;  
braceleted arms became stirring paddles.  
A warrior-demon cook prepared flesh-rice  
and knowingly fed the feast. **(242–244)**

“May the just-sceptred victor  
who made a field of righteousness through this special feast  
live through the ages!” they cried. **(245–246)**

Having completed the warrior’s field,  
keen-sword Kuṭṭuvaṉ commanded:

“Honour and protect the Veda-guarding brahmins  
of the northern region—  
people of cool and abundant life  
whose broad sacrificial fires never die.” **(247–250)**

With Villavaṉ Kōtai he sent out  
several divisions of the many-speared victorious army.  
At the golden-crested Himalaya,  
the guardian took a stone  
for the peerless goddess of chastity. **(251–254)**

# 27. The Canto of the Water Consecration

After taking from the great northern Himalaya  
the stone for the celestial goddess of chastity,  
the close-ankleted king placed it upon  
the radiant crowns of Kanakaṉ and Vicayaṉ,  
who had stood before his wrathful spear in fierce battle. **(1–4)**

The Aryan kings had fought without knowing  
the power of southern Tamil.  
To increase the work of ancient Death,  
the Cēra army consumed in a single day  
the number of lives he would take in eighteen years—  
reckoned by year, month, day, and watch  
across the water-gathered world. **(5–12)**

With Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s wrathful, speared army  
he entered the bank of the great Gaṅgā.  
Following the proper divisions of ritual,  
hundreds of skilled men performed  
the water-consecration for the goddess of chastity. **(13–16)**

On the clear-water Gaṅgā’s southern bank,  
the Aryan rulers had beautifully prepared  
a great royal temple, jewelled halls,  
a gold-adorned stage, flower-worked pavilions,  
private chambers, broad flowering groves,  
sacred lotus pools, theatres for *vari* performances,  
and all else suited to a great-famed king. **(17–23)**

The king entered the open camp there.  
He honoured warriors of many kinds:

those who destroyed the refuge  
in the hearts of rulers over long lands;  
those who entered battle as eagerly  
as celestials entering a marriage festival; **(24–26)**

those who drove through unyielding fierce combat,  
whose heads and shoulders lay as the price of valour;  
those who completed sword-work in the good battle  
of the day’s allotted wage and died with warrior-honour; **(27–30)**

those who fell with their swords  
while pit-eyed demon women began a *kuravai*  
and praised them along the road;  
young men whose garlanded wives died  
with their children upon flower-decked chests; **(31–34)**

those who wore the victory garland upon their crowns  
after men who came against them in combat  
fell beneath the sword;  
those who shone in wound-wet blood  
when charioteers fell with the carved fronts of their strong chariots; **(35–38)**

those who cut down the black, jewelled-crowned heads  
of enemies of matchless renown  
while looking Death in the eye  
and claimed the battlefield; **(39–40)**

and sword-warriors who returned alive from battle,  
their armour discoloured and their bodies filled with wounds. **(41–42)**

“Let them come,” the king commanded.  
He granted them golden victory flowers  
in honour of the great day,  
and crowned them with petal-heavy palmyra and *tumpai*. **(43–45)**

The victorious ruler, having completed every mode of praise,  
sat upon the radiant royal throne  
with his dancing-warrior chest.

Then Māṭalaṉ, the brahmin, appeared and said:

“Long live our king!  
The shore-grove mode of the maiden Mādhavi  
has crushed the crowned heads of Kanakaṉ and Vicayaṉ. **(46–51)**

Long live the ruler who has subdued and governed  
the ancient-watered world!”

The king asked:

“Four-Veda brahmin, you speak a laughing riddle  
unknown to the many kings of enemy lands.  
What is the meaning of your words?” **(52–55)**

Māṭalaṉ replied:

“During the sea-games in the cool shore-grove,  
Mādhavi sang the *vari* mode.  
When a lover’s quarrel arose, past action took form.  
Kōvalaṉ did not reunite with her; he left,  
and with the daughter of his noble clan  
entered ancient, towered Madurai. **(56–61)**

There Kōvalaṉ was slain at the execution ground,  
and the leaf-garlanded king reached the beautiful heavens.  
His wife entered your country, O king of the westerners,  
and has now mounted the jewelled crowns  
of northern rulers. **(62–65)**

Hear also, broad-handed, battle-speared king,  
the reason I came here.

After circumambulating the sage’s Potiyil mountain  
and bathing at Kumari’s great ford,  
I intended to return. **(66–69)**

Perhaps through the fruit of past action,  
I went instead to the renowned Madurai  
of the keen-sword southern king.

I heard: ‘The jewelled woman has conquered  
the king and his victorious army with her anklet.’ **(70–73)**

In the assembly of the cowherd settlement, Mātari rose and cried:

‘Kōvalaṉ was guiltless; the king’s son erred.  
I have lost the man placed in my refuge, O people of the herding clan.  
Have parasol and sceptre both failed?’ **(74–77)**

At midnight she entered the fire. **(78)**

Kavunti, eminent through austerity,  
was seized by anger.  
The woman of perfect endurance said:

‘Was it through my own action that these deeds took form,  
while the life of the long-land king  
whose red sceptre had risen high departed?’ **(79–82)**

She undertook a fast without food  
and left the life seated in her body. **(83–84)**

Learning without omission all that had befallen  
the great city of Madurai ruled by golden-charioted Ceḻiyaṉ,  
I left that place carrying my sorrow  
and went to tell the eminent people  
of the Cōḻa’s ancient city. **(85–87)**

Hearing what had happened to the young man,  
the maiden, and the red-sceptred king,  
Kōvalaṉ’s father entered terrible grief. **(88–90)**

He gave away his heavenly wealth in a vast act of charity,  
entered the seven Indra monasteries,  
and before fifty wandering ascetics  
who strove to end rebirth within their present bodies,  
he himself attained renunciation. **(91–95)**

His wife could not endure the sorrow for her son.  
She entered the grief of death  
and abandoned her body. **(96–97)**

Kaṇṇaki’s father, in the form of a religious devotee,  
performed meritorious charity  
before great Ājīvaka ascetics  
and embraced righteousness. **(98–100)**

The wife of the man who had given charity  
released her good life at the appointed day  
and left her body. **(101–102)**

Hearing these things, Mādhavi said to her good mother:

‘I shall not continue along a path  
that abandons goodness.  
Let Maṇimēkalai never behold  
the courtesan’s adornment that produces heavenly sorrow.’ **(103–106)**

She removed her flower-garland and hair-ornaments,  
made a gift at the Bodhi sanctuary,  
and embraced righteousness. **(107–108)**

Because those who heard the words from my mouth died,  
I came to bathe in the good-water Gaṅgā.  
King of kings, live long!” **(109–111)**

The celestial-like lord of unfading Vañci,  
who had crowned himself with petal-heavy palmyra and *tumpai*, asked:

“After the king died,  
what harm befell the prosperous southern country?” **(112–115)**

“Long may you live, king of the long earth,” Māṭalaṉ replied.

“Nine rulers of equal disposition,  
who would not accept the young prince  
or obey commands, opposed your brother-in-law Vaḷavaṉ Kiḷḷi. **(116–120)**

Because they were capable of destroying the fertile country,  
you eliminated nine parasols in a single day  
and brought the gold-adorned wheel of rule under one course. **(121–123)**

Hear also, palmyra-garlanded Poṟaiyaṉ,  
whose lifted sword first felled  
the long-branched neem guarded by Paḻaiyaṉ. **(124–126)**

Victorious-speared Ceḻiyaṉ, who remained at Korkai,  
offered as a single day’s living sacrifice  
one thousand goldsmiths  
to the great chaste woman who had torn away one breast. **(127–130)**

When ancient Madurai—its fame and authority rejected—  
wandered in distress after losing its king,  
he mounted the ancestral throne  
that guards the southern land’s subjects  
with faultless distinction. **(131–134)**

Like the morning Sun-god  
ascending the peerless divine chariot  
whose jewelled horse is yoked for the world,  
he mounted it while the Moon-born line  
rose at evening. **(135–138)**

May he guard the world age after age.  
Long live our king; long may he live!” **(139–140)**

While the lord listened to all the brahmin’s words,  
day matured toward its end.  
Dense evening swallowed the broad-mouthed world in darkness;  
red fire spread and made the directions glow;  
the white crescent appeared in the crimson dusk. **(141–146)**

The great man looked upon the crescent’s colour.  
The astrologer rose in the king’s presence and said:

“Ruler of the earth, live long!  
It is thirty-two months  
since we left Vañci.” **(147–150)**

They passed through one section of the royal avenue,  
between strong, bannered, long walls  
and houses short and tall like hills,  
set along a street crossed by tall gateways. **(151–154)**

The prince mounted the palace throne  
upon a red-gold platform  
beneath a painted canopy  
whose design displayed the craftsmen’s art. **(155–157)**

He summoned Māṭalaṉ through the gatekeepers and asked:

“After the young king died,  
has the king of the fertile good country  
ruled victoriously and kept the red sceptre without fault?” **(158–161)**

“Long live our king,” replied Māṭalaṉ,  
the auspicious brahmin.

“The victory of the battle-spear Lord  
who destroyed the three forts  
while his gem ornaments shone like the Sun  
caused the celestials to marvel. **(162–165)**

The righteous sceptre of the king  
who cut flesh from his own body  
to remove the long suffering of a short-stepping dove  
and free it from the attacking hawk—  
could there ever be a time when that sceptre turns aside? **(166–169)**

There is no evil.  
May the lord of the country nourished by the Kāviri  
continue without decline.” **(170–171)**

Hearing the master of the rare Vedas,  
the great king honoured the brahmin  
and gave Māṭalaṉ, saying “Take this,”  
fifty measures of gold, weighed in full,  
equal to the king’s own bodily weight. **(172–176)**

He ordered twenty Aryan kings  
to return to their prosperous good lands. **(177–178)**

He also commanded that the great army-princes  
who had escaped alive by adopting ascetic disguises  
be shown together with Aryan dancing women  
whose curling beards, bewildering flower-hair,  
long carp-eyes, white petal-like teeth, red mouths, **(179–184)**

lined bracelets, bamboo-like shoulders,  
growing young breasts, yielding gait, lightning waists,  
and little ankleted feet formed their costume. **(185–186)**

One thousand cuirassed officers,  
who never failed to oppose a ruler’s command,  
were sent to display Kanakaṉ and Vicayaṉ—  
men who had fought without understanding  
the power of rare Tamil beneath the lion-palmyra banner—  
to the two great Tamil kings. **(187–191)**

Before the king settled into proper sleep,  
young lotuses across the green-leaved wetlands  
of the broad-water Gaṅgā opened,  
and many bees made yāḻ-music. **(192–194)**

The young lord Sun spread his wide rays  
and rose upon the high crest of the eastern mountain. **(195–196)**

The victorious king of the western quarter,  
crowned with northern *tumpai* and victory garlands,  
turned south with his triumphant army. **(197–199)**

In the long and lofty treasure-sleeping city,  
a golden palace shut out the Sun’s rays.  
Pearl-strung banners encircled a painted canopy;  
worked flowers and rows of shining gems  
were set at intervals with radiant diamonds  
and sheets of polished gold. **(200–205)**

Within the hall stood a heavenly gold couch  
with close-set golden legs,  
and a paired-pillowed bed  
stuffed with the soft young down of mating swans. **(206–209)**

The nurse-women, skilled in speaking of the king’s  
chariot-victory that had cast down the field,  
sang blessings for many years:

“May the sorrow of shoulders separated from their mates  
cease here.” **(210–213)**

Little hunchbacks and dwarfs came and said:

“Receive your auspicious hour—  
the great man has returned.  
O fragrant-haired woman, receive the ornament of the day!” **(214–216)**

They sang mountain songs:

“A forest man drank well-fermented liquor.  
Abandoning his sling and his guarding hand,  
he slept. **(217–219)**

A tall elephant came desiring  
to eat the swollen millet field  
and entered deep sleep. **(220)**

‘Move from the path of the swift elephant  
that wears northern victory and *tumpai*,’  
the Kurava women sang from their high watch-platforms.” **(221–224)**

They sang farmers’ songs:

“The king of the westerners has returned  
after breaking the forts of northern rulers  
and sowing white mustard with a donkey-plough. **(225–227)**

O ploughing ox, tomorrow wear the heavy yoke—  
it is the white-festival day  
when the king removes the fetters of captive rulers.” **(228–230)**

They sang cowherds’ flute modes:

“At the great ford where people bathe  
in the cool Porunai, colours, lime, and flowers spread  
and made the place shine like a rainbow in heaven. **(231–233)**

Bees drank from jewelled-petalled kuvalai flowers;  
their hair, crowned with thorn-lotus garlands,  
touched full-blown fragrant lotuses. **(234–236)**

From the screw-pine branch where herons gathered they called:  
‘Villavaṉ has returned. Drive your many herds  
toward the broad Himalaya.’” **(237–239)**

The guardian king brought the cattle  
to the watering-place. **(240)**

They sang the evening mode of seashore women:

“On the white sands struck by white waves,  
near the deep-water bank and clustered black puṉṉai,  
good pearls born from right-turning conches  
are lifted in the blossoming hands  
of bangle-armed women playing at *kaḻaṅku*. **(241–246)**

The celestial king has returned  
to enjoy the beauty of your growing young breasts.  
Women, let us sing Vañci  
with *tumpai* and palmyra.” **(247–250)**

The great queen listened to all the songs.  
White conch bangles tightened upon her arms,  
and right-turning conches sounded auspiciously. **(251–252)**

Beneath the garlanded white parasol,  
the victory-crowned king shone  
upon the back of his swift elephant.  
Moving with an elephant’s stately gait,  
he entered Vañci while the royal city came out to receive him. **(253–256)**

Thus Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ entered Vañci.

# 28. The Canto of the Memorial Stone

The long golden parasol, cool as the moon,
cast its shade upon the earth;
and the tall king, whose victorious sword
had won him sovereign fortune,
returned to ancient Vañci in triumph. **(1–4)**

Women with bright bangles on their broad arms
scattered shining flowers in offering,
held lamps with white wicks,
and cried in praise,
“Long live the monarch of the world!” **(5–7)**

Garlanded maidens came in crowds to worship him.
Warriors wearing palmyra wreaths
and splendid flower-garlands,
their lifted swords in their right hands,
had completed the king’s campaign. **(8–10)**

Some bore upon their breasts
the pressure-marks of elephants’ white tusks;
some had long wounds torn by tall spears;
some broad, beautiful chests were rent by arrows;
some jewel-covered breasts were gashed by sharp swords. **(11–14)**

The heat of longing breasts,
striped and swelling beneath dark flower-like eyes,
would not be restrained.
Soft hair, loosened to drink the perfume of aloes,
spread beneath brows curved like dark bows,
as the moon seems to enter cloud. **(15–18)**

The god with the makara banner
shot his flower-arrows;
and glances, darting from rich outer corners of eyes
where fine red lines spread,
were praised as both wound and medicine
in that evening of desire. **(19–21)**

Young-moon smiles opened
upon fruit-red lips;
black carp-like eyes moved in lovely faces;
perfect teeth budded in welcoming laughter. **(22–24)**

Tender-bodied women bestowed these delights
upon young warriors with ornamented chests. **(25–26)**

To view their spotless sword-bright faces,
where dark beauty-marks lay beneath faultless tilakam,
their curling hair entwined with bees,
their garlands and adornments,
they set polished mirrors before themselves. **(27–30)**

They drew close the small yāḻ with its curved frame;
and on the joined strings, over the meaningful resonator,
they made the tonic follow tonic,
and, in the ancient order of the *pālai* mode,
raised *tuttam* as the governing note. **(31–34)**

Like mountain women singing sweet *kuṟiñci*,
they offered a new feast of music to the young men. **(35–36)**

Then the flower-rayed moon,
which the world worships when it rises,
showed itself above the celebrated ancient city
and passed onward—
like the gracious face of Kuṭṭuvaṉ,
whose ankleted feet rub against the crowns of kings,
when he protects his people. **(37–40)**

Young men and women listened to songs handed down by tradition.
In the moonlit courtyards where the long-bowed Love-god
sat enthroned with his five arrows,
in flower-strewn beds,
in plastered dancing-halls,
in blossom-filled pavilions,
on couches with white legs,
and upon canopied platforms,
the cool-rayed moon itself seemed to keep watch. **(41–46)**

As Mount Mēru rises at the centre
of the vast, perilous earth ringed by beating seas,
so, in the middle of the old city with bannered walls,
rose the golden palace
and its jewel-decked hall. **(47–50)**

The auspicious queen Vēṇmāḷ,
bride of the king,
came to behold the moon-like splendour. **(51–52)**

On one side, women with bright bangles
carried lamps and spread through the hall,
crying blessings for many years. **(53–54)**

On another side sounded
broad-faced drums,
curved yāḻs,
and songs ripened in melody. **(55–56)**

On one side, hunchbacks and dwarfs
carried musk-sandal and striped white sandal-paste. **(57–58)**

On one side, attendants dressed in women’s ornaments
bore pigments, fragrant powders,
and garlands of flowers. **(59–60)**

On one side, flowers, incense,
and mingled perfumes
surrounded the strewn, lovely couch. **(61–62)**

On another, maidservants gracefully carried
mirrors, garments, and vessels of adornment. **(63–64)**

The ruler of the sea-swollen world
entered the jewel-bright hall with the queen. **(65–66)**

There the anklets on holy red feet seemed to lament,
the drum in the circling red hand thundered,
a thousand red eyes bestowed sacred signs,
and red matted hair swept across the quarters. **(67–70)**

The foot-ornament did not tremble;
the bracelet did not move;
the girdle made no sound;
the soft breast did not sway;
the long earring did not dance;
the jewelled hair did not loosen. **(71–73)**

Śiva, with Umā forming one half of him,
performed the fierce *koṭṭic cētam* dance. **(74–75)**

The Sākkaiyan dancer of Paṟaiyūr,
versed in the four Vedas and their measured divisions,
performed it;
the king rejoiced, praised him, and dismissed him. **(76–78)**

After the ruler of the two lands
had entered the royal audience hall,
Nīlaṉ and the other cuirass-wearing officers
appeared with the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ. **(79–81)**

The doorkeepers announced them to the king;
then, among the palace officials,
they bowed before the conqueror. **(82–83)**

Nīlaṉ said:

“O king whose anklets ring round the fierce *tumpai* battle,
we entered the ancient city of the Cōḻa. **(84–85)**

There, in the painted hall where the Cōḻa king sat
with the rulers of Vajra, Avanti, and Magadha,
the captured northern kings came among our retinue
and bowed at his noble feet. **(86–89)**

The lord of the chariot-army said to us:

‘To seize in victory those who,
after showing great manliness in prolonged battle,
left sword and parasol upon the field
and escaped alive in the harmless garb of ascetics—
that is no victory.’ **(90–94)**

Thus spoke the great scion of the Cempiyars,
his broad chest wreathed with garlands. **(95)**

“Leaving there, O king whose sceptre is righteousness,
we went to show them to the glorious king of Madurai. **(96–97)**

The spear-bearing Ceḻiyaṉ said:

‘The shaft of the fine white parasol,
raised from the northern kings’ battlefield,
has become like the ceremonial staff
in the form of Jayanta. **(98–100)**

They displayed their parasol from atop elephant heads,
then worshipped the One who shares Umā as half his form
at the resounding heights of Himālaya. **(101–103)**

Having abandoned the battlefield to the king
and assumed the great guise of penance,
these men escaped.
To blaze with furious anger against such men—
that is a new kind of victory.’ **(104–107)**

Thus spoke the battle-speared Ceḻiyaṉ.” **(108)**

Nīlaṉ repeated every long speech
spoken by those two kings. **(109)**

The lotus-red eyes of the prince
took on the colour of flame,
and he laughed in wrath. **(110–111)**

Then Māṭalaṉ, whose learning knew no defect,
rose and praised him:

“O king of kings,
long live your victory! Long live!” **(112–113)**

“You destroyed Viyalūr,
where elephants sleep in groves of ripening pepper
and the small-voiced *neytal* flowers bloom. **(114–115)**

At Nēri-vāyil you won the fixed battle
against nine kings wearing full-flowered garlands. **(116–117)**

With your long-charioted army
you camped beyond Iṭumpil;
you passed through cruel war,
drove your force across the long sea,
and defeated upon the mighty, fierce-flowing Gaṅgā
the northern kings who advanced against you. **(118–121)**

O great army-king crowned with long garlands,
o lion among rulers,
who knows how to stand in fellowship with worthy men—
let your anger now be calmed. **(122–124)**

O ruler of the earth,
may the days of your life
outnumber the sands of the cool great Porunai!
Long live, lord of the world ringed by the deep sea! **(125–127)**

Do not disdain my words;
you must hear them. **(128)**

Fifty years have passed
since you took upon yourself the guardianship of the world. **(129–130)**

Yet you have performed no sacrifice in the field of righteousness;
wherever you go,
you have performed only sacrifice in the field of war. **(131–132)**

Among the ancient kings of your lineage,
whose right hands bore raised swords,
whose brows wore palmyra wreaths— **(133–134)**

one guardian hurled the sea-born kadamba tree; **(135)**

one mighty king carved his bow upon the mountain cleft; **(136)**

one, celebrated in the verse of a Brahmin,
was sent from this world to the higher realm; **(137–138)**

one conqueror halted Death itself,
saying, ‘Take living beings only in their proper order’; **(139–140)**

one ruled the rich land of harsh-speaking Yavanas
and entered the long mountain where gold is found; **(141–142)**

one drove a vast army through a great war
and destroyed the fortress of Akappā; **(143–144)**

one bathed the ancestral goddess Ayirai
and himself bathed in the waters of both seas; **(145–146)**

one brought the guardian spirits of the market-square into Vañci
and performed a sacrifice rich with intoxicating drink. **(147–148)**

None of those highly praised kings remains.
From this you know that the body does not endure. **(149–150)**

O guardian king,
have you not seen, in the northern rulers
who scorned refined Tamil and whom you defeated,
that wealth does not endure
among those who live in this strong, wide world? **(151–154)**

There is no need for discerning men
to explain that youth does not endure. **(155–156)**

O king of the straight sceptre and broad radiant chest,
you too have seen your body mature into grey age. **(157–158)**

A good soul that has attained a celestial form
may return in the form of an earth-born being. **(159–160)**

A living soul that has taken human embodiment
may, despite its greatness, attain an animal birth. **(161–162)**

A living soul once confined in an animal body
may enter the distressing hells. **(163–164)**

Like dancers who assume changing costumes,
the precious soul does not remain fixed
in one form it has once joined. **(165–166)**

It travels according to the path of the deeds it has done—
this is the true doctrine of those whose vision does not lie. **(167–168)**

O warrior-king,
may the discus borne upon your seven-crowned chest
prosper through generation after generation! **(169–170)**

I am no seeker of rare gifts. **(171)**

Your good soul inhabits a body of great renown;
you have surpassed the ordinary measure of men.
I cannot bear that you too should depart
by the common path through which life leaves
this flower-faced world. **(172–174)**

Therefore a sacrificial Brahmin,
versed in the four Vedas,
will show you the path praised by the gods. **(175–176)**

You must perform the great and noble sacrifice
prescribed by the rare Vedas for exalted kings. **(177–178)**

Do not say, ‘We shall perform righteousness tomorrow.’
The well-instructed soul may depart today. **(179–180)**

No one in this ancient ocean-ringed world
has lived knowing with certainty,
‘This is the fixed limit of my life.’ **(181–182)**

Join with this queen, the lady of the sacrifice.
May kings with low-hanging anklets praise your feet;
may you guard the world age after age
and live long, O great one!” **(183–186)**

The Brahmin ploughed with his Vedic tongue
and sowed heavenly truth thickly
in the ears of the lord. **(187–188)**

The great seed he planted sprouted,
grew abundant grain,
and made the ankleted king desire its harvest. **(189–190)**

According to the course Māṭalaṉ had prescribed,
the king summoned sacrificial priests
whose discerning tongues had completed
all learning in the four-Veda tradition,
and ordered them to celebrate
the rites of sacrificial peace. **(191–194)**

He released the northern kings from their harsh captivity
and showed them, outside renowned ancient Vañci,
the cool flower-garden enclosed by low waters
and the palace of Vēḷāvik Kōmāṉ. **(195–198)**

After the great and auspicious sacrifice was completed,
he said:

“Later they may return to their own vast cities.
For now, provide every honour proper to kings.” **(199–201)**

He gladly entrusted that duty to Villavaṉ Kōtai. **(202)**

He then ordered Aḻumpil Vēḷ
and the officers of the revenue department:

“Open the prison-fort.
Throughout every taxable good town,
grant remission of taxes.” **(203–206)**

He sent them through the ancient city
girdled by roaring waters. **(207)**

The noble Tamil saying of the ancients declares:

“Only when kings govern according to justice
can chastity flourish among women of great renown.” **(208–210)**

Because the Pattini whom the world worships and praises
had embodied that truth,
she gave its lesson to the Cōḻa king
whose crown wore the ātti garland. **(211)**

She gave to the Pāṇḍya,
guardian of the southern land,
the lesson that a ruler cannot live
when his straight sceptre bends. **(212–213)**

And, so that the kings of the northern quarter might know
that sovereigns do not lay aside fierce anger
until the vow they have uttered is fulfilled,
she gave that lesson to the victorious king
who rules in the western quarter. **(214–217)**

She had kindled the boiling fire of her wrath
from her breast
and brought ruin upon the great ancient city of Madurai. **(218–219)**

She then reached this good land
and stood beneath the fresh golden shade
of a cool-branched vēṅkai tree. **(220–221)**

The king went there with:
Brahmins of the sacrificial hall,
the royal preceptor,
the great astrologer,
and master craftsmen. **(222–223)**

In the Pattini shrine,
arranged according to the scriptural path desired by the gods
and apportioned by learned men according to proper classes,
he worshipped the deity brought from the summit
of the red Himālaya where the immortals dwell. **(224–227)**

Upon the divine image,
completed by skilled hands
and fashioned by masters in a radiant form,
he placed every perfect ornament and jewel. **(228–230)**

He offered flowers,
established the protective threshold,
and ordered:

“Appoint sacrifices and festivals for every day;
perform the auspicious rites of the goddess.” **(231–233)**

So acted the lion among kings,
who had made the northern quarter bow.

# 29. The Canto of Benediction

## Prose Prelude

Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ was the son born to Cēralātaṉ—
who ruled the world from Kumari to the northern Himālaya
under one language—
by the daughter of the radiant Sun-like Cōḻa.
After fighting the Koṅkar on the red battlefield
and reaching the bank of the mighty Gaṅgā,
he returned to Vañci, his wrath still proud. **(1)**

The northern Ārya kings had once sat together in their residence,
garlanded by a maiden there,
and had laughed with one voice:

“When the southern Tamil kings rose eager for war
and engraved the shining bow, tiger, and carp
upon the brow of Himālaya,
there can have been no crowned rulers like us living here.”

Ascetics dwelling there came and reported those words.
They drove through him as a stick drives a rolling jewelled disc.
The declaration that a sacred stone must be taken
from great Himālaya was thrust into action.

He routed the northern kings,
made their crowned heads bear
the great Himālayan stone belonging to the goddess,
and returned.
At the mighty Gaṅgā he performed the water-consecration
for the Lady;
the heat born of his fierce anger subsided.

He entered the great city of Vañci
and displayed the image,
which many worshipped with the long crowns of earthly kings.
After performing the divine auspicious consecration
for the broad-breasted woman
who had waged her righteous quarrel,
he received tribute from kings
in Kaṇṇaki’s own shrine.

There the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ recounted her story:

Tears had flowed like red carp
from her wandering moon-like face;
dust had covered her body like a dark cloud.
She had condemned failed justice.
Kōvalaṉ’s former deed had ripened,
and the base goldsmith had caused the king
to order his death.
Kaṇṇaki went to the ruler’s presence;
on seeing her tears,
the great sovereign lost the water of life and died.

Hearing this,
Mācāttuvāṉ renounced the world,
and his wife died.
Many other great sorrows followed.
The nurse, the intimate maid,
and Tēvanti—who had lived with the deity Cāttaṉ—
joined together and entered the great city of Madurai,
saying, “Let us see the red-ornamented lady.”

There they heard of the quarrel raised by the young-breasted woman.
They reached the place where the cowherd woman,
who had lost the one entrusted to her protection,
had given up her life.
Taking Aiyai, that woman’s daughter, with them,
they followed the Vaigai by one route,
climbed the summit of the great mountain,
and entered the temple of the royal daughter.

They then told their history
to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ,
who was celebrating the Lady with distinction. **(1)**

## Tēvanti Speaks

“I am the friend of the bangle-armed woman
born on the great northern Himālaya,
the divine mountain guarded and honoured
by all three crowned kings,
who bathed in the rushing waters of the Gaṅgā
and came south—behold me! **(2)**

I am the friend of the maiden of the Cōḻa land—
behold me!”

## The Nurse Speaks

“I am the mother-like nurse
of the great wide-eyed woman
whose beloved husband took her hand,
without yielding himself to Mādhavi of gentle beauty,
and led her through the cruel, waterless forest
without first entering another household—behold me! **(3)**

I am the mother of the maiden of cool Pukār—
behold me!”

## The Intimate Maid Speaks

“She had no word for the mother who bore her;
she had no word for the mother who guarded her from harm;
nor did she have a word for me. **(4)**

She took upon herself the burden of chastity
and followed her husband.
I am the friend of that gold-bangled lady—behold me!
I am the friend of the maiden of flower-rich Pukār—behold me!”

## Tēvanti’s Lament

“I, who had earned no merit,
heard the evil dream,
yet failed to understand what was coming.
What have I done? **(5)**

When you heard of the quarrel
raised by the full-haired woman with her breast,
did you hear that your mother died, my friend?
Did you hear that your mother-in-law too died, my friend?”

## The Nurse’s Lament

“When the base man falsely ensnared Kōvalaṉ
and the king gave up his own life on hearing the truth,
Mācāttuvāṉ grieved and declared death itself to be life;
after giving many gifts,
he renounced the world. **(6)**

Did you hear of his renunciation, mother?
Did you hear of Māṉāykaṉ’s renunciation, mother?”

## The Intimate Maid’s Lament

“Grieving over your husband’s death,
over the suffering you endured,
and over the slander uttered by strangers,
Mādhavi performed meritorious gifts
before ascetics beneath the Bodhi tree
and renounced the world. **(7)**

Did you hear of Mādhavi’s renunciation, my friend?
Did you hear of Maṇimēkalai’s renunciation, my friend?”

## Tēvanti Points to Aiyai and Laments

“The old woman who had faithfully guarded the refuge
lost her reason, crying,
‘I was unable to protect what was entrusted to me,’
and gave up her life. **(8)**

This is her daughter—
Aiyai, of bright teeth,
who has not yet worn the marriage ornament.
Have you seen her, my friend?
Have you seen your aunt’s innocent daughter, my friend?”

## Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ Speaks

“What is this? What is this?
What is this—what can it be? **(9)**

Above the golden anklet,
with jewelled girdle, bangle-bearing hand,
fine diamond ear-ornaments,
and chains of refined gold,
a single lightning-creeper appears
in the upper sky!”

## Kaṇṇaki Reveals Her Divine Form to Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ

“The southern king was free from fault.
He has become an honoured guest
in the palace of the king of the gods. **(10)**

I am his daughter.
I will never leave the hill of the victorious spear-bearer,
where I play.
Come, all of you, my companions—come with me!”

## The Women of Vañci Speak

“Women of Vañci!
Women dwelling within Vañci!
Soft-footed companions of the warrior—
come, all of you! **(11)**

Come, let us sing of the woman
who destroyed the city of Kūṭal with her breast
and conquered its king with her red anklet!
Come, let us sing the daughter of the southern king!

Our king’s tongue of justice proclaimed,
‘The Pāṇḍyas do not live when their straight sceptre bends.’
Come, let us sing the green-bangled maiden
who reached this land!
Come, let us sing the daughter of the Pāṇḍya!”

## The Companions Speak

“We call her the daughter of our celestial king;
she calls herself the daughter
of the lord of the Vaigai people. **(12)**

Let us praise our heavenly king;
this daughter of the gods
will praise the lord of the Vaigai.”

## Benedictions

“Long live the royal king
who gave up his life
when her tears—shed through grief
caused by ancient deeds—
struck him down! **(13)**

Long live, long live
the ancient line of the lord of Madurai,
encircled by the ever-flowing waters of the Vaigai!”

“Long live the king
who raised the innocent maiden,
daughter of the mountain king,
upon the long crowns of earthly rulers! **(14)**

Long live, long live
the ancient line of the lord of Vañci,
encircled by the cool Porunai
of ever-flowing waters!”

“Now, all of us—
let us sing, let us sing
the lord of the Kāviri land,
O flower-haired women of Pukār!” **(15)**

## The Ammāṉai Songs

“Who is the mighty one
who ruled the world ringed by swelling waters
and protected the lofty fortress
of the king of the gods—tell me, *ammāṉai*! **(16)**

The one who guarded that lofty fortress
is the Cōḻa who hurled down
the three suspended forts in the high sky—see, *ammāṉai*!
Sing the Cōḻa’s city of Pukār, *ammāṉai*!”

“Who is the conqueror
who entered the balance for a dove
and, while the golden world praised him,
cut flesh without stint from his own body—tell me, *ammāṉai*! **(17)**

The guardian who came after that faultless self-sacrificing king
was the ruler who rendered justice to a cow—see, *ammāṉai*!
Sing the guardian’s flower-rich Pukār, *ammāṉai*!”

“Who is the king who,
while the eight directional elephants watched without blinking,
engraved the fierce tiger upon the northern mountain—tell me, *ammāṉai*! **(18)**

The conqueror who marked the northern mountain
also gathered all eight quarters
beneath the shade of his parasol—see, *ammāṉai*!
Sing the conqueror’s flower-rich Pukār, *ammāṉai*!”

“Take the *ammāṉai* in your hand, sister,
and sing in the houses of the jewel-decked women—
what grace, *ammāṉai*! **(19)**

May every charm sung in their houses
join the striped, swelling breast
of the garlanded king, *ammāṉai*!
When it joins his swelling breast,
sing the clan-king’s gentle city of Pukār, *ammāṉai*!”

## The Ball Songs

“The golden flower-creeper bends like a bow;
bright garlands gleam;
lightning-like girdles roar and roar everywhere. **(20)**

We strike the ball as we move,
crying, ‘Long live, long live the southern king!’
We strike the ball, crying,
‘Long live the chest adorned with the gods’ garland!’”

“Moving back and forth everywhere,
rising and circling,
like a young lightning-creeper
fallen upon the broad earth— **(21)**

we strike the ball as we move,
crying, ‘Long live, long live the southern king!’
We strike the ball, crying,
‘Long live the chest adorned with the gods’ garland!’”

“Rushing close, the ball does not remain in the palm;
nor does it rise from the wide earth
to Mount Mantara—so it seems. **(22)**

We strike the ball as we move,
crying, ‘Long live, long live the southern king!’
We strike the ball, crying,
‘Long live the chest adorned with the gods’ garland!’”

## The Swing Songs

“Sit upon the gem-strung swing, Aiyai!
Let one of us raise both hands
and send it high. **(23)**

Let us sing of the guardian
who first felled the sea-kadamba;
let us swing until our long cup-like eyes roll!
Let us sing the emblem of the curved bow
and swing!”

“In the war where five and a hundred,
and twice fifty more,
rose together in fury,
he gave the Great Meal unstintingly. **(24)**

Let us sing the greatness
of the Cēraṉ, the Poṟaiyaṉ, the mountain lord,
and swing until our rain-cloud hair flies!
Let us sing how he felled the kadamba
and swing!”

“He ruled the rich land of harsh-speaking Yavanas,
the hard great mountain,
and southern Kumari;
in battle he bore the emblems of carp and tiger. **(25)**

Let us sing the greatness of the king
who guards the peoples of the earth,
and swing until our lightning waists bend!
Let us sing the emblem of the mighty bow
and swing!”

## The Pestle Songs

“The women of Pukār,
beneath the shade of flowering kāñci trees,
pound splendid pearls
with sweet sugarcane as their fine pestle. **(26)**

Their true song is the song
of the broad, mountain-like shoulders
of the Cempiyan,
whose firm chariot bears the discus-banner
and whose garland is thick with fresh flowers.
It is the song that makes maidens cry aloud!”

“The women of towered Madurai
pound song-worthy pearls
with a coral pestle. **(27)**

Their true song is the song
of the fish-banner of the Pañcavaṉ,
whose shoulders shine with the garland
received from the king of the gods.
It is the song that crushes the hearts
of those who wear neem garlands!”

“The women of Vañci
pour excellent jewelled pearls
into a sandalwood mortar
and pound them with a pestle of elephant tusk. **(28)**

Their true song is the fame
of the Cēraṉ who crossed and destroyed his enemies
and felled the kadamba.
It is the song that spreads over and covers the earth;
it is the song that captures hearts
with the palmyra wreath!”

Thus, since it is hard for the kings of the wide earth
to praise anyone who does not worship
the good feet of the long-bowed Poṟaiyaṉ,
our royal maiden too praised him,
her vow fulfilled:

“Long live Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ through the long ages!”

# 30. The Canto of the Granted Boon

After the great celestial king
who had made the northern quarter bow
had beheld the goddess’s divine form,
he looked intently at Tēvanti and asked: **(1–3)**

“Maṇimēkalai’s mother raised her voice and lamented.
What caused that maiden to renounce the world?
Tell us here.” **(4–5)**

Tēvanti first praised him:

“May the prince’s victory rise without decline;
may the land pour forth abundant wealth!” **(6–7)**

Then she recounted the heavenly renunciation
of Maṇimēkalai,
who had grown among women adorned with beautiful girdles. **(8–9)**

“Her dark, abundant hair had reached the age
when it could be arranged in five distinct styles. **(10–11)**

Her rain-cloud eyes,
rich at their outer corners with flowing red lines,
had learned the sidelong arts of love,
though she herself had not learned them. **(12–13)**

Her young pearl-like smile,
whose inner light shone within lips like matched coral,
had not yet grown full. **(14–15)**

Her breasts had joined and risen;
the space between them had disappeared. **(16)**

Her loosening waist had grown slender;
her beautiful hips had widened. **(17)**

Her paired thighs had become rounded,
yet could not bear their own beauty. **(18)**

Her little feet, bright in colour,
had become tender as shoots soaked in ghee. **(19)**

Though she had reached the age
when the chief dance-master should stand behind her,
the leaders of her hereditary community
would not receive her according to their custom. **(20–22)**

Her good mother told Mādhavi:

‘What is your intention?
What shall I do?’ **(23)**

Mādhavi said,

‘Come, my innocent daughter Maṇimēkalai.’ **(24)**

She made the bodiless Love-god
cast his great bow and fragrant flower-arrows
upon the empty ground. **(25–26)**

She removed the garland from the girl’s hair,
performed a gift at the Bodhi tree,
and established her in the path of righteousness. **(27–28)**

When the king and the city heard of it,
they suffered deeply,
like those who drop a fine, radiant jewel
into the swelling sea. **(29–31)**

The red-ornamented maiden said with loving grace:

‘The true-speaking ascetics
have declared her renunciation to us.’ **(32–34)**

Because the green-bangled girl,
though not beyond the proper season,
had laid aside the festive ornaments desired by Fortune,
I lamented.” **(35–37)**

After Tēvanti had said this to the king,
her thick hair came loose and fell behind her. **(38)**

Her brows twitched;
her red lips folded;
her budding teeth showed;
her emerging speech became confused. **(39–40)**

Her sacred face perspired;
her red eyes grew crimson;
she flung up her hands;
she shifted her feet and rose. **(41–42)**

She entered a trance unknown to ordinary people;
she was bewildered,
her tongue grew dry,
and she uttered exalted words. **(43–44)**

Thus Tēvanti rose possessed by the deity. **(45)**

Before the king of the fresh-leaved mountain land,
among the innocent-speaking, richly ornamented women
who had come to witness the goddess’s consecration,
she said: **(46–48)**

“Here are two twin girls,
born to the jewelled wife of Araṭṭaṉ Ceṭṭi. **(49–50)**

And here too is the little daughter
of the servant-family of the One
who lies upon the serpent-bed
in the golden temple. **(51–52)**

At the shrine of the auspicious Lady,
on the lofty slopes of the high red mountain,
there are many beautiful pools
filled by the water that gathers
upon the broad back of a long rock shaped like an elephant’s face. **(53–56)**

Among them is one spring
whose water contains:
fine grains like white mustard,
small reddish stones like flower petals,
and a glittering stream the colour of powdered lime. **(57–59)**

Those who enter and bathe in that spring
become conscious of their former births. **(60)**

I brought that water here.
O you who sit in the tall, great assembly
within the shrine of the jewelled woman,
I gave it into your hand,
saying, ‘Receive this marked vessel.’ **(61–63)**

The water-pot hanging from its cord
is still in your hand. **(64)**

Its divine power will not fade
until the sun’s light itself fades. **(65–66)**

Sprinkle that water upon these three young girls.
Then you will see them reveal
the births that have been hidden.” **(67–68)**

The possessed woman then cried:

“I am the deity Pācaṇṭaṉ.
O Brahmin Māṭalaṉ,
I entered this Brahmin woman!” **(69–70)**

The king was filled with wonder
and looked at Māṭalaṉ’s face. **(71)**

Māṭalaṉ rejoiced greatly and said:

“Hear this, O king.
May evil perish from you. **(72–73)**

A woman named Mālati
nursed the child of a co-wife.
An old deed ripened;
Death took the infant’s life. **(74–76)**

Grieving for the child,
unable to endure,
and overcome by terrible anguish,
she lay before Pācaṇṭaṉ. **(77–78)**

He assumed the form of that faultless child,
came to her, and said:

‘Mother, abandon your great sorrow.’ **(79–80)**

The righteous one removed her grief,
then grew in beauty in the ancient line of Kāppiya
with both the Brahmin woman
and his former mother. **(81–83)**

He circled the marriage fire with Tēvanti,
and eight years passed. **(84–85)**

Then Cāttaṉ showed his unfading youthful form
and said:

‘Come to my shrine;
do not remain apart.’ **(86–87)**

He departed.
Later, at the shrine of the auspicious Lady,
he appeared as the Brahmin dwelling there,
placed the cord-hung water-pot in my hand,
gave the identifying instructions,
and went away.
He did not return. **(88–91)**

Because I brought that vessel here,
the knowing god has now appeared upon this Brahmin woman
and commanded:

‘Sprinkle that water.’ **(92–94)**

O king of kings,
let us sprinkle it upon the maidens
and learn the truth here.” **(95–96)**

He sprinkled the water upon them.
Their hidden former births came upon them. **(97)**

One looked toward Kaṇṇaki and cried:

“Look upon me too,
who grieve because those I praised
turned away from the conduct worthy of love! **(98–99)**

You suffered cruel sorrow
alone with your one beloved
in a good land where you had no relatives. **(100–101)**

My daughter whom I bore!
My companion who was as two to me!
Lady who removes great sorrow—come!” **(102–103)**

Another lamented toward Kōvalaṉ:

“The radiant-bangled woman who lived with me
went away with you,
enduring solitary grief in the middle of the night. **(104–105)**

My heart laments her departure;
I cannot bear it.
My son—come!” **(106–107)**

The third cried:

“The man who left the heavenly bank
of the ever-flowing Vaigai—
I came to this ancient city of stirring beauty,
heard the townspeople’s words,
but did not find you in the house. **(108–110)**

My lord! My young one!
Where have you hidden yourself?” **(111)**

Thus they lamented,
grieving again and again. **(112)**

Before the warrior whose broad chest bore hanging gold,
the little bangle-wearing girls,
their mouths still babbling like children,
stood in the courtyard and wept
with the words of old women. **(113–115)**

The palmyra-wreathed, ankleted king
looked at the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ. **(116–117)**

Māṭalaṉ praised him:

“O king of kings, live long!” **(118)**

Then the sacred-threaded man
explained what had occurred: **(119)**

“To remove the great sorrow
that befell the Brahmin,
Pācaṇṭaṉ entered the elephant-trunked vessel
and took a divine form. **(120–122)**

Those who had been the loving mothers
of the woman who attained that form
had loved her deeply;
yet they had not performed the unfailing good deed
that would carry them with her
to the higher world among its inhabitants. **(123–125)**

Because they approached ancient Vañci without fear,
drawn by love for the golden-creeper-like woman,
the greatness of their affection caused both of them
to be born together from one womb,
to the delight of Araṭṭaṉ Ceṭṭi
and his innocent-speaking good wife. **(126–131)**

The aged cowherd woman
had loved the jewelled woman in her former birth.
Because of that attachment,
she was born as the little daughter
of the servant-family of the One
who lies upon the serpent couch
and for whom the cowherd dance was performed. **(132–135)**

Those who practise goodness
attain golden feet;
those whose love is intense
follow the objects of their attachment. **(136–137)**

The fruit of righteousness ripens;
the fruit of unrighteousness ripens. **(138)**

Those born die;
those dead are born. **(139)**

This is nothing new;
it is the ancient nature of life. **(140)**

You were born by the grace
of the One who rides the bull,
as a king who illumines the great earth. **(141–142)**

Have you not now seen,
as though held within your palm,
the fruits of performed austerity
and the forms taken by noble souls? **(143–144)**

May you guard the world age after age
and live long, O great one!” **(145–146)**

The king rejoiced with the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ. **(147)**

For the Pattini of unfading divine body,
who twisted off her breast
and caused swift fire to engulf bustling Kūṭal
in the song-renowned good Pāṇḍya land,
he arranged the precincts of the shrine. **(148–151)**

He commanded:

“Let the adornment of a daily festival be observed.” **(152)**

He graciously ordered Tēvanti:

“Offer flowers, incense,
and mingled fragrances.” **(153–154)**

He circled the shrine three times to the right
and worshipped. **(155)**

Before the ruler of the world stood:
the northern kings released from harsh captivity;
the kings freed from the great prison-fort;
the rulers of Kuṭaku and Koṅku;
the king of Māḷuva;
and Kayavāku, king of sea-girt Laṅkā. **(156–160)**

They bowed and petitioned:

“On whatever day you celebrate,
in your good country,
the festival-sacrifice of the Himālaya-bounded goddess,
come also to our lands.” **(161–163)**

A voice rose and said:

“I grant the boon.” **(164)**

Hearing it,
the king, the other rulers,
and the tall, great army
praised in proper order. **(165–166)**

With the Brahmin Māṭalaṉ,
who desired the true path
like one who has seen liberation,
and while low-ankleted kings praised his feet,
the king departed for the sacrificial hall. **(167–170)**

I too went there. **(171)**

The young celestial creeper appeared radiantly
upon Tēvanti before me
in the jewelled hall of ancient Vañci. **(172–173)**

She turned in wrath toward the one
who had once told you,
while you sat beneath your father’s cool shade,
that you bore the sacred marks of royal sovereignty. **(174–176)**

Then, to remove Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ’s sorrow—
he whose bannered chariot-army wears fragrant garlands
with loosened pollen—
she said before the people at the gateway of departing day: **(177–179)**

“Lay down the burden of this broad earth.
Far beyond the reach of troubled thought,
rule as king in the realm of endless bliss.” **(180–182)**

Thus the young creeper of the immortals
told me my own destiny;
and in noble words she told her own. **(183–184)**

You worthy people who have listened clearly— **(185)**

put away attachment and affliction in proper measure. **(186)**

Know the divine;
cherish those who have attained clear understanding. **(187)**

Fear false speech;
honour words that do not slander others. **(188)**

Abstain from eating flesh;
withdraw from killing living beings. **(189)**

Give gifts;
undertake many forms of austerity. **(190)**

Do not destroy gratitude for benefits received;
despise evil friendship. **(191)**

Do not go to bear false witness;
do not depart from words of truth and substance. **(192)**

Approach and never leave assemblies of the righteous;
avoid and depart from assemblies of the unrighteous. **(193–194)**

Fear another’s spouse;
protect every life that has fallen into distress. **(195)**

Desire the house of righteousness;
cast away what is not righteous. **(196)**

With deliberate resolve avoid:
liquor,
theft,
misdirected desire,
falsehood,
and idle gatherings of empty speech. **(197–198)**

Youth, wealth, and the body do not endure. **(199)**

The span of life is not fixed;
what must come cannot be escaped. **(200)**

Seek the sure companion
for the land toward which you must travel. **(201)**

So live in this strong and vast world. **(202)**

## Concluding Statement on the Vañci Book

Among the three crowned kings,
those born in the Cēra line—
the unfailing victors who rule the western quarter,
their chests adorned with garlands—
possessed righteousness, valour, and power. **(1–4)**

Their ancient and mighty capital grew in excellence;
its festivals multiplied in splendour;
the gods came there;
in that land of unceasing delight,
the people possessed wealth
and food was abundant. **(5–8)**

Its songs and circular dances mingled by custom.
With a sword-bearing army that,
in the sphere of exterior action,
completed the work of valour in harmony with righteousness,
Ceṅkuṭṭuvaṉ drove across the surging breadth of the sea
and reached the bank of the mighty Gaṅgā. **(9–14)**

The *Vañci Kāṇḍam*,
which lay composed in correspondence with him,
is complete. **(15)**

# Concluding Statement on the Whole Work

Within the cool Tamil realm of the earth,
whose boundaries are Kumari, Vēṅkaṭam,
and the eastern and western seas,
there are the two divisions:
refined Tamil and regional Tamil. **(1–3)**

Through the five landscapes,
righteousness, wealth, and pleasure
are joined to codes of conduct
shared in fitting tradition
by the two orders of humans and gods. **(4–6)**

From letters joined into words,
meaning rises within language;
without metrical fault,
interior and exterior themes unfold. **(7–8)**

Following their paths,
there flourish in excellence:
song,
seven-note music,
melodic modes,
styles of performance,
stage,
conventions,
and dance. **(9–12)**

Together embracing all of these,
the work contains
*vari* songs,
*kuravai* dances,
and *cētam* performances. **(13)**

By revealing them clearly
in the nature of refined Tamil,
it makes its thought visible
as one might reveal a long, great mountain
through the good shade of a dancing mirror. **(14–16)**

The matter of its narrative,
which continues into *Maṇimēkalai*,
is fulfilled. **(17)**

***Cilappatikāram* is complete.**
