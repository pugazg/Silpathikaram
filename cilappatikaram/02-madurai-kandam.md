# 11. The Canto of Seeing the Forest

Beneath the sacred triple parasol, layered like three moons,  
where the red-rayed sun’s splendour shone,  
they worshipped the Primal Knower seated  
in the rich shade of the aśoka whose garlands hung low. **(1–4)**

Within the broad grove beside Kantaṉ’s monastery,  
the wandering ascetics had spoken noble and fitting words  
in the evening assembly.  
The woman of great austerity also spoke with dignity. **(5–8)**

That night they stayed quietly at the ascetics’ residence.  
Desiring to travel toward the southern country,  
they set out during the last watch before dawn,  
after the cock had called and the fierce sun  
appeared shining in the east. **(9–12)**

They entered a young woodland  
where fertile watered fields and tanks shone.

There a voice praised:

“Long live our king, the great one among rulers!  
May he protect the world age after age! **(13–16)**

When the cruel sea, unable to endure  
the shaped spear he cast against the heavenly foe,  
swallowed the Kumari ridge, the many mountain ranges,  
and the river Paḷḷuḷi,  
the southern king took the Ganges and Himalaya in the north  
and ruled the southern land. Long live the southern king! **(17–22)**

May the sacred lineage of the moon-god flourish!  
Long live the king who wears upon his radiant chest  
the mighty garland of the thousand-red-eyed lord! **(23–25)**

The king who broke the crown-bangle  
upon the head of the foremost ruler  
bound the rain-clouds so that thunderous storms did not escape,  
and ruled while unfailing harvests poured forth abundance.  
Long live that king!” **(26–29)**

Having praised the faultless southern ruler,  
the aged Vedic scholar who had arrived and sat there  
was asked by Kōvalaṉ:

“What is your home? What has brought you here?” **(30–33)**

The great Vedic scholar of undiminishing distinction explained:

“I belong to Māṅkāṭu in the western mountain.

I came because my heart longed for my eyes to see  
two sacred forms: **(34–36)**

first, the Lord whose chest is the dwelling of the goddess—  
lying upon his serpent bed of a thousand spreading hoods,  
worshipped by multitudes,  
on the great island embraced by the wide-waved Kāviri,  
like a blue cloud spread without moving  
upon a long golden mountain streaked with milk; **(37–42)**

and second, the red-eyed Tall One standing  
upon the summit of the swelling-watered Vēṅkaṭam mountain,  
where the broad-rayed sun and moon shine on either side,  
like a dark and beautiful cloud wearing a lightning-creeper,  
bearing a radiant bow,  
holding in lotus hands the enemy-destroying discus  
and milk-white conch,  
wearing a splendid garland upon his chest  
and a gold-flowered garment. **(43–51)**

My heart was troubled with the desire,  
‘Show these to my eyes.’

I came from Māṅkāṭu in the western mountain.  
Having cooled my eyes by seeing  
the excellence and deeds of the southern king’s land,  
I remained here praising it.  
This is the cause of my coming.” **(52–58)**

Kōvalaṉ heard the words of the man  
who had undertaken the sacred purpose and said:

“Foremost among great Vedic scholars,  
tell us the true road to Madurai.” **(59–61)**

The scholar replied:

“Like a vast land from which the king  
has departed together with his ordered workers,  
the summer elder and the blazing sun-king  
have withdrawn their natural goodness. **(62–64)**

The mullai woodland and kuṟiñci mountain tracts  
have departed from their proper order,  
lost their good nature, and inflicted trembling pain.  
At such a time they assume the form called pālai, the wasteland. **(65–67)**

You have reached that season together with this lady.

Crossing this vast and difficult road—  
with rocky chambers, hillocks, bewildering mountain passes,  
and stretches ringed by full waters—  
you will enter the region of the long tank at Koṭumpai. **(68–71)**

There the rare path divides like the three-pointed trident  
held by the great crescent-crowned Lord.

If you take the road lying to the right,  
you will pass:

the shrieking-headed marā tree, the withered-headed ōmai,  
the scorched-barked uḻiñcil, poor thorny bamboo,  
striped cactus lying shrivelled beside charcoal-black ground,  
forest where deer stand and cry in thirst for water,  
and the difficult tracts of hunters. **(72–79)**

Beyond them will appear the southern king’s Little Mountain,  
surrounded and rising with:

white hill-rice, jointed sugarcane,  
flowering millet, rich-field varaku,  
garlic, turmeric, fine-vined kavala,  
plantain, areca, low-clustered coconut,  
mango, and jackfruit. **(80–85)**

Go around that mountain to the right  
and continue toward the great city.

If you travel by the left-hand road,  
you will pass wide, low fields where captive bees hum  
in the cevvāḻi mode, cool flower gardens,  
and forests containing many pools. **(86–91)**

Should you go toward Tirumāl’s Hill,  
there is a cavern that destroys great delusion.  
Within it are three unfailing pools  
of wondrous tradition, praised by the gods:

Puṇṇiya Saravaṇam,  
Pavakāraṇi,  
and Iṭṭa Citti. **(92–98)**

If you enter Puṇṇiya Saravaṇam,  
you will obtain the excellent grammar  
of the king of heaven.

If you bathe in Pavakāraṇi,  
you will know your ancient births.

If you reach Iṭṭa Citti,  
you will attain the accomplishment you desire. **(99–104)**

Should you wish to enter that cavern,  
worship the exalted Lord upon the lofty mountain,  
place his red feet in your mind,  
and circle the mountain three times in reverence. **(105–108)**

Then, where the Silampāṟu falls cleaving the earth,  
a bangled-armed woman will appear  
beside a fragrant flowering kaṉṉikāram tree,  
her five-fold hair like a storm-cloud  
split by a golden lightning-creeper. **(109–113)**

She will say:

‘Tell me what brings happiness in this life,  
what brings happiness in the next,  
and what righteous state stands beyond  
both this world and the other.

I am Varōttamai, who lives at the mountain’s foot.  
I belong to those who answer.’ **(114–118)**

If you answer, she will say,  
‘I have opened the door for people of perfected insight.’

Past the door she opens, along the good path she shows,  
are many gateways that must be passed.  
Among them is one closely fitted gate. **(119–122)**

Above it, a woman like a painted flower-creeper will appear and say:

‘Tell me what happiness is at the end.  
Then you will obtain here the thing you cherish.’

Even if you give no answer, she will cause you no pain;  
she will send you away along the outer long road. **(123–127)**

If anyone answers, she will lead them to the shores  
of the three pools already described and depart.

Then take into your minds, as a single recitation,  
the two mantras formed from the letters  
that arise in groups of five and eight  
within the tradition of the rare Vedas. **(128–132)**

If you desire one chosen thing,  
those mantras possess the tradition by which it may be seen.

But do not think of those other objects.  
Set your heart upon the golden lotus feet  
of the One who stands upon the mountain. **(133–136)**

If your mind becomes one with him,  
his long bird-emblazoned banner  
will appear joined before you.

After it appears, worship his twin flower feet.  
You will reach the bliss that destroys crushing sorrow.  
Then go to Madurai, city of noble tradition. **(137–140)**

This is the vision found in the wondrous cavern.

If you take neither side and travel the middle way,  
it is the true straight road.  
Crossing many forests interspersed  
with villages clothed in honey-rich groves,  
you will encounter a deity amid a difficult pass. **(141–146)**

It will appear in an attractive form  
that does not greatly frighten;  
it harms no traveller but delays those who pass.  
Beyond it lies the broad road to Madurai.

‘I shall go to worship the feet  
of the tall-crowned Lord who crossed the long earth,’”  
said the great Vedic scholar. **(147–151)**

Having heard his description of the roads,  
Kavunti spoke one firm statement:

“Four-Veda scholar whose purpose tends toward good,  
we have no need to enter the cavern. **(152–154)**

The realities shown in the book  
that Indra once gave are already visible  
in the nature of lived truth.

Have you not seen in this birth  
all that was attained in a former one? **(155–158)**

For those who protect living beings  
without departing from truth,  
what rare thing exists that cannot be attained?

Go and worship at the feet of the god you love.  
We shall travel on the long road.” **(159–162)**

They made their harmonious reply known to the Vedic scholar.  
Then, together with Kōvalaṉ of unfailing purpose,  
they stayed that day in a rare settlement  
and set out again by that road on the following day. **(163–166)**

The broad dark-eyed woman and Kavunti Aṭikaḷ,  
wearied by their divided journey, rested beside the road.  
Kōvalaṉ went along a side path  
to a pool and stood at its long ford, longing for water. **(167–171)**

The forest deity approached him in desire, thinking:

“I shall grant him what he wishes  
because of the love he once cherished.”

It appeared in the form of Vayantamālai.  
Like a creeper cut at its root,  
it fell at his feet and poured out dark tears. **(172–176)**

It said:

“In the fragrant garland, I wrote a message.  
Though I was free from fault, you spoke words accusing me.

Before me Kōvalaṉ acted cruelly;  
Mādhavi became confused and endured heaven-high sorrow. **(177–181)**

Whether they are exalted, learned in texts,  
or wise in the divisions of life,  
men treat the life of courtesans as a disease,  
cast it aside, and depart at the end. **(182–184)**

From rain-cloud eyes, red-lined at the corners,  
she shed white pearls that shone like moonlight.  
With her own hand she broke  
a single strand of cool white pearls.

In grief she abandoned me also. **(185–188)**

I travelled with a caravan, suffering solitary sorrow,  
and reached ancient, great Madurai.  
Those who met me on the opposite road told me of you.

What is the gracious command  
of one whose nature is worthy of worship?” **(189–193)**

Because the wondrous Vedic scholar had warned,  
“There is a bewildering deity in this hard forest,”  
Kōvalaṉ thought:

“I shall know who this five-fold-haired woman is  
by means of the mantra that removes deception.” **(194–196)**

He uttered the mantra of the leaping-antelope goddess.  
Then the forest-ranging spirit said:

“I, Vaṉacāriṇi, caused this delusion.  
Do not tell the peacock-soft maiden  
or the holy woman of merit what I have done.”

It departed. **(197–201)**

Kōvalaṉ brought cool water  
from the broad lotus-covered pond  
and relieved the suffering of the exhausted woman.

As the fierce sun climbed and its heat began,  
they found it difficult to cross the hostile forest. **(202–204)**

Kōvalaṉ, the heavy-earringed woman,  
and the woman of great austerity  
reached a beautiful dwelling in the bewildering wilderness,  
amid a flower grove mingled with kuravam, maravam, kōṅku, and vēṅkai. **(205–208)**

It was the shrine of Aiyai, the flawless heavenly maiden  
with the eye upon her brow,  
who waits to repay the debt of warriors—  
bow-bearing hunters who go to foreign lands,  
win noble victory, and endure the duty  
of unsurpassed courage,  
though rain brings no abundance  
except to those who travel that difficult pālai road.

There they arrived.

# 12. The Hunters’ Song

The fierce sun twisted with heat.  
They suffered trembling distress along the road;  
the little feet of the fragrant, many-tressed woman reddened,  
and she breathed many short breaths. **(1–3)**

They sat in one side of Aiyai’s shrine,  
a place no enemy could approach,  
to ease the pain of their weariness. **(4–5)**

Among the warrior clan whose broad arms wielded generous bows  
was Cāliṉi, the loud-voiced priestess  
who bore the community’s ancient debt.

The goddess entered her.  
The hair upon her body stood erect.  
She raised her hands and cried out, astonishing the forest people. **(6–9)**

In the central assembly of the thorn-fenced hunters’ settlement,  
where the hunters shared their food,  
she stamped her feet and danced:

“The herds of the stone-roaring great settlements have multiplied,  
but the assembly of the strong-bowed hunters lies deserted.  
Prosperity does not pour forth along the ancestral warrior line;  
the hunters have become subdued like a peaceful clan. **(10–15)**

Unless you discharge the debt owed  
to the goddess who rides the antelope,  
she will not grant victory to the bow.

If you desire a life of drinking honey-liquor,  
warriors bound as captives will cross your path.” **(16–19)**

The hunters who counted severed heads  
prepared the maiden of their ancient clan,  
whose head had never been scorched in defeat.

They wound the young of a little white serpent around her hair  
as a fastening cord and piled her short hair into a tall crown.  
From a boar that had torn through a fenced field  
they broke the curved white tusk  
and set it upon her like the young white moon. **(20–27)**

They opened the jaws of a ferocious tiger  
and made a necklace of its garland-like white teeth.  
They wrapped around her waist the hide  
whose stripes and spots mingled upon its broad back. **(28–31)**

They placed a strong bow in her hand  
and mounted her upon a wandering, horned blackbuck.  
They gave her a doll, a parrot, a jungle fowl  
with spreading five-coloured wings, a blue peacock,  
a ball, and knucklebones, praising her. **(32–36)**

Female hunter-attendants followed, carrying:

colour powder and lime;  
cool, fragrant sandal paste;  
boiled grain and sesame cakes;  
rich food mixed with fat;  
flowers, incense, and fitting perfume. **(37–40)**

The road-beating parai, the raiding signal-drum,  
horn, flute, and honour-giving bell  
sounded in a mass.  
They placed her before the goddess. **(41–43)**

They worshipped and praised the antelope-mounted one  
at the flower-offering platform where she receives  
the blood-price of victory.

The weary woman with twin flower-like little feet—  
she whose fragrant hair had lived beside her husband—  
stood sorrowing. **(44–47)**

Then the goddess spoke in possession:

“Is this the princess of Koṅku,  
the lady of the western mountain,  
the maiden of southern Tamil,  
the shoot born of accomplished austerity—  
the single great jewel rising in the world,  
the sacred great jewel?” **(48–51)**

The woman of ancient wisdom spoke bewildering words.  
Kaṇṇaki, smiling a new and shy smile,  
withdrew behind the broad back of her rare husband. **(52–54)**

The goddess was she:

whose head wears the white disc of the moon;  
whose unblinking forehead-eye opened by splitting the brow;  
whose mouth is coral, whose teeth shine white;  
whose throat is dark from drinking poison;  
who, in fierce anger, strung a serpent as the cord  
and bent the long mountain as her bow; **(55–59)**

whose breasts are girdled by a belt of a perforated-fanged snake;  
whose bangled hand bears the trident;  
who wears an elephant’s hide over her body  
and a lion’s skin as a girdle;  
whose little feet lament with both anklet and warrior-anklet; **(60–63)**

Korravai of victorious splendour and the keen sword,  
the maiden standing upon the head  
of the broad-shouldered demon made of two different forms—  
Amari, Kumari, Kauri, Samari,  
Sūli, Nīli, younger sister of Māl,  
Aiyai the red one, broad-armed wielder of the fierce sword,  
the antelope-riding maiden, the green-bangled maiden,  
maiden of the arts and maiden of rare ornaments. **(64–72)**

The ever-young maiden, come in a virgin’s form  
to receive her companions’ worship, bestowed her grace.  
Thus the patterned rite was fulfilled.

## Narrative-song interlude

Fragrant nāka and narantam trees stood thick;  
ā and āram rose everywhere;  
ceṉpu and mango trees grew crowded  
in the sacrificial courtyard of the woman  
who shares the body of the brow-eyed Lord. **(1)**

Golden vēṅkai trees shed their red petals;  
fine-branched ilavam flowers gathered in clusters;  
white pollen fell like puffed rice  
in the sacred courtyard of the woman  
whose matted hair bears the young moon. **(2)**

Maravam, pāthiri, and fragrant puṉṉai,  
kuravam and kōṅku flowered upon the branches;  
buzzing swarms of bees made yāḻ music together  
in the sacred courtyard of the younger sister of Tirumāl. **(3)**

What austerity has this gold-bangled woman performed,  
standing adorned with Korravai’s ornaments?

The clan born from the clan  
in which the gold-bangled woman was born  
is indeed the clan of hunters skilled with the bow. **(4)**

What austerity has this serpent-waisted woman performed,  
standing adorned with Aiyai’s sacred ornaments?

The clan born from the clan  
in which the serpent-waisted woman was born  
is indeed the clan of hunters with arrow-bearing bows. **(5)**

What austerity has this fine-bangled woman performed,  
standing adorned as the antelope-riding maiden?

The clan born from the clan  
in which the fine-bangled woman was born  
is indeed the clan of hunters with bamboo bows. **(6)**

Wearing an elephant hide and clothing yourself in tiger skin,  
you stand upon the black head of the forest buffalo.

The gods worship you;  
you are the Veda beyond the Vedas,  
the flame of wisdom standing without trembling. **(7)**

With a sword in your striped-bangled hand,  
having slain the great buffalo,  
you stand upon the dark, twisting-horned antelope.

You abide as the radiance of spreading rays  
within the heart-lotuses of Ari, Araṉ, and the Lotus-Born. **(8)**

Holding conch and discus in lotus hands,  
red-eyed Māl stands upon the fierce bull.

You stand as the female form  
in one half of the brow-eyed Lord  
who wears the Ganges upon his crown,  
while the Vedas praise you. **(9)**

Then, wearing upon her shoulders  
a dense flower garland woven of koṉṟai and sacred tulasi,  
she entered the dance of the virgin form  
which she performed for the gods  
while the demons withered. **(10)**

Her fine golden striped anklets, bracelets, and girdle rang and rang.  
It seemed the maiden danced the sword-dance  
upon a wooden platform as deceptive sword-bearing demons fell.

If she danced that sword-dance upon the wooden platform  
while deceptive sword-bearing demons fell,  
it seemed the gods praised her dark kāyā-flower body  
and showed a rain of flowers cast by their hands. **(11)**

When the lone warrior of an enclosed little town  
went to seize another settlement’s cattle,  
the white-sworded woman demanded  
that he first wear the veṭci flower.

If the white-sworded woman demanded the veṭci flower,  
the black bird in the foreign settlement  
seemed to utter a sharp cry and reveal the omen. **(12)**

When the liquor-seller refused him drink,  
the warrior, unable to endure it, took his bow  
and seemed to set out for the enemy’s herd,  
while birds followed along the road.

As he went toward the enemy herd  
with birds following the path,  
Korravai seemed to raise her conquering banner  
and walk before his cruel bow. **(13)**

Young, bright-toothed hunter girl, look!  
The good cattle your brothers brought  
from yesterday’s hunt have filled  
the courtyards of the blacksmith, the drum-maker,  
and the fine-yāḻ bards skilled in song. **(14)**

Tender-smiling girl, look!  
The cattle your brothers seized  
while the karantai defenders cried  
have filled the courtyard of the liquor-seller,  
the forest man who knows good bamboo,  
and the omen-reader who interpreted the bird’s cry. **(15)**

Lotus-eyed girl, look!  
The fine cattle your brothers drove away  
while the neighbouring village wailed  
have filled the courtyards of the grey, old-bearded hunters  
and hunter-women whose speech lacks softness. **(16)**

## Song at the sacred ford

We worship your twin feet,  
which grant release from suffering  
to sages who move with radiance  
and to the immortals.

This is the debt placed at your feet  
by hunters of fierce strength:  
the victorious price—blood flowing from the throat. **(17)**

We worship your flower feet, O jewelled form,  
before whom the crowned king of the gods bows.

This is the debt offered by hunters  
who gain herds in abundance:  
the incomparable conquering price—  
blood streaming with fat. **(18)**

As the tuṭi and little parai,  
the horn and war-instruments sound,  
hunters who come bursting through midnight  
offer this debt at your feet, O Maiden:

a face presented in sacrifice  
and the blood that flows from it. **(19)**

So that strangers may multiply  
and the roads may prosper,  
receive the debt of the strong-bowed hunters armed with arrows.

Śaṅkari, Antari, Nīli—  
you who join upon your red-matted head  
the red-eyed serpent and the crescent moon! **(20)**

Receive the debt offered by fierce hunters  
who strike sleeping towns  
to the hard sound of the tuṭi.

The gods drank nectar and yet can die;  
you alone drank the poison none would consume  
and remained, granting grace. **(21)**

Receive the debt offered by merciless hunters  
who wound others only after taking their wealth.

You walked between the maruta trees  
and kicked away the rolling cart  
sent by your maternal uncle’s deceit. **(22)**

After the ancient lord of the Vedas,  
the mountain king of lofty Potiyil  
has entered another’s land.

Let the enemy’s cattle-watch and karantai defence be ruined;  
let the fierce warrior wear the veṭci garland!

# 13. The Canto of Resting in the Outlying Settlement

They entered the ancient outlying settlement  
and stayed there as they wished.

After the possessed woman’s adornment had been removed,  
they worshipped the faultless feet of the holy woman.  
The lady could not endure the fierce-rayed summer;  
her little feet had never before trodden  
the stony, burning forest. **(1–4)**

Yet the strong-clawed bear did not dig up cruel anthills;  
the sword-striped tiger did not attack the herds of deer;  
serpents, demons, prey-seeking crocodiles,  
and thunder did not harm those who approached.

The fame had spread everywhere  
that this was the land guarded  
by the red-sceptred southern kings. **(5–10)**

They judged that in the long stretches illuminated by moonlight—  
a lamp that preserves many lives more gently than daylight—  
there would be no danger in travelling by night.  
Their elders agreed with that decision. **(11–14)**

Like subjects under a cruel king  
who wait for the burning sun to set,  
they waited.

Then the first lord of the southern king’s lineage appeared,  
spreading milk-white rays among an army of many stars. **(15–18)**

The garland of stars and the paste of sandal  
did not touch the young forest-like breasts;  
the cool flower wreath of pollen-rich red water-lilies  
did not join the blossom-filled hair;  
the garland of green shoots and many tender flowers  
did not touch the red-shoot body. **(19–24)**

A southern breeze, rising in Malaya  
and growing within Madurai,  
beloved of poets’ tongues,  
poured pale moonbeams upon the maiden.

“Even in the summer month, you are desired,” it seemed to say.

After the earth-maiden’s weary breath had subsided,  
they looked upon the women exhausted  
by the difficult road. **(25–30)**

Kōvalaṉ pointed with his bangled hand toward his shoulder and said:

“Let the striped tiger wander;  
let the owl cry;  
let the thunder-like bear go without joining us.”

Having heard the teaching of virtue  
from the flawless learned woman  
and abandoned warrior speech,  
they crossed the difficult road. **(31–35)**

At dawn, when jungle elephants announced  
the coming of the sun in the bamboo-black forest,  
they reached the settlement of sacred-threaded men  
whose conduct followed the Vedic texts  
and whose patterned speech did not err. **(36–39)**

Kōvalaṉ placed the beloved woman  
together with the woman of great austerity  
inside a faultless protected enclosure.

Leaving the thorn fence,  
he reached a water place beside the long road. **(40–43)**

There Kaucikaṉ, seeing him, became confused.  
His inner awareness failed and his perception altered.

He breathed like a crane at a furnace, distressed,  
thinking of Mādhavi with great flower-like eyes  
who had endured cruel sorrow  
after Kōvalaṉ’s departure. **(44–49)**

He addressed a kurukatti creeper in a green-leafed arbor:

“You too seem to have lost your blossoms  
and to suffer together with Mādhavi  
in this summer of rare strength.”

Kōvalaṉ heard the student Kaucikaṉ’s words and asked:

“What is the meaning of what you have said here?” **(50–54)**

Kaucikaṉ realised he had found the blameless man,  
went to him, and explained his purpose:

“The master of two treasures and the mistress of the great house  
have become like a serpent that has lost a precious jewel.  
Their close relatives have fallen into a sea of sorrow  
like a body that has lost its sweet life. **(55–60)**

Servants were sent in every direction  
with the command, ‘Search for Kōvalaṉ and bring him back.’

The great city of famous name became deeply distraught,  
like Ayōdhyā after the mighty prince  
left for the rare forest, obeying no command  
other than that of the great king. **(61–66)**

Vayantamālai told Mādhavi.  
Her body grew pale and she was seized by spreading illness.  
She fell upon a furnished couch  
on the middle floor of a high mansion. **(67–70)**

Hearing of the distress of the woman who had fallen,  
I went to her, myself sinking into grief.

I worshipped the twin feet of the woman  
who suffered while remaining there.

With her flower-like hand she wrote:

‘Show this to the one who is like the jewel of my eye.  
Remove his painful sorrow.’ **(71–75)**

Mādhavi gave me the earth-marked sealed letter.

Carrying it, I travelled along the middle road,  
stayed beside the paths, and explained faultlessly  
the regions toward which the man of destructive action had gone. **(76–79)**

The flower-creeper maiden with loosened, blossom-opening hair,  
whose heart was being destroyed by grief,  
held out Mādhavi’s letter with her flower-like hand.

Because the scent of the ghee used when they lived together  
still clung to it,  
and the impression of earth from her short hair was visible,  
Kōvalaṉ could not cast it aside. **(80–85)**

He opened the palm-leaf case and understood its message:

‘I fall at the feet of the elders.  
Let them take into their minds my unpolished words.

Apart from serving the elders,  
I do not understand what fault I committed  
by travelling at night  
with the woman born into my noble clan. **(86–91)**

Let the helpless heart cast away its harshness.  
Praise to you, noble one whose true vision is free from falsehood.’

Understanding the harmonious words she had written,  
he shed his weakness, realised that she was without fault,  
and recognised that the fault was his own. **(92–96)**

He said:

“The meaning of this earth-marked sealed letter  
will appear fitting and honourable  
to those who gave me birth.

I have worshipped the flower feet of my flawless elders.  
Student Kaucikaṉ, show it to them.”

He gave it to him and sent him away  
to remove the distress that had shaken  
their good inner hearts. **(97–102)**

Then he approached a blameless Jain ascetic  
who lived with his chaste wife.

They joined, in proper relation,  
a group of bards singing the role of Antari  
according to the conventions of dance. **(103–106)**

Upon a red-stemmed yāḻ,  
made according to the proper method,  
they tightened the strings against the fretted hand,  
joined each gripping point to its proper place,  
and tuned the three traditional registers  
from uḻai through kaikilai. **(107–112)**

They listened to the song-mode of the antelope-riding goddess  
as arranged according to the master’s rule.  
Mixing their own melody with it, they asked:

“Tell us the distance to Kūṭal.” **(113–114)**

Then came fragrances:

sandal of black eaglewood;  
flower-rich saffron;  
civet paste and fine unguent;  
musk-sandal and the sweet, rich paste  
used in divine bathing; **(115–119)**

pollen-filled red water-lilies and champak garlands;  
mādhavi flowers, jasmine, and mullai grown in courtyards;  
flower-opening wreaths and flower-couches; **(120–122)**

smoke from kitchens and broad ovens;  
the uninterrupted steam of sweet-cake makers;  
the evening incense raised by young men and women  
from the terraces;  
and the smoke of sacrificial fires. **(123–126)**

All these flower-smokes mingled  
with the blended perfumes from the palace  
of the ornamented, battle-victorious Pāṇḍiya king—  
a mixture that enchained the immeasurable life of the senses  
and appeared beautiful. **(127–132)**

It was unlike the Potiyil breeze  
celebrated upon poets’ red tongues.

“Behold—the Madurai breeze has come!  
The prosperous ancient city is not very far.

Even if we cross by a solitary water channel,  
there will be no one to stop us,” they said. **(133–138)**

Following the former arrangement,  
they set out again during the night  
with the ancient ascetic woman.

They heard the city’s morning sounds:

the deep roar of the great temple  
of the fierce and difficult god;  
the resounding temple of the famous king;  
the thunderous morning drum  
of the great shrine rich in diverse music; **(139–143)**

the chanting practised by four-Veda Brahmins;  
the recitation of ascetics;  
the festive drums raised by sword-warriors  
in honour of the king of unfailing victory; **(144–147)**

the trumpet of war-elephants captured in battle;  
the cry of newly taken rutting elephants;  
the neighing of horses tied in rows;  
and the dawn song of paired court-bards. **(148–151)**

The clamour of noisy Kūṭal received them  
like the sound of the rain-bearing sea.  
Their severe distress fell away. **(152–153)**

Along the Vaigai were:

kuravam, vakuḷam, kōṅku, vēṅkai,  
maravam, nāka, tilakam, marutam,  
cēṭal, cerunti, tall champak,  
pāṭalam, and many other flowers; **(154–157)**

kuruku, taḷavam, rich-climbing mucuṇṭai,  
broad-blossomed atiral, white kūtāḷam,  
kuṭacam, bamboo, rich-climbing pakaṉṟai,  
piṭavam, and mayilai, all intertwined and fragrant. **(158–161)**

The river’s curved bank was a jewelled girdle.  
Wide sandbanks, heaped like broad, uplifted hips,  
formed flowered islands surrounded by milk-white water. **(162–164)**

Opposing banks rose like bright young breasts.  
Red petals shed from the shore formed coral lips.  
Waterfall-white mullai flowers were the beautiful teeth. **(165–168)**

Long dark-carp eyes moved side to side;  
fragrant flowers never left the shining dark hair.

The Vaigai—true banner of its lineage,  
the flower-creeper celebrated by poets  
for the lofty order that feeds and sustains the world— **(169–173)**

seemed to know what would happen to the woman.  
It clothed itself in fragrant flowers of merit  
and concealed, restraining them,  
the long waters filling its eyes.

“This is not merely a water-river;  
it is a river of flower-filled water,” they said. **(174–177)**

The swan-walking woman and her lord worshipped it.

Without moving toward the great ford  
where horse-faced, elephant-faced, and lion-faced boats operated,  
they crossed by a wooden raft  
together with the woman of great austerity. **(178–182)**

They reached the fragrant flower grove on the southern bank  
and turned right around Madurai, where the gods dwell.

“That is an act of great propriety,” they said. **(183–184)**

They passed the deep moat clothed in rare defensive forest.  
Black long-stemmed lilies, blue water-lilies, and lotuses  
seemed to know without doubt  
the solitary suffering that would come  
to the woman and her husband. **(185–188)**

Musical bees wailed in distress;  
the flowers trembled in the wind with tears.

The long banners of the difficult fortress,  
raised through battle and war,  
waved blocking gestures, as though saying,  
“Do not come!” **(189–191)**

They reached a dwelling place  
where bird-filled fields and groves,  
flood-watered farms and broad lakes,  
fruit-clustered coconut, plantain, areca,  
and bright bamboo pavilions flourished—  
a place entered only by people who practise virtue.

# 14. The Canto of Seeing the City

At dawn, birds rose crying  
from the outer grove, shining-watered farms,  
and fields where low rays descended.

The world-worshipped sun opened  
the petal-wrapped lotus of the morning pond. **(1–4)**

The sword-bearing Ceḻiyaṉ—  
before whom kings’ heads grew cold—  
awoke lofty Kūṭal from sleep.

Morning drums thundered in proper order from:

the temple of the Lord with the eye upon his forehead;  
the shrine of the One whose high banner bears Garuḍa;  
the white city of the strong plough-bearer;  
the sanctuary of the cock-bannered god;  
the monastery of the righteous where virtue shone;  
and the king’s palace where valour shone. **(5–14)**

Kōvalaṉ went forth.  
He worshipped Kavunti, who remained there, and said:

“I became a man who departed from the right path.  
The fragrant-flower-bodied woman trembled and suffered;  
I wandered with her among strangers in an unknown country  
and came to humiliation, O accomplished ascetic. **(15–20)**

I shall explain my condition  
to those who stand next to the king in this ancient city.

Until I return, this green-bangled woman  
is under the protection of your feet.  
Is there any danger here, Aṭikaḷ?” **(21–24)**

Kavunti answered:

“You have suffered lonely sorrow  
beside your beloved, far from those who practise austerity.

People of virtue proclaim in many ways:  
‘Leave the path of violence,  
for powerful karma will feed you its fruit.’ **(25–28)**

Even if they beat the drum of truth  
with the tongue as its striking stick,  
those bound by the drum-string of desire  
do not receive it according to its nature. **(29–30)**

When evil and painful karma ripens,  
they are overwhelmed by great bewilderment  
because of ignorance.

But learned and discerning people do not become helpless  
when the unfailing fruit of action must be consumed. **(31–34)**

Separation is suffering; union too is suffering;  
the bodiless Love-god’s torment is suffering.

Except for those joined to women with curling hair,  
there is no solitary life for the strong. **(35–38)**

Those in the world who have taken women and food as happiness  
encounter suffering they did not choose.

Seeing this, and suffering through love  
bound to the desire ordained by the gods,  
many have reached affliction without refuge—  
not only today, but from ancient times. **(39–45)**

Do you not know the long tale  
of the one born to the foremost Vedic sage,  
who went with a woman at his father’s command  
and endured fierce grief when separated from his beloved? **(46–49)**

And the king who lost his kingdom in a cruel game,  
entered the fierce forest with his gentle wife,  
and was separated from her—  
he did not abandon her through lack of love;  
nor was his beloved a little woman stained by evil. **(50–54)**

It was powerful karma, not the woman’s fault,  
that made him leave the ornamented lady  
in the midnight darkness of the forest.

Can you say anything resembling that of yourself? **(55–57)**

You are not like them.  
You have gained a life without separation from your jewelled wife.

Go without grief.  
Learn where you may properly stay in the king’s Kūṭal  
and return here.” **(58–61)**

Kōvalaṉ went toward the city.

Along a tunnel-road beside the moat—  
whose radiant waters curved clockwise  
and which lay with surrounding defensive forest—  
great herds of elephants passed. **(62–65)**

He entered without fear of the fierce-sworded Yavana guards  
who excelled in protecting the gate of the strong wall.

Within, the walled city opened like the treasure-casket  
of the thousand-eyed king of heaven.  
A western wind struck through it;  
banners bent and twisted across the streets. **(66–70)**

Women from the outer quarters came with beloved men  
to the marutam ford of the flowing Vaigai,  
its broad flower-islands and white-sand banks.  
They moved boats beside lofty waterfront mansions,  
embraced flower rafts, and delighted in water-play. **(71–75)**

They filled their hair with cool fragrant mullai,  
low-water blue lilies, and wide-eyed neytal flowers;  
wore garlands of cool red water-lilies with spreading pollen,  
linked with expanding white jasmine;  
and adorned themselves with pearls from Korkai’s great harbour. **(76–81)**

They bathed in the rich mud from southern Malaya  
and enjoyed garden sport in the golden-creeper ancient city.

At sunset, in moonlit courtyards,  
they praised the grace of lowered ornaments  
and rested sweetly upon flower-scattering couches. **(82–85)**

They wore red flower-silk around their waists,  
set kuṭacam flowers in their clustered hair,  
wove day-blooming kuṟiñci flowers  
with red kūtāḷam from the Little Mountain,  
painted saffron colour upon their breasts,  
and wore red-stemmed, honey-rich flower garlands  
over bodies dusted with vermilion powder,  
together with coral necklaces and other ornaments. **(86–94)**

Thus they displayed the red adornment of noisy Kūṭal  
to the thunderbolt-bearing king who clipped the mountain’s wings.

Even outside the season when the dark-cloud king  
arrives with the north wind,  
during the cold season prescribed in learned texts,  
innocent women in cloud-touching mansions  
sat beside fires fed with eaglewood. **(95–101)**

Together with their beloved men,  
inside fragrant sandal-smeared rooms,  
they closed their narrow windows.

In the early-dew season, wealthy household women and young men  
desired to enjoy the young sunlight in moon-courtyards,  
when the broad-rayed sun moved south  
and white clouds appeared only rarely. **(102–105)**

Beyond these came the cloud-mass  
bearing goods brought by ships from Toṇṭi:

eaglewood, silk, garlands, perfumes,  
and concentrated camphor. **(106–110)**

Entering the prince’s Kūṭal,  
it arrived for the Paṅkuni festival  
of the red-eyed long-speared god’s bow,  
when the frost-king’s rule had ended. **(111–113)**

The garlanded mādhavi creeper lifted its rich vine;  
groves and forests bore fragrant flowers.  
The sweet young summer entered  
together with the breeze from the southern king’s Potiyil  
and embraced its joyful companion, the king’s Kūṭal. **(114–117)**

Women with full-figured husbands  
counted the seasons in relief from longing:

“Where is the young summer?” **(118–119)**

Then, when herds of calves and elephants trembled,  
forest fire arose in the fine mountain-land  
where the hot season had stood.  
Flames roared and spread.

The summer king entered with the dry wind, ruled Kūṭal,  
and then moved toward foreign lands  
on the final day of the hard-standing fierce heat. **(120–125)**

Gold-bangled women, whose wealth and life did not fail,  
enjoyed new love with men who had given them:

carriages, palanquins, jewelled-legged couches,  
delightful companionship in pleasure gardens,  
chowries, golden boxes, and sharp-pointed swords. **(126–131)**

Handmaids carried evening liquor  
in red-gold cups.  
The women drank and became intoxicated. **(132–133)**

When striped bees sought flowers,  
they avoided every empty place  
in the fragrant garlands.

Their ilavam-petal-red mouths sprouted young pearls.  
During lovers’ quarrels they uttered words without respect,  
yet also spoke playful phrases  
not expressed by the tongue  
within their eightfold reproaches. **(134–139)**

Their red-carp long eyes,  
like opening red water-lily buds,  
sent rich accusations from the outer corners.  
Their bow-like brows curled at the ends;  
sweat budded upon their small tilaka-marked foreheads. **(140–143)**

With prosperous men waiting for reconciliation,  
there was a street that gave joy even to world-guarding kings. **(144–145)**

There were tightly guarded mansions  
in which crowned rulers stayed,  
built with unfired clay walls of faultless excellence. **(146–147)**

There were two traditions, royal and public,  
in which performers knew without confusion  
the measure, dance, patterned song, melody, and rhythm;  
understood the ensemble instruments;  
and expanded performance across  
the four forms of expressive acting  
and the seven stage positions. **(148–153)**

There were:

the head-staff dancer of overwhelming distinction;  
the senior woman who sang the introductory verse;  
the principal dancer;  
and the intermediate dancer. **(154–156)**

Following four distinct and graceful traditions,  
they stood at eight doors and received  
one thousand and eight kalañcu, unfailingly, each day. **(157–159)**

Caught in the gaze-net of women fierce as attacking spirits,  
men let their rare intelligence become something else:

ascetics;  
young men watching for the smile of flower-like bees;  
and innocent guests of desire—  
all lived there in sweet sleep without safeguard. **(160–165)**

There were two great streets of women skilled in the sixty-four arts,  
whose sweet speech could shame both musical mode and its branch-tones. **(166–167)**

There were streets selling:

carriages, pāṇṭil vehicles, jewelled chariot fronts;  
body-fitting armour and hanging-bell elephant goads;  
hide-covered shields, rare battle harnesses;  
curved clubs and long white fly-whisks;  
boar-emblazoned shields, round shields, and forest shields;  
iron-studded leg-guards;  
objects made of copper and worked in bell-metal;  
things finished with fragrant paste and ornamented with garlands;  
things carved from ivory and worked from horn;  
incense, sandal paste, and flower-made ornaments. **(168–177)**

Their varieties could not be fully distinguished;  
wealth mingled in abundance.

This was the market street of sovereign-seeking fortune. **(178–179)**

There were jewellers’ streets where experts examined gems:

diamonds free of crow’s-foot, stain, dark spot, and flaw;  
piercingly fine-cut gems spoken of by treatise-writers;  
stones shining with the excellent hues of the four social colours;  
green-rayed stones whose bodies were free of  
single-coloured clouding, garlands, and darkness; **(180–185)**

paduma, nīla, vinta, and paṭita varieties  
whose proper classifications did not deviate;  
clear golden stones in the colour of polishing powder;  
pure, ray-bright crystals;  
stones that clarified darkness;  
stones with two distinct forms;  
and gems of a single origin displaying five kinds of beauty  
and casting radiant beams. **(186–192)**

There were round clusters named after  
Candra, Guru, and Aṅgāraka,  
purified of every flaw arising from wind, earth, stone, or water. **(193–196)**

Pearl experts sorted:

pearls with internal holes;  
pearls enclosed within stone;  
straight red-coral branches free from twisting;  
and other classes arranged by knowledgeable men.  
The fearful street knew no hostile destruction. **(197–200)**

There were gold streets where experts removed confusion  
among the recognised kinds:

cātarūpa, kiḷicciṟai, āṭakam, and jāmbūnada.  
Radiant banners were raised above the prosperous shops. **(201–204)**

There was the cloth street,  
where hundreds of layers of fragrant folded textiles  
were packed together—  
so fine that one could not distinguish  
whether they were woven from thread, hair, or spun silk. **(205–207)**

There was the grain market:

men with balance beams and scales;  
men with broad-eyed parai drums;  
men with standard measuring vessels  
moved everywhere.  
Though it was not harvest season,  
heaps of grain and sacks of black pepper were piled there. **(208–211)**

There were four streets distinguished by social division;  
evening squares, crossroads, document streets,  
public halls, junctions, and lanes.

Beneath green-creepered cloth pavilions  
where the fierce sun twisting the sky could not enter,  
Kōvalaṉ saw and rejoiced in the guardian king’s great city.

Then he returned outside the bannered wall.

# 15. The Canto of Refuge

Kōvalaṉ returned after seeing great ancient Madurai—  
a city whose excellence had never known migration,  
where the Pāṇḍiyas rolled the shadow-casting discus  
of land-giving fortune and wore the kadamba garland;  
where the justice of the sceptre, coolness of the parasol,  
and victory of the spear shone. **(1–6)**

He entered the grove of the outlying settlement  
where many righteous people with virtue-giving hearts lived  
and told the woman of great austerity  
of faultless Madurai and the southern king’s victory. **(7–10)**

There Māṭalaṉ, foremost among great Vedic scholars,  
complete in the four Vedas and of beneficent purpose, arrived.  
He had circled the mountain of the great ascetic sage,  
bathed according to custom at Kumari’s great ford,  
and was returning toward his companions. **(11–16)**

He entered Kavunti’s presence  
to remove the heaven-high sorrow  
caused by the hardship of his journey.  
Kōvalaṉ went and worshipped his red feet.

The Brahmin of Nāvalam spoke: **(17–20)**

“When the mango-shoot-bodied maiden Mādhavi—  
who had attained distinguished fame under royal honour—  
gave birth to a milk-mouthed child  
and the days of postnatal impurity had passed,  
the aged courtesans said:

‘Let us establish an auspicious, noble name  
for Mādhavi’s daughter.’ **(21–27)**

They heard a delightful story:

‘In the midnight darkness, long ago,  
our king’s ship was broken  
upon the great wave-hurling sea.

Because he had formerly performed meritorious charity,  
he swam for several days though there was no shore to approach. **(28–31)**

By Indra’s command I dwell here.  
I have come—do not fear.  
I am Maṇimēkalai.

Because your great charity remains firm,  
leave suffering and cross beyond the sea of sorrow.’

She carried him by supernatural power  
and ended his distress. **(32–36)**

‘Give the child here the name  
of our clan-goddess,’ they said.

A thousand ornamented courtesans blessed her:  
‘Maṇimēkalai!’

On that day, together with the auspicious maiden Mādhavi,  
Kōvalaṉ poured a rain of red gold from his hand. **(37–41)**

A man who came to receive charity  
according to the good path of wisdom  
was an aged, stooping Brahmin,  
leaning upon a staff with faltering steps. **(42–45)**

A swift elephant, coming amid drumbeats  
after escaping its keepers,  
seized the Brahmin in its fury.

Kōvalaṉ sprang up quickly,  
freed the high-born man from the elephant’s grasp,  
entered the place from which the hand had been withdrawn,  
and restrained the curved, deceitful trunk  
between the white tusks. **(46–50)**

Seated upon the animal’s neck,  
he subdued the battle-mad elephant  
whose great anger had not changed,  
like a supernatural man upon a black mountain.

Warrior of compassion! **(51–53)**

Another time, a housewife despised a young mongoose  
and, when it had grown distant, followed it in grief.

Her husband, a great Vedic scholar, left for the north, saying:

‘A life supported by food from your hand  
is not proper for me.

Take this good palm-leaf written in northern speech  
and give it to people who understand obligation.’ **(54–59)**

She wandered from house to house  
through mansioned streets of great merchants  
in the marketplace, crying:

‘Accept the fruit of my destructive act!’

Kōvalaṉ called the rare Vedic woman near and asked:

“What misfortune has come upon you?” **(60–64)**

She told him the heaven-high grief she had suffered:

‘Take this leaf on which the matter is written.  
Give me wealth from your hand  
and remove my cruel sorrow.’

He said:

“Do not fear. I shall end your rare suffering.  
Let the sorrow within your heart depart.” **(65–69)**

Following the written rule in the Brahmins’ explanatory texts,  
he gave charity so that the woman  
who had committed the harmful act might be freed from sorrow.

He removed her grief, brought back  
the husband who had gone to the forest,  
gave them unfailing wealth of substantial value,  
and established them upon a good path. **(70–75)**

Another time, a chaste woman of inexhaustible wealth  
was falsely accused.

A poor man gave false testimony  
about her husband without knowing the facts,  
and was seized in the stained noose  
of the spirit that devours false accusers. **(76–79)**

Seeing the terrible suffering of the captured man,  
his mother ran swiftly to the binding noose and cried:

“Take my life here—give back his life!”

The great and righteous spirit refused:

“I possess no nature by which I may take a good life  
and grant it to a hell-bound one,  
causing that good life to lose its higher destiny.  
Abandon your intention.” **(80–86)**

It struck down the guilty life before her.  
Kōvalaṉ went away with the woman  
whose heart was being destroyed.

He relieved the hunger-sickness  
of the dead man’s relatives, attached families,  
and dependent kin,  
and supported them for many years—  
a noble guardian of households. **(87–90)**

These good deeds you performed in this life are known to me.

What fruit of another life has made you,  
Vṛddha Gopāla, wander alone  
with the beautiful shoot of the great sacred jewel?” **(91–94)**

Kōvalaṉ answered:

“In the middle of night I saw a dream  
as vivid as waking:

because of a petty man,  
within the guarded city of the king,  
the fragrant-haired woman trembled in distress;  
I was stripped of my garment  
and mounted upon a horned animal. **(95–99)**

Together with the ornamented, curling-haired woman,  
I attained the state of those freed from bondage.

The flower-arrowed Love-god cast his bow  
upon empty earth and lamented helplessly  
when Mādhavi gave Maṇimēkalai  
to the righteous man beneath the radiant Bodhi tree. **(100–104)**

I saw this dream in the midnight watch as though awake.  
It will happen swiftly here.”

The woman of great austerity and the foremost Vedic scholar said:

“This outer settlement is not a fitting place  
except for people of the path of virtue. **(105–108)**

Those next to the king will receive you within the inner city  
when they learn your story.  
Do not remain here.

Before the sun’s rays depart,  
enter the great mansioned city of Madurai  
together with your beloved.” **(109–113)**

While they were speaking to Kōvalaṉ,  
Mātari, an elder woman of the cowherds, came.

She had given a milk-offering  
to Pūṅkaṇ Iyakkī in the outlying ancient settlement  
where many righteous people of virtuous hearts lived. **(114–118)**

She worshipped Kavunti’s feet.

Kavunti thought:

“The life of cowherds,  
who protect cows and give the yield of the herd,  
contains no cruelty.

This old woman is faultless, upright, and compassionate.  
There will be no danger  
in placing the maiden with Mātari.” **(119–124)**

She said:

“Mātari, listen.

If the merchants of this woman’s husband’s clan  
hear the name of his father,  
they will receive him like men gaining rare treasure  
and settle him with the broad dark-eyed woman  
in a fragrant household. **(125–129)**

Until he enters the mansion of those wealthy people,  
I give her as a refuge to you, woman of the herding clan.

Bathe this auspicious maiden in good water;  
paint collyrium upon her long red-carp eyes;  
place a few flowers in her sweet, soft hair;  
clothe her in pure garments. **(130–134)**

Become for this ornamented woman  
her companions, her guardian, and her mother.  
Support her here.

The earth-maiden has never known  
the coloured little feet  
of this young creeper who came with me. **(135–138)**

Under the fierce sun’s heat,  
she suffered trembling pain for her beloved,  
her tongue dried and her body withered,  
yet the noble flower-creeper did not notice her own suffering. **(139–141)**

Apart from this goddess—  
who has undertaken the heavy vow of chastity,  
indispensable to loving women—  
we have seen no deity possessing such splendour. **(142–144)**

Have you not heard the good ancient saying:

‘In a land where chaste women dwell,  
the sky does not lie,  
abundance does not fail,  
and the victory of long-earth kings does not decay’? **(145–148)**

Listen: even a small refuge offered to an ascetic  
brings immense joy.

In the city at the Kāviri’s estuary,  
beneath the sacred shade of the flower-spreading aśoka,  
world-renouncing ascetics sat together  
upon a radiant stone platform  
while wandering teachers expounded virtue. **(149–155)**

Before them appeared a heaven-strong being  
of shining form, garlanded and adorned with golden ornaments,  
worshipped by many though unseen by ordinary people.  
One hand retained the small hand of a black-fingered monkey. **(156–160)**

All the lay devotees worshipped the wandering ascetics and asked:

“What is the reason for this being’s appearance?”

The divine teacher explained:

“In the house of Eṭṭi Cāyalaṉ,  
into which many fasting mendicants entered,  
the mistress of the great house  
received a foremost ascetic at the proper time  
so that every fault might be removed. **(161–166)**

A little village monkey slipped inside,  
reached the feet of the milk-disciplined ascetic,  
ate a little of the food remaining after his meal,  
drank the poured water with unending thirst,  
and looked upon his face with a joyful expression. **(167–171)**

The steadfast wise man delighted in it and said:

“House-mistress, guard it as one of your children.”

She preserved the great one’s truthful command.  
When the beloved monkey reached its final day,  
she assigned one share to it whenever she gave charity, saying:

“May its evil be removed.” **(172–177)**

Because of this, it was born in the good central country  
as the son of Uttara Kauttaṉ in Vāraṇam.  
It excelled in form, sacred fortune, and understanding  
and performed many powerful charities. **(178–182)**

After it died in its thirty-second year,  
it gained the form of a heavenly being.

All the great fruit of the fortune it obtained  
was due to the excellence of the charity  
performed by the woman who protected and gave refuge. **(183–185)**

To reveal that truth, the being retained in one part  
the little hand of the monkey from its former birth.

‘This form was produced by the charity  
of Cāyalaṉ’s wife—know this,’  
it said and showed itself to all the lay devotees. **(186–190)**

Thus a divine youth appeared.

Those who had entered the path of virtue,  
taking the wandering ascetics’ good teaching as protection—  
the great ascetics in that city,  
the lay devotees of disciplined lives,  
Eṭṭi and his wife who made the gift—  
all reached the final world of unbroken joy. **(191–198)**

You have heard it.  
Now do not delay with the garland-haired woman. Go!”

Kavunti spoke. Mātari rejoiced and praised her. **(199–200)**

Together with the mature woman  
of young forest-like breasts, bamboo shoulders,  
and sprouting white teeth,  
she left as the setting sun’s light declined.

Cows seeking their calves gave loud cries. **(201–204)**

Some closely bangled cowherd women surrounded them,  
together with leather-covered axe-bearers  
and men carrying hanging food-pots.

They passed defensive works:

the thorn forest and moat;  
curved bows and mechanical devices;  
black-fingered ape-machines and stone-spitting slings;  
cauldrons of boiling ghee and furnaces for molten metal;  
stone-dropping baskets; **(205–211)**

hooks and chains;  
bird-headed devices, கழு stakes, concealed pits and openings;  
mustard-seed balances and hand-operated needles;  
projectile beaks, boar-engines, and bamboo traps;  
lifting beams, ladders, full-strength arrow machines;  
staffs, lances, spears, and others. **(212–216)**

They passed the gate,  
where the festival banner waved above the outer wall,  
and entered her house.

The cowherd woman accepted the responsibility of refuge.

# 16. The Canto of the Execution Ground

The woman of the herding clan rejoiced deeply  
to receive the rare maiden as a refuge.

She did not settle her merely  
in the cowherds’ common enclosure surrounded by outer defence.  
Instead, she placed her in a small guarded, fragrant house  
beneath a well-made pavilion plastered with red earth. **(1–6)**

Together with closely bangled cowherd women,  
she bathed the fragrant-garlanded woman  
in modest water and said:

“You have come without the adornment  
worn by the women of Kūṭal,  
without green-gold ornaments of immeasurable value. **(7–11)**

My daughter Aiyai—see her.  
Let her serve at your feet.

I have wrapped her in gold  
and adorned her with flower garlands.  
Lady, stay here with me.” **(12–14)**

She worshipped the woman of great austerity,  
removed the suffering of the road,  
and settled Kaṇṇaki in a place free from danger. **(15–16)**

She said:

“What sorrow can now come to your noble husband,  
since the Aṭikaḷ are lay observers of religious vows?

Quickly give us the good vessels  
needed to prepare cooked rice  
according to the day’s discipline,  
together with the tongue-supported lady.” **(17–21)**

The women of the herding clan, without failing their custom,  
gave her, together with their respected cooking vessels:

clusters of rich jackfruit;  
striped, curved cucumbers;  
green pomegranates;  
mangoes and sweet plantain fruit;  
sāli rice and the produce of their milk. **(22–28)**

“Bangle-wearing lady, receive them,” they said.

Her soft fingers reddened  
as she cut many green vegetables  
with the curved-mouthed knife.  
Her sacred face sweated; her red eyes grew redder. **(29–32)**

When the charcoal-dark cooking was complete,  
she moved away.

With Aiyai, who had kindled the fierce fire,  
she guarded her beloved through the skill of her own hands. **(33–34)**

After the wealthy man sat upon a crafted seat  
beautifully woven by skilful women  
from the long white sheaths of palmyra grass,  
she worshipfully washed her beloved’s feet  
with water held in her fragrant flower-like hands  
and poured it into an earthen bowl. **(35–39)**

As though clearing the earth-maiden’s bewilderment,  
she sprinkled cool water and smoothed the ground with her hand.

She spread the tender inner leaf of a young plantain and said:

“Aṭikaḷ, eat the nectar here.” **(40–43)**

She completed, in a single order,  
all the rites proper to men descended from kings  
according to the rare Vedic tradition.

Aiyai and her mother marvelled:

“Is this the dark flower-coloured Lord  
whom Yaśōdā bore and raised  
in the cowherds’ settlement? **(44–48)**

And is this bangle-armed woman  
the radiant lady of our ancient clan  
who once relieved the distress  
of the pure-jewelled dark Lord in the Yamunā river?” **(49–52)**

“The sight of these two cannot be contained by our eyes,”  
they said in wonder.

After the great-natured man had eaten sweetly,  
Kaṇṇaki gave him betel leaf with soft areca nut.  
Then the dark-haired woman came near. **(53–57)**

Kōvalaṉ said:

“Could your soft feet cross  
the stony and difficult road?

Thinking of the fierce, rare forest through which you came,  
I grieve for our aged elders.  
What has happened to them? **(58–61)**

Was it illusion? Was it powerful karma?  
My heart is confused; I understand nothing.

I entered long laughter and frivolous talk  
with men of empty speech and vulgar courtesans.  
I forgot myself. **(62–66)**

Is there any good road  
even for a man whom truth-speaking people reject like poison?

I failed the command of my two aged elders.  
I brought humiliation upon this young and mature woman. **(67–69)**

Without considering it a fault,  
when I said beside this great city, ‘Rise,’  
you rose. What have you done? **(70–71)**

I lost:

the giving of charity to the virtuous;  
the honouring of Brahmins;  
the welcoming of ascetics;  
and receiving guests according to the ancient tradition. **(72–74)**

Will the great master of immense treasure,  
renowned beneath the great name of chiefhood,  
whose former anger did not remain,  
praise me together with his great daughter? **(75–77)**

When I mixed words of compassion  
with the excellence of love,  
it seemed the hidden illness and sorrow in my heart  
would disappear in an instant. **(78–80)**

Yet you followed a conduct without reverence toward them.”

Kaṇṇaki answered:

“My life was one that never opposed you.  
That is why I rose and came.” **(81–83)**

Kōvalaṉ said:

“You left the principal circle of your clan,  
young relatives, household servants, and companions.

You took as your great support  
modesty, innocence, and cherished chastity praised by the good,  
came with me, and removed my heavy sorrow. **(84–88)**

Gold! Creeper! Flower-garlanded woman!  
Image of modesty! Lamp of the long earth!  
Shoot of chastity! Mistress of splendour! **(89–92)**

I shall take one anklet from your little feet,  
go, exchange it, and return.  
Do not become confused.”

He embraced his long dark-carp-eyed beloved. **(93–96)**

Seeing her alone without anyone beside her,  
his heart burned.  
He hid the dew that came to his eyes.

He left the house of the many-cowed herders  
and walked powerfully through the street. **(97–99)**

A hump-backed bull faced him.  
He did not recognise the dangerous omen  
because his clan had no knowledge of such signs. **(100–102)**

He passed the milk-market assembly,  
walked through the women’s street,  
and entered the marketplace road.

There he saw a goldsmith carrying a hand-staff,  
followed by a hundred fine craftsmen  
whose eye-skilled hands had perfected subtle work. **(103–108)**

Recognising him as the master goldsmith  
who had gained distinction in the southern king’s service,  
Kōvalaṉ asked:

“Are you able to value a foot-ornament  
fit for the guardian king’s queen?” **(109–113)**

The goldsmith—messenger of Death—bowed and praised him:

“Though I do not know its value,  
I make ornaments beginning with crowns for kings.” **(114–116)**

Kōvalaṉ opened the wrapping of the precious anklet.

The deceitful goldsmith examined all its workmanship:

the line of golden filigree;  
the settings of ruby and diamond;  
the green-gold hollow work;  
and the intricate design of the painted anklet. **(117–121)**

He said:

“This anklet can be worn by no one  
except the great queen.”

Then he went ahead:

“Stay in my small hut.  
I shall announce my arrival to the mighty king.” **(122–124)**

Kōvalaṉ entered a small enclosure  
beside a temple in the petty man’s quarter.

The goldsmith thought:

“Before the foot-ornament I stole  
becomes widely known here,  
I shall send this foreign newcomer away  
through the king’s punishment.” **(125–130)**

He concealed his intention in an untroubled heart and went.

The dances of the women of Kūṭal,  
the divisions of song, and the effects of musical modes  
had captured the guardian king’s mind. **(131–134)**

The queen concealed inwardly her heart of estrangement,  
laid the blame upon a headache,  
and departed without joining the king.

The king left the council circle  
and went toward the great queen’s palace  
together with long, sidelong-eyed maidservants. **(135–139)**

At the protected gate, when the goldsmith gained audience,  
he fell and lay before the king, bowed low, and praised him repeatedly.

He said:

“Without a digging tool and without a crowbar,  
using only a fitting spell as support,  
he bewildered the gatekeepers into enchanted sleep. **(140–144)**

The thief who stole the palace anklet  
hid from the noisy great city’s guards  
and is now in my little hut.” **(145–148)**

Because the hour had come for karma to bear fruit,  
the neem-garlanded king did not investigate at all.

He summoned the city guards and ordered:

“If the anklet belonging to my low-garlanded queen  
is in the hands of this hardened thief,  
kill him and bring that anklet here.” **(149–153)**

The black-working goldsmith went away  
to complete what his commissioned mind had conceived.

He approached Kōvalaṉ,  
who had become caught in the ripened net of evil karma. **(154–158)**

When the men sent by the king’s victorious army arrived  
to inspect the anklet,  
the false-dealing goldsmith showed them  
every feature of the crafted anklet. **(159–162)**

Some men of rare skill said privately:

“By his appearance and proper bearing,  
this man does not look like one who should be killed.”

The black-working goldsmith laughed inwardly and replied: **(163–166)**

“Spell, deity, drug, omen,  
strategy, place, time, and instrument—  
these eight are the companions  
with which boundless thieves travel  
according to their deceptive tradition. **(167–170)**

If you are caught by their drugs,  
all of you will fall into great disgrace before the famous king.

If they utter spells with their tongues,  
where could we see them, even if they stood  
like the sons of Indra? **(171–174)**

If they make a divine apparition appear clearly,  
they will show the object in their hand and then disappear.

If their drug bewilders our eyes,  
who remains who can name the place  
where we had been standing? **(175–178)**

If the omen is favourable,  
they will not abandon the attempt  
even when an almost inaccessible treasure comes into their hands.

If they devise the method and means,  
they will even obtain the garland upon Indra’s chest. **(179–182)**

If they judge that this place is proper  
for taking this object,  
who there could see them?

If they secure the object at the chosen time,  
can even exalted men prevent them? **(183–186)**

If men equipped with the right instrument  
obtain the rare object,  
who on either side of the two lands could see them?

For them there is no distinction of night and day.  
If their hidden place is discovered,  
there remains no place of refuge. **(187–189)**

One thief stood at the gate in the form of a messenger  
and entered the deep night in the form of a woman.  
He moved without trembling even in the lamp’s shadow. **(190–193)**

When the young prince woke from sleep,  
he did not find upon his shoulder  
the radiant necklace whose diamonds flashed like lightning in sunlight.

He drew the sword at his waist.  
Every time he struck, the thief blocked it like wind,  
showing only a jewelled pillar for the blow. **(194–199)**

If any of you has seen the thief  
who escaped through such skill, point him out here.

Does this man have an equal in the world?” **(200–203)**

After the black-working goldsmith spoke,  
a young warrior with a strong spear-bearing arm said:

“One night during the rainy season,  
in bewildering, powerful darkness  
when the city slept,  
a man appeared, like a fierce tiger  
desiring the ornaments of a blue-clad lord. **(204–208)**

He drew his hand-sword.  
I drew mine.

Wherever I looked,  
I could not see him.

His action is rare indeed.”

The king’s force was disturbed.

“Tell us something appropriate, armed men,” they said. **(209–212)**

Then an untrained, liquor-dazed soldier  
threw his white sword from his hand.  
It cut through Kōvalaṉ’s body.

Blood poured from the wound and spread.  
The earth-maiden’s heaven-high grief grew sharp. **(213–216)**

The guardian king’s red sceptre bent and fell.

Kōvalaṉ’s ancient karma ripened.

## Veṇpā

Both good and evil deeds come near—  
therefore draw near to good virtue.

Because of the fate of Kaṇṇaki’s husband,  
the sceptre that had never bent upon earth  
was bent by the deed whose fruit came from the past.

# 17. The Cowherd Women’s Kuravai

The broad land ruled by the Pāṇḍya beneath his white royal parasol—
the king before whom the rulers of the cool groves of Jambu-land bowed—
bore upon the forehead of the Himalaya not only the fish,
but beside it the tiger and the bow.

In that Pāṇḍya’s palace,
the morning drum now sounded in a thunderous voice.

“Today it is our turn to supply the ghee,”
said the elderly cowherd woman Māṭari.
Calling her daughter Aiyai,
she came carrying the churning rope and the churning staff. **(1–10)**

## A spoken interlude: the evil omens

“The milk placed in the pot does not set.

Tears flow from the gentle eyes
of the hump-backed bull.  
Some suffering is about to come. **(1)**

The fragrant butter in the hanging pot
will not melt.

The young kid does not leap and play.  
Some suffering is about to come. **(2)**

The four-teated cows stand trembling and cry.

The bells fall from the necks of the deer-like cattle.  
Some suffering is about to come.” **(3)**

## Prose explanation

Seeing that the milk in the pot had not curdled,
that tears ran from the mild eyes of the humped bull,
that the butter in the hanging vessel would not melt,
that the young goat lay still instead of bounding,
and that the bells had fallen to the ground,
Māṭari understood that some sorrow was approaching.

“Do not let your mind be troubled, daughter,” she said.

“So that Kaṇṇaki—the ornament of all women upon the earth—
may also witness it,
we shall perform in the cattle-yard of the cowherd settlement
the *kuravai* danced in ancient dramatic tradition
by Māyavaṉ with his elder brother
and with long-spear-eyed Piññai.

Let us dance,
so that the distress of the milk-cows and their calves may cease.”

## Assigning the seven maidens

“This flower-garlanded maiden
will love the man who fearlessly masters
the raging black bull. **(1)**

The shoulders of this golden-braceleted girl
belong to the man who subdues
the red-spotted bull marked upon the forehead. **(2)**

This maiden with hair fragrant with jasmine
belongs to the man who rides
the powerful young bull. **(3)**

The shoulders of this vine-like girl
belong only to the man who masters
the white bull with fine markings. **(4)**

The tender breasts of this gracious creeper
belong only to the man who subdues
the white bull with golden spots. **(5)**

This maiden with hair adorned by kōṉṟai flowers
belongs to the man who rides
the victorious young bull. **(6)**

This fresh pūvai-flower maiden
belongs to the man who masters
the pure white bull.” **(7)**

Thus she pointed out the seven young maidens
who had been reared with the seven bulls of the cattle pen.

Looking toward her daughter,
the elderly cowherd woman arranged them
according to the ancient order
and assigned each of them a musical name:

*Kural, Tuttam, Kaikkiḷai, Uḻai, Iḷi, Viḷari,* and *Tāram*—
names suited to their luxuriant flower-like hair.

She called *Kural* “Māyavaṉ,”
the heroic dark one.

She called *Iḷi* “the white cowherd,” his elder brother.

She called one *Tuttam* “Piṉṉai,”
and assigned the remaining maidens
their places according to order.

Māyavaṉ stood beside the musically rich Piṉṉai and Tāram;
the fair elder brother stood beside Uḻai and Viḷari.
Kaikkiḷai stood on Piṉṉai’s left;
good Viḷari stood on the elder maiden’s right.

Among them, the cowherd woman placed
the fragrant basil garland upon Māyavaṉ.
Then, entering the unbroken circle-dance, she said:

“Our Piṉṉai is the bangled maiden
whose sacred gaze never leaves
the chest of him who measured the world.”

## Entering the dance

The maidens joined hands in the crab-shaped formation
within a perfect circular arrangement
and took up the prescribed positions of the dance.

Then the maiden called *Kural*
looked toward her companions and said:

“Let us sing the sweet Mullai melody
of him who broke the kuruntu tree
in the pasture grove.”

*Kural* sounded low;
*Iḷi* held the balanced pitch;
*Tuttam* came next, strong in due order;
Viḷari, soft and restrained, sustained the lower tone;
and her companion who stood behind
began the song.

## Song

“Should Māyavaṉ,
who used a calf as a throwing-stick
and brought down the fruit,
come among our cattle today,
shall we not hear from his lips,
friend,
the sweet flute fashioned from kōṉṟai wood? **(1)**

Should Māyavaṉ,
who used a serpent as the rope
and churned the ocean,
come among our cattle here,
shall we not hear from his lips,
friend,
the sweet flute made from the water-lily stalk? **(2)**

Should Māyavaṉ,
who broke the kuruntu tree
on the beautiful pasture slope,
come among our cattle before evening,
shall we not hear from his lips,
friend,
the sweet flute of the jasmine land? **(3)**”

“Now let us sing the lovely beauty of Piṉṉai,
who danced with the lord of the Yamunā’s bank.”

“As her soft, slender form swayed and swayed,
he hid her garment—
what shall we call his beauty?

As the one who hid her garment stood bewildered,
what shall we call
the face of the fragrant, tender maiden
who drooped in shyness? **(1)**

Within the waters of the Yamunā,
the trickster stole her heart—
what shall we call her modesty?

He who stole her heart
also stole her composure and her bangles—
what shall we call
the beauty of that deceiver? **(2)**

Having lost both garment and bracelets,
she covered herself with her hands—
what shall we call her face?

Seeing the face of her who hid herself with her hands,
he fell into love’s bewilderment—
what shall we call his beauty? **(3)**”

## One part of the dance

The dark ocean-hued one,
who concealed the sun’s wheel,
stood at the left.

His elder brother,
whose fragrant body shone like the moon,
stood at the right.

Nārada, master of the ancient Vedas,
plucked the strings in the primordial mode,
guarding the praise of Piṉṉai
whose loosened flowers adorned her hair. **(1)**

Māyavaṉ, whose body gleamed like a peacock’s neck,
stood at the right.

His elder brother, whose body resembled a full-blown flower,
stood at the left.

Nārada, foremost among musicians,
joined melody to string,
guarding the praise of Piṉṉai
who bent the neck of the fierce bull. **(2)**

## Praising the dancers

With Māyavaṉ and his elder brother,
with bangled Piṉṉai,
and with the cowherd maidens
whose flower-garlands slipped from their hair,

as Yaśōdā changed the graceful steps
and worshipped with folded hands,
how fitting was the *kuravai*
danced in the fragrant cattle-yard!

“Let us all praise,
let us praise,
the god who rides the bird Garuḍa,
taking up an inner-line melody.”

## Inner-line benediction

“The mountain garland gathered from Kōvā,
the garland born from the sea,
and the necklace of the king of the gods
rest upon the breast of the southern king.

They say that he who wore Indra’s necklace
once tended cattle in flourishing Dvārakā
and broke the kuruntu tree. **(1)**

The Cōḻa Vaḷavaṉ,
the king who dwells in walled Pukār,
engraved the tiger upon the golden peak of the Himalaya
and ruled the earth.

They say that this king of walled Pukār
is the lord whose golden discus
is a conquering weapon. **(2)**

The Cēra king,
lord of fertile Vañci,
entered the threefold sea
and felled the undecaying kaṭampu tree.

They say that this king of prosperous Vañci
raised his mountain-like arms
and churned the ocean. **(3)**”

## Direct praise

“O ocean-hued Lord!

Long ago you made the northern mountain your churning staff,
Vāsuki your rope,
and stirred the belly of the sea.

Yet those very hands
were bound by mother Yaśōdā’s churning cord.

Lotus-navelled one—
is this illusion?
It overwhelms the mind! **(1)**

The hosts of immortals worship you,
proclaiming that you are the sixfold reality.

Without suffering even a trace of hunger,
you swallowed the whole world.

Yet the mouth that swallowed the world
also stole and ate the butter
from the hanging pot.

Lord garlanded with bee-filled basil—
is this illusion?
It overwhelms the mind! **(2)**

O Tirumāl, worshipped by gathered gods!

With your two red-lotus feet
you strode across the three worlds
and freed them from darkness.

Those same feet walked as messenger
for the five Pāṇḍava brothers.

Lion-like destroyer of foes—
is this illusion?
It overwhelms the mind! **(3)**”

## Praise addressed to those at a distance

“With two feet he leapt
and completed the measure of all three worlds.

Then those red feet,
with his younger brother beside him,
walked into the forest.

He destroyed ancient Laṅkā’s stronghold
until Sō’s army fell in war.

What kind of ears are ears
that do not hear the fame of that hero?

What kind of ears are ears
that do not hear the glory of Tirumāl? **(1)**

The Great One, the Enchanter,
the heavenly Lord in whose broad lotus-navel
the vast worlds lie—

his sacred feet, hands, and mouth are red,
while his body is dark.

What kind of eyes are eyes
that do not behold him?

Even the eyes that behold him
and then blink—
what kind of eyes are those? **(2)**

He overcame the treachery of Kaṃsa,
whose heart was sunk in deceit.

As the four quarters praised him
and the forest of armies resounded,
he walked as messenger for the five brothers.

What kind of tongue is a tongue
that does not praise him?

What kind of tongue is a tongue
that does not cry, ‘Nārāyaṇa!’? **(3)**”

“The god whom we praised
within the circle-dance we joined
must end the sorrow that has befallen our cattle.

May the fierce drum of the southern king—
the king with ornamented shoulders,
who shattered the crowned head of Indra
with his thunderbolt-like weapon—
day after day bewilder rival kings,
destroy opposition,
and bring forth victory.”

# 18. The Garland of Sorrow

When the cowherd women’s dance was over,
the elderly woman Māṭari,
whose bearing was graceful,
took flowers, incense,
fragrant sandal paste, and a garland.

She went to the long-watered Vaigai
to scatter them, bathe at the ford,
and worship the feet of the great Lord.

At the end of the *kuravai*
she heard a clamour rising from the city
and returned swiftly.

She came and stood before Kaṇṇaki,
but did not speak.
She did not speak.

Yet her silence itself spoke
to that noble woman. **(1–10)**

“Tell me, dear one!

I do not see my beloved.
Confusion and pain overwhelm me.
My heart breathes as heavily
as a blacksmith’s bellows.

If my heart labours like a furnace bellows,
what is it that strangers have said?
Tell me, my friend—may you live! **(11–15)**

At the very height of noon
a trembling anguish overwhelms me.

Unable to see the man I love,
my heart wanders in distress.

If my heart suffers because it cannot see him,
what have the people said?
Tell me, my friend—may you live!

Is there no refuge, my friend?
I do not see my lord returning.

Has some deceit occurred?
My heart is bewildered.

If my heart is confused because some treachery has happened,
what have those without affection said?
Tell me, my friend—may you live!” **(16–24)**

What had been said was this:

“He was called a thief
who had taken the queen’s jewelled anklet
without paying its price.

He was called a thief
who had taken it without paying.

The warriors with resounding anklets
marked him out for death.” **(25–29)**

Hearing this,
she surged upward,
fell,
and rose again.

It was as though the moon,
together with its cloud,
had fallen from the sky upon the broad earth.

Her long eyes reddened;
she wept.

“Where are you, my husband?”
she cried,
sobbing, sighing, and sinking in grief. **(30–34)**

“When the husbands with whom they found delight
are plunged into the inward fire of disaster,
women undertake painful forest austerities
and suffer sorrow.

But shall I,
because the king has committed a wrong
and the world heaps slander upon me,
lose the man I love
and perish in misery? **(35–39)**

When they lose the friends
whose broad chests are rich with fragrant garlands,
women wander through many sacred bathing places
and suffer grief.

But shall I,
because the king’s sceptre has strayed from justice,
lose the law of righteousness,
suffer misery,
and perish? **(40–44)**

When their great husbands
are plunged into consuming fire,
women overwhelmed by widowhood
enter the waters of austerity.

But shall I,
because the southern king’s sceptre
has departed from rectitude,
lose my honour in this very life,
sigh in sorrow,
and perish?” **(45–49)**

“Look at me!

All you innocent cowherd maidens
who came and gathered
for this well-performed *kuravai*—
listen!

All you cowherd maidens,
listen closely.

O radiant Sun,
you know all that happens
within the wave-girt world:

is my husband a thief?” **(50–53)**

A voice answered:

“He is not a thief,
O women with dark carp-like eyes.

A consuming flame
will devour this city.”


# 19. The Song of Circling the City

When the Sun had spoken,
the radiant, bracelet-shouldered woman
did not remain where she stood.

Holding one anklet in her hand,
she went through the city and cried:

“O women of perfect chastity
who live in the city of a king
whose justice is properly ordered—

hear one thing that has happened to me.

At this hour I suffer a sorrow
that no woman should suffer.

Are there women here?

# 21. The Garland of the Vow

Kaṇṇaki turned to the king’s queen and said:

“Queen of the royal king,
I am a woman driven by cruel karma.

Though I do not know every truth,
know this nature of action:

the ruin one person causes another in the morning

I was born in the city of women

One woman placed before the world
a vaṉṉi tree and a temple kitchen

that flower-like woman

The face of the command-obeying god of fire opened.

like the cool-rayed moon,

the well-balanced scale,

## The king who punished himself

Once the wife of a poor man named Kīrantaṉ
lived alone while her husband was away.

The king came by night
and knocked upon the outer door.

She had earlier said:

“There is no flawless protecting fence
other than the king’s protection.”

But when the door was struck,
she cried:

“Does that protecting fence
fail to guard me today?” **(42–47)**

Those words entered the king’s ear
like a red-hot iron pin
and burned his heart.

Afraid and trembling,
he punished the hand
that had done the wrong.

It became the hand of gold—
like the radiant circlet
upon the crown of Indra,

## The Brahmin Parāśara and the imprisoned Vārttikaṉ

In the flourishing land ruled from Pukār—
the king of deep-watered fields,
whose righteous red sceptre and long martial sword
had once given his own flesh for a dove
and rendered justice for a cow—

He heard of the generosity
of the spear-bearing Cēra king
who had given heaven-like gifts
to a Tamil-knowing Vedic scholar.

He resolved:
“I shall go and see
that strong Cēra of the long spear.” **(55–64)**

He travelled through forest, country, and town,
passed beyond the long-standing Malaya mountain,
and came among twice-born men
united in one discipline—

Brahmins enriched by the three sacred fires,

performers of the five great sacrifices,
and guardians of the six prescribed duties.

After receiving the proper honours,
he won the Brahmin’s victory in debate,
accepted fine gifts,

He reached a town called Taṅkāl,

and rested. **(74–79)**

He sang:

“Long live the hero
whose guarding white parasol
has ripened into victory!

Long live the guardian
who felled the sea-born kaṭampu!

Long live the king
who engraved his bow upon the mountain cleft!

Long live the Poruṇai lord
of cool flowered waters!

Long live the king
Māntarañ Cēral!” **(80–84)**

Children with side-locks and topknots,
red mouths still lisping,
and unsteady steps
left their playmates and gathered around him.

The Brahmin said:

“Little Brahmins with round bellies,
recite with me.

Whoever does so
may take my small bundle of goods
and go.” **(85–89)**

Among them stood Takkiṇaṉ,
the son of the distinguished Vārttikaṉ,
who had been raised bearing the name
of the Lord who sits beneath the banyan tree.

Though his tongue was still childish
and his mouth smelled of milk,
he recited before the others
without departing from the proper Vedic sound.

He matched the learned Brahmin’s recitation
with inward delight. **(90–95)**

The elder marvelled at Takkiṇaṉ.

He gave him
a pearl sacred thread,
fine ornaments,
bracelets,
ear-ornaments,
and armlets,
then returned to his own city. **(96–99)**

Others could not endure
that the boy wore and adorned himself
with such fine gifts.

The king’s junior officials seized Vārttikaṉ,
guarded him,
and imprisoned him.

They said:

“This Brahmin has stolen hidden treasure,
contrary to royal law.” **(100–104)**

Vārttikaṉ’s wife Kārttikai
wandered in anguish,
sighed,
wept,
fell upon the ground,
cried aloud,
rolled in distress,
and raged.

Seeing it,
the firm temple door of the faultless goddess Aiyai
refused to open for worship. **(105–109)**

The spear-bearing king heard of it
and was bewildered.

“Has cruel government arisen?
What suffering has offended Korravai?
Learn everything,” he commanded.

The servants bowed to the guardian officials,
brought Vārttikaṉ before the king,
and truthfully told what had happened. **(110–114)**

“This is not justice,”
said the king in grave words.

“To forgive the error in royal procedure
caused by ignorant men
is your duty,” he told Vārttikaṉ.

He granted him
the fertile wet fields of Taṅkāl
together with prosperous Vayalūr.

Before Kārttikai’s husband Vārttikaṉ,
the king bowed his sacred chest—
the chest destined to embrace
the great maiden Earth.

Thus he somewhat calmed
the woman’s unappeased anguish. **(115–123)**

Through the long, broad streets
of established Koodal,
so that every mountain-like mansion could hear,
the door of the goddess rich in arts opened.

Then the victorious king
caused the ornamented drum
to be mounted upon an elephant
and proclaimed royal justice:

“Release every prison enclosure.

Free all people
held under penal claims.

Whether an object was discovered as treasure
or granted as a gift,
let it belong securely
to the one who found or received it.” **(124–132)**

You have heard how this victorious ruler
corrected even his own failure of procedure.

## The foretold destruction of Madurai

Now hear this also,
braceleted woman:

There was an ancient prophecy that,
on a Friday,
the eighth lunar day
of the great dark fortnight
in the month of Āṭi,
when the fiery constellation joined the heavens,
bright flame would consume renowned Madurai
and the kingdom would fall. **(133–137)**

## The deed from Kōvalaṉ’s former birth

In the good country of Kaliṅga,
clothed in guarded groves,
there were two kings:

Vasu,
whose broad hand held a shaped spear,
ruled prosperous Siṅgapuram,
a city of sweet-watered fields;

and Kumaraṉ ruled Kapilapuram,
surrounded by forests thick with bamboo. **(138–141)**

They were kings of ancient noble line,
wearing full garlands
and possessing undiminishing wealth.

When enmity arose between them,
no trader could safely pass
through the intervening land
for a distance of six kātams. **(142–146)**

Yet, desiring rare wealth,
a merchant named Saṅkamaṉ
carried costly goods
and, with his beloved wife,
travelled secretly like one moving through ambush.

He entered famous Siṅgapuram,
set up in its market,
and sold rare merchandise. **(147–152)**

In that former birth,
your green-braceleted husband
was a royal servant named Bharataṉ,
serving the fierce king.

Because he had abandoned a religious vow
and had grown hostile,
he seized Saṅkamaṉ and said:

“This man is a spy.” **(153–156)**

He brought him before the victorious spear-bearing king,
and Saṅkamaṉ was killed.

Saṅkamaṉ’s wife,
a woman named Nīli,
could find no ground upon which justice stood. **(157–160)**

She wandered through assembly and street,
crying:

“Is this the justice of kings?
Is this the justice of merchants?
Is this the justice of the townspeople?
Is this the justice of the settlement?” **(161–163)**

When fourteen days had passed,
the day came upon which rites were due.

She praised her husband,
climbed to the summit of a mountain
that stood like a ladder to the great sky,
and prepared to join the man
who had been killed. **(164–166)**

As she fell, she uttered an unfailing curse:

“May those who caused us this sorrow
suffer this very sorrow themselves.”

You have endured its binding force.

Listen to this explanation. **(167–170)**

When the karma of a former birth
comes and presses for its fruit,
even the austerity once performed
does not aid those
who have departed from justice.

O woman with long, sounding hair,
after fourteen days have passed
you will see your bridegroom—

not in the form of mortal men,
but only in the form of the dwellers in heaven.” **(171–176)**

Having explained the order of fate
to the great chaste woman,
the guardian goddess of Madurai
withdrew from the fire.

Kaṇṇaki said:

“Until I see the husband
who fills my thought,
I shall neither sit nor stand.” **(177–180)**

At the gateway of Korravai
she broke her golden bracelet.

“I entered through the eastern gate
with my husband.

Now, destitute,
I shall depart through the western gate,” she said.

Night and day,
helpless and bewildered,
she travelled along one bank
of the powerful Vaigai. **(181–185)**

She did not say,
“This is a hollow,”
but descended through hollows in distress.

She did not say,
“This is high ground,”
but climbed the heights.

It was as though the long radiant spear
that once tore open the belly of the sea
and split the heart of a mountain
had cut through the path of the demons.

She set her foot upon the hill of Neṭuvēḷ
and climbed. **(186–190)**

Beneath a flowering vēṅkai tree
heavy with blossoms,
she cried:

“I am a woman
who has performed a deed of fire.”

When fourteen days had passed,
the day of worship arrived. **(191–194)**

The glorious woman appeared in exalted form.

A rain of great unfading flowers fell.

The attendants of Indra,
king of the immortals,
came and praised her.

Together with Kōvalaṉ,
who had escaped the fault of the royal city,
she mounted a celestial vehicle.

Thus Kaṇṇaki,
whose forest-fragrant hair was curled,
ascended to the world of heaven. **(195–200)**

## Veṇpā

The woman who did not worship a god
but worshipped her husband
became one whom the gods themselves worship.

Kaṇṇaki,
who became a goddess
and an ornament to the women of earth,
became a welcome guest
among the women of heaven.

# Concluding Essay on Madurai Kāṇḍam

Among the lineages
of the three crown-bearing Tamil kings,
this book has shown the Pāṇḍya clan—
its warriors with broad, weapon-bearing arms;

the advancement of righteousness,
valour, and strength;

the excellence of the ancient,
victory-rich city’s character;

its festivals of abundant distinction;

the coming of heavenly beings;

the people of a land
whose delight did not diminish;

the abundance of food and prosperity;

the great Vaigai river
pouring forth and feeding wealth;

the truthful sky
showering fresh rain;

the two dramatic modes,
*Ārapaṭi* and *Cāttuvati*;

the line-songs and *kuravai*
that arose at their proper moments;

and the distinctive unity
in which all these appeared,
together with many other subjects. **(1–14)**

It has shown also the Pāṇḍya Neṭuñceḻiyaṉ—
the king who had overcome the armies of the northern Aryas
so that the whole southern Tamil land might behold it—

lying upon the royal couch
beside his queen of faultless chastity,
both fallen in one and the same manner.

With this,
the Madurai Kāṇḍam is complete.

**End of Book Two.**
